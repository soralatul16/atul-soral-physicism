StateDefine half-life.[1]Show
Half-life is the time taken for half the undecayed nuclei in a sample to decay, or equivalently, the time for the activity of a source to fall to half its initial value.
CalculateA sample has activity 800 Bq. After 24 days it is 100 Bq. Find the half-life.[3]Show
800/100 = 8 = 2³ → 3 half-lives in 24 days → t½ = 24/3 = 8 days.
CalculateA bone contains 12% of its original C-14 (t½ = 5730 yr). Estimate its age.[3]Show
λ = ln2/5730 = 1.21×10⁻⁴ yr⁻¹
t = −ln(0.12)/λ = 2.12/1.21×10⁻⁴ ≈ 17,500 years
ExplainWhy does activity never reach exactly zero?[2]Show
Decay is exponential: N = N₀e−λt approaches zero asymptotically but never equals it. As long as any undecayed nucleus exists, there is a non-zero probability of further decay.
ExplainWhy can we not predict when a specific nucleus will decay?[2]Show
Decay is spontaneous and random, governed by quantum mechanics. Each nucleus has a fixed probability λ of decaying per unit time, but the exact moment is fundamentally unpredictable. Only the statistical behaviour of large numbers is predictable.
DiscussCompare I-131 (t½ = 8 d) and Co-60 (t½ = 5.27 yr) for medical imaging.[4]Show
I-131: short t½ limits dose but also the imaging window; emits β⁻ and γ.
Co-60: long t½ = prolonged exposure; used for external beam therapy.
For imaging, Tc-99m (t½ = 6 hr, γ only) is preferred — minimal dose, optimal γ energy.
