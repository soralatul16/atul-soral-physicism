The Second Law of

The first law says energy is conserved. The second law says which direction it flows. Heat moves from hot to cold — never the reverse — spontaneously. Entropy is the quantity that captures this irreversibility: it always increases in the universe. This is the arrow of time.

The Second Law — Three Statements

CLAUSIUS STATEMENT

"Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a cold body to a hot body."

You need to do work (like a refrigerator) to move heat from cold to hot.

KELVIN-PLANCK STATEMENT

"It is impossible to construct a heat engine that converts ALL heat into work with no other effect."

Some heat must always be wasted to a cold reservoir. No engine is 100% efficient.

ENTROPY STATEMENT

"The total entropy of an isolated system can never decrease."

$\Delta S_{\text{total}} \geq 0$ always. Equality holds only for reversible processes.

All three statements are equivalent. If you could violate one, you could violate all. They're different ways of saying the same thing: nature has a preferred direction — toward greater disorder.

What is Entropy?

$$\Delta S = \frac{Q}{T}$$

Change in entropy = heat transferred / absolute temperature (for reversible processes)

Two ways to understand entropy

  • Thermodynamic (Clausius): $\Delta S = Q/T$ — a measure of energy dispersal. Heat flowing into a system at temperature T increases its entropy by $Q/T$.
  • Statistical (Boltzmann): $S = k_B \ln \Omega$ — where $\Omega$ is the number of microstates (ways to arrange molecules that give the same macroscopic state). More arrangements = higher entropy = more disorder.
Entropy is NOT "disorder" in the everyday sense. It's a precise count of how many microscopic arrangements are consistent with the macroscopic state. A gas filling a room has high entropy because there are vastly more ways for molecules to spread out than to cluster in one corner. Nature tends toward the most probable arrangement.
The Boltzmann equation $S = k_B \ln \Omega$ is carved on Boltzmann's tombstone in Vienna. It connects the microscopic (number of microstates $\Omega$) with the macroscopic (entropy $S$). It's one of the most profound equations in physics.

Calculating Entropy Changes

Hot body losing heat Q

$\Delta S_H = -\frac{Q}{T_H}$

Negative — entropy decreases (energy leaves, fewer microstates).

Cold body gaining heat Q

$\Delta S_C = +\frac{Q}{T_C}$

Positive — entropy increases (energy enters, more microstates).

$$\Delta S_{\text{total}} = \Delta S_H + \Delta S_C = -\frac{Q}{T_H} + \frac{Q}{T_C} = Q\left(\frac{1}{T_C} - \frac{1}{T_H}\right) > 0$$

Since $T_C < T_H$, $\frac{1}{T_C} > \frac{1}{T_H}$, so $\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$ ✓ Entropy increases!

Example: Heat flow between objects

500 J flows from a 400 K body to a 300 K body.

$\Delta S_H = -500/400 = -1.25$ J/K  |  $\Delta S_C = +500/300 = +1.67$ J/K

$\Delta S_{\text{total}} = -1.25 + 1.67 = +0.42$ J/K > 0 ✓

The cold body gains more entropy than the hot body loses. Net entropy increases — as the second law demands.

The same heat transfer produces MORE entropy at lower temperature. Adding 500 J to a 300 K body ($\Delta S = 1.67$) creates more entropy than adding it to a 400 K body ($\Delta S = 1.25$). This is why low-grade waste heat is hard to use — it carries lots of entropy.

Reversible vs Irreversible Processes

Reversible (ideal)

  • $\Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0$ — no net entropy change
  • Process can be reversed with no change to surroundings
  • Occurs infinitely slowly (quasi-static)
  • No friction, no turbulence, no free expansion
  • Theoretical ideal — never actually achieved

Irreversible (real)

  • $\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$ — net entropy increase
  • Cannot be undone without extra work
  • All real processes are irreversible
  • Friction, turbulence, free expansion, heat across ΔT
  • Every process you've ever experienced
Examples of irreversibility: a cup of coffee cooling, gas expanding into a vacuum, mixing milk into tea, a bouncing ball coming to rest, burning fuel. None of these spontaneously reverse. In each case, $\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$.
The arrow of time: The second law is the only law of physics that distinguishes past from future. Newton's laws, Maxwell's equations, quantum mechanics — all work the same forward and backward in time. Only the second law says "entropy increases" — giving time a direction. The reason the past is different from the future is because the universe started in an extraordinarily low-entropy state (the Big Bang).

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

State [2]

(a) State the second law of thermodynamics in terms of entropy.

The total entropy of an isolated system can never decrease: $\Delta S_{\text{total}} \geq 0$ [1]. Entropy remains constant only for reversible processes; for all real (irreversible) processes, entropy increases [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) 2000 J of heat flows from a body at 500 K to one at 250 K. Calculate the total entropy change and verify the second law is satisfied.

$\Delta S_H = -Q/T_H = -2000/500 = -4.0$ J/K [1]
$\Delta S_C = +Q/T_C = +2000/250 = +8.0$ J/K [1]
$\Delta S_{\text{total}} = -4.0 + 8.0 = +4.0$ J/K > 0 ✓ Second law satisfied [1].
Explain [3]

(c) Explain why it is impossible to build a heat engine with 100% efficiency, using the second law.

The Kelvin-Planck statement says it's impossible to convert all heat from a source into work with no other effect [1]. Some heat must be rejected to a cold reservoir to ensure $\Delta S_{\text{total}} \geq 0$. If all heat became work, the entropy of the source would decrease ($-Q/T_H$) with no compensating increase elsewhere — violating the second law [1]. The cold reservoir provides the necessary entropy increase ($+Q_C/T_C$) that balances the entropy decrease of the hot source [1].
Distinguish [2]

(d) Distinguish between a reversible and an irreversible process in terms of entropy.

A reversible process produces zero total entropy change ($\Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0$) — it can be reversed without any net change to the system or surroundings [1]. An irreversible process produces a positive total entropy change ($\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$) — it cannot be reversed without additional work being done, and entropy has been permanently created [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Second law: heat flows hot→cold spontaneously. No 100% efficient engine. $\Delta S_{\text{total}} \geq 0$.
  • Three equivalent statements: Clausius (heat direction), Kelvin-Planck (no perfect engine), entropy (always increases).
  • $\Delta S = Q/T$ — entropy change from reversible heat transfer. Lower T = more entropy per joule.
  • $S = k_B \ln \Omega$ — Boltzmann's statistical definition. More microstates = higher entropy.
  • Heat flow: cold body gains more $\Delta S$ than hot body loses → $\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$.
  • Reversible: $\Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0$ (ideal). Irreversible: $\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$ (all real processes).
  • Arrow of time: the second law gives time a direction — the universe evolves toward higher entropy.
