The Second Law & Entropy

The arrow of time

The first law says energy is conserved, but it never tells you which way a process runs. The second law does: it introduces entropy, the quantity that always increases in an isolated system, and explains why heat flows hot→cold, why engines can never be perfect, and why time has a direction.

Why the First Law Isn't Enough

Many processes conserve energy in both directions, yet only one direction ever happens in reality.

Always happens

  • Hot coffee cools to room temperature
  • Gas fills the whole container
  • A dropped egg smashes

Never happens

  • Cool coffee spontaneously reheats
  • Gas crowds into one corner
  • A smashed egg reassembles
The first law permits both columns — energy is conserved either way. Something else forbids the right-hand column. That something is the second law of thermodynamics.
The key idea: nature moves from ordered, unlikely arrangements toward disordered, probable ones. The measure of that disorder is entropy (S).

Three Equivalent Statements

Entropy statement (most general)

The total entropy of an isolated system never decreases; it increases for any spontaneous (irreversible) process and is constant only for a reversible one. $\Delta S_{universe} \ge 0$.

Clausius statement (heat flow)

Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter body. (It needs work input — that's what a fridge does.)

Kelvin statement (engines)

No heat engine can convert heat entirely into work with no other effect. Some heat must always be dumped to a cold reservoir — 100% efficiency is impossible.

All three say the same thing. Violating any one would let you violate the others. They are three faces of "entropy of the universe cannot decrease."

Entropy: The Macroscopic Definition

$$\Delta S = \frac{\Delta Q}{T}$$

change in entropy = heat transferred (reversibly) ÷ absolute temperature

  • Units: J K⁻¹ (joules per kelvin).
  • $T$ in kelvin, and it is the temperature at which the transfer occurs.
  • Heat into a system ($\Delta Q > 0$) → entropy increases; heat out → entropy decreases.
  • Same heat, different effect: transferring heat at low T raises entropy more than at high T (T is in the denominator).
This is why heat flows hot→cold. Hot object loses $Q$ at high $T_H$: $\Delta S_H = -Q/T_H$. Cold object gains $Q$ at low $T_C$: $\Delta S_C = +Q/T_C$. Since $T_C < T_H$, the gain outweighs the loss → $\Delta S_{total} > 0$. The reverse would decrease total entropy, so it's forbidden.

Entropy & Microstates (Boltzmann)

Entropy also has a statistical meaning: it counts how many microscopic arrangements (microstates) produce the same macroscopic state.

$$S = k_B \ln \Omega$$

$\Omega$ = number of microstates for the given macrostate

Why disorder wins: there are vastly more ways to arrange molecules spread throughout a box than crammed in one corner. The spread-out state is overwhelmingly more probable, so systems drift toward higher $\Omega$ — higher entropy. The second law is really a statement about probability.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — entropy change of a reservoir

A large reservoir at 400 K absorbs 2000 J of heat. Find its entropy change.

$\Delta S = \dfrac{\Delta Q}{T} = \dfrac{2000}{400} = \mathbf{+5.0\ \text{J K}^{-1}}$

Example 2 — heat flow between two bodies

600 J of heat flows from a body at 500 K to one at 300 K. Find the total entropy change and confirm it obeys the second law.

Hot: $\Delta S_H = -600/500 = -1.2$ J K⁻¹. Cold: $\Delta S_C = +600/300 = +2.0$ J K⁻¹.

$\Delta S_{total} = -1.2 + 2.0 = \mathbf{+0.8\ \text{J K}^{-1}} > 0$ ✓ (allowed)

Example 3 — the reverse is forbidden

If the same 600 J flowed cold→hot instead: $\Delta S_{total} = +1.2 - 2.0 = -0.8$ J K⁻¹.

$\Delta S_{total} < 0$ ✗ — violates the second law, so it never happens spontaneously.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

State & Explain [3]

(a) State the second law of thermodynamics in terms of entropy, and explain what it implies about the direction of natural processes.

The second law states that the total entropy of an isolated system (the universe) never decreases: $\Delta S_{total} \ge 0$ [1]. It is greater than zero for spontaneous/irreversible processes and equal to zero for reversible processes [1]. This implies natural processes proceed in the direction of increasing total entropy (increasing disorder / more probable microstates), giving time a preferred direction [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) 800 J of heat flows from a hot reservoir at 600 K to a cold reservoir at 250 K. Calculate the total change in entropy.

$\Delta S_{hot} = -800/600 = -1.33$ J K⁻¹ [1]
$\Delta S_{cold} = +800/250 = +3.20$ J K⁻¹ [1]
$\Delta S_{total} = -1.33 + 3.20 = \mathbf{+1.87\ \text{J K}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) Explain, using entropy, why heat does not spontaneously flow from a cold object to a hot object.

For heat Q flowing cold→hot, the cold body loses entropy $Q/T_C$ and the hot body gains $Q/T_H$, with $T_H > T_C$ [1]. The magnitude of the loss ($Q/T_C$) exceeds the gain ($Q/T_H$), so $\Delta S_{total} < 0$, which violates the second law — hence it cannot happen spontaneously [1].
Discuss [2]

(d) A fridge makes its contents colder, decreasing their entropy. Explain how this does not violate the second law.

The second law applies to the total entropy of an isolated system, not to one part of it [1]. The fridge uses electrical work to dump heat into the warmer room; the entropy increase of the surroundings (room) exceeds the entropy decrease of the contents, so the total entropy of the universe still increases ($\Delta S_{total} > 0$) [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Second law: total entropy of an isolated system never decreases, $\Delta S_{total} \ge 0$.
  • Three statements (entropy, Clausius heat-flow, Kelvin engine) are equivalent.
  • Macroscopic entropy: $\Delta S = \Delta Q / T$, units J K⁻¹, with T in kelvin.
  • Microscopic entropy: $S = k_B \ln\Omega$ — more microstates means higher entropy.
  • Direction of heat flow: hot→cold gives $\Delta S_{total}>0$; the reverse is forbidden.
  • Local order is fine: entropy can fall in one place if it rises more elsewhere (e.g. a fridge).
