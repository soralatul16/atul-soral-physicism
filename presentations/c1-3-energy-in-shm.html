C.1.3

Energy in SHM

The endless exchange of kinetic and potential

In an ideal oscillator no energy is lost — it simply flows back and forth between kinetic and potential twice every cycle, while the total stays fixed. Here we derive the exact expressions, learn to read the parabolic energy–displacement graph, and see why total energy depends on the square of the amplitude.

Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy is $E_k = \tfrac12 mv^2$. Substituting the SHM velocity $v = \pm\omega\sqrt{x_0^2 - x^2}$:

$$E_k = \tfrac12 m\omega^2\left(x_0^2 - x^2\right)$$
  • At the centre ($x = 0$): $E_k$ is maximum $= \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x_0^2$ (all energy is kinetic).
  • At the extremes ($x = \pm x_0$): $E_k = 0$ (object momentarily at rest).
  • As a function of displacement, $E_k$ is an inverted parabola — highest at the middle, zero at the edges.
Maximum kinetic energy can also be written $E_{k,max} = \tfrac12 m v_{max}^2$, since $v_{max} = \omega x_0$. Both forms give the same value — use whichever data you're given.

Potential & Total Energy

The total energy is fixed. Since $E_T = E_{k,max}$ (evaluated at the centre where all energy is kinetic):

$$E_T = \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x_0^2 \;=\; \text{constant}$$

Potential energy is whatever is left over: $E_p = E_T - E_k$:

$$E_p = \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x^2$$
  • At the centre ($x=0$): $E_p = 0$.
  • At the extremes ($x=\pm x_0$): $E_p$ is maximum $= \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x_0^2 = E_T$.
  • $E_p$ vs displacement is an upright parabola — mirror image of the $E_k$ curve.
Energy ∝ amplitude². Double the amplitude and the total energy quadruples. This is why loud sounds and large waves carry so much more energy than small ones.

Energy vs Displacement

The two parabolas always add to a constant horizontal line — the total energy:

E_k (teal) + E_p (gold) = E_T (blue) at every displacement.

Reading it: at the centre the teal curve touches E_T (all kinetic); at the edges the gold curve touches E_T (all potential). Everywhere between, they share the total.

Energy vs Time

Because $E_k \propto v^2$ and $E_p \propto x^2$, and both $v$ and $x$ are sinusoids, the energies vary as sine-squared and cosine-squared — oscillating at twice the frequency of the motion:

Both energies complete two full cycles per oscillation; their sum is flat.

Why double frequency? Energy doesn't care about direction — it's maximum at both extremes and both ends of the swing. The object passes through max E_k twice and max E_p twice in a single period, so the energy pattern repeats at $2f$.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — total energy

A 0.30 kg mass oscillates with amplitude 0.050 m at angular frequency 12 rad s⁻¹. Find the total energy.

$E_T = \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x_0^2 = \tfrac12(0.30)(12)^2(0.050)^2$

$= 0.5\times0.30\times144\times0.0025$

$= \mathbf{0.054\ \text{J}}$

Example 2 — splitting KE and PE

For the same oscillator, find $E_k$ and $E_p$ when $x = 0.030$ m.

$E_p = \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x^2 = 0.5(0.30)(144)(0.030)^2 = 0.0194$ J

$E_k = E_T - E_p = 0.054 - 0.0194$

$E_k = \mathbf{0.035\ \text{J}}$, $E_p = \mathbf{0.019\ \text{J}}$

Example 3 — amplitude scaling

If the amplitude of an oscillator is tripled, by what factor does the total energy change?

Since $E_T \propto x_0^2$, tripling amplitude multiplies energy by $3^2 = \mathbf{9}$.

State [2]

(a) For an object undergoing SHM, state where in the cycle its kinetic energy is maximum and where its potential energy is maximum.

Kinetic energy is maximum at the equilibrium position (x = 0), where the speed is greatest [1]. Potential energy is maximum at the extremes of the motion (x = ±x₀), where the object is momentarily at rest [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A 0.20 kg mass oscillates in SHM with amplitude 0.080 m and period 0.40 s. Calculate the total energy of the oscillation.

$\omega = 2\pi/T = 2\pi/0.40 = 15.7$ rad s⁻¹ [1]
$E_T = \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x_0^2 = 0.5(0.20)(15.7)^2(0.080)^2$ [1]
$= 0.5\times0.20\times246\times0.0064 = \mathbf{0.16\ \text{J}}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) For the oscillator in (b), calculate its kinetic energy when its displacement is 0.040 m.

$E_k = \tfrac12 m\omega^2(x_0^2-x^2) = 0.5(0.20)(246)(0.080^2-0.040^2)$ [1]
$= 24.6\times(0.0064-0.0016) = 24.6\times0.0048 = \mathbf{0.12\ \text{J}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why the kinetic-energy–time graph of an oscillator has twice the frequency of the displacement–time graph.

Kinetic energy depends on $v^2$, so it is always positive and reaches its maximum every time the object passes through the centre — which happens twice per oscillation [1]. A quantity that peaks twice per period repeats at twice the frequency of the displacement, hence the $2f$ pattern [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Kinetic: $E_k = \tfrac12 m\omega^2(x_0^2-x^2)$ — max at centre, zero at extremes.
  • Potential: $E_p = \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x^2$ — zero at centre, max at extremes.
  • Total: $E_T = \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x_0^2$ — constant (energy conserved in ideal SHM).
  • Energy ∝ amplitude²: double x₀ → four times the energy.
  • E vs x: two parabolas (E_k inverted, E_p upright) summing to a flat E_T.
  • E vs t: sin²/cos² curves oscillating at twice the motion's frequency.
