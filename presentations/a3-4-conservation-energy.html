A.3.4

Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transformed from one form to another. This is perhaps the most fundamental law in all of physics, governing everything from falling apples to exploding stars.

Atul Soral Physicism

The Law of Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transferred from one form to another or from one object to another. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant.
$$E_{total,before} = E_{total,after}$$

For mechanical systems (no friction):

$$KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2$$
$$\frac{1}{2}mv_1^2 + mgh_1 = \frac{1}{2}mv_2^2 + mgh_2$$

With friction (energy "lost" to heat):

$$KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 + W_{friction}$$
"Energy lost" doesn't mean energy disappeared. It was converted to thermal energy (heat), sound, light, or deformation. Total energy is ALWAYS conserved — but useful mechanical energy decreases when friction is present.

Common Energy Transformations

Falling object (no air)

GPE → KE
$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
$v = \sqrt{2gh}$

Object thrown upward

KE → GPE
$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mgh$
$h = \frac{v^2}{2g}$

Pendulum swing

GPE ↔ KE ↔ GPE
Max height: all GPE, v = 0
Bottom: all KE, max speed

Spring release

Elastic PE → KE
$\frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
$v = x\sqrt{k/m}$

Braking car

KE → Thermal (heat)
$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = f \times s$
$s = \frac{mv^2}{2f}$

Roller coaster

GPE ↔ KE continuously
Speed depends only on height
Max speed at lowest point

The energy method is often faster than $F = ma$ + SUVAT. If you only need speed at a point, use conservation of energy — no need to find acceleration or time.

The Energy Method — Step by Step

1Identify the system — what objects and energy forms are involved?
2Choose two points — initial state and final state.
3Set the reference level — where is $h = 0$? (Usually the lowest point.)
4List all energies at each point — KE, GPE, elastic PE at both states.
5Account for friction — if present, add $W_{friction} = f \times d$ as energy lost.
6Apply conservation: $E_{total,1} = E_{total,2} + E_{lost}$
7Solve for the unknown. Mass often cancels!
When to use energy vs $F = ma$:
• Use energy when you need speed at a point and don't care about time or acceleration
• Use $F = ma$ when you need acceleration, time, or forces
• Use both for complex problems — energy for speeds, forces for accelerations

Worked Example 1 — Pendulum 5/10

A pendulum bob of mass 0.30 kg is released from a height of 0.40 m above its lowest point. Find (a) the speed at the lowest point (b) the height when its speed is 2.0 m/s.

(a) Speed at lowest point (all GPE → KE):

→$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ (mass cancels)
$$v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(0.40)} = \sqrt{7.84} = \mathbf{2.80 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$

(b) Height when $v = 2.0$ m/s:

→$mgh_1 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh_2$ (mass cancels)
$$gh_1 = \frac{1}{2}v^2 + gh_2 \implies h_2 = h_1 - \frac{v^2}{2g} = 0.40 - \frac{4.0}{19.6} = 0.40 - 0.204 = \mathbf{0.196 \text{ m}}$$
Notice: At half the maximum height, the speed is NOT half the max speed. At $h = 0.20$ m: $v = \sqrt{2(9.8)(0.20)} = 1.98$ m/s ≈ 2.0 m/s. Speed scales with $\sqrt{h}$, not $h$.

Worked Example 2 — Ramp with Friction 7/10

A 5.0 kg block slides from rest down a 3.0 m ramp at 30°. The friction force is 8.0 N. Find the speed at the bottom.
1Height: $h = 3.0 \sin 30° = 1.5$ m
2GPE at top: $mgh = 5.0 \times 9.8 \times 1.5 = 73.5$ J
3Energy lost to friction: $W_f = f \times d = 8.0 \times 3.0 = 24.0$ J
4Apply conservation: $GPE = KE + W_{friction}$
$$73.5 = \frac{1}{2}(5.0)v^2 + 24.0$$
$$\frac{1}{2}(5.0)v^2 = 49.5 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{99}{5}} = \sqrt{19.8} = \mathbf{4.45 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$
Without friction: $v = \sqrt{2(9.8)(1.5)} = 5.42$ m/s. Friction reduced the speed from 5.42 to 4.45 m/s — and 24 J was converted to heat.
Friction uses the DISTANCE along the surface (3.0 m), not the vertical height (1.5 m). Friction acts along the path, not vertically. This is why path length matters when friction is present — unlike the frictionless case.

Worked Example 3 — Spring + Height 8/10

A vertical spring ($k = 600$ N/m) is compressed 0.15 m. A 0.25 kg ball sits on top. When released, to what maximum height above the compressed position does the ball rise?
1Energy stored: $E_{el} = \frac{1}{2}(600)(0.15)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(600)(0.0225) = 6.75$ J
2At max height: $v = 0$, all energy is GPE
3$E_{elastic} = mgh$: $6.75 = 0.25 \times 9.8 \times h$
$$h = \frac{6.75}{2.45} = \mathbf{2.76 \text{ m}}$$
Energy chain: Elastic PE → KE (as ball leaves spring) → GPE (as ball rises) → KE = 0 at top. At the top, the ball has converted all its elastic PE into GPE.
If there's air resistance: Some energy goes to heat, so $h$ would be less. $E_{elastic} = mgh + W_{air}$. The ball wouldn't reach as high.

IB-Style Question Set 9/10

Context: A 2.0 kg ball is released from rest at point A, which is 10 m above the ground. It slides down a frictionless curved track to point B at ground level, then along a rough horizontal surface (friction = 6.0 N) for 5.0 m to point C, where it hits a spring ($k = 800$ N/m).
Calculate [2]

(a) Find the speed of the ball at point B.

$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$: $v = \sqrt{2(9.8)(10)} = \sqrt{196} = \mathbf{14.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Calculate [2]

(b) Find the speed of the ball at point C (just before hitting the spring).

$\frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv_C^2 + fd$
$\frac{1}{2}(2)(196) = \frac{1}{2}(2)v_C^2 + 6(5)$
$196 = v_C^2 + 30$ → $v_C = \sqrt{166} = \mathbf{12.9 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Calculate [2]

(c) Find the maximum compression of the spring.

$\frac{1}{2}mv_C^2 = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$ (ignoring friction during compression)
$\frac{1}{2}(2)(166) = \frac{1}{2}(800)x^2$
$166 = 400x^2$ → $x = \sqrt{0.415} = \mathbf{0.644 \text{ m}}$ [2]
Determine [3]

(d) The spring rebounds the ball. Does the ball reach its original height at A? Justify with energy calculations.

No. The ball lost 30 J to friction on the rough surface (B→C) [1]. Even if it passes over B→C again on return (losing another 30 J), total friction loss = 60 J [1]. Original GPE = 196 J. Remaining = 196 - 60 = 136 J → max height = $136/(2 \times 9.8) = 6.94$ m, which is less than 10 m [1].
Explain [2]

(e) Explain why energy is conserved despite friction being present.

Total energy IS conserved — it's a law of physics [1]. The "lost" energy is converted to thermal energy (heat) in the surfaces. The total (mechanical + thermal) is constant. What decreases is the useful mechanical energy (KE + PE), not total energy [1].

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A 1.0 kg ball falls from 20 m (no air resistance). What is its speed just before hitting the ground?

$v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(20)} = \sqrt{392} = \mathbf{19.8 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A ball is thrown upward at 15 m/s. Using energy, find the maximum height. Then find its speed at 5.0 m.

$h_{max} = \frac{v^2}{2g} = \frac{225}{19.6} = 11.5$ m [1]
At 5.0 m: $\frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh$
$v = \sqrt{v_0^2 - 2gh} = \sqrt{225 - 98} = \sqrt{127} = \mathbf{11.3 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Hard

A 0.50 kg block slides down a 4.0 m ramp at 25° with $\mu_k = 0.15$. Find the speed at the bottom.

$h = 4\sin25° = 1.69$ m. $N = mg\cos25° = 4.44$ N. $f = 0.15 \times 4.44 = 0.667$ N [1]
$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + fd$: $0.50(9.8)(1.69) = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)v^2 + 0.667(4)$ [1]
$8.28 = 0.25v^2 + 2.67$ → $v = \sqrt{22.4} = \mathbf{4.74 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [2] Hard

A ball is thrown horizontally at 10 m/s from a 20 m cliff. Without calculating, explain why the speed at the ground is the same whether thrown horizontally or dropped vertically (ignoring air resistance).

Both start with the same total energy. Dropped: $E = mgh$. Thrown: $E = mgh + \frac{1}{2}mv_0^2$ [1]. Wait — the thrown ball has MORE total energy (extra KE from horizontal throw), so it hits FASTER than the dropped ball. The final speeds are NOT the same. The thrown ball has $v = \sqrt{v_0^2 + 2gh} = \sqrt{100 + 392} = 22.2$ m/s vs dropped $= 19.8$ m/s [1]. The question is a trap!

Key Takeaways

  • Energy is ALWAYS conserved — it transforms but never appears or disappears.
  • Without friction: $KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2$. Mass cancels.
  • With friction: $KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 + W_{friction}$. Friction uses path distance, not height.
  • Speed from height: $v = \sqrt{2gh}$ (frictionless). Independent of mass and path.
  • Pendulum: max speed at bottom, zero speed at max height. GPE ↔ KE continuously.
  • Energy method is faster than $F = ma$ when you need speed at a point.
  • "Energy lost" = converted to thermal energy. Total energy (including heat) is constant.
