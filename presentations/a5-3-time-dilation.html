A.5.3

Time Dilation

Moving Clocks Run Slow

If you could travel at 99% the speed of light for what feels like one year, you'd return to Earth and find that seven years had passed. Time itself stretches for a moving observer. This isn't science fiction — it's measured every day in particle accelerators.

The Light Clock Thought Experiment

A "light clock" bounces a photon between two mirrors separated by distance $d$. One tick = one round trip.

Clock at rest (proper frame)

Clock moving at v (lab frame)

The photon must travel a longer diagonal path in the lab frame. Since its speed is still $c$ (postulate 2), it takes more time. The moving clock ticks slower.

Deriving the Time Dilation Formula

Using the light clock geometry and Pythagoras:

In the rest frame: light travels $2d$ in time $\Delta t_0$, so $d = \frac{c\Delta t_0}{2}$

In the lab frame: light travels a diagonal. By Pythagoras on the triangle:

$$\left(\frac{c\Delta t}{2}\right)^2 = d^2 + \left(\frac{v\Delta t}{2}\right)^2$$

Substituting $d = \frac{c\Delta t_0}{2}$:

$$\frac{c^2\Delta t^2}{4} = \frac{c^2\Delta t_0^2}{4} + \frac{v^2\Delta t^2}{4}$$

Rearranging: $c^2\Delta t^2 - v^2\Delta t^2 = c^2\Delta t_0^2$ → $\Delta t^2(c^2 - v^2) = c^2\Delta t_0^2$

$$\Delta t = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \gamma \Delta t_0$$
$\Delta t > \Delta t_0$ always (since $\gamma \geq 1$). The time measured by the moving clock ($\Delta t_0$, proper time) is always the shortest. Everyone else measures a longer time.

Proper Time — The Key Concept

PROPER TIME (Δt₀)

The time interval measured by a clock that is present at both events — i.e., the clock travels with the object. It is the shortest possible time interval between the two events.

How to identify proper time

  • The clock is in the same place for both events
  • The observer doesn't need to use two separate clocks
  • It's the time on the traveller's watch

Common mistakes

  • "Proper" does NOT mean "correct" — it means "own" (from French propre)
  • Proper time is NOT the "real" time — both measurements are equally valid
  • Don't confuse who measures $\Delta t_0$ — it's always the frame where both events happen at the same location

Example: Muon decay

A muon is created and later decays — both events happen where the muon is. The muon's own clock measures proper time $\Delta t_0$. An Earth observer, watching the muon fly by, measures the dilated time $\Delta t = \gamma\Delta t_0$ — which is longer.

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Example 1: Spacecraft clock

A spacecraft travels at 0.80c. The pilot measures a journey time of 5.0 years on her clock. How long does the journey take according to Earth?

Identify: Pilot's clock = proper time ($\Delta t_0 = 5.0$ years, present at both departure and arrival).

Calculate γ: $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.64}} = \frac{1}{0.60} = 1.667$

Apply: $\Delta t = \gamma\Delta t_0 = 1.667 \times 5.0 = \mathbf{8.33 \text{ years}}$

Example 2: Muon lifetime

A muon at rest has a lifetime of 2.2 μs. It is created in the upper atmosphere at 0.995c. What is its lifetime as measured by a ground observer?

Proper time: $\Delta t_0 = 2.2$ μs (muon's own frame)

$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.990}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.010}} = \frac{1}{0.0999} = 10.01$

$\Delta t = 10.01 \times 2.2 = \mathbf{22 \text{ μs}}$ — lives 10× longer! This is why muons reach Earth's surface.

Evidence: Muons from Cosmic Rays

Muons are created ~15 km above Earth when cosmic rays hit the atmosphere. At 0.995c, they should only travel ~660 m before decaying. Yet we detect them at ground level. Time dilation explains why.

Two equivalent explanations:
• Earth's frame: Muon's clock runs slow (time dilation) → it lives long enough to reach the ground.
• Muon's frame: The atmosphere is length-contracted (15 km shrinks to ~1.5 km) → the ground reaches the muon before it decays. Both are correct!
Other evidence: Hafele-Keating experiment (1971) — atomic clocks flown on jets measured time dilation of ~59 ns. GPS satellites lose 7 μs/day due to their speed — corrected by relativity in real time.

The Twin Paradox

Twin A stays on Earth. Twin B travels to a star at 0.90c and returns. When they meet, B is younger than A. But doesn't B see A moving? Shouldn't A be younger?

The resolution

The situation is not symmetric. Twin B must accelerate to turn around — B switches inertial frames. Twin A stays in one inertial frame the entire time. The accelerating twin (B) is always the one who ages less. Special relativity unambiguously predicts B is younger.

Numerical example: B travels for 5 years (B's clock) at 0.90c each way. $\gamma = 2.294$.

B ages: $2 \times 5.0 = 10.0$ years (proper time — B's clock)

A ages: $2 \times \gamma \times 5.0 = 2 \times 2.294 \times 5.0 = \mathbf{22.9 \text{ years}}$

When they meet: A is 22.9 years older. B is only 10 years older. B is 12.9 years younger than A.

IB exam note: The twin paradox is resolved by noting that the travelling twin changes frames (accelerates). It is NOT a genuine paradox — SR gives a clear, testable prediction.

(a) Define proper time.

Proper time is the time interval between two events as measured by a clock that is present at both events / measured in the frame where the two events occur at the same position [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A particle has a half-life of 1.5 × 10⁻⁸ s in its rest frame. It travels at 0.92c in the lab. Calculate the half-life measured in the lab frame.

$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.8464}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.1536}} = \frac{1}{0.392} = 2.55$ [1]
$\Delta t = \gamma\Delta t_0 = 2.55 \times 1.5 \times 10^{-8}$ [1]
$\Delta t = \mathbf{3.83 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) An astronaut on a spaceship measures 200 heartbeats in 3.0 minutes. An Earth observer measures the same 200 heartbeats taking 5.0 minutes. Calculate the speed of the spaceship.

$\Delta t_0 = 3.0$ min (astronaut), $\Delta t = 5.0$ min (Earth)
$\gamma = \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta t_0} = \frac{5.0}{3.0} = 1.667$ [1]
$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}}$ → $1 - v^2/c^2 = \frac{1}{\gamma^2} = 0.36$ [1]
$v^2/c^2 = 0.64$ → $v = \mathbf{0.80c}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain how the detection of muons at Earth's surface provides evidence for time dilation.

Muons have a rest-frame lifetime of ~2.2 μs, which at 0.995c would only allow them to travel ~660 m. They are created ~15 km above Earth [1]. Time dilation increases their observed lifetime by a factor γ ≈ 10, allowing them to travel ~6600 m — far enough to reach the surface. The detection of muons at ground level confirms time dilation [1].

Calculate [3]

A pion has a rest lifetime of 2.6 × 10⁻⁸ s. In the lab, it is observed to travel 40 m before decaying. Calculate the speed of the pion.

Lab time: $\Delta t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{40}{v}$
$\Delta t = \gamma\Delta t_0$ → $\frac{40}{v} = \frac{2.6 \times 10^{-8}}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$ [1]
Let $\beta = v/c$: $\frac{40}{\beta c} = \frac{2.6 \times 10^{-8}}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}$
$\frac{40}{3 \times 10^8 \times 2.6 \times 10^{-8}} = \frac{\beta}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}$ → $5.128 = \frac{\beta}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}$ [1]
$26.3 = \frac{\beta^2}{1-\beta^2}$ → $\beta^2 = \frac{26.3}{27.3} = 0.963$ → $v = \mathbf{0.982c}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

An astronaut travels to a star 4.0 light-years away at 0.80c. How long does the journey take according to (i) Earth, (ii) the astronaut?

(i) Earth: $\Delta t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{4.0}{0.80} = 5.0$ years [1]
(ii) Astronaut (proper time): $\Delta t_0 = \frac{\Delta t}{\gamma} = \frac{5.0}{1.667} = \mathbf{3.0 \text{ years}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

In the twin paradox, explain why the situation is not symmetric and identify which twin is younger upon reunion.

The travelling twin must decelerate, turn around, and accelerate back — they change inertial frames and experience acceleration, which breaks the symmetry [1]. The stay-at-home twin remains in a single inertial frame. The travelling twin's proper time is shorter, so they are younger upon reunion [1].

Key Takeaways

  • $\Delta t = \gamma\Delta t_0$ — moving clocks run slow. The dilated time is always longer.
  • Proper time ($\Delta t_0$): measured by a clock present at both events. Always the shortest interval.
  • Light clock derivation: Pythagoras + constant $c$ → time dilation formula.
  • Muon evidence: muons travel 15 km despite a 2.2 μs lifetime because $\gamma \approx 10$ at 0.995c.
  • Finding speed: $v = c\sqrt{1 - 1/\gamma^2}$ — rearrange $\gamma$ when you know time ratios.
  • Twin paradox: Not a paradox. The accelerating twin ages less. Symmetry is broken by the turnaround.
  • Real-world: GPS, particle accelerators, and cosmic ray experiments all confirm time dilation daily.
