B.1.7

Black-Body Radiation

& the Solar Constant

Heat a metal bar and it glows red, then orange, then white-hot. The colour tells you the temperature. This is black-body radiation — and understanding it lets us measure the temperature of stars, planets, and the entire universe.

What is a Black Body?

DEFINITION

A black body is an idealised object that absorbs all incident electromagnetic radiation (at all wavelengths) and re-emits it with a characteristic spectrum that depends only on its temperature.

  • Perfect absorber: absorbs 100% of incoming radiation — reflects nothing ($e = 1$)
  • Perfect emitter: emits the maximum possible radiation at every wavelength for its temperature
  • Spectrum depends only on T — not on material, shape, or size
  • Real approximations: a cavity with a small hole, the Sun, the Earth (approximately), stars
Why "black"? At room temperature, a perfect absorber looks black — it reflects no visible light. But at high temperatures, it glows — emitting visible light. The Sun is a nearly perfect black body at ~5800 K.
"Black body" doesn't mean it looks black. It means it absorbs perfectly. The Sun is a black body — and it's blindingly bright. The name refers to absorption, not appearance.

Wien's Displacement Law

The wavelength at which a black body emits most intensely is inversely proportional to its temperature:

$$\lambda_{\max} T = 2.90 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$$

Wien's constant $= 2.90 \times 10^{-3}$ m K (in the data booklet)

The Solar Constant & Luminosity

Solar Constant (S)

The power per unit area received from the Sun at Earth's mean distance (1 AU).

$$S \approx 1361 \text{ W m}^{-2}$$

Luminosity (L)

Total power output of a star in all directions.

$$L = \sigma AT^4 = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T^4$$

Sun: $L_\odot = 3.85 \times 10^{26}$ W

Connecting S and L (inverse square law)

$$S = \frac{L}{4\pi d^2}$$

Luminosity spreads over a sphere of radius $d$ (distance to Earth). At distance $d$, intensity $= L / (4\pi d^2)$.

The inverse square law: Double the distance → quarter the intensity. Move to Mars (1.5 AU) → solar constant drops to $1361 / 1.5^2 = 604$ W m⁻². That's why Mars is cold.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Finding a star's temperature from its peak wavelength

A star has peak emission at $\lambda_{\max} = 350$ nm. Find its surface temperature.

$T = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{\lambda_{\max}} = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{350 \times 10^{-9}} = \mathbf{8290 \text{ K}}$ — a hot blue-white star

Example 2: Finding the Sun's luminosity

$R_\odot = 6.96 \times 10^8$ m, $T_\odot = 5800$ K. Calculate $L$.

$L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T^4 = 4\pi (6.96 \times 10^8)^2 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 5800^4$

$= 4\pi \times 4.84 \times 10^{17} \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 1.13 \times 10^{15} = \mathbf{3.9 \times 10^{26} \text{ W}}$ ✓

Example 3: Solar constant verification

Using $L = 3.85 \times 10^{26}$ W and $d = 1.50 \times 10^{11}$ m (Earth-Sun distance).

$S = \frac{L}{4\pi d^2} = \frac{3.85 \times 10^{26}}{4\pi (1.50 \times 10^{11})^2} = \frac{3.85 \times 10^{26}}{2.83 \times 10^{23}} = \mathbf{1361 \text{ W m}^{-2}}$ ✓

Estimating Earth's Surface Temperature

Treating Earth as a black body in radiative equilibrium: power absorbed from Sun = power radiated by Earth.

Power absorbed: Sun illuminates Earth's cross-section ($\pi R_E^2$), but only fraction $(1 - \alpha)$ is absorbed ($\alpha$ = albedo ≈ 0.3):

$$P_{\text{in}} = S \cdot \pi R_E^2 \cdot (1 - \alpha)$$

Power radiated: Earth radiates from its entire surface ($4\pi R_E^2$) as a black body at temperature $T_E$:

$$P_{\text{out}} = \sigma \cdot 4\pi R_E^2 \cdot T_E^4$$

Equilibrium ($P_{\text{in}} = P_{\text{out}}$):

$$T_E = \left(\frac{S(1-\alpha)}{4\sigma}\right)^{1/4}$$

$T_E = \left(\frac{1361 \times 0.7}{4 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8}}\right)^{1/4} = \left(\frac{952.7}{2.268 \times 10^{-7}}\right)^{1/4} = (4.20 \times 10^9)^{1/4} = \mathbf{255 \text{ K} = -18°C}$

But Earth's actual average is ~288 K (+15°C). The 33 K difference is due to the greenhouse effect — atmosphere traps outgoing IR radiation. Without it, Earth would be frozen. This connects directly to B.1.8.

Define [1]

(a) Define a black body.

A black body is an idealised object that absorbs all incident electromagnetic radiation at all wavelengths and emits a continuous spectrum that depends only on its temperature [1].
Calculate [2]

(b) The Sun's surface temperature is 5800 K. Calculate the peak wavelength of its emission spectrum.

$\lambda_{\max} = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{T} = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{5800}$ [1]
$\lambda_{\max} = \mathbf{5.0 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} = 500 \text{ nm}}$ (green-yellow visible light) [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) A star has luminosity $2.0 \times 10^{28}$ W and surface temperature 12,000 K. Calculate its radius.

$L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T^4$ → $R^2 = \frac{L}{4\pi\sigma T^4}$ [1]
$R^2 = \frac{2.0 \times 10^{28}}{4\pi \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 12000^4}$ [1]
$= \frac{2.0 \times 10^{28}}{4\pi \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 2.07 \times 10^{16}} = \frac{2.0 \times 10^{28}}{1.48 \times 10^{10}} = 1.35 \times 10^{18}$
$R = \mathbf{3.7 \times 10^9 \text{ m}}$ (about 5× the Sun's radius) [1]
Explain [2]

(d) State two features of a black-body radiation curve that change as the temperature of the body increases.

1. The peak of the curve shifts to shorter wavelengths (Wien's law: $\lambda_{\max} \propto 1/T$) [1].
2. The total area under the curve increases (total power radiated increases as $T^4$, Stefan-Boltzmann law) [1].

Calculate [3]

The solar constant at Mars (1.52 AU from the Sun) is what fraction of Earth's? Calculate the expected surface temperature of Mars if it has albedo 0.25 and no atmosphere.

$S_{\text{Mars}} = \frac{S_{\text{Earth}}}{1.52^2} = \frac{1361}{2.31} = 589$ W m⁻² [1]
$T = \left(\frac{589 \times 0.75}{4 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8}}\right)^{1/4} = \left(\frac{441.8}{2.268 \times 10^{-7}}\right)^{1/4}$ [1]
$= (1.95 \times 10^9)^{1/4} = \mathbf{210 \text{ K} = -63°C}$ (close to actual average of −60°C) [1]
Calculate [2]

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) has $T = 2.725$ K. Calculate $\lambda_{\max}$ for the CMB.

$\lambda_{\max} = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{2.725}$ [1]
$= \mathbf{1.06 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m} = 1.06 \text{ mm}}$ (microwave region — hence the name) [1]
Explain [3]

The calculated temperature of Earth without an atmosphere is 255 K, but the actual average is 288 K. Explain this discrepancy.

The calculation assumes Earth radiates as a perfect black body directly to space [1]. In reality, greenhouse gases (CO₂, H₂O, CH₄) in the atmosphere absorb outgoing infrared radiation and re-emit some back toward the surface [1]. This greenhouse effect warms the surface by an additional ~33 K above the no-atmosphere prediction [1].
The CMB is the oldest light in the universe — emitted 380,000 years after the Big Bang when the universe cooled enough for atoms to form. Originally ~3000 K (orange glow), it has been redshifted by cosmic expansion to 2.725 K (microwaves). Its near-perfect black-body spectrum is the strongest evidence for the Big Bang.

Key Takeaways

  • Black body: absorbs all radiation, emits spectrum depending only on T. $e = 1$.
  • Wien's law: $\lambda_{\max} T = 2.90 \times 10^{-3}$ m K. Hotter → peak at shorter wavelength (bluer).
  • Stefan-Boltzmann: $L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T^4$. Total power ∝ $T^4$ and surface area.
  • Solar constant: $S = L/(4\pi d^2) \approx 1361$ W m⁻². Inverse square law with distance.
  • Earth's temperature: $T_E = [S(1-\alpha)/(4\sigma)]^{1/4} = 255$ K without greenhouse effect.
  • Actual 288 K — the 33 K difference is from greenhouse gases trapping IR radiation.
  • Star analysis: measure $\lambda_{\max}$ → find $T$ (Wien's). Know $L$ and $T$ → find $R$.
  • CMB: 2.725 K black-body radiation — echo of the Big Bang.
