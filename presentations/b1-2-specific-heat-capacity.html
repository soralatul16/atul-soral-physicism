B.1.2

Specific Heat Capacity

How Much Energy to Heat Things Up

Why does a metal spoon get burning hot in seconds while the soup stays warm for ages? Why does sand scorch your feet but the sea stays cool? The answer is specific heat capacity — a material's resistance to temperature change.

Definition: The energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C).

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Example 1: Heating water in a kettle

How much energy is needed to heat 2.0 kg of water from 15°C to 100°C?

Identify: $m = 2.0$ kg, $c = 4186$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, $\Delta T = 100 - 15 = 85$ K

Calculate: $Q = mc\Delta T = 2.0 \times 4186 \times 85 = \mathbf{711\,620 \text{ J} \approx 712 \text{ kJ}}$

Example 2: Finding specific heat capacity

A 0.40 kg metal block absorbs 9000 J and its temperature rises from 20°C to 70°C. Find c and identify the metal.

Rearrange: $c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta T} = \frac{9000}{0.40 \times 50} = \frac{9000}{20} = \mathbf{450 \text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}}$

Identify: $c = 450$ → Iron / Steel

Example 3: With a heater (linking power)

A 500 W heater runs for 3 minutes to heat 0.50 kg of oil from 20°C to 56°C. Find c for the oil.

$Q = Pt = 500 \times 180 = 90\,000$ J  |  $c = \frac{90000}{0.50 \times 36} = \mathbf{5000 \text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}}$

Exam tip: When a heater is involved, $Q = Pt$ (power × time). When current and voltage are given, $Q = VIt$. Always convert time to seconds.

Mixing & Thermal Equilibrium Problems

When a hot object is placed in cold liquid, energy flows until both reach the same final temperature:

$$Q_{\text{lost}} = Q_{\text{gained}}$$
$$m_1 c_1 (T_1 - T_f) = m_2 c_2 (T_f - T_2)$$

Example: Hot iron in cold water

A 0.25 kg iron block at 300°C is dropped into 0.80 kg of water at 20°C. Find the final temperature. ($c_{\text{Fe}} = 450$, $c_w = 4186$)

$m_{\text{Fe}} c_{\text{Fe}} (300 - T_f) = m_w c_w (T_f - 20)$

$0.25 \times 450 \times (300 - T_f) = 0.80 \times 4186 \times (T_f - 20)$

$112.5(300 - T_f) = 3348.8(T_f - 20)$

$33750 - 112.5T_f = 3348.8T_f - 66976$ → $100726 = 3461.3T_f$ → $T_f = \mathbf{29.1°C}$

Why so close to water's temperature? Water has ~9× the specific heat capacity of iron AND 3× the mass. The same energy transfer causes a huge drop in iron's temperature but barely raises water's. Always check: final T should be between the two initial temperatures, closer to the substance with larger $mc$.

Measuring c Experimentally

Electrical method (solid block)

  1. Weigh the block ($m$)
  2. Insert heater + thermometer
  3. Record initial temperature ($T_1$)
  4. Heat for time $t$ at power $P = VI$
  5. Record final temperature ($T_2$)
  6. $c = \frac{VIt}{m(T_2 - T_1)}$

Method of mixtures (liquid)

  1. Heat a metal block to known $T$
  2. Drop it into known mass of liquid at known $T$
  3. Stir and record final $T$
  4. $m_{\text{metal}}c_{\text{metal}}\Delta T_{\text{metal}} = m_{\text{liquid}}c_{\text{liquid}}\Delta T_{\text{liquid}}$
  5. Solve for unknown c

Sources of error (IB loves these)

  • Heat loss to surroundings — measured c will be too high (more energy needed than theory)
  • Thermometer lag — reading may not reflect true temperature
  • Heat absorbed by container — energy goes into the calorimeter, not just the substance
  • Impure or wet samples — water contamination raises apparent c
IB improvement: Use insulation (polystyrene cup, lagging), stir continuously, use a lid, start slightly below room temperature and finish above (so heat gained from surroundings ≈ heat lost).

Calculate [3]

(b) A 3.0 kW heater heats 5.0 kg of water from 18°C to 100°C. Calculate the minimum time required. ($c_w = 4186$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹)

$Q = mc\Delta T = 5.0 \times 4186 \times 82 = 1\,716\,260$ J [1]
$t = \frac{Q}{P} = \frac{1716260}{3000}$ [1]
$t = \mathbf{572 \text{ s} \approx 9.5 \text{ min}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) 0.15 kg of copper ($c = 385$) at 200°C is placed in 0.30 kg of water ($c = 4186$) at 15°C in an insulated container. Calculate the final equilibrium temperature.

$m_{\text{Cu}}c_{\text{Cu}}(200 - T_f) = m_w c_w(T_f - 15)$
$0.15 \times 385 \times (200 - T_f) = 0.30 \times 4186 \times (T_f - 15)$ [1]
$57.75(200 - T_f) = 1255.8(T_f - 15)$
$11550 - 57.75T_f = 1255.8T_f - 18837$ [1]
$30387 = 1313.55T_f$ → $T_f = \mathbf{23.1°C}$ [1]

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

A 12 V, 4.0 A heater is immersed in 0.60 kg of liquid. After 5.0 minutes, the temperature rises from 22°C to 40°C. Calculate the specific heat capacity of the liquid.

$Q = VIt = 12 \times 4.0 \times 300 = 14\,400$ J [1]
$c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta T} = \frac{14400}{0.60 \times 18}$ [1]
$c = \mathbf{1333 \text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

Equal masses of water ($c = 4186$) and oil ($c = 2000$) are heated by identical heaters. If the water temperature rises by 10°C, calculate the temperature rise of the oil.

Same mass, same heater, same time → same energy input $Q$ [1].
$Q = mc_w \times 10 = mc_{\text{oil}} \times \Delta T_{\text{oil}}$
$c_w \times 10 = c_{\text{oil}} \times \Delta T_{\text{oil}}$ → $\Delta T_{\text{oil}} = \frac{4186 \times 10}{2000}$ [1]
$\Delta T_{\text{oil}} = \mathbf{20.9°C}$ — oil heats up about twice as fast [1]
Explain [2]

Explain why coastal cities have milder climates than inland cities at the same latitude.

Water has a very high specific heat capacity (4186 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹) compared to land (~800), so oceans absorb large amounts of energy with minimal temperature change [1]. In summer, the ocean stays cool and cools coastal air. In winter, the ocean releases stored energy and warms coastal air. This moderating effect (maritime climate) is absent inland, where land heats and cools rapidly [1].
Car engines use water as coolant precisely because of its high c. It absorbs enormous heat from the engine with minimal temperature rise, then releases it through the radiator. No other common liquid works as well.

Key Takeaways

  • $Q = mc\Delta T$ — the core formula. Rearrange for any unknown.
  • c = energy per kg per degree. High c = hard to heat (water). Low c = easy to heat (copper).
  • Water's c (4186) is exceptionally high — coolant, climate moderator, thermal buffer.
  • Mixing problems: $Q_{\text{lost}} = Q_{\text{gained}}$ → solve for $T_f$.
  • With heaters: $Q = Pt$ or $Q = VIt$ → feed into $Q = mc\Delta T$.
  • Experimental errors: heat loss, container absorption, thermometer lag. Fix with insulation, stirring, lids.
  • Sanity checks: final T between initial Ts; closer to the substance with larger $mc$ product.
