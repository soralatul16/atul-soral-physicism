Three Types of Collision

All collisions conserve momentum. They differ in what happens to kinetic energy:

Elastic

  • KE conserved — no energy lost
  • Objects bounce apart
  • Rare in real life (billiard balls, atomic collisions approximate)
  • Two equations: momentum + KE

Inelastic

  • KE NOT conserved — some energy lost
  • Objects bounce but deformed
  • Most real collisions
  • Energy → heat, sound, deformation

Perfectly Inelastic

  • Maximum KE lost
  • Objects stick together
  • Move as one mass after
  • $v = \frac{m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2}{m_1 + m_2}$
In ALL three types: momentum is conserved. The ONLY difference is what happens to KE. Don't confuse "inelastic" with "momentum not conserved" — that's the biggest IB error.

How to Determine Collision Type

1Calculate $KE_{before} = \frac{1}{2}m_1 u_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 u_2^2$
2Calculate $KE_{after} = \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_2^2$
3Compare:

$KE_{before} = KE_{after}$

→ Elastic

$KE_{before} > KE_{after}$

→ Inelastic (objects separate) or Perfectly Inelastic (stick)

Useful shortcut for elastic collisions with equal masses: If a moving object hits an identical stationary object elastically, the moving object stops completely and the stationary one moves off at the original speed. (Newton's cradle!)
Coefficient of restitution: $e = \frac{\text{relative speed after}}{\text{relative speed before}}$. Elastic: $e = 1$. Perfectly inelastic: $e = 0$. In between: $0 < e < 1$. IB doesn't test this formula but understanding it helps.

Worked Example — Elastic Collision 7/10

A 2.0 kg ball at 6.0 m/s hits a stationary 2.0 kg ball. The collision is elastic. Find the velocities after.

Equal masses, elastic, one at rest → special case!

→The moving ball stops completely: $v_1 = 0$
→The stationary ball moves at the original speed: $v_2 = 6.0$ m/s

Verify momentum:

$$2(6) + 2(0) = 2(0) + 2(6) \implies 12 = 12 \quad ✓$$

Verify KE:

$$\frac{1}{2}(2)(36) + 0 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(2)(36) \implies 36 = 36 \quad ✓$$
This only works for equal masses. For unequal masses, you need to solve two simultaneous equations (momentum + KE). The algebra is harder — IB usually gives you one velocity and asks for the other.
Newton's Cradle: This is why only one ball swings out when one ball swings in — momentum AND KE must both be conserved, and the only solution for equal masses is complete transfer.

Worked Example — Perfectly Inelastic 6/10

A 3.0 kg ball at 8.0 m/s collides with a 5.0 kg ball at rest. They stick together. Find (a) the velocity after (b) the KE lost (c) the fraction of KE lost.

(a) Velocity after (stuck together):

$$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v$$
$$3(8) + 5(0) = 8v \implies v = \frac{24}{8} = \mathbf{3.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$

(b) KE lost:

→$KE_{before} = \frac{1}{2}(3)(64) = 96$ J
→$KE_{after} = \frac{1}{2}(8)(9) = 36$ J
$$\Delta KE = 96 - 36 = \mathbf{60 \text{ J lost}}$$

(c) Fraction lost:

$$\frac{\Delta KE}{KE_{before}} = \frac{60}{96} = \mathbf{62.5\%} \text{ of KE lost}$$
Where did 60 J go? Heat (deformation of balls), sound (the "thud"), internal energy of the objects. Total energy is conserved — KE just converted to other forms.

The Ballistic Pendulum 9/10

A 0.010 kg bullet at speed $v$ embeds in a 2.0 kg wooden block hanging from strings. The block+bullet swings up 0.15 m. Find the bullet's speed.

Two-stage problem: Stage 1 = collision (momentum). Stage 2 = swing up (energy).

Stage 1: Collision (perfectly inelastic)

1$m_b v = (m_b + m_w)V$: $0.010v = 2.010V$

Stage 2: Swing up (energy conservation)

2$\frac{1}{2}(m_b + m_w)V^2 = (m_b + m_w)gh$
3$V = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(0.15)} = 1.71$ m/s

Combine:

4$0.010v = 2.010 \times 1.71$
$$v = \frac{2.010 \times 1.71}{0.010} = \mathbf{344 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$
Why two stages? During collision: momentum conserved (fast, internal forces only), KE NOT conserved. During swing: KE conserved into PE (no collision, gravity is conservative). You can't use energy for the collision or momentum for the swing.

IB-Style Question Set 9/10

Context: A 4.0 kg trolley A moves at 3.0 m/s towards a stationary 2.0 kg trolley B on a frictionless track. After collision, A moves at 1.0 m/s in the same direction.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the velocity of trolley B after the collision.

$4(3) + 2(0) = 4(1) + 2v_B$ → $12 = 4 + 2v_B$ → $v_B = \mathbf{4.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Determine [3]

(b) Determine whether the collision is elastic, inelastic, or perfectly inelastic. Show your working.

$KE_{before} = \frac{1}{2}(4)(9) = 18$ J [1]
$KE_{after} = \frac{1}{2}(4)(1) + \frac{1}{2}(2)(16) = 2 + 16 = 18$ J [1]
$KE_{before} = KE_{after}$ → Elastic collision [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) If instead the trolleys stuck together, calculate their common velocity.

$4(3) = (4+2)v$ → $v = 12/6 = \mathbf{2.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Calculate [2]

(d) For the perfectly inelastic case in (c), calculate the percentage of KE lost.

$KE_{after} = \frac{1}{2}(6)(4) = 12$ J
$\%\text{ lost} = \frac{18-12}{18} \times 100 = \mathbf{33.3\%}$ [2]
Explain [2]

(e) Explain why the perfectly inelastic collision loses exactly $\frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2}$ of the KE when one object is initially at rest.

In perfectly inelastic with $u_2 = 0$: $v = \frac{m_1}{m_1+m_2}u_1$ [1].
$\frac{KE_{after}}{KE_{before}} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}(m_1+m_2)v^2}{\frac{1}{2}m_1 u_1^2} = \frac{m_1}{m_1+m_2}$. So fraction lost $= \frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2}$ [1]. Here: $2/(4+2) = 1/3 = 33.3\%$ ✓

Practice Questions

Calculate [3] Medium

A 1.0 kg ball at 5.0 m/s hits a 1.0 kg ball at rest. After collision, the first ball moves at 1.0 m/s. Is this elastic?

Momentum: $1(5)+0 = 1(1)+1(v_2)$ → $v_2 = 4.0$ m/s [1]
$KE_{before} = \frac{1}{2}(1)(25) = 12.5$ J. $KE_{after} = \frac{1}{2}(1)(1) + \frac{1}{2}(1)(16) = 8.5$ J [1]
$12.5 \neq 8.5$ → NOT elastic (inelastic). 32% KE lost [1].
Calculate [3] Hard

Two objects of equal mass undergo a head-on elastic collision. Object A has speed $u$, object B has speed $u/2$ (opposite direction). Find both velocities after.

For equal mass elastic: objects exchange velocities.
$v_A = u_B = -u/2$ (reverses to A's original direction of B) [1]
$v_B = u_A = +u$ (takes A's original velocity) [1]
Verify momentum: $mu + m(-u/2) = m(-u/2) + mu$ → $mu/2 = mu/2$ ✓ [1]
Explain [2] Hard

Is it possible for KE to increase in a collision? If so, give an example.

Yes — in an explosion (sometimes called a "superelastic" collision) [1]. A compressed spring between two trolleys releases: KE increases because stored PE converts to KE. Momentum is still conserved (zero before and after if at rest initially). Internal energy → KE [1].
