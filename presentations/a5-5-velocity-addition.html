A.5.5

Relativistic Velocity Addition

Why Nothing Can Exceed c

Two spaceships approach each other, each at 0.9c relative to Earth. Common sense says they see each other at 1.8c. Einstein says 0.994c. The universe has a speed limit — and the math enforces it automatically.

A.5 Galilean & Special Relativity  |  A.5.5 Relativistic Velocity AdditionThe Problem

Why Galilean Addition Fails

Galilean (WRONG at high speed)

$$u' = u + v$$

Simple addition. Works for trains and cars.

Problem: $0.9c + 0.9c = 1.8c$ — exceeds light speed!

Einstein (CORRECT always)

$$u' = \frac{u + v}{1 + \frac{uv}{c^2}}$$

The denominator prevents exceeding c.

Result: $0.9c + 0.9c = 0.994c$ — stays below c!

The denominator $1 + uv/c^2$ is the key. At low speeds, $uv/c^2 \approx 0$, so the denominator ≈ 1 and Einstein's formula reduces to Galileo's. At high speeds, the denominator grows and "squashes" the result below $c$.
IB data booklet gives: $u' = \frac{u + v}{1 + uv/c^2}$ — where $u$ is the object's velocity in frame S, $v$ is the velocity of frame S′ relative to S, and $u'$ is the object's velocity in frame S′. Watch the signs carefully.

Understanding the Variables

u
Object's velocity
in frame S (Earth)
v
Velocity of S′
relative to S
u′
Object's velocity
in frame S′ (rocket)

Two useful forms

Finding u (velocity in S)

$$u = \frac{u' + v}{1 + \frac{u'v}{c^2}}$$

Finding u′ (velocity in S′)

$$u' = \frac{u - v}{1 - \frac{uv}{c^2}}$$
Sign convention: Positive = same direction as $v$. If the object moves opposite to the rocket, use a negative velocity. Be consistent.

Example 1: Two Rockets — Same Direction

Rocket A moves at 0.70c relative to Earth. Rocket A fires a probe forward at 0.80c relative to itself. What speed does Earth measure for the probe?

Identify: $v = 0.70c$ (rocket relative to Earth), $u' = 0.80c$ (probe relative to rocket)

Galilean prediction: $0.70c + 0.80c = 1.50c$ — impossible!

Einstein:

$$u = \frac{u' + v}{1 + \frac{u'v}{c^2}} = \frac{0.80c + 0.70c}{1 + \frac{(0.80)(0.70)c^2}{c^2}} = \frac{1.50c}{1 + 0.56}$$
$$u = \frac{1.50c}{1.56} = \mathbf{0.962c}$$
The denominator (1.56) pulled the result down from 1.50c to 0.962c. No matter how large $u'$ and $v$ are (as long as each < c), the result is always < c. The formula mathematically enforces the cosmic speed limit.

Example 2: Two Ships Approaching

Ship A moves at 0.90c to the right. Ship B moves at 0.90c to the left. Both speeds are relative to Earth. What speed does Ship A measure for Ship B?

Setup: $v = 0.90c$ (A relative to Earth), $u = -0.90c$ (B relative to Earth, negative = opposite)

Speed of B in A's frame:

$$u' = \frac{u - v}{1 - \frac{uv}{c^2}} = \frac{-0.90c - 0.90c}{1 - \frac{(-0.90)(0.90)c^2}{c^2}} = \frac{-1.80c}{1 + 0.81}$$
$$u' = \frac{-1.80c}{1.81} = \mathbf{-0.994c}$$

Ship A sees Ship B approaching at 0.994c — fast, but still below $c$. Galileo would have said 1.8c.

Special Case: Adding c

What if a spaceship at speed $v$ shines a light beam forward ($u' = c$)? The formula must give $u = c$.

$$u = \frac{c + v}{1 + \frac{cv}{c^2}} = \frac{c + v}{1 + \frac{v}{c}} = \frac{c + v}{\frac{c + v}{c}} = c$$

Light + anything = light speed. Always $c$.

This works for any $v$. Whether the spaceship moves at 0.01c or 0.999c, the light beam is always measured at exactly $c$ by all observers. The formula is perfectly consistent with postulate 2.

Also works in reverse

What if both $u'$ and $v$ equal $c$? (A photon emitting a photon?)

$u = \frac{c + c}{1 + c^2/c^2} = \frac{2c}{2} = c$

Even $c + c = c$. The speed of light is the absolute ceiling.

This is the deepest reason nothing can exceed $c$: the velocity addition formula mathematically prevents any combination of sub-light speeds from ever producing a super-light result.

Low Speed Limit: Recovering Galileo

At everyday speeds ($u, v \ll c$), Einstein's formula reduces to Galileo's. The correction term becomes negligible:

Example: Car and ball

A car moves at 30 m/s. Passenger throws ball at 20 m/s forward.

$u = \frac{20 + 30}{1 + \frac{20 \times 30}{(3\times10^8)^2}} = \frac{50}{1 + 6.67 \times 10^{-15}} \approx 50.000 \text{ m/s}$

The correction is $10^{-15}$ — utterly unmeasurable. Galileo works perfectly.

Calculate [3]

(a) A spaceship moves at 0.60c relative to Earth. It fires a missile at 0.50c relative to itself in the forward direction. Calculate the speed of the missile as measured by Earth.

$u = \frac{u' + v}{1 + u'v/c^2} = \frac{0.50c + 0.60c}{1 + (0.50)(0.60)}$ [1]
$u = \frac{1.10c}{1 + 0.30} = \frac{1.10c}{1.30}$ [1]
$u = \mathbf{0.846c}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(b) Two particles move toward each other, each at 0.80c relative to the lab. Calculate the speed of one particle relative to the other.

$u = 0.80c$, $v = 0.80c$ (frame of one particle), $u_{\text{other}} = -0.80c$ in lab
$u' = \frac{u - v}{1 - uv/c^2} = \frac{-0.80c - 0.80c}{1 - (-0.80)(0.80)}$ [1]
$u' = \frac{-1.60c}{1 + 0.64} = \frac{-1.60c}{1.64}$ [1]
$|u'| = \mathbf{0.976c}$ (approaching) [1]
Show [2]

(c) Show that if $u' = c$, the relativistic velocity addition formula gives $u = c$ regardless of $v$.

$u = \frac{c + v}{1 + cv/c^2} = \frac{c + v}{1 + v/c} = \frac{c + v}{(c + v)/c}$ [1]
$u = c$ [1]. The speed of light is invariant under the velocity addition formula.
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why the relativistic velocity addition formula is consistent with the postulates of special relativity, while the Galilean formula is not.

The second postulate requires that the speed of light is $c$ in all inertial frames. The relativistic formula gives $u = c$ when $u' = c$, preserving light speed invariance [1]. The Galilean formula gives $u = c + v$, which violates the second postulate by predicting different light speeds in different frames [1].

$v = 0.99c$, $u' = c$ (photon in electron's frame)

As required by the second postulate — light is always $c$.

Galilean would give 1.20c — impossible. Einstein gives 0.882c.

Key Takeaways

  • Einstein's formula: $u = \frac{u' + v}{1 + u'v/c^2}$ replaces Galilean $u = u' + v$.
  • The denominator $1 + uv/c^2$ prevents exceeding $c$. Larger at high speeds.
  • $c + \text{anything} = c$. Light speed is invariant — the formula enforces postulate 2.
  • At low speeds ($uv/c^2 \approx 0$), Einstein reduces to Galileo. No contradiction.
  • Opposite direction: use negative velocity. Two ships at 0.9c toward each other see 0.994c, not 1.8c.
  • Backward firing: probe at $-u'$ from a ship at $v$ can still move forward relative to Earth if $v > u'$.
  • Chain addition: apply the formula step by step. $0.6c + 0.6c = 0.882c$, not $1.2c$.
