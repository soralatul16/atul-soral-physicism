The Language of Thermal Physics

Students confuse temperature and heat every day. They are fundamentally different quantities — one measures how hot, the other measures how much energy flows. Getting this distinction right is the foundation of all thermal physics.

B.1 Thermal Energy Transfers  |  B.1.1 Temperature, Heat & Internal EnergyThree Concepts

Three Different Things

Temperature (T)

A measure of the average kinetic energy per particle in a substance.

  • Measured in K (kelvin) or °C
  • Does NOT depend on the amount of substance
  • Tells you how hot — not how much energy

Heat (Q)

The transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures.

  • Measured in joules (J)
  • Flows from hot → cold
  • Not something an object "has" — it's energy in transit

Internal Energy (U)

The total energy of all particles — kinetic (motion) + potential (bonds/interactions).

  • Measured in joules (J)
  • Depends on mass, temperature, and phase
  • Something an object genuinely possesses
Common mistake: "Heat" is NOT something an object contains. You cannot say "this cup has a lot of heat." The cup has internal energy. Heat is the process of energy transfer between objects at different temperatures. Once transferred, it becomes internal energy.

Temperature Scales

Celsius (°C)

Based on water: 0°C = freezing, 100°C = boiling (at 1 atm).

Convenient for everyday use but has an arbitrary zero.

Kelvin (K)

Absolute scale. 0 K = absolute zero — particles have minimum possible energy.

No negative values. Used in all physics equations.

$$T(K) = T(°C) + 273$$

B.1 Thermal Energy Transfers  |  B.1.1 Temperature, Heat & Internal EnergyInternal Energy

Internal Energy = KE + PE of All Particles

$$U = \sum E_{k,\text{particles}} + \sum E_{p,\text{particles}}$$

Kinetic part (random motion)

  • Translational KE — particles moving in straight lines
  • Rotational KE — molecules spinning
  • Vibrational KE — atoms oscillating in bonds

Linked to temperature. Higher T → more KE.

Potential part (interactions)

  • Intermolecular bonds — forces between molecules
  • Changes during phase transitions — breaking/forming bonds
  • Does NOT depend on temperature during phase change

Linked to phase. Changing phase → PE changes.

Heating increases internal energy. If temperature rises → KE increased. If phase changes (at constant T) → PE increased. Both are increases in internal energy.
B.1 Thermal Energy Transfers  |  B.1.1 Temperature, Heat & Internal EnergyThermal Equilibrium

Thermal Equilibrium

Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold until both objects reach the same temperature. At that point, they are in thermal equilibrium — net heat transfer = zero.

Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

If object A is in thermal equilibrium with object C, and object B is also in thermal equilibrium with object C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

This is why thermometers work — the thermometer (C) reaches equilibrium with the object, and we read C's temperature.

At thermal equilibrium: temperatures are equal, but internal energies are generally NOT equal. A bathtub and a teaspoon at the same temperature have vastly different internal energies.

Average KE and Temperature

For an ideal gas, the average translational kinetic energy per molecule is directly proportional to absolute temperature:

$$\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$$
$k_B$
Boltzmann constant
$1.38 \times 10^{-23}$ J K⁻¹
$T$
Absolute temperature
Must be in kelvin
This is why absolute zero matters. At $T = 0$ K, $\bar{E}_k = 0$ — particles have minimum kinetic energy (quantum mechanics prevents truly zero energy, but classically it's zero). You can't go below 0 K because you can't have negative kinetic energy.
Key implication: Temperature is a direct measure of molecular KE. Double the kelvin temperature → double the average KE per molecule. This is why we must use kelvin in gas laws and energy equations.
For solids and liquids this formula is approximate — intermolecular forces affect the energy distribution. But for gases, $\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$ is exact (for an ideal gas).

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

Distinguish [2]

(a) Distinguish between temperature and heat.

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance [1]. Heat is the transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures, from hot to cold [1].
Define [2]

(b) Define internal energy of a substance.

Internal energy is the total of the random kinetic energies [1] and the intermolecular potential energies [1] of all the particles in a substance.
Calculate [2]

(c) Calculate the average translational kinetic energy of a gas molecule at 27°C.

$T = 27 + 273 = 300$ K [1]
$\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT = \frac{3}{2} \times 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 300 = \mathbf{6.21 \times 10^{-21} \text{ J}}$ [1]
Explain [3]

(d) A large swimming pool and a cup of boiling water are both considered. State which has the higher temperature and which has the greater internal energy. Explain your reasoning.

The cup has a higher temperature (100°C vs ~25°C for a pool) [1]. The pool has greater internal energy because it contains vastly more particles, so the total KE and PE is much larger despite the lower average KE per particle [1]. Internal energy depends on the total number of particles and their individual energies, while temperature depends only on the average energy per particle [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [2]

At what temperature would the average translational KE of gas molecules be double that at 20°C?

At 20°C = 293 K, $\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_B(293)$
For double: $\frac{3}{2}k_B T_2 = 2 \times \frac{3}{2}k_B(293)$ → $T_2 = 2 \times 293 = 586$ K [1]
$= 586 - 273 = \mathbf{313°C}$ [1]
Doubling the kelvin temperature doubles the KE — not doubling the Celsius temperature.
Explain [3]

Water is heated from 90°C to 100°C, then continues to be heated at 100°C as it boils. Compare what happens to the internal energy during (i) the heating phase and (ii) the boiling phase.

(i) During heating (90→100°C): temperature rises, so the average KE of molecules increases. Internal energy increases due to increased kinetic energy [1].
(ii) During boiling (at 100°C): temperature stays constant, so average KE doesn't change. Energy input goes into breaking intermolecular bonds — increasing the PE component of internal energy [1]. Internal energy still increases, but only the PE part — not the KE part [1].
Explain [2]

State the zeroth law of thermodynamics and explain why it is needed to define temperature.

If two systems A and B are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other [1]. This establishes that temperature is a well-defined, transitive property — allowing us to use system C (a thermometer) to compare temperatures of A and B without bringing them into direct contact [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Temperature: average KE per particle. Measured in K or °C. $T(K) = T(°C) + 273$.
  • Heat: energy transferred due to temperature difference. Not a property of an object.
  • Internal energy: total KE + PE of all particles. Property of the object. $U = \sum E_k + \sum E_p$.
  • $\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$ — average translational KE per molecule (ideal gas). Must use kelvin.
  • Thermal equilibrium: no net heat flow, equal temperatures. Zeroth law makes thermometers possible.
  • Heating: increases KE (temperature rises). Phase change: increases PE (temperature constant).
  • More particles at same T = more internal energy. Pool vs cup example.
