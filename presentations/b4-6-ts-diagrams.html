TS Diagrams & Thermodynamic Cycles

Reading heat off a graph

If PV diagrams show work as area, temperature-entropy (TS) diagrams show heat as area. On a TS plot the Carnot cycle becomes a simple rectangle, efficiency is read at a glance, and the enclosed area equals the net work per cycle. This is the elegant closing tool of thermodynamics.

Temperature vs Entropy

A TS diagram plots absolute temperature $T$ (y-axis) against entropy $S$ (x-axis). Its defining property comes straight from $\Delta S = Q_{rev}/T$, rearranged:

$$Q = \int T\,dS$$
$$Q = \text{area under the curve on a TS diagram}$$

PV diagram

Area under curve = work $W = \int P\,dV$.

TS diagram

Area under curve = heat $Q = \int T\,dS$.

Two views of the same cycle. The PV diagram tells you the work; the TS diagram tells you the heat. Together they give a complete energy picture — and for a full cycle, the enclosed area is the same on both, equal to the net work $= $ net heat.

How Each Process Looks on TS Axes

  • Isothermal: horizontal line (T constant). Area beneath = $Q = T\Delta S$ — heat in or out.
  • Adiabatic (isentropic): vertical line. $Q = 0$, so $\Delta S = 0$ — entropy is constant.
  • Heating: a rising curve; the swept area equals the heat absorbed.
Beautiful simplification: the two curved processes that look awkward on a PV diagram (isotherm = hyperbola, adiabat = steep curve) become perfectly straight horizontal and vertical lines on a TS diagram. That is why the Carnot cycle is a clean rectangle here.

The Carnot Cycle as a Rectangle

  • Top edge (isothermal at $T_H$): heat in, $Q_H = T_H\,\Delta S$.
  • Bottom edge (isothermal at $T_C$): heat out, $Q_C = T_C\,\Delta S$.
  • Left & right edges (adiabatic): vertical, $\Delta S = 0$.
  • Enclosed area $= (T_H - T_C)\,\Delta S = W_{net}$ — the net work per cycle.
Efficiency by eye: $\eta = \dfrac{W_{net}}{Q_H} = \dfrac{(T_H - T_C)\Delta S}{T_H\,\Delta S} = 1 - \dfrac{T_C}{T_H}$ — the Carnot formula drops straight out of the rectangle's geometry.

Worked example

A Carnot cycle runs between $T_H = 500$ K and $T_C = 300$ K, with an entropy change $\Delta S = 4.0$ J K⁻¹ along each isotherm. Find $Q_H$, $Q_C$ and the net work.

$Q_H = T_H\Delta S = 500\times4.0 = 2000$ J; $Q_C = 300\times4.0 = 1200$ J; $W_{net} = 2000-1200 = \mathbf{800\ \text{J}}$

Check: $\eta = W/Q_H = 800/2000 = 0.40 = 1 - 300/500$ ✓

Direction sets the type: a clockwise loop on a TS diagram is a heat engine (net heat in, net work out); anticlockwise is a refrigerator/heat pump.

Why Engineers Use TS Diagrams

  • Heat is visible directly. Since area = heat, you can read energy input/output straight off the plot without extra calculation.
  • Efficiency is geometric. For a Carnot rectangle, efficiency is literally the fraction of the rectangle's height above $T_C$.
  • Irreversibility shows up. Real processes generate entropy, widening the cycle to the right and increasing wasted heat $Q_C$ — instantly visible as a fatter bottom area.
  • Used for real cycles. Power-plant Rankine cycles and refrigeration cycles are routinely analysed on TS diagrams because water/refrigerant phase changes appear as clean horizontal lines.

TS vs PV — when to use which

Use a PV diagram when you care about work and mechanical detail (pistons, expansion). Use a TS diagram when you care about heat flow, efficiency, and entropy generation. Expert problem-solving often sketches both.

Exam reminder: temperature on a TS diagram must be absolute (kelvin), and the area only equals heat for a reversible path. For irreversible steps, area under the actual path underestimates the true entropy generated.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

State & Explain [2]

(a) State what the area under a curve on a temperature-entropy (TS) diagram represents, and explain why.

The area under a curve on a TS diagram represents the heat transferred, $Q = \int T\,dS$ [1]. This follows from the definition of entropy $\Delta S = Q_{rev}/T$, rearranged to $Q = T\,\Delta S$; summing (integrating) $T\,dS$ over the process gives the total heat, which is the area beneath the curve [1].
Describe [3]

(b) Describe the shape of a Carnot cycle on a TS diagram and identify each of the four processes.

A Carnot cycle is a rectangle on a TS diagram [1]. The two horizontal edges are the isothermal processes: the top edge (at $T_H$) is isothermal heat absorption $Q_H$, and the bottom edge (at $T_C$) is isothermal heat rejection $Q_C$ [1]. The two vertical edges are the adiabatic (isentropic) processes, where entropy is constant ($\Delta S = 0$) as the gas changes temperature between $T_H$ and $T_C$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A Carnot cycle operates between 620 K and 310 K with $\Delta S = 5.0$ J K⁻¹ on each isotherm. Determine $Q_H$, the net work, and the efficiency.

$Q_H = T_H\Delta S = 620 \times 5.0 = 3100$ J [1]
$W_{net} = (T_H - T_C)\Delta S = (620-310)\times5.0 = 1550$ J [1]
$\eta = W/Q_H = 1550/3100 = 0.50$ (= $1 - 310/620$) [1]
Compare [2]

(d) Explain what information a TS diagram shows more directly than a PV diagram for the same cycle.

A TS diagram shows the heat transferred directly as the area under each process (and net heat as the enclosed area), plus entropy changes as horizontal displacement [1]. A PV diagram instead shows work as its area; reading heat from a PV diagram requires applying the first law separately, whereas the TS diagram makes heat, entropy generation, and efficiency immediately visible [1].

Key Takeaways

  • TS diagram: plots T (kelvin) vs S; area under the curve = heat, $Q = \int T\,dS$.
  • PV shows work, TS shows heat — two complementary views of the same cycle.
  • Processes: isothermal = horizontal line; adiabatic (isentropic) = vertical line.
  • Carnot cycle = rectangle: $Q_H = T_H\Delta S$, $Q_C = T_C\Delta S$, $W_{net} = (T_H-T_C)\Delta S$.
  • Efficiency is geometric: $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H$ falls straight out of the rectangle.
  • Clockwise = engine; anticlockwise = fridge/heat pump. Enclosed area = net work.
