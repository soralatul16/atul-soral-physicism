Seeing Heat and Entropy Together

PV diagrams show work as area. TS diagrams show heat as area. Together they give the complete picture: how much heat goes in, how much comes out, and how much becomes work. The Carnot cycle on a TS diagram is the simplest shape in all of thermodynamics — a rectangle.

B.4 Thermodynamics  |  B.4.6 TS DiagramsWhat is a TS Diagram?

Temperature-Entropy (TS) Diagrams

A TS diagram plots temperature (T) on the y-axis against entropy (S) on the x-axis. The key insight:

$$Q = \int T \, dS = \text{area under the curve on a TS diagram}$$

PV diagram

Area under curve = Work (W)

$W = \int P \, dV$

TS diagram

Area under curve = Heat (Q)

$Q = \int T \, dS$

For a complete cycle: the enclosed area on a PV diagram = net work. The enclosed area on a TS diagram = net heat = net work (since $\Delta U = 0$ for a cycle). Both give the same answer for $W_{\text{net}}$.
Processes on a TS diagram:
• Isothermal: horizontal line (constant T)
• Adiabatic (reversible): vertical line (constant S, since $Q = 0$ → $\Delta S = 0$)
• Isobaric: curved line (T changes as S changes)
• Isochoric: curved line (steeper than isobaric)

The Carnot Cycle on a TS Diagram — A Rectangle

Reading the Carnot rectangle

  • Top edge (A→B): isothermal at $T_H$. $Q_H = T_H(S_2 - S_1) = T_H \Delta S$
  • Bottom edge (C→D): isothermal at $T_C$. $Q_C = T_C(S_2 - S_1) = T_C \Delta S$
  • Left & right edges: adiabatic ($Q = 0$, vertical — no entropy change)
  • Net work: $W = Q_H - Q_C = (T_H - T_C)\Delta S$ = area of rectangle
  • Efficiency: $\eta = W/Q_H = (T_H - T_C)\Delta S / (T_H \Delta S) = 1 - T_C/T_H$ ✓
The Carnot cycle is the ONLY cycle that's a rectangle on a TS diagram. Any other cycle has curved sides (irreversibilities or non-isothermal heat transfer), making the enclosed area smaller relative to $Q_H$ → lower efficiency. This is another way to see why Carnot is the maximum.

Real Cycles on TS Diagrams

Otto Cycle (petrol engine)

Two adiabatic (vertical) + two isochoric (curved) processes. Heat added/rejected at changing T → not isothermal → enclosed area < Carnot rectangle → less efficient.

$\eta_{\text{Otto}} = 1 - r^{1-\gamma}$ where $r$ = compression ratio

Diesel Cycle

Two adiabatic + one isobaric (heat in) + one isochoric (heat out). Higher compression ratio than Otto → higher efficiency. No spark plug needed (adiabatic compression ignites fuel).

Typical $\eta \approx 40$–$45\%$

Comparing PV and TS for the same cycle

PV diagram
Area = net work done
Isothermal = hyperbola
Adiabatic = steeper curve
Isobaric = horizontal
Isochoric = vertical
TS diagram
Area = net heat = net work
Isothermal = horizontal
Adiabatic = vertical
Isobaric = curve (log shape)
Isochoric = curve (steeper)
IB exam: You may be asked to sketch a given cycle on both PV and TS diagrams, or identify which process is which from a TS diagram. The key: horizontal = isothermal, vertical = adiabatic. Everything else is curved.
B.4 Thermodynamics  |  B.4.6 TS DiagramsIB Questions

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

State [2]

(a) State what the area under a curve on a TS diagram represents, and what the enclosed area of a complete cycle represents.

The area under a curve on a TS diagram represents the heat transferred during that process: $Q = \int T \, dS$ [1]. The enclosed area of a complete cycle represents the net heat transferred, which equals the net work done by the gas (since $\Delta U = 0$ for a complete cycle) [1].
Sketch [3]

(b) Sketch the Carnot cycle on a TS diagram. Label the four processes and identify which involve heat transfer.

The Carnot cycle is a rectangle: top edge = isothermal expansion at $T_H$ (heat $Q_H$ absorbed, entropy increases), right edge = adiabatic expansion (vertical, $\Delta S = 0$, no heat) [1], bottom edge = isothermal compression at $T_C$ (heat $Q_C$ rejected, entropy decreases), left edge = adiabatic compression (vertical, $\Delta S = 0$, no heat) [1]. Only the top and bottom edges involve heat transfer. The area of the rectangle = $W = (T_H - T_C)(S_2 - S_1)$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A Carnot engine operates between 500 K and 300 K. The entropy change during the isothermal expansion is 20 J K⁻¹. Calculate $Q_H$, $Q_C$, and $W$.

$Q_H = T_H \Delta S = 500 \times 20 = \mathbf{10\,000 \text{ J}}$ [1]
$Q_C = T_C \Delta S = 300 \times 20 = \mathbf{6000 \text{ J}}$ [1]
$W = Q_H - Q_C = 10000 - 6000 = \mathbf{4000 \text{ J}}$ (= area of rectangle) [1]
Explain [2]

(d) On a TS diagram, explain why a reversible adiabatic process is represented by a vertical line.

In a reversible adiabatic process, $Q = 0$ (no heat exchange). Since $\Delta S = Q_{\text{rev}}/T$, and $Q = 0$, the entropy change is zero: $\Delta S = 0$ [1]. On a TS diagram where S is on the x-axis, constant S means the process is a vertical line — entropy doesn't change even though temperature does [1].

Key Takeaways

  • TS diagram: T on y-axis, S on x-axis. Area under curve = heat ($Q = \int T\,dS$).
  • Enclosed area of a cycle = net heat = net work ($\Delta U = 0$ for cycles).
  • Isothermal: horizontal line. Adiabatic (reversible): vertical line ($\Delta S = 0$).
  • Carnot on TS = rectangle. $Q_H = T_H\Delta S$ (top), $Q_C = T_C\Delta S$ (bottom), $W = (T_H - T_C)\Delta S$.
  • Carnot efficiency from TS: $\eta = W/Q_H = (T_H - T_C)/T_H = 1 - T_C/T_H$.
  • Real cycles have curved (non-isothermal) heat transfer → smaller enclosed area → lower efficiency than Carnot.
  • PV gives work, TS gives heat. Both give the same net work for a complete cycle.
