B.3.2

Pressure in Gases

Molecular Origin

A balloon stays inflated because trillions of gas molecules slam into its walls every microsecond. Each collision is tiny, but the cumulative effect of $10^{23}$ impacts per second creates a measurable, steady force — what we call pressure. Understanding pressure at the molecular level is the foundation of the kinetic theory of gases.

How Molecules Create Pressure

The mechanism — step by step

  1. Gas molecules move randomly at high speeds (N₂ at room temp: ~500 m/s)
  2. They collide with container walls — each collision exerts a tiny force (Newton's 2nd: $F = \Delta p / \Delta t$)
  3. The molecule's momentum changes direction at the wall: $\Delta p = 2mv$ (elastic collision, reversal)
  4. Billions of collisions per second on every cm² of wall create a steady average force
  5. Pressure = average force per unit area: $P = F/A$
Pressure is an emergent property. A single molecule doesn't create "pressure" — it creates a brief impulse. Pressure only makes sense as a statistical average over huge numbers of molecules (~$10^{23}$). This is why kinetic theory is a statistical theory.

What Affects Gas Pressure?

From the molecular picture, three factors control pressure:

1. Temperature ↑ → P ↑

Higher T → molecules move faster → hit walls harder (more $\Delta p$ per collision) AND more frequently → pressure increases.

$P \propto T$ (at constant V, n)

2. Volume ↓ → P ↑

Smaller container → same molecules hit walls more often (shorter distance between walls) → more collisions per second per unit area → pressure increases.

$P \propto 1/V$ (at constant T, n)

3. More molecules → P ↑

More molecules (larger n or N) → more collisions per second on the walls → pressure increases. Pumping up a tyre adds molecules.

$P \propto n$ (at constant V, T)

All three combine into one equation: $P = \frac{nRT}{V}$ (the ideal gas law, B.3.4). Every gas law is just a special case of holding one or two variables constant and seeing how the others relate.
Atmospheric pressure is caused by the weight of the entire column of air above you. At sea level, ~10 tonnes of air press on every m². We don't feel it because the pressure acts equally in all directions, and our bodies have internal pressure that balances it.

The Ideal Gas Model — Assumptions

An ideal gas is a theoretical model that simplifies real gas behaviour. The kinetic theory assumes:

  1. Molecules are point particles — their volume is negligible compared to the container volume
  2. No intermolecular forces — molecules don't attract or repel each other (except during collisions)
  3. Collisions are perfectly elastic — total kinetic energy is conserved in every collision
  4. Motion is random — molecules move in all directions with a distribution of speeds
  5. Collision time is negligible — the time spent in contact is much less than the time between collisions
  6. Newton's laws apply to the motion of each molecule

When does ideal gas work well?

High temperature (molecules move fast, KE >> PE of intermolecular forces) and low pressure (molecules far apart, volume negligible).

When does it fail?

Low temperature (intermolecular forces matter — gas can liquefy) and high pressure (molecular volume becomes significant).

IB exam: "State the assumptions of the kinetic theory of an ideal gas" is a common 3–4 mark question. Memorise the list — at least 4 assumptions for full marks.

Deriving Pressure from Molecular Collisions

Consider one molecule of mass $m$ moving with x-velocity $v_x$ in a cubic box of side $L$:

Step 1: Momentum change per collision with one wall: $\Delta p = 2mv_x$

Step 2: Time between consecutive hits on the same wall: $\Delta t = \frac{2L}{v_x}$

Step 3: Force from one molecule: $F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{2mv_x}{2L/v_x} = \frac{mv_x^2}{L}$

Step 4: For N molecules, using the average: $F = \frac{Nm\overline{v_x^2}}{L}$

Step 5: Since motion is random: $\overline{v_x^2} = \frac{1}{3}\overline{v^2}$ (equal in x, y, z)

Step 6: Pressure = $F/A = \frac{Nm\overline{v^2}}{3L \times L^2} = \frac{Nm\overline{v^2}}{3V}$

$$P = \frac{1}{3}\frac{Nm\overline{v^2}}{V} = \frac{1}{3}\rho\overline{v^2}$$
This links the macroscopic (P, V) to the microscopic ($m$, $\overline{v^2}$, $N$). Pressure depends on the number density ($N/V$), the mass of each molecule ($m$), and the mean square speed ($\overline{v^2}$). This is the kinetic theory of gases.

Root Mean Square (RMS) Speed

Not all molecules move at the same speed. The rms speed is the square root of the mean of the squared speeds — the speed that correctly represents the average kinetic energy.

$$v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\overline{v^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_BT}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}}$$
GasM (kg/mol)$v_{\text{rms}}$ at 300 K (m/s)Comparison

CO₂0.044413Heavier → slower
Lighter molecules move faster at the same temperature. $v_{\text{rms}} \propto 1/\sqrt{M}$. At the same T, H₂ moves ~4× faster than O₂ ($\sqrt{32/2} = 4$). This is why H₂ and He escape Earth's atmosphere but N₂ and O₂ don't.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Explain [3]

(a) Explain, in terms of molecular behaviour, how a gas exerts pressure on the walls of its container.

Gas molecules move randomly at high speeds and collide with the container walls [1]. Each collision involves a change in momentum ($\Delta p = 2mv$), which by Newton's second law exerts a force on the wall ($F = \Delta p/\Delta t$) [1]. The cumulative effect of billions of such collisions per second per unit area produces a steady macroscopic pressure ($P = F/A$) [1].
State [3]

(b) State three assumptions of the kinetic theory of an ideal gas.

1. Gas molecules are point particles with negligible volume compared to the container [1].
2. There are no intermolecular forces between molecules except during collisions [1].
3. All collisions (between molecules and with walls) are perfectly elastic — kinetic energy is conserved [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) Calculate the rms speed of nitrogen molecules (M = 0.028 kg/mol) at 27°C. (R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹)

$T = 27 + 273 = 300$ K [1]
$v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}} = \sqrt{\frac{3 \times 8.314 \times 300}{0.028}}$ [1]
$= \sqrt{\frac{7483}{0.028}} = \sqrt{267250} = \mathbf{517 \text{ m/s}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain, using kinetic theory, why increasing the temperature of a gas at constant volume increases its pressure.

Increasing temperature increases the average kinetic energy (and therefore speed) of the gas molecules [1]. Faster molecules hit the walls more frequently and with greater momentum change per collision ($\Delta p = 2mv$), resulting in a greater average force per unit area on the walls — hence higher pressure [1].
B.3 Gas Laws  |  B.3.2 Pressure in GasesSummary

Key Takeaways

  • $P = F/A$ — force per unit area. SI unit: Pa (= N m⁻²). 1 atm ≈ $1.01 \times 10^5$ Pa.
  • Molecular origin: random molecular collisions with walls → momentum change → force → pressure.
  • Three factors: P increases with temperature (faster hits), decreases with volume (less frequent hits), increases with more molecules.
  • Ideal gas assumptions: point particles, no forces, elastic collisions, random motion, negligible collision time.
  • Kinetic theory result: $P = \frac{1}{3}\frac{Nm\overline{v^2}}{V}$ — links macro (P, V) to micro (m, v, N).
  • RMS speed: $v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{3RT/M}$. Lighter molecules move faster. H₂: 1920 m/s, N₂: 517 m/s at 300 K.
  • Ideal gas works well at high T, low P. Fails near liquefaction (low T, high P).
