A.2.3

Newton's Second Law

$$F = ma$$

The most important equation in mechanics. It connects force, mass, and acceleration — and tells you exactly how an object responds to any push or pull.

Atul Soral Physicism

A.2 Forces & Momentum  |  A.2.3 Newton's Second LawThe Law

The Law — What It Says

The net force acting on an object is equal to the product of its mass and its acceleration. The acceleration is in the same direction as the net force.
$$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$$

Three ways to read this equation:

  • More force → more acceleration (for the same mass). Double the push, double the acceleration.
  • More mass → less acceleration (for the same force). Heavier objects are harder to accelerate.
  • Direction: acceleration is always in the same direction as the net force. Not the velocity — the FORCE.
It's the NET force, not just any force. $F_{net} = ma$, not $F = ma$. If multiple forces act, find the resultant first, THEN apply $F_{net} = ma$. This is the #1 error in IB.
Units check: $F = ma \implies \text{N} = \text{kg} \times \text{m s}^{-2}$. So 1 Newton = the force needed to accelerate 1 kg at 1 m s$^{-2}$.

The Two Proportionalities

$a \propto F$ (mass constant)

Double the force → double the acceleration. Gradient = $1/m$. Steeper line = smaller mass.

$a \propto 1/m$ (force constant)

Double the mass → half the acceleration. Plot $a$ vs $1/m$ for a straight line through origin.

IB experiment: Verify $F = ma$ by changing force (add weights) while keeping mass constant. Plot $a$ vs $F$ — should get a straight line through origin with gradient $= 1/m$.

The $F = ma$ Problem-Solving Method

1Draw a Free Body Diagram. Identify ALL forces on the object.
2Choose a coordinate system. Usually: direction of acceleration = positive.
3Resolve forces into components along your axes (if angled).
4Apply $\sum F = ma$ in each direction separately.
5Solve for the unknown. Check units and sign.
Common mistake: Students write $F = ma$ using a single force instead of net force. If a 10 N push and 4 N friction act on a 2 kg box: $a = (10-4)/2 = 3$ m/s$^2$, NOT $a = 10/2 = 5$.
When $a = 0$: $F = ma$ reduces to $\sum F = 0$ — that's Newton's First Law. The second law contains the first as a special case.

Worked Example 1 — Horizontal Push 3/10

A 5.0 kg box is pushed with a 30 N horizontal force on a surface with 12 N friction. Find the acceleration.

Step 1: FBD

Step 2–5: Apply $F_{net} = ma$

Vertical: $N - W = 0$ → balanced (no vertical motion)

Horizontal:

→$F_{net} = 30 - 12 = 18$ N (right)
→$a = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \frac{18}{5.0}$
$$a = \mathbf{3.6 \text{ m s}^{-2}} \text{ to the right}$$

Worked Example 2 — Inclined Plane 6/10

A 4.0 kg block slides down a frictionless incline at 30° to the horizontal. Find the acceleration.

FBD and resolve weight:

Along slope: W sin30° = mg sin30°
Perpendicular: N = mg cos30°
No friction (frictionless)

Apply $F_{net} = ma$ along slope:

→Only force along slope: $mg\sin30°$
$$ma = mg\sin30°$$
→Mass cancels:
$$a = g\sin30° = 9.8 \times 0.5 = \mathbf{4.9 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$$
Key result: $a = g\sin\theta$ for a frictionless incline. The mass cancels — all objects slide at the same rate regardless of mass (just like free fall). At $\theta = 90°$: $a = g$ (free fall). At $\theta = 0°$: $a = 0$ (flat surface).

Worked Example 3 — Elevator (Apparent Weight) 7/10

A 60 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator. Find the scale reading (normal force) when the elevator: (a) accelerates up at 2.0 m/s², (b) moves at constant velocity, (c) accelerates down at 2.0 m/s².

The scale reads the normal force $N$, not the weight. Apply $\sum F_y = ma_y$ with up = +.

(a) Accelerating UP:

→$N - mg = ma$
$$N = m(g + a) = 60(9.8 + 2.0) = \mathbf{708 \text{ N}}$$

You feel HEAVIER — scale reads more than your weight (588 N)

(b) Constant velocity ($a = 0$):

$$N = mg = 60 \times 9.8 = \mathbf{588 \text{ N}}$$

Normal weight — whether moving up, down, or at rest

(c) Accelerating DOWN:

→$N - mg = m(-a)$
$$N = m(g - a) = 60(9.8 - 2.0) = \mathbf{468 \text{ N}}$$

You feel LIGHTER — scale reads less than your weight

Free fall ($a = g$): $N = m(g - g) = 0$. The scale reads zero. You're "weightless" — this is what astronauts experience. You still have weight ($mg$), but no normal force to feel it.

Connected Objects — Atwood Machine 8/10

Two masses $m_1 = 5$ kg and $m_2 = 3$ kg are connected by a string over a frictionless pulley. Find the acceleration and the tension.

Key insight: same string → same $T$ and same $|a|$

For $m_1$ (heavier, goes down):

1$m_1 g - T = m_1 a$ → $5(9.8) - T = 5a$

For $m_2$ (lighter, goes up):

2$T - m_2 g = m_2 a$ → $T - 3(9.8) = 3a$

Add equations (T cancels):

$$(m_1 - m_2)g = (m_1 + m_2)a$$
$$a = \frac{(5-3) \times 9.8}{5+3} = \frac{19.6}{8} = \mathbf{2.45 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$$

Find T:

$$T = m_2(g + a) = 3(9.8 + 2.45) = \mathbf{36.75 \text{ N}}$$

Check: $T < m_1 g$ (36.75 < 49) and $T > m_2 g$ (36.75 > 29.4) ✓

Worked Example — Block on Table with Hanging Mass 8/10

A 4.0 kg block on a frictionless table is connected by a string over a pulley to a 2.0 kg hanging mass. Find the acceleration and tension.

Block on table (horizontal):

1$T = m_1 a = 4a$

Hanging mass (vertical):

2$m_2 g - T = m_2 a$ → $19.6 - T = 2a$

Substitute (1) into (2):

$$19.6 - 4a = 2a \implies 6a = 19.6$$
$$a = \mathbf{3.27 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$$
$$T = 4 \times 3.27 = \mathbf{13.1 \text{ N}}$$

Check: $T < m_2 g$ (13.1 < 19.6) ✓ — if $T = m_2 g$, there'd be no acceleration

IB-Style Question Set 9/10

Context: A 1200 kg car on a flat road has an engine force of 3500 N and experiences a total resistive force of 800 N.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the acceleration of the car.

$F_{net} = 3500 - 800 = 2700$ N [1]
$a = F_{net}/m = 2700/1200 = \mathbf{2.25 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(b) The car reaches 30 m/s. Calculate the distance travelled from rest.

$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $900 = 0 + 2(2.25)s \implies s = \frac{900}{4.5} = \mathbf{200 \text{ m}}$ [2]
Explain [2]

(c) As the car speeds up, the acceleration decreases even though the engine force stays constant. Explain why.

As speed increases, air resistance (drag) increases [1]. The net force ($F_{engine} - F_{drag}$) decreases, so by $F = ma$, acceleration decreases [1].
Determine [3]

(d) The car eventually reaches a constant (terminal) velocity. Determine the resistive force at this speed and the terminal velocity if the engine force is 3500 N and the drag force is proportional to $v^2$ with $F_{drag} = 0.5v^2$.

At terminal velocity: $F_{net} = 0$ → $F_{engine} = F_{drag}$ [1]
$3500 = 0.5v^2 \implies v^2 = 7000 \implies v = \mathbf{83.7 \text{ m/s}}$ (301 km/h) [2]
Draw [2]

(e) Sketch a v-t graph for the car from rest to terminal velocity.

Curve: starts steep (high acceleration), gradually flattens, approaches a horizontal asymptote at $v_T$ [1]. NOT a straight line — the acceleration is not constant because drag increases with speed [1].

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A 2.0 kg object experiences a net force of 10 N. Calculate the acceleration.

$a = F/m = 10/2.0 = \mathbf{5.0 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A 70 kg person in an elevator feels an apparent weight of 840 N. Is the elevator accelerating up or down, and what is the acceleration?

$N > mg$ (840 > 686), so accelerating UP [1].
$N - mg = ma$: $840 - 686 = 70a$ → $a = 154/70 = \mathbf{2.2 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$ upward [2]
Calculate [3] Hard

A 3.0 kg block on a 25° frictionless incline is connected over a pulley to a hanging 2.0 kg mass. Find the acceleration of the system.

Block on slope: $T - 3g\sin25° = 3a$ → $T - 12.4 = 3a$
Hanging mass: $2g - T = 2a$ → $19.6 - T = 2a$
Add: $19.6 - 12.4 = 5a$ → $a = 7.2/5 = \mathbf{1.44 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$ [3]

Key Takeaways

  • $F_{net} = ma$ — always use NET force, not individual forces.
  • $a \propto F$ (mass constant) — straight line through origin, gradient $= 1/m$.
  • $a \propto 1/m$ (force constant) — inverse relationship, hyperbola.
  • Incline: $a = g\sin\theta$ (frictionless). With friction: $a = g\sin\theta - \mu g\cos\theta$.
  • Elevator: $N = m(g \pm a)$. Up acceleration → feel heavier. Down → lighter. Free fall → weightless.
  • Atwood: $a = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)g}{m_1 + m_2}$. Same $T$ and $|a|$ on both sides.
  • Method: FBD → choose axes → resolve → $\sum F = ma$ each direction → solve.
