B.3.1

The Mole, Molar Mass
& the Avogadro Constant

Counting the Uncountable

A single drop of water contains 1,670,000,000,000,000,000,000 molecules. We can't count them one by one — so we count them in batches. That batch is called a mole. It's the physicist's dozen — except instead of 12, it's 602 sextillion.

What is a Mole?

DEFINITION (SI, 2019)

One mole is exactly $6.02214076 \times 10^{23}$ elementary entities (atoms, molecules, ions, electrons, photons — whatever you're counting).

$$N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}$$

The Avogadro constant — the number of entities in one mole

Why $6.022 \times 10^{23}$? Where does this number come from?

It's defined so that 1 mole of carbon-12 atoms has a mass of exactly 12 grams. This links the atomic scale (atomic mass units) to the macroscopic scale (grams). One atomic mass unit (u) = $\frac{1}{N_A}$ grams = $1.661 \times 10^{-27}$ kg.

How big is $6 \times 10^{23}$?

  • If you counted 1 atom per second, it would take 19 quadrillion years — a million times the age of the universe
  • A mole of grains of sand would cover the entire Earth's surface to a depth of several kilometres
  • A mole of sheets of paper stacked would reach from Earth to the Sun and back 8 billion times
The mole is a counting unit, not a mass unit. Just like "a dozen" means 12 regardless of what you're counting, "a mole" means $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ regardless of the particle. A mole of hydrogen atoms (1 g) and a mole of lead atoms (207 g) contain the same number of atoms.

Molar Mass (M)

Molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance, in grams per mole (g/mol) or kilograms per mole (kg/mol).

$$n = \frac{m}{M}$$

Numerically, molar mass in g/mol = relative atomic/molecular mass (no units). Carbon's relative atomic mass is 12.01 → its molar mass is 12.01 g/mol. This is by design — the mole was defined to make this work.

Connecting Moles, Particles & Mass

Three fundamental relationships link the microscopic and macroscopic worlds:

MOLES FROM PARTICLES

$$n = \frac{N}{N_A}$$

N = number of particles
$N_A$ = Avogadro constant

MOLES FROM MASS

$$n = \frac{m}{M}$$

m = mass (g or kg)
M = molar mass

MASS OF ONE PARTICLE

$$m_0 = \frac{M}{N_A}$$

Mass of a single atom
or molecule (kg)

Combining: total number of particles from mass

$n = \frac{N}{N_A} = \frac{m}{M}$ → $N = \frac{m \cdot N_A}{M}$

This tells you how many actual molecules are in a sample of known mass — the bridge between what you weigh and what exists.

Unit consistency matters. If $m$ is in grams, $M$ must be in g/mol. If $m$ is in kg, $M$ must be in kg/mol (divide g/mol by 1000). IB typically uses grams for chemistry-style problems and kg for physics problems. Always check.

Worked Examples

Example 1: How many molecules in 100 g of water?

Step 1: $M_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} = 2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.02$ g/mol

Step 2: $n = \frac{m}{M} = \frac{100}{18.02} = 5.55$ mol

Step 3: $N = nN_A = 5.55 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = \mathbf{3.34 \times 10^{24} \text{ molecules}}$

Example 2: Mass of a single oxygen molecule

$m_0 = \frac{M}{N_A} = \frac{32.00 \times 10^{-3}}{6.022 \times 10^{23}} = \mathbf{5.31 \times 10^{-26} \text{ kg}}$

Example 3: How many moles of gas in a container with $2.5 \times 10^{24}$ molecules?

$n = \frac{N}{N_A} = \frac{2.5 \times 10^{24}}{6.022 \times 10^{23}} = \mathbf{4.15 \text{ mol}}$

IB tip: These conversions appear in nearly every gas law problem. You need to go from mass → moles (using $M$) or from number of particles → moles (using $N_A$) before applying $PV = nRT$.

The Unified Atomic Mass Unit (u)

DEFINITION

$1 \text{ u} = \frac{1}{12}$ the mass of a carbon-12 atom $= 1.661 \times 10^{-27}$ kg

The atomic mass unit provides a convenient scale for atomic masses. A proton ≈ 1.007 u, a neutron ≈ 1.009 u, an electron ≈ 0.00055 u.

The beautiful connection

$1 \text{ u} = \frac{1}{N_A}$ grams $= \frac{M(\text{in g/mol})}{N_A}$ for any atom

This means: an atom's mass in u = its molar mass in g/mol (numerically).

Oxygen atom: 16.00 u = 16.00 g/mol. Hydrogen atom: 1.008 u = 1.008 g/mol.

Energy equivalent of 1 u (from E = mc²)

$E = mc^2 = 1.661 \times 10^{-27} \times (3 \times 10^8)^2 = 1.494 \times 10^{-10}$ J

$= 931.5$ MeV — this is why 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c² (from Topic A.5.7)

Three ways to express atomic mass: (1) in u (convenient for atoms), (2) in kg (SI, for physics equations), (3) in g/mol (for macroscopic quantities). Convert between them using $N_A$.

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

Define [1]

(a) Define the Avogadro constant.

The Avogadro constant is the number of entities (atoms, molecules, etc.) in one mole of a substance: $N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}$ mol⁻¹ [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) Calculate the number of molecules in 44 g of carbon dioxide (CO₂). ($M_{\text{CO}_2} = 44.01$ g/mol)

$n = \frac{m}{M} = \frac{44}{44.01} = 1.00$ mol [1]
$N = nN_A = 1.00 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23}$ [1]
$N = \mathbf{6.02 \times 10^{23} \text{ molecules}}$ (exactly 1 mole) [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) Calculate the mass of a single nitrogen molecule (N₂) in kg. ($M_{\text{N}_2} = 28.02$ g/mol)

$m_0 = \frac{M}{N_A} = \frac{28.02 \times 10^{-3}}{6.022 \times 10^{23}}$ [1]
$m_0 = \mathbf{4.65 \times 10^{-26} \text{ kg}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(d) A gas sample has mass 6.4 g and contains $1.2 \times 10^{23}$ molecules. Calculate the molar mass of the gas and suggest its identity.

$n = \frac{N}{N_A} = \frac{1.2 \times 10^{23}}{6.022 \times 10^{23}} = 0.199$ mol [1]
$M = \frac{m}{n} = \frac{6.4}{0.199} = 32.1$ g/mol [1]
$M \approx 32$ g/mol → likely O₂ (oxygen gas, $M = 32.00$) or S (sulfur, $M = 32.07$) [1]

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

How many individual atoms are there in 1.00 mol of methane (CH₄)?

1 mole of CH₄ = $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ molecules [1].
Each CH₄ molecule contains 1 C + 4 H = 5 atoms [1].
Total atoms = $5 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = \mathbf{3.01 \times 10^{24} \text{ atoms}}$ [1].
Calculate [2]

Air is approximately 78% N₂ and 21% O₂ by volume. Calculate the average molar mass of air. ($M_{\text{N}_2} = 28.0$, $M_{\text{O}_2} = 32.0$)

$M_{\text{air}} = 0.78 \times 28.0 + 0.21 \times 32.0 + 0.01 \times 40$ (Ar) [1]
$= 21.84 + 6.72 + 0.40 = \mathbf{29.0 \text{ g/mol}}$ [1]
(The "remaining 1%" is mostly argon at 40 g/mol.)
Explain [2]

Explain the relationship between the unified atomic mass unit (u) and the molar mass in g/mol.

The atomic mass unit is defined so that 1 u = $\frac{1}{12}$ of the mass of a carbon-12 atom = $\frac{1}{N_A}$ grams [1]. This means an atom's mass in u is numerically equal to its molar mass in g/mol. For example, an oxygen atom has mass 16.00 u, and 1 mole of oxygen atoms has mass 16.00 g — the same number because $1 \text{ u} \times N_A = 1$ g [1].
Connection to gas laws: Every gas law problem starts with knowing how many moles ($n$) you have. $n = m/M$ or $n = N/N_A$. Once you have $n$, you can use $PV = nRT$ (coming in B.3.4). The mole is the bridge between weighing a gas and predicting its behaviour.

Key Takeaways

  • 1 mole = $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ entities (Avogadro constant $N_A$).
  • $n = N/N_A$ — moles from particle count. $n = m/M$ — moles from mass.
  • Molar mass M: mass of 1 mole in g/mol. Numerically = relative atomic/molecular mass.
  • Mass of one particle: $m_0 = M/N_A$.
  • Total particles from mass: $N = mN_A/M$.
  • 1 u = $1.661 \times 10^{-27}$ kg = $1/N_A$ grams = 931.5 MeV/c².
  • The mole bridges micro and macro — lets you go from atomic masses to grams on a balance.
