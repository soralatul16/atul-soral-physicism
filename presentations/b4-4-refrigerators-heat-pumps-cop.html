Refrigerators, Heat Pumps
& the COP

Running Heat Engines in Reverse

A heat engine takes heat from hot → does work → dumps waste to cold. A refrigerator does the opposite: uses work to move heat from cold → hot. Same physics, reversed direction. The second law says you must pay with work — you can't move heat uphill for free.

Refrigerator vs Heat Pump — Same Device, Different Purpose

Refrigerator / Air Conditioner

Purpose: keep the cold space cold by removing heat from it.

Useful output: heat extracted from cold space ($Q_C$).

$$\text{COP}_{\text{ref}} = \frac{Q_C}{W} = \frac{Q_C}{Q_H - Q_C}$$

Carnot: $\text{COP}_{\text{ref}} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C}$

Heat Pump

Purpose: heat a warm space by pumping heat into it from outside.

Useful output: heat delivered to hot space ($Q_H$).

$$\text{COP}_{\text{hp}} = \frac{Q_H}{W} = \frac{Q_H}{Q_H - Q_C}$$

Carnot: $\text{COP}_{\text{hp}} = \frac{T_H}{T_H - T_C}$

COP can be > 1! Unlike efficiency (always < 1), COP is often 3–5. A heat pump with COP = 4 delivers 4 kWh of heat for every 1 kWh of electricity. It's not creating energy — it's moving 3 kWh from outside and adding 1 kWh of work.
Connection: $\text{COP}_{\text{hp}} = \text{COP}_{\text{ref}} + 1$ always. Because $Q_H = Q_C + W$, so $Q_H/W = Q_C/W + 1$.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Refrigerator COP

A fridge operates between −18°C (inside) and 30°C (kitchen). Find the maximum COP and the minimum work to extract 500 J from inside.

$T_C = 255$ K, $T_H = 303$ K

$\text{COP}_{\text{ref}} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C} = \frac{255}{303 - 255} = \frac{255}{48} = \mathbf{5.31}$

$W_{\min} = \frac{Q_C}{\text{COP}} = \frac{500}{5.31} = \mathbf{94.2 \text{ J}}$

Example 2: Heat pump for home heating

A heat pump heats a house at 20°C using outside air at 0°C. It consumes 2.0 kW of electrical power. Find the maximum heat delivery rate.

$\text{COP}_{\text{hp}} = \frac{T_H}{T_H - T_C} = \frac{293}{293 - 273} = \frac{293}{20} = 14.65$

$Q_H = \text{COP} \times W = 14.65 \times 2.0 = \mathbf{29.3 \text{ kW}}$ (maximum theoretical)

Real: COP ≈ 3–5, so actual $Q_H \approx 6$–$10$ kW. Still 3–5× better than direct electric heating.

Why heat pumps beat electric heaters: A 2 kW electric heater delivers exactly 2 kW of heat ($\eta = 100\%$). A heat pump using 2 kW delivers 6–10 kW of heat (COP = 3–5) because it moves heat from outside rather than creating it. This is why heat pumps are the most efficient heating technology.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Calculate [3]

(a) A heat pump operates between 5°C and 25°C. Calculate: (i) the maximum COP, (ii) the heat delivered per kJ of work input.

(i) $\text{COP}_{\text{hp}} = \frac{T_H}{T_H - T_C} = \frac{298}{298 - 278} = \frac{298}{20} = \mathbf{14.9}$ [1]
(ii) $Q_H = \text{COP} \times W = 14.9 \times 1 = \mathbf{14.9 \text{ kJ}}$ per kJ of work [1]
In practice, real COP ≈ 3–5, so ~3–5 kJ per kJ of work [1].
Explain [2]

(b) Explain why the COP of a refrigerator decreases as the temperature difference between inside and outside increases.

$\text{COP} = T_C/(T_H - T_C)$. As $(T_H - T_C)$ increases, the denominator grows, so COP decreases [1]. Physically, a larger temperature difference means more work is needed per joule of heat extracted — it's "harder" to push heat across a bigger temperature gap, consistent with the second law [1].
Explain [2]

(c) Explain why $\text{COP}_{\text{hp}} = \text{COP}_{\text{ref}} + 1$.

By energy conservation: $Q_H = Q_C + W$. Dividing by $W$: $Q_H/W = Q_C/W + 1$, which gives $\text{COP}_{\text{hp}} = \text{COP}_{\text{ref}} + 1$ [1]. The heat pump delivers everything the refrigerator extracts PLUS the work input itself as heat — so its COP is always 1 more [1].
Discuss [3]

(d) Compare a heat pump (COP = 4) with a gas boiler (efficiency 90%) for home heating. Which delivers more heat per unit of primary energy, and why?

The heat pump delivers 4 kWh of heat per 1 kWh of electricity. If electricity is generated at 40% efficiency, 1 kWh of primary energy produces 0.4 kWh of electricity, which delivers $0.4 \times 4 = 1.6$ kWh of heat [1]. A gas boiler converts 1 kWh of gas into 0.9 kWh of heat [1]. The heat pump delivers 1.6 vs 0.9 kWh — nearly twice as much heat per unit of primary energy, even accounting for electricity generation losses. With renewable electricity (100% "efficiency"), the advantage is even larger: 4 kWh vs 0.9 kWh [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Reversed heat engine: uses work W to move heat from cold → hot. $Q_H = Q_C + W$.
  • Refrigerator COP: $Q_C/W = T_C/(T_H - T_C)$. Measures cooling per unit work.
  • Heat pump COP: $Q_H/W = T_H/(T_H - T_C)$. Measures heating per unit work.
  • $\text{COP}_{\text{hp}} = \text{COP}_{\text{ref}} + 1$ always (energy conservation).
  • COP > 1 is normal — not violating energy conservation (moving heat, not creating it).
  • Smaller ΔT → higher COP. Easier to pump heat across a small temperature gap.
  • Heat pumps are 3–5× more efficient than direct electric heating — the most efficient heating technology available.
