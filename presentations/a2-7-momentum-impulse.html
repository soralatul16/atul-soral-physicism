A.2.7

Momentum & Impulse

A truck at 20 km/h is harder to stop than a bicycle at 20 km/h. Why? Because it has more momentum. Momentum connects force to motion in the most powerful way — and it's one of the truly conserved quantities in the universe.

Linear Momentum

Momentum is the product of an object's mass and its velocity. It measures "how hard it is to stop."

$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$
  • Vector quantity — direction same as velocity
  • SI unit: kg m s$^{-1}$ (or equivalently, N s)
  • Large mass OR large velocity → large momentum
  • An object at rest has zero momentum
Why momentum matters: Newton's Second Law is really about momentum. $F = ma$ is a special case. The full version: $F = \frac{dp}{dt}$ — force equals the rate of change of momentum.

Newton's Second Law — The Momentum Version

The deeper form of Newton's Second Law:

$$\vec{F}_{net} = \frac{\Delta \vec{p}}{\Delta t} = \frac{m\vec{v} - m\vec{u}}{\Delta t}$$

Derivation (connecting to $F = ma$):

1Start: $F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{p_{final} - p_{initial}}{\Delta t}$
2If mass is constant: $= \frac{mv - mu}{\Delta t} = \frac{m(v-u)}{\Delta t}$
3Since $\frac{v-u}{\Delta t} = a$: $\quad F = ma$ ✓
Why the momentum form is MORE general: $F = ma$ only works if mass is constant. $F = \frac{dp}{dt}$ works even when mass changes (rockets burning fuel, conveyor belts adding sand, etc.). The IB Data Booklet gives the momentum form.
When mass changes: A rocket ejects fuel → its mass decreases → $F = ma$ is tricky. But $F = dp/dt$ handles it naturally. This is why the momentum form is the "real" Newton's Second Law.

Impulse — The Force-Time Product

Impulse is the product of force and the time for which it acts. It equals the change in momentum.

$$J = F \cdot \Delta t = \Delta p = m\Delta v = mv - mu$$
  • Vector quantity — same direction as the force
  • SI unit: N s (= kg m s$^{-1}$, same as momentum)
  • A large force for a short time can produce the same impulse as a small force for a long time
The Impulse-Momentum Theorem:
$$F \cdot \Delta t = \Delta p$$
This is the most important equation in this subtopic. It connects force (cause) to momentum change (effect).

Same impulse, different ways:

1000 N × 0.01 s = 10 N s

100 N × 0.1 s = 10 N s

10 N × 1 s = 10 N s

All produce the same Δp

Real-world applications:

Airbags: increase $\Delta t$ → reduce $F$

Bending knees on landing: increase $\Delta t$

Cricket catch: pulling hands back

Same $\Delta p$, longer time = less force = less injury

Force-Time Graph — Area = Impulse

The area under a Force-time graph gives the impulse (= change in momentum).

IB exam: "From the F-t graph, determine the change in momentum." → Find the area under the graph. For rectangles: $F \times t$. For triangles: $\frac{1}{2} \times F \times t$. For curves: count grid squares.

Worked Example 1 — Ball Hitting a Wall 5/10

A 0.15 kg ball hits a wall at 20 m/s and bounces back at 15 m/s. The contact time is 0.02 s. Find (a) the change in momentum (b) the average force on the ball.

(a) Change in momentum:

→Take "toward wall" as positive: $u = +20$, $v = -15$ (bounced back)
$$\Delta p = mv - mu = 0.15(-15) - 0.15(+20) = -2.25 - 3.0 = \mathbf{-5.25 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}}$$

Magnitude: 5.25 N s. Direction: away from wall (negative = bounce direction).

(b) Average force:

$$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{-5.25}{0.02} = \mathbf{-262.5 \text{ N}}$$

262.5 N directed away from wall (pushing ball back).

The bounce doubles the impulse: If the ball stuck to the wall ($v = 0$), $\Delta p = -3.0$ N s. The bounce makes it $-5.25$ N s — almost double. Bouncing produces MORE force than sticking. This is why bullet-proof vests that catch bullets are preferred over hard surfaces that deflect them.

Worked Example 2 — Car Safety 7/10

A 70 kg driver in a car crash decelerates from 25 m/s to 0. Compare the force experienced with (a) no seatbelt (stops in 0.05 s by hitting dashboard) and (b) with seatbelt + airbag (stops in 0.3 s).

Both cases: same $\Delta p$

$$\Delta p = 0 - 70(25) = -1750 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}$$

(a) No seatbelt:

$$F = \frac{1750}{0.05} = \mathbf{35{,}000 \text{ N}}$$

That's 35 kN — about 50× body weight. Fatal.

(b) With seatbelt + airbag:

$$F = \frac{1750}{0.3} = \mathbf{5{,}833 \text{ N}}$$

Still large, but 6× less — survivable.

The physics: Same impulse ($\Delta p$) in both cases — the driver goes from 25 m/s to 0 regardless. But increasing the time ($\Delta t$) by 6× reduces the force by 6×. Seatbelts, airbags, crumple zones, crash helmets — all work by increasing $\Delta t$.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A 0.40 kg ball is dropped from rest and hits the ground at 8.0 m/s. It bounces back up at 6.0 m/s. The ball is in contact with the ground for 0.015 s.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the momentum of the ball just before hitting the ground.

Taking downward as positive: $p = mv = 0.40 \times 8.0 = \mathbf{3.2 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}}$ downward [2]
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the change in momentum of the ball during the bounce.

$\Delta p = mv_{after} - mv_{before} = 0.40(-6.0) - 0.40(+8.0) = -2.4 - 3.2 = \mathbf{-5.6 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
(Negative = upward direction)
Calculate [2]

(c) Calculate the average force exerted by the ground on the ball during the bounce.

$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{5.6}{0.015} = \mathbf{373 \text{ N}}$ upward [2]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why the ball bounces to a lower height than it was dropped from.

The rebound speed (6.0 m/s) is less than the impact speed (8.0 m/s) [1]. Kinetic energy is lost during the collision (converted to heat, sound, deformation) — it's not a perfectly elastic bounce. Less KE → lower height [1].
Explain [2]

(e) If the ball landed on soft sand instead and did not bounce (stopped completely), would the force be greater or less? Explain.

The force would be less for two reasons: (1) $|\Delta p|$ is smaller — $|0 - 3.2| = 3.2$ vs $|-5.6| = 5.6$ (no bounce) [1], and (2) $\Delta t$ would be longer (sand deforms, increasing contact time) [1]. Both reduce $F = \Delta p / \Delta t$.

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A 1200 kg car travels at 15 m/s. Find its momentum.

$p = mv = 1200 \times 15 = \mathbf{18{,}000 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A constant force of 50 N acts on a 10 kg object for 4.0 s. If it starts from rest, find the final velocity.

$J = F \Delta t = 50 \times 4.0 = 200$ N s [1]
$J = \Delta p = mv - 0$ → $200 = 10v$ [1]
$v = \mathbf{20 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [3] Hard

A cricketer catches a fast ball by pulling their hands backward as they catch. Explain, using impulse, why this reduces the force on the hands.

The change in momentum of the ball is the same regardless of technique [1]. By pulling hands back, the catcher increases the time $\Delta t$ over which the ball decelerates [1]. Since $F = \Delta p / \Delta t$, increasing $\Delta t$ reduces $F$ — less force on the hands [1].
Calculate [3] Hard

A water hose delivers 2.0 kg of water per second at 15 m/s against a wall. The water doesn't bounce — it runs down the wall. Find the force on the wall.

Each second: $\Delta p = mv - mu = 0 - 2.0(15) = -30$ kg m/s (water stops) [1]
$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{30}{1} = \mathbf{30 \text{ N}}$ [1]
Note: if water bounced back, force would be higher (up to 60 N) [1]

Key Takeaways

  • Momentum: $p = mv$. Vector. Unit: kg m s$^{-1}$.
  • Newton's 2nd (momentum form): $F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$. More general than $F = ma$.
  • Impulse: $J = F \Delta t = \Delta p$. Area under F-t graph.
  • Bouncing produces MORE impulse than stopping — $\Delta p$ is larger.
  • Safety devices increase $\Delta t$ to reduce $F$ for the same $\Delta p$: airbags, helmets, crumple zones.
  • F-t graph: area = impulse = change in momentum.
  • Continuous force: for flows (hose, sand), $F = \frac{dm}{dt} \times v$.
