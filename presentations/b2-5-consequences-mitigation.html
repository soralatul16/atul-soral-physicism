Physical Consequences of Climate Change

Sea Level Rise

  • +0.3–1.0 m by 2100
  • Coastal flooding, erosion
  • Saltwater intrusion into freshwater
  • Small island nations at existential risk
  • ~1 billion people live below 10 m elevation

Extreme Weather

  • Heatwaves: 5× more frequent
  • Heavy rainfall: +7% per °C (Clausius-Clapeyron)
  • Stronger hurricanes (warmer SST = more energy)
  • Prolonged droughts in subtropics
  • Wildfire seasons lengthening

Cryosphere Loss

  • Arctic ice-free summers by 2040s
  • Greenland ice sheet destabilising
  • Mountain glacier retreat → water crisis
  • Permafrost thaw → infrastructure collapse
  • Polar amplification (2–4× faster warming)

The physics behind stronger storms

Warmer oceans provide more energy to hurricanes/cyclones (which are heat engines). The Clausius-Clapeyron relation: for every 1°C of warming, the atmosphere holds ~7% more water vapour → heavier rainfall. Warmer air also means more latent heat released during condensation → more powerful updrafts → stronger storms. The physics is straightforward — warmer = more energetic.

The 7% rule: Atmospheric moisture increases ~7% per °C (Clausius-Clapeyron). This is basic thermodynamics, not a model prediction. At +2°C, extreme rainfall events are ~14% heavier. At +4°C, ~28% heavier. This is why floods are becoming more intense globally.

Ecological & Human Consequences

Ecosystems

  • Coral reefs: bleaching at +1.5°C; 70–90% loss at +2°C; near-total loss at +3°C
  • Forests: Amazon dieback risk, boreal fires, species migration lag
  • Oceans: acidification (pH drop), deoxygenation, disrupted food chains
  • Biodiversity: 1 million species threatened with extinction (IPBES 2019)
  • Phenology mismatch: pollinators emerge before flowers bloom; predators miss prey migration

Human Systems

  • Food security: crop yields decline in tropics (heat stress, drought), some gains in high latitudes
  • Water: 2 billion+ face water stress; glacial melt threatens Himalayan supply
  • Health: heat deaths, expanded disease ranges (dengue, malaria), air quality decline
  • Migration: climate refugees from flood/drought zones — estimated 200 million by 2050
  • Infrastructure: coastal cities, roads on permafrost, power grids during heatwaves
IB exam context: Consequences questions often ask you to connect physics to impacts. Example: "Explain how rising sea surface temperatures affect tropical storms" → warmer SST → more evaporation → more latent heat → stronger updrafts → more intense storms. Always link the physical mechanism to the consequence.
Disproportionate impact: The countries least responsible for emissions (sub-Saharan Africa, small island states) face the worst consequences. This raises profound questions of climate justice — a key TOK/ethics discussion point in IB.

Mitigation — Reducing Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Mitigation = actions that reduce the cause of climate change (lowering GHG emissions or increasing sinks).

Renewable Energy

  • Solar PV: photovoltaic effect, ~20% efficiency, cost dropped 90% since 2010
  • Wind: KE → electrical, onshore and offshore, intermittent
  • Hydroelectric: GPE → KE → electrical, 85–90% efficient
  • Nuclear: fission, zero direct CO₂, waste and safety concerns
  • Geothermal: Earth's internal heat, location-limited

Energy Efficiency

  • Building insulation: reduces conduction losses (Fourier's law: lower $kA\Delta T/d$)
  • LED lighting: ~90% less energy than incandescent (converts electricity to light, not heat)
  • Electric vehicles: ~3× more efficient than ICE (no Carnot limit on electric motors)
  • Heat pumps: COP of 3–5, meaning 3–5 kWh heat per 1 kWh electricity
  • Industrial processes: waste heat recovery, process optimisation

Carbon removal (negative emissions)

  • Reforestation/afforestation: trees absorb CO₂ via photosynthesis (~5 tonnes CO₂/hectare/year)
  • Direct Air Capture (DAC): machines that chemically extract CO₂ from air — currently very expensive (~$600/tonne)
  • BECCS: burn biomass for energy + capture CO₂ = net negative emissions
  • Enhanced weathering: spread crusite minerals that react with CO₂ — slow but permanent
The physics constraint: Even if all emissions stopped today, ~1°C of additional warming is committed (ocean thermal inertia). CO₂ persists for 300–1000 years. To stabilise temperature, net emissions must reach zero. To lower temperature, emissions must go negative.

Adaptation — Living with the Change

Adaptation = actions that reduce the impact of climate change (accepting some warming will occur and preparing for it).

Infrastructure & Engineering

  • Sea walls, flood barriers, storm surge defences
  • Building codes for extreme heat/storms
  • Elevated roads and buildings in flood zones
  • Cooling centres and urban green spaces
  • Water storage and desalination plants

Agricultural & Social

  • Drought-resistant crop varieties
  • Shifting planting seasons and crop locations
  • Early warning systems for extreme weather
  • Insurance schemes for climate disasters
  • Managed retreat from high-risk coastal areas

Geoengineering — controversial last resorts

Solar Radiation Management (SRM)
Inject aerosols (SO₂) into stratosphere to reflect sunlight → reduce incoming S. Mimics volcanic eruptions. Fast, cheap, but doesn't address CO₂ or ocean acidification. Stops working immediately if stopped. Geopolitical risks.
Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR)
DAC, BECCS, ocean fertilisation, enhanced weathering. Addresses root cause but slow, expensive, and unproven at scale. Required in all IPCC pathways to meet 1.5°C.
Adaptation has limits. Beyond certain thresholds (e.g. wet-bulb temperature >35°C, where human body cannot cool itself), no adaptation is possible. Some ecosystems (coral reefs at +2°C) have no adaptation pathway. Mitigation remains essential — adaptation alone cannot solve the problem.

The Physics Behind Key Solutions

Solar panels

Physics: Photovoltaic effect. Photons with $E > E_g$ (band gap, ~1.1 eV for Si) excite electrons from valence to conduction band, creating current. Max theoretical efficiency ~33% (Shockley-Queisser). Practical ~20-25%.

Energy payback: A solar panel generates more energy in ~1-2 years than was used to manufacture it. Lifetime: 25-30 years.

Wind turbines

Physics: KE of air → rotational KE → electrical. Power $\propto v^3$ (doubling wind speed gives 8× power). Betz limit: max 59.3% of wind's KE extractable. Practical ~35-45%.

$P = \frac{1}{2}\rho A v^3 \times C_p$ where $C_p$ ≤ 0.593 (Betz).

Heat pumps

Physics: Reversed heat engine. Uses work to move heat from cold → hot (against natural direction). COP = $Q_H / W$ = 3–5 typically. Moves 3-5 kWh of heat for every 1 kWh of electricity.

3× more efficient than direct electric heating. Best replacement for gas boilers.

Nuclear fission

Physics: $E = \Delta mc^2$. U-235 fission releases ~200 MeV per event. Energy density: 1 kg uranium = ~80 TJ (vs ~30 MJ for 1 kg coal — 2.7 million × more).

Zero direct CO₂. Reliable baseload. Waste is tiny in volume but radioactive for millennia.

The intermittency problem: Solar and wind are variable — they don't produce when the sun doesn't shine or wind doesn't blow. Solutions: battery storage, pumped hydro, grid interconnection, hydrogen production, demand management. A mix of sources is needed.

The Carbon Budget — How Much Can We Still Emit?

The carbon budget is the total remaining CO₂ that can be emitted while staying below a temperature target.

TargetRemaining budget (from 2024)Years at current rate
1.5°C (50% chance)~200 Gt CO₂~5 years
1.5°C (67% chance)~100 Gt CO₂~2.5 years
2.0°C (67% chance)~900 Gt CO₂~22 years
Current annual emissions~40 Gt CO₂/year (and still rising)
The 1.5°C budget is nearly exhausted. At current emission rates (~40 Gt/year), the 1.5°C budget runs out in ~5 years. The 2.0°C budget gives ~22 years. This is why scientists say the next decade is critical — the physics sets a hard deadline that politics cannot negotiate with.
Net zero by when? To stay below 1.5°C: net zero CO₂ by ~2050. To stay below 2.0°C: net zero by ~2070. "Net zero" means any remaining emissions are balanced by removal (forests, DAC). It doesn't mean zero emissions — it means zero net addition to the atmosphere.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Outline [3]

(a) Outline three consequences of a 2°C rise in global average temperature.

1. Sea level rise of 0.3–0.6 m due to thermal expansion and ice melt, threatening coastal populations and infrastructure [1].
2. Loss of 70–90% of coral reefs due to bleaching from elevated sea surface temperatures and ocean acidification [1].
3. Increased frequency and intensity of extreme weather events (heatwaves 5× more likely, rainfall 14% heavier due to Clausius-Clapeyron) affecting agriculture, infrastructure, and human health [1].
Distinguish [2]

(b) Distinguish between mitigation and adaptation as responses to climate change.

Mitigation aims to reduce the cause of climate change by lowering GHG emissions or increasing carbon sinks (e.g., switching to renewable energy, reforestation) [1]. Adaptation aims to reduce the impact of climate change that has already occurred or is unavoidable (e.g., building sea walls, developing drought-resistant crops) [1].
Discuss [4]

(c) Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of using nuclear power as a mitigation strategy for climate change.

Advantages: Zero direct CO₂ emissions during operation [1]. Extremely high energy density ($E = \Delta mc^2$, 1 kg U-235 ≈ 2.7 million kg coal equivalent) — small fuel requirement, reliable baseload power independent of weather [1].
Disadvantages: Produces radioactive waste that remains hazardous for thousands of years, requiring long-term geological storage [1]. Risk of accidents (Chernobyl, Fukushima), high construction costs ($5–10 billion per plant), weapons proliferation concerns, and long build times (10–15 years) [1].
Explain [2]

(d) Explain what is meant by the "carbon budget" and why it imposes a time constraint on climate action.

The carbon budget is the total cumulative CO₂ emissions allowed while keeping global warming below a specific target (e.g., 1.5°C or 2.0°C) [1]. Since the relationship between cumulative CO₂ and temperature is approximately linear, the budget is finite and shrinks with every year of continued emissions. At current rates (~40 Gt/year), the 1.5°C budget is nearly exhausted, creating an urgent time constraint — every year of delay uses up more of the fixed budget, requiring steeper emission cuts later [1].

More Practice 8/10

Explain [3]

Explain, using physics, why electric vehicles are more energy-efficient than petrol cars, even if both ultimately derive their energy from fossil fuels.

Petrol engines are heat engines limited by the Carnot efficiency: $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H$, giving practical efficiencies of ~25–30% (most energy is wasted as heat) [1]. Electric motors convert electrical energy to mechanical energy at ~85–95% efficiency — no Carnot limit applies because they're not heat engines [1]. Even accounting for power station losses (~40% efficient for gas) and transmission losses (~5%), the well-to-wheel efficiency of an EV (~30%) exceeds a petrol car (~20%). With renewable electricity, EV efficiency approaches 80%+ [1].
Calculate [3]

A wind turbine has blade radius 50 m. Wind speed is 12 m/s. Air density = 1.2 kg/m³. Calculate the maximum power output, assuming the Betz limit applies (59.3%).

$A = \pi r^2 = \pi \times 50^2 = 7854$ m² [1]
$P_{\text{wind}} = \frac{1}{2}\rho Av^3 = \frac{1}{2} \times 1.2 \times 7854 \times 12^3 = \frac{1}{2} \times 1.2 \times 7854 \times 1728 = 8.14 \times 10^6$ W [1]
$P_{\text{max}} = 0.593 \times 8.14 \times 10^6 = \mathbf{4.83 \text{ MW}}$ [1]
Discuss [3]

A government proposes stratospheric aerosol injection (spraying SO₂ into the upper atmosphere) to reduce global warming. Evaluate this approach from a physics perspective.

SO₂ aerosols increase Earth's albedo by reflecting incoming solar radiation, effectively reducing the solar constant $S$ at the surface. This directly reduces $P_{\text{in}}$ in the energy balance, cooling the planet — similar to the cooling observed after major volcanic eruptions [1]. However, this does not reduce atmospheric CO₂ — ocean acidification continues unabated. If injection stops, the aerosols settle out within 1–2 years and all suppressed warming returns rapidly (termination shock) [1]. It also doesn't address the root cause (GHG emissions), risks altering precipitation patterns (monsoons), and raises governance issues — who controls the global thermostat? It should be viewed as a temporary emergency measure, not a solution [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Consequences: sea level rise, extreme weather (7%/°C more rainfall), ice loss, ecosystem collapse, food/water insecurity.
  • Mitigation (reduce the cause): renewables, efficiency, nuclear, carbon removal, reforestation.
  • Adaptation (reduce the impact): sea walls, drought crops, early warning, managed retreat. Has limits.
  • Physics of solutions: Solar (photovoltaic, ~20%), wind ($P \propto v^3$, Betz 59.3%), heat pumps (COP 3–5), nuclear ($E = \Delta mc^2$).
  • Carbon budget: 1.5°C budget ≈ 200 Gt remaining (~5 years). 2.0°C ≈ 900 Gt (~22 years). Net zero needed by 2050.
  • Geoengineering: SRM (aerosols) is fast but doesn't fix CO₂. CDR (DAC) fixes root cause but is slow and expensive.
  • Both mitigation AND adaptation are needed. Mitigation prevents the worst; adaptation handles the unavoidable.
