C.2.6

Polarisation

Giving a wave a single direction

Polarisation restricts a transverse wave's oscillations to a single plane. It's the one wave property that only transverse waves possess — proof that light is transverse — and it powers the screen you're reading, cuts glare from sunglasses, and reveals stress in engineering.

Unpolarised vs Polarised

In an unpolarised transverse wave the oscillations occur in all directions perpendicular to the travel direction. In a plane- (linearly-) polarised wave, the oscillations are confined to a single plane.

Unpolarised

Oscillates in all planes.

Plane-polarised

Oscillates in one plane only.

Only transverse waves can be polarised. Longitudinal waves (like sound) oscillate along the travel direction — there is no perpendicular plane to restrict — so they cannot be polarised. The fact that light can be polarised is direct evidence that light is a transverse wave.

Polarisers & Analysers

A polariser (e.g. a Polaroid filter) has a transmission axis. It only lets through the component of oscillation parallel to that axis, blocking the rest.

  • Unpolarised light through one polariser: emerges plane-polarised, with intensity reduced to half ($I = \tfrac12 I_0$), since on average only half the oscillation aligns with the axis.
  • A second polariser (the "analyser") then tests the polarised light: how much passes depends on the angle between the two axes.
Crossed polarisers: when the two axes are at 90° (crossed), no light passes through — the field extinguishes. Rotate one and the brightness varies smoothly, reaching maximum when the axes are parallel.

Malus's Law

For light that is already plane-polarised, passing through an analyser at angle θ to the polarisation direction, the transmitted intensity is:

$$I = I_0\cos^2\theta$$
  • θ = 0° (axes parallel): $\cos^2 0 = 1$, so $I = I_0$ — maximum transmission.
  • θ = 45°: $\cos^2 45° = 0.5$, so $I = 0.5\,I_0$ — half transmitted.
  • θ = 90° (axes crossed): $\cos^2 90° = 0$, so $I = 0$ — full extinction.

The full chain (from unpolarised)

First filter halves it: $I_1 = \tfrac12 I_0$. Then Malus's law on the second: $I_2 = I_1\cos^2\theta = \tfrac12 I_0\cos^2\theta$. Always halve first (unpolarised → polarised), then apply $\cos^2\theta$.

Polarisation by Reflection & Applications

Light reflecting off a non-metallic surface (water, glass, road) becomes partially polarised horizontally — this is the origin of glare.

Polaroid sunglasses

Have a vertical transmission axis to block the horizontally-polarised glare reflected from water and roads.

LCD screens

Use two crossed polarisers with a liquid crystal between them; a voltage twists the crystal to let light through pixel-by-pixel.

Stress analysis

Stressed transparent plastics rotate polarisation by different amounts, producing coloured fringes between crossed polarisers that reveal stress concentrations.

Photography & 3D

Polarising filters darken skies and cut reflections; polarised 3D glasses send a different image to each eye.

Optical activity: some substances (e.g. sugar solutions) rotate the plane of polarisation. Measuring this rotation with a polarimeter reveals concentration — used in the sugar industry and chemistry.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Malus's law

Plane-polarised light of intensity 12 W m⁻² passes through an analyser at 30° to its polarisation direction. Find the transmitted intensity.

$I = I_0\cos^2\theta = 12\cos^2 30° = 12\times(0.866)^2 = 12\times0.75$

$= \mathbf{9.0\ \text{W m}^{-2}}$

Example 2 — unpolarised through two filters

Unpolarised light of intensity 20 W m⁻² passes through two polarisers with axes at 60°. Find the final intensity.

After the first: $I_1 = \tfrac12(20) = 10$ W m⁻².

After the second: $I_2 = 10\cos^2 60° = 10\times(0.5)^2 = 10\times0.25$

$= \mathbf{2.5\ \text{W m}^{-2}}$

Example 3 — finding the angle

Plane-polarised light drops to 25% of its intensity through an analyser. Find the angle θ.

$\cos^2\theta = 0.25 \Rightarrow \cos\theta = 0.5$

$\theta = \mathbf{60^\circ}$

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

Explain [2]

(a) Explain what is meant by plane-polarised light, and why sound waves cannot be polarised.

Plane-polarised light has its oscillations (of the electric field) confined to a single plane perpendicular to the direction of travel [1]. Sound is a longitudinal wave, oscillating along the direction of travel, so there is no perpendicular plane to restrict — it cannot be polarised [1].
State [2]

(b) State what happens to the intensity of unpolarised light passing through a single ideal polariser, and why.

The intensity is reduced to half its original value, $I = \tfrac12 I_0$ [1], because on average only the component of the oscillations parallel to the transmission axis is passed, and this averages to half over all random orientations [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) Unpolarised light of intensity 16 W m⁻² passes through two polarisers with transmission axes at 45°. Calculate the transmitted intensity.

After first polariser: $I_1 = \tfrac12\times16 = 8.0$ W m⁻² [1]
After second (Malus): $I_2 = 8.0\cos^2 45° = 8.0\times0.5$ [1]
$= \mathbf{4.0\ \text{W m}^{-2}}$ [1]
Apply [2]

(d) Explain how Polaroid sunglasses reduce glare from a horizontal water surface.

Light reflected from the horizontal surface is partially polarised in the horizontal plane [1]. The sunglasses have a vertical transmission axis, so they absorb the horizontally-polarised reflected glare while still transmitting useful light, reducing the glare seen [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Polarisation restricts a transverse wave's oscillations to one plane; only transverse waves can be polarised (proof light is transverse).
  • Unpolarised → one polariser: emerges polarised at half intensity ($\tfrac12 I_0$).
  • Malus's law: $I = I_0\cos^2\theta$ for already-polarised light through an analyser.
  • Crossed (90°) polarisers block all light; parallel (0°) transmit maximum.
  • Reflection off non-metallic surfaces partially polarises light → glare.
  • Uses: Polaroid sunglasses, LCD screens, stress analysis, 3D glasses, polarimetry.
