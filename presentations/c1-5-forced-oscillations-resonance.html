C.1.5

Forced Oscillations & Resonance

Driving a system at its favourite frequency

Push a system with a periodic force and it will oscillate at the driving frequency. But push it at exactly its natural frequency and the amplitude explodes — this is resonance. It collapses bridges, tunes radios, and lets an MRI scanner see inside your body.

Natural & Forced Oscillations

  • Natural frequency ($f_0$): the frequency at which a system oscillates freely when displaced and released, with no external driving force. It's fixed by the system (e.g. mass and spring constant, or pendulum length).
  • Free oscillation: the system swings at $f_0$; without damping it would continue forever.
  • Forced oscillation: a periodic external driving force of frequency $f_d$ is continuously applied.

The key result

Once steady, a forced system oscillates at the driving frequency $f_d$ — not its own natural frequency. But the amplitude of that response depends strongly on how close $f_d$ is to $f_0$.

Two frequencies, one motion: don't confuse them. $f_0$ is a fixed property of the oscillator; $f_d$ is imposed by whatever drives it. Resonance is what happens when we tune $f_d$ toward $f_0$.

Resonance

Resonance occurs when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency ($f_d = f_0$). The system absorbs energy from the driver most efficiently, and the amplitude reaches a maximum.

Why the amplitude peaks: at $f_d = f_0$, the driving force is always in step with the motion — it pushes in the direction the system is already moving, doing positive work on every cycle. Energy is transferred into the oscillator faster than damping removes it, so the amplitude builds until the (larger) energy loss from damping finally balances the energy input.

Useful resonance

Radio/TV tuning (LC circuit), microwave ovens (water molecules), MRI scanners (nuclear spins), musical instruments, pushing a swing.

Destructive resonance

Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940), Millennium Bridge wobble, machinery vibrating apart, wine glass shattered by sound.

Soldiers break step when crossing a bridge so their marching frequency can't accidentally match the bridge's natural frequency and drive it into large-amplitude resonance.

The Resonance Curve & Effect of Damping

Plot amplitude against driving frequency and you get a peak at $f_0$. The amount of damping changes the peak's height and sharpness:

  • Less damping → taller and sharper peak; maximum amplitude much larger.
  • More damping → lower, broader, flatter peak; the resonance is "duller".
  • Heavier damping also shifts the peak to a driving frequency slightly below $f_0$.
Design lesson: to prevent dangerous resonance, engineers add damping — it caps the maximum amplitude the structure can reach even if it is driven at $f_0$.

Phase of the Response

The phase difference between the driver and the oscillator's displacement depends on the driving frequency:

Well below f₀

Displacement is nearly in phase (0°) with the driving force — the system follows the driver.

At resonance (f₀)

Displacement lags the driver by 90° ($\pi/2$). The driving force is exactly in phase with the velocity — so power transfer is maximum.

Well above f₀

Displacement is nearly anti-phase (180°) with the driver — the system can't keep up.

Why 90° = max power

Power = force × velocity. With force in phase with velocity, the driver does positive work throughout the cycle — peak energy input.

Steady state: the resonance description assumes the driving has been applied long enough that transients (the system's own natural response) have died away and the amplitude has settled to a constant value.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — natural frequency of a spring

A 0.50 kg mass hangs on a spring of constant $k = 200$ N m⁻¹. At what driving frequency will resonance occur?

Resonance at $f_0$: $f_0 = \tfrac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{k/m} = \tfrac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{200/0.50}$

$= \tfrac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{400} = \tfrac{20}{2\pi}$

$= \mathbf{3.2\ \text{Hz}}$

Example 2 — tuning a pendulum driver

A pendulum of length 0.80 m is driven. Find the driving period that produces the largest amplitude ($g=9.8$).

Largest amplitude at resonance, $T_d = T_0 = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.80/9.8}$

$= 2\pi\sqrt{0.0816} = 2\pi(0.286) = \mathbf{1.8\ \text{s}}$

Example 3 — reading a resonance curve

Two identical oscillators are driven, one in air and one in oil, producing resonance curves. Which shows the taller, sharper peak?

The one in air — it has less damping, so it absorbs more energy at resonance and reaches a larger, sharper peak amplitude. The one in oil is more heavily damped: a lower, broader peak.

Key Takeaways

  • Natural frequency $f_0$: the free-oscillation frequency, fixed by the system.
  • Forced oscillation: a periodic driver makes the system oscillate at the driving frequency $f_d$.
  • Resonance: when $f_d = f_0$, amplitude is maximum — energy transfer is most efficient.
  • Damping shapes the peak: less damping → taller, sharper; more damping → lower, broader, shifted below $f_0$.
  • Phase: displacement lags the driver by 90° at resonance (force in phase with velocity).
  • Uses & dangers: tuning, MRI, microwaves vs collapsing bridges and shattered glass.
