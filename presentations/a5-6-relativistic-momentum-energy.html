A.5.6

Relativistic Momentum & Energy

When p = mv Breaks Down

At everyday speeds, doubling the force doubles the acceleration forever. At relativistic speeds, pushing harder barely increases velocity — instead, the object gets "heavier." Momentum grows without limit, but speed never reaches c.

Relativistic Momentum

Classical momentum $p = mv$ works at low speeds. At high speeds, momentum must include the Lorentz factor:

Classical

$$p = mv$$

Linear in v. Fails at high speed.

Relativistic

$$p = \gamma mv$$

Grows without limit as $v \to c$.

Why $p = mv$ fails: If $p = mv$ were correct, a constant force would keep accelerating an object past $c$. The $\gamma$ factor ensures that as $v \to c$, $\gamma \to \infty$, so $p \to \infty$. Infinite momentum would require infinite force — which is why you can never reach $c$.
$m$ is always the rest mass. IB no longer uses "relativistic mass." The mass doesn't change — it's the $\gamma$ factor that makes momentum grow beyond the classical prediction.

Total Relativistic Energy

The total energy of a particle includes both its kinetic energy and its rest energy:

$$E = \gamma mc^2$$
Total energy = Lorentz factor × rest mass × speed of light squared

Rest Energy

$$E_0 = mc^2$$

Energy when $v = 0$
($\gamma = 1$)

Kinetic Energy

$$E_k = (\gamma - 1)mc^2$$

Extra energy from motion

Total

$$E = E_0 + E_k$$

$\gamma mc^2 = mc^2 + (\gamma-1)mc^2$

$E_k = (\gamma - 1)mc^2$ replaces $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$. At low speeds, $\gamma - 1 \approx \frac{v^2}{2c^2}$, so $E_k \approx \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ — the classical formula is recovered. At high speeds, $E_k$ grows without limit.

The Energy-Momentum Relation

The most important equation in relativistic mechanics connects total energy, rest energy, and momentum:

$$E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$$

Special cases

Particle at rest ($p = 0$):
$E^2 = 0 + (mc^2)^2$
$E = mc^2$ ← rest energy
Photon ($m = 0$):
$E^2 = (pc)^2 + 0$
$E = pc$ ← all momentum, no mass
This is the master equation. It works for everything — massive particles, photons, systems of particles. Many IB problems give you two of the three quantities and ask for the third. Use this triangle.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Proton at 0.80c

Find the relativistic momentum, total energy, and kinetic energy of a proton ($m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg) travelling at 0.80c.

γ: $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.64}} = \frac{1}{0.60} = 1.667$

Momentum: $p = \gamma mv = 1.667 \times 1.67\times10^{-27} \times 0.80 \times 3\times10^8 = \mathbf{6.68 \times 10^{-19} \text{ kg m s}^{-1}}$

Total energy: $E = \gamma mc^2 = 1.667 \times 1.67\times10^{-27} \times (3\times10^8)^2 = \mathbf{2.51 \times 10^{-10} \text{ J} = 1567 \text{ MeV}}$

Rest energy: $E_0 = mc^2 = 1.503 \times 10^{-10}$ J $= 938.3$ MeV

Kinetic energy: $E_k = E - E_0 = (\gamma - 1)mc^2 = 0.667 \times 938.3 = \mathbf{626 \text{ MeV}}$

Compare with classical: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}(938.3)(0.64) = 300$ MeV. The relativistic KE (626 MeV) is more than double the classical prediction. At 0.80c, $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$ is significantly wrong.

Why Nothing with Mass Can Reach c

Three ways to see the cosmic speed limit:

1. Infinite energy required

$E = \gamma mc^2$. As $v \to c$, $\gamma \to \infty$, so $E \to \infty$. You'd need infinite energy to accelerate any mass to $c$. The universe doesn't have infinite energy.

2. Infinite momentum required

$p = \gamma mv \to \infty$ as $v \to c$. Since $F = dp/dt$, you'd need to sustain a force for infinite time (or apply infinite force) to reach $c$.

3. Diminishing returns

Each additional unit of energy produces less speed increase. Going from 0 to 0.9c takes a certain energy. Going from 0.9c to 0.99c takes 3× more. Going from 0.99c to 0.999c takes 3× more again. The gap never closes.

The LHC example: Protons at the LHC have $\gamma \approx 7000$ and travel at 0.999999991c. Their total energy is about 7000 times their rest energy. Despite this enormous energy, they are still 0.000000009c short of light speed — and no amount of energy can close that gap.

Photons: Massless but Not Momentumless

Photons have zero mass but carry both energy and momentum:

Photon Energy

$$E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

Photon Momentum

$$p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}$$

From $E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$ with $m = 0$: $E = pc$, so $p = E/c$.

Example: Momentum of a photon

A gamma ray has energy 2.0 MeV. What is its momentum?

$p = E/c = 2.0 \text{ MeV}/c = 2.0 \text{ MeV/c}$

In SI: $p = \frac{2.0 \times 10^6 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}}{3 \times 10^8} = 1.07 \times 10^{-21}$ kg m s⁻¹

Photons always travel at $c$. They have no rest frame — you can't "stop" a photon. The formula $p = \gamma mv$ doesn't apply to photons ($\gamma$ is undefined for $v = c$ and $m = 0$). Use $p = E/c$ instead.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Calculate [3]

(a) An electron ($m = 0.511$ MeV/c²) travels at 0.90c. Calculate its relativistic momentum in MeV/c.

$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.81}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.19}} = 2.294$ [1]
$p = \gamma mv = 2.294 \times 0.511 \times 0.90c$ [1]
$p = 2.294 \times 0.511 \times 0.90 = \mathbf{1.055 \text{ MeV/c}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(b) Calculate the total energy and kinetic energy of the electron in (a).

$E = \gamma mc^2 = 2.294 \times 0.511 = \mathbf{1.172 \text{ MeV}}$ [1]
$E_0 = mc^2 = 0.511$ MeV [1]
$E_k = E - E_0 = 1.172 - 0.511 = \mathbf{0.661 \text{ MeV}}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) A particle has total energy 5.0 GeV and momentum 4.8 GeV/c. Calculate its rest mass in GeV/c².

$E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$ → $(mc^2)^2 = E^2 - (pc)^2$ [1]
$(mc^2)^2 = 5.0^2 - 4.8^2 = 25.0 - 23.04 = 1.96$ → $mc^2 = 1.4$ GeV
$m = \mathbf{1.4 \text{ GeV/c}^2}$ (likely a charm quark bound state) [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why a constant force applied to a particle produces a decreasing acceleration as the particle's speed approaches $c$.

$F = \frac{dp}{dt} = \frac{d(\gamma mv)}{dt}$. As $v$ increases, $\gamma$ increases rapidly, making the particle harder to accelerate [1]. The same force produces a smaller change in velocity because most of the energy goes into increasing $\gamma$ (effectively increasing the particle's inertia) rather than increasing $v$ [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

A proton ($mc^2 = 938.3$ MeV) has kinetic energy 2000 MeV. Find: (i) its Lorentz factor, (ii) its speed as a fraction of c.

(i) $E_k = (\gamma - 1)mc^2$ → $2000 = (\gamma - 1) \times 938.3$
$\gamma - 1 = 2.131$ → $\gamma = \mathbf{3.131}$ [1]
(ii) $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}}$ → $1 - v^2/c^2 = 1/\gamma^2 = 0.102$ [1]
$v = c\sqrt{1 - 0.102} = c\sqrt{0.898} = \mathbf{0.948c}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

An electron and a positron (both $mc^2 = 0.511$ MeV), each with kinetic energy 1.5 MeV, annihilate to produce two photons. Calculate the energy of each photon.

Total energy of electron: $0.511 + 1.5 = 2.011$ MeV [1]
Total energy of positron: $0.511 + 1.5 = 2.011$ MeV
Total system energy: $2 \times 2.011 = 4.022$ MeV [1]
Each photon: $4.022 / 2 = \mathbf{2.011 \text{ MeV}}$ [1]
Show [2]

Show that at low speeds ($v \ll c$), the relativistic kinetic energy $E_k = (\gamma - 1)mc^2$ reduces to $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

For $v \ll c$: $\gamma = (1 - v^2/c^2)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}\frac{v^2}{c^2}$ (binomial approximation) [1]
$\gamma - 1 \approx \frac{v^2}{2c^2}$, so $E_k = \frac{v^2}{2c^2} \times mc^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ ✓ [1]

Key Takeaways

  • Relativistic momentum: $p = \gamma mv$. Grows without limit as $v \to c$.
  • Total energy: $E = \gamma mc^2 = E_0 + E_k$.
  • Rest energy: $E_0 = mc^2$. Kinetic energy: $E_k = (\gamma - 1)mc^2$.
  • Energy-momentum relation: $E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$ — the master equation.
  • Photons: $m = 0$, $E = pc = hf$, $p = h/\lambda$. Always travel at $c$.
  • Speed limit: reaching $c$ requires infinite energy. LHC protons at $\gamma = 7000$ are still below $c$.
  • Units: Energy in MeV, mass in MeV/c², momentum in MeV/c. Know particle rest masses.
  • Low speed limit: $E_k \to \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ and $p \to mv$ when $v \ll c$.
