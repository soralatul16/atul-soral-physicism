Thermal Energy Transfer

Specific Heat Capacity & Phase Changes

When you heat something, where does the energy go? How much energy does it take to boil a kettle? Why does sand burn your feet but ocean water stays cool? It all comes down to one property — specific heat capacity.

Thermal Energy vs Temperature

Thermal Energy (Q)

  • Total kinetic energy of all particles in a substance
  • Measured in Joules (J)
  • Depends on: mass, temperature, and material
  • A bathtub of warm water has more thermal energy than a cup of boiling water

Temperature (T)

  • Average kinetic energy per particle
  • Measured in K (kelvin) or °C
  • Does NOT depend on mass
  • The cup of boiling water is hotter (higher T) even though it has less total energy
Key distinction for IB: Temperature = average KE per particle. Thermal energy = total KE of all particles. A large body at low temperature can have more thermal energy than a small body at high temperature.

Specific Heat Capacity (c)

Definition: The energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C).

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Example 1: Heating water in a kettle

A kettle heats 1.5 kg of water from 20°C to 100°C. How much energy is needed?
(c for water = 4186 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹)

Step 1: Identify quantities: $m = 1.5$ kg, $c = 4186$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, $\Delta T = 100 - 20 = 80$ K

Step 2: Apply formula: $Q = mc\Delta T = 1.5 \times 4186 \times 80$

Step 3: $Q = 502\,320$ J $= \mathbf{502 \text{ kJ}}$ (or $5.0 \times 10^5$ J)

Example 2: Finding specific heat capacity

A 0.5 kg metal block absorbs 11,250 J and its temperature rises from 25°C to 75°C. Find c.

Step 1: $\Delta T = 75 - 25 = 50$ K. Rearrange: $c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta T}$

Step 2: $c = \frac{11250}{0.5 \times 50} = \frac{11250}{25} = \mathbf{450 \text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}}$ → Iron/Steel

Exam tip: Always check your answer makes physical sense. If you get c = 50,000, something is wrong — most metals are 100–1000 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. Water (4186) is unusually high.

Thermal Equilibrium & Heat Transfer

Heat always flows from hot → cold until thermal equilibrium (same temperature).

Conservation of energy: Energy lost by hot object = Energy gained by cold object
$Q_{\text{lost}} = Q_{\text{gained}}$
$m_1 c_1 \Delta T_1 = m_2 c_2 \Delta T_2$

Three Methods of Heat Transfer

Conduction
Particle vibrations pass through solid material. Metals are best conductors.
Convection
Hot fluid rises, cold sinks → circulation currents. Only in fluids (liquids & gases).
Radiation
Electromagnetic waves (infrared). No medium needed — travels through vacuum.

Phase Changes & Latent Heat

During a phase change, temperature stays constant — all energy goes into breaking/forming bonds, not increasing KE.

$$Q = mL$$
Specific Latent Heat of Fusion ($L_f$)
Energy to melt/freeze 1 kg at constant temperature
Water: $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5$ J/kg
Specific Latent Heat of Vaporisation ($L_v$)
Energy to boil/condense 1 kg at constant temperature
Water: $L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6$ J/kg
IB exam favourite: "Explain why the temperature remains constant during a phase change." Answer: All energy supplied is used to break intermolecular bonds (increase PE, not KE). Since temperature depends on average KE, it stays constant until all bonds are broken.

Multi-Step Problem: Ice to Steam

Calculate the total energy to convert 0.2 kg of ice at −10°C to steam at 120°C.

$c_{\text{ice}} = 2090$, $c_{\text{water}} = 4186$, $c_{\text{steam}} = 2010$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹  |  $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5$, $L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6$ J/kg

Step 1 — Heat ice from −10°C to 0°C: $Q_1 = mc_{\text{ice}}\Delta T = 0.2 \times 2090 \times 10 = 4\,180$ J

Step 2 — Melt ice at 0°C: $Q_2 = mL_f = 0.2 \times 3.34 \times 10^5 = 66\,800$ J

Step 3 — Heat water from 0°C to 100°C: $Q_3 = mc_{\text{water}}\Delta T = 0.2 \times 4186 \times 100 = 83\,720$ J

Step 4 — Boil water at 100°C: $Q_4 = mL_v = 0.2 \times 2.26 \times 10^6 = 452\,000$ J

Step 5 — Heat steam from 100°C to 120°C: $Q_5 = mc_{\text{steam}}\Delta T = 0.2 \times 2010 \times 20 = 8\,040$ J

Total: $Q = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3 + Q_4 + Q_5 = 4180 + 66800 + 83720 + 452000 + 8040 = \mathbf{614\,740 \text{ J} \approx 615 \text{ kJ}}$

Notice: The vaporisation step ($Q_4 = 452$ kJ) alone accounts for 73% of the total energy. Boiling requires far more energy than any heating step — that's why a watched pot takes so long!

A 0.3 kg copper block at 200°C is dropped into 0.5 kg of water at 15°C in an insulated container. (c for copper = 385 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, c for water = 4186 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹)

State [1]

(a) State the direction of thermal energy transfer between the copper block and water.

Thermal energy transfers from the copper block (hot, 200°C) to the water (cold, 15°C) [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) Calculate the final equilibrium temperature of the system. Assume no energy loss to surroundings.

Heat lost by copper = Heat gained by water [1]
$m_{\text{Cu}} c_{\text{Cu}} (200 - T_f) = m_w c_w (T_f - 15)$
$0.3 \times 385 \times (200 - T_f) = 0.5 \times 4186 \times (T_f - 15)$
$115.5(200 - T_f) = 2093(T_f - 15)$
$23100 - 115.5T_f = 2093T_f - 31395$
$54495 = 2208.5T_f$
$T_f = \mathbf{24.7°C}$ [1] [1]
Explain [2]

(c) Explain why the final temperature is much closer to the initial water temperature than the initial copper temperature.

Water has a much higher specific heat capacity (4186 vs 385 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹) and a larger mass (0.5 vs 0.3 kg) [1]. It requires much more energy per degree of temperature change, so the same energy transfer causes a much smaller temperature rise in the water [1].
Suggest [2]

(d) In practice, the measured final temperature is lower than the calculated value. Suggest two reasons for this discrepancy.

1. Heat is lost to the container walls and surroundings (not perfectly insulated) [1].
2. Some energy is absorbed by the container itself, which also warms up [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

A 2.0 kW electric heater is used to heat 1.5 kg of water from 20°C. How long does it take to reach 100°C? (Assume no heat loss.)

Energy needed: $Q = mc\Delta T = 1.5 \times 4186 \times 80 = 502\,320$ J [1]
Power: $P = \frac{Q}{t}$ → $t = \frac{Q}{P} = \frac{502320}{2000}$ [1]
$t = \mathbf{251 \text{ s} \approx 4.2 \text{ minutes}}$ [1]
Calculate [4]

How much additional energy is needed to convert the 1.5 kg of water at 100°C completely to steam? ($L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6$ J/kg). How long would this take with the same 2.0 kW heater?

$Q = mL_v = 1.5 \times 2.26 \times 10^6 = 3\,390\,000$ J $= 3.39$ MJ [1] [1]
$t = \frac{Q}{P} = \frac{3390000}{2000} = \mathbf{1695 \text{ s} \approx 28.3 \text{ minutes}}$ [1]
Vaporisation takes ~7× longer than heating to 100°C, despite no temperature change [1].
Explain [3]

On a sunny day, a car's metal bonnet feels much hotter than its glass windshield, even though both receive the same solar radiation. Explain this observation.

Metal (e.g. steel, c ≈ 450) has a lower specific heat capacity than glass (c ≈ 840) [1]. For the same energy input, the metal undergoes a larger temperature rise ($\Delta T = \frac{Q}{mc}$, smaller c → larger ΔT) [1]. Metal is also a better thermal conductor, so heat concentrates at the surface where your hand touches [1].
Real-world link: This is why coastal cities have milder climates than inland cities. Water's high specific heat capacity means oceans absorb and release heat slowly, moderating nearby temperatures. This is called the maritime effect.

Key Takeaways

  • Thermal energy = total KE of all particles. Temperature = average KE per particle.
  • $Q = mc\Delta T$ — the core formula. Know how to rearrange for $m$, $c$, or $\Delta T$.
  • High c (like water, 4186) = hard to heat/cool. Low c (like copper, 385) = heats/cools quickly.
  • $Q = mL$ — energy for phase changes at constant temperature.
  • $L_v \gg L_f$ — vaporisation needs much more energy than melting (breaking all bonds vs loosening them).
  • Phase change plateau: Temperature stays constant because energy goes into breaking bonds (PE), not increasing KE.
  • Thermal equilibrium: $Q_{\text{lost}} = Q_{\text{gained}}$ — conservation of energy.
  • Heat transfers: conduction (solids), convection (fluids), radiation (all, no medium needed).
