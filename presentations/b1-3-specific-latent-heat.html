Specific Latent Heat

Phase Changes Without Temperature Change

You keep heating ice at 0°C and the temperature doesn't budge — for minutes. All that energy is going somewhere, but not into making things hotter. It's breaking bonds. That hidden energy is latent heat.

What is Latent Heat?

"Latent" means hidden. During a phase change, energy is absorbed or released without any temperature change. The energy goes into changing the arrangement of molecules — breaking or forming intermolecular bonds.

Specific Latent Heat of Fusion ($L_f$)

Energy to melt (or freeze) 1 kg at constant temperature.

Water: $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5$ J/kg

Specific Latent Heat of Vaporisation ($L_v$)

Energy to boil (or condense) 1 kg at constant temperature.

Water: $L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6$ J/kg

$L_v$ is always much larger than $L_f$. Vaporisation completely separates molecules (breaks all bonds). Melting only loosens them. For water: $L_v \approx 7 \times L_f$.

Why Doesn't Temperature Change During a Phase Change?

The IB Answer (memorise this)

During a phase change, all energy supplied is used to overcome intermolecular forces (increase the potential energy of the molecules). Since temperature depends on average kinetic energy, and KE does not change during a phase change, the temperature remains constant.

Melting (solid → liquid)

  • Energy breaks some bonds between fixed particles
  • Particles gain freedom to move but don't speed up
  • PE increases, KE stays the same
  • Internal energy increases (PE part only)

Boiling (liquid → gas)

  • Energy breaks all remaining bonds
  • Particles escape the liquid completely
  • PE increases dramatically, KE stays the same
  • Much more energy needed than melting
Reverse processes release energy: Freezing releases $L_f$ (bonds form, PE decreases). Condensing releases $L_v$ (bonds reform). That's why steam burns are worse than boiling water burns — condensing steam releases 2.26 MJ/kg onto your skin.

The Heating Curve of Water

Slopes = $\frac{1}{mc}$. Steeper slope = smaller c (heats faster). Ice and steam have steeper slopes than water because $c_{\text{ice}}$ and $c_{\text{steam}}$ (~2000) are less than $c_{\text{water}}$ (4186).
IB exam: "Sketch and label a heating curve" is a common question. Include: axis labels, 5 regions (3 slopes + 2 plateaus), temperatures (0°C and 100°C), and state which formula applies ($Q = mc\Delta T$ or $Q = mL$) in each region.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Melting ice

How much energy is needed to melt 0.50 kg of ice at 0°C?

$Q = mL_f = 0.50 \times 3.34 \times 10^5 = \mathbf{167\,000 \text{ J} = 167 \text{ kJ}}$

Example 2: Ice at −20°C to steam at 120°C (multi-step)

Calculate the total energy to convert 0.10 kg of ice at −20°C to steam at 120°C.

Step 1: Heat ice −20→0°C: $Q_1 = 0.10 \times 2090 \times 20 = 4180$ J

Step 2: Melt ice at 0°C: $Q_2 = 0.10 \times 3.34 \times 10^5 = 33\,400$ J

Step 3: Heat water 0→100°C: $Q_3 = 0.10 \times 4186 \times 100 = 41\,860$ J

Step 4: Boil water at 100°C: $Q_4 = 0.10 \times 2.26 \times 10^6 = 226\,000$ J

Step 5: Heat steam 100→120°C: $Q_5 = 0.10 \times 2010 \times 20 = 4020$ J

Total: $Q = 4180 + 33400 + 41860 + 226000 + 4020 = \mathbf{309\,460 \text{ J} \approx 309 \text{ kJ}}$

Vaporisation alone = 73% of total energy. Boiling dominates.

Evaporation vs Boiling

EVAPORATIONBOILING
TemperatureAt any temperatureOnly at boiling point
WhereSurface onlyThroughout the liquid
BubblesNo bubblesBubbles form inside liquid
RateSlow, gradualRapid, vigorous
Energy sourceInternal energy of the liquid itselfExternal heat source
Effect on TCools the liquid downTemperature stays constant
Why evaporation cools: The fastest molecules (highest KE) escape from the surface. The remaining molecules have lower average KE → lower temperature. This is how sweating cools you — evaporating sweat removes energy from your skin.

Factors that increase evaporation rate

  • Higher temperature — more molecules have enough KE to escape
  • Larger surface area — more molecules at the surface
  • Air movement (wind) — removes escaped molecules, preventing return
  • Lower humidity — less saturated air above the surface

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Define [1]

(a) Define specific latent heat of vaporisation.

The energy required to change 1 kg of a substance from liquid to gas at constant temperature [1].
Explain [2]

(b) Explain why the temperature of a substance remains constant during a phase change, even though energy is being supplied.

The energy supplied is used to overcome/break intermolecular bonds, increasing the potential energy of the molecules [1]. Since temperature depends on average kinetic energy and the KE does not change during the phase change, the temperature remains constant [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A 2.0 kW heater is used to boil water already at 100°C. How long does it take to boil away 0.50 kg of water? ($L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6$ J/kg)

$Q = mL_v = 0.50 \times 2.26 \times 10^6 = 1.13 \times 10^6$ J [1]
$t = \frac{Q}{P} = \frac{1.13 \times 10^6}{2000}$ [1]
$t = \mathbf{565 \text{ s} \approx 9.4 \text{ min}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why $L_v$ is much larger than $L_f$ for the same substance.

During vaporisation, molecules must completely overcome all intermolecular forces and separate entirely, gaining enough energy to escape the liquid [1]. During fusion, bonds are only partially broken — molecules gain freedom to slide past each other but remain close together. Much less energy is needed for this partial separation [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [4]

0.30 kg of ice at −15°C is placed in a container and heated until it becomes water at 25°C. Calculate the total energy required. ($c_{\text{ice}} = 2090$, $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5$, $c_w = 4186$)

Heat ice: $Q_1 = 0.30 \times 2090 \times 15 = 9405$ J [1]
Melt ice: $Q_2 = 0.30 \times 3.34 \times 10^5 = 100\,200$ J [1]
Heat water: $Q_3 = 0.30 \times 4186 \times 25 = 31\,395$ J [1]
Total: $9405 + 100200 + 31395 = \mathbf{141\,000 \text{ J} = 141 \text{ kJ}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

A 500 W heater takes 8.0 minutes to completely melt a 1.2 kg block of a solid at its melting point. Calculate $L_f$ for the substance.

$Q = Pt = 500 \times 480 = 240\,000$ J [1]
$L_f = \frac{Q}{m} = \frac{240000}{1.2}$ [1]
$L_f = \mathbf{2.0 \times 10^5 \text{ J kg}^{-1}}$ [1]
Distinguish [3]

Compare and contrast evaporation and boiling. State two similarities and two differences.

Similarities: Both involve liquid→gas phase change [1]. Both require energy (latent heat of vaporisation) [½].
Differences: Evaporation occurs at any temperature / boiling only at boiling point [1]. Evaporation occurs at the surface / boiling occurs throughout the liquid [1].
Steam burns are worse than water burns: 100°C steam touching your skin first condenses, releasing $L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6$ J/kg. Then the resulting 100°C water cools from 100°C to skin temperature. You get hit by the latent heat AND the cooling heat — far more energy than 100°C water alone.

Key Takeaways

  • $Q = mL$ — energy for phase change at constant temperature.
  • $L_f$ = latent heat of fusion (melting/freezing). $L_v$ = latent heat of vaporisation (boiling/condensing).
  • $L_v \gg L_f$ — vaporisation breaks all bonds; melting only loosens them.
  • Constant T during phase change because energy → PE (breaking bonds), not KE (temperature).
  • Heating curve: 5 regions — 3 slopes ($Q = mc\Delta T$) + 2 plateaus ($Q = mL$).
  • Multi-step problems: add up energy for each segment (heating + melting + heating + boiling + heating).
  • Evaporation ≠ boiling. Evaporation: any T, surface only, cools liquid. Boiling: at boiling point, throughout, constant T.
  • Steam burns are worse because condensation releases $L_v$ on top of the hot water's energy.
