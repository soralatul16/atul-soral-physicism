Kinetic Energy &
the Work-Energy Theorem

Energy of motion. The faster you go, the more energy you carry — and it scales with the SQUARE of speed. Double the speed, quadruple the energy. This is why car crashes at high speed are devastating.

Atul Soral Physicism

Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses due to its motion.

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
  • Scalar quantity — always positive (or zero)
  • SI unit: Joule (J)
  • Depends on mass (linearly) and speed (squared)
  • An object at rest has zero kinetic energy
The $v^2$ is critical. Double the speed → 4× the KE. Triple the speed → 9× the KE. This is why:
• A car at 60 km/h has 4× the KE of a car at 30 km/h
• Braking distance scales with $v^2$ — double speed = 4× stopping distance
• A 200 km/h crash is 4× worse than 100 km/h (not 2×)
Connection to momentum: $p = mv$ and $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. So $E_k = \frac{p^2}{2m}$. Same momentum doesn't mean same KE — a heavy object with the same $p$ has less KE than a light one.

Deriving $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$

Start from $W = Fs$ and use SUVAT to eliminate $s$:

1Work done by net force: $W_{net} = F_{net} \cdot s$
2From Newton's 2nd: $F_{net} = ma$, so $W_{net} = mas$
3From SUVAT: $v^2 = u^2 + 2as \implies as = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2}$
4Substitute: $W_{net} = m \cdot \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2$
$$\boxed{W_{net} = \Delta E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2}$$
This IS the Work-Energy Theorem. The net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy. It connects force (cause) to energy change (effect).
If $u = 0$ (starts from rest): $W_{net} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. All the net work becomes kinetic energy. This is where the KE formula comes from — it's the work needed to accelerate an object from rest to speed $v$.
A.3 Work, Energy & Power  |  A.3.2 KE & Work-Energy TheoremThe Theorem

The Work-Energy Theorem

The net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy.
$$W_{net} = \Delta E_k = E_{k,final} - E_{k,initial}$$

Three important consequences:

  • $W_{net} > 0$: KE increases → object speeds up
  • $W_{net} < 0$: KE decreases → object slows down
  • $W_{net} = 0$: KE unchanged → constant speed (or at rest)
It's the NET work, not individual work. If you push a box forward ($W_{push} = +200$ J) and friction acts backward ($W_{friction} = -150$ J), then $W_{net} = 50$ J. The box gains 50 J of KE.
Why is this so powerful? The W-E theorem lets you find final speed WITHOUT knowing acceleration or time. If you know forces and displacement, you go straight to speed. It's an energy shortcut past $F = ma$ + SUVAT.

Worked Example 1 — Finding Speed 3/10

A net force does 500 J of work on a 4.0 kg object initially at rest. Find the final speed.
1$W_{net} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2$
2$500 = \frac{1}{2}(4.0)v^2 - 0$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 500}{4.0}} = \sqrt{250} = \mathbf{15.8 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$

Worked Example 2 — Braking Distance 6/10

A 1200 kg car at 25 m/s brakes to rest. The braking force is 8000 N. Find the braking distance.
1$W_{net} = \Delta E_k$: $-F \times s = 0 - \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
2$-8000 \times s = -\frac{1}{2}(1200)(625)$
$$s = \frac{\frac{1}{2}(1200)(625)}{8000} = \frac{375000}{8000} = \mathbf{46.9 \text{ m}}$$
At 50 m/s (double speed): $s = \frac{\frac{1}{2}(1200)(2500)}{8000} = 187.5$ m — 4× the distance. Braking distance $\propto v^2$.

Worked Example 3 — Push with Friction 7/10

A 6.0 kg box is pushed 10 m along a floor by a 50 N horizontal force. Friction is 20 N. Starting from rest, find the final speed.
1Work by push: $W_{push} = 50 \times 10 = +500$ J
2Work by friction: $W_{friction} = -20 \times 10 = -200$ J
3Net work: $W_{net} = 500 - 200 = 300$ J
4$W_{net} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - 0$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 300}{6.0}} = \sqrt{100} = \mathbf{10 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$
Compare with $F = ma$ method: $a = (50-20)/6 = 5$ m/s$^2$. Then $v^2 = 0 + 2(5)(10) = 100 \implies v = 10$ m/s. Same answer — the W-E theorem is just a shortcut that avoids finding $a$ first.
Where did the 200 J from friction go? Converted to thermal energy (heat) in the box and floor surfaces. Total energy is conserved: 500 J input = 300 J KE + 200 J heat.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A 0.50 kg ball is thrown vertically upward at 12 m/s from ground level. Air resistance is negligible.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the kinetic energy of the ball at launch.

$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)(144) = \mathbf{36 \text{ J}}$ [2]
Calculate [2]

(b) Using the work-energy theorem, calculate the maximum height reached.

At max height, $v = 0$ so $E_k = 0$. Work by gravity: $W = -mgh$
$W_{net} = \Delta E_k$: $-mgh = 0 - 36$
$h = \frac{36}{mg} = \frac{36}{4.9} = \mathbf{7.35 \text{ m}}$ [2]
Calculate [2]

(c) Find the speed of the ball at half the maximum height (3.67 m).

$W_{gravity} = -mg(3.67) = -0.50(9.8)(3.67) = -18.0$ J
$-18 = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)v^2 - 36$ → $\frac{1}{2}(0.50)v^2 = 18$ → $v = \sqrt{72} = \mathbf{8.49 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why the speed at half the maximum height is NOT half the launch speed.

KE depends on $v^2$, not $v$ [1]. At half height, half the KE has been converted to PE, so $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 36 = 18$ J. This gives $v = \sqrt{72} = 8.49$ m/s, not $12/2 = 6$ m/s. The relationship between height and speed is non-linear because of the $v^2$ dependence [1].
Determine [3]

(e) With air resistance of 0.80 N, determine the actual maximum height and the energy lost to air resistance.

Total upward forces doing negative work: gravity + air resistance
$W_{net} = -(mg + f)h = -(4.9 + 0.80)h = -5.7h$
$-5.7h = 0 - 36 \implies h = \frac{36}{5.7} = \mathbf{6.32 \text{ m}}$ [2]
Energy to air: $W_{air} = 0.80 \times 6.32 = \mathbf{5.05 \text{ J}}$ [1]

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A 2.0 kg ball moves at 8.0 m/s. Find its kinetic energy.

$E_k = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(64) = \mathbf{64 \text{ J}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A 1500 kg car decelerates from 30 m/s to 10 m/s. Find the net work done on the car.

$W = \frac{1}{2}m(v^2 - u^2) = \frac{1}{2}(1500)(100 - 900) = \frac{1}{2}(1500)(-800) = \mathbf{-600{,}000 \text{ J}}$ [3]
Negative → energy removed (by braking)
Calculate [3] Hard

A 3.0 kg block slides 5.0 m down a frictionless 40° incline from rest. Use the W-E theorem to find its speed at the bottom.

Work by gravity: $W = mgs\sin40° = 3(9.8)(5)(0.643) = 94.5$ J [1]
Normal does zero work (perpendicular). $W_{net} = 94.5$ J [1]
$94.5 = \frac{1}{2}(3)v^2 \implies v = \sqrt{63} = \mathbf{7.94 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [2] Hard

Car A has twice the mass of car B but both have the same KE. Which has greater speed? By what factor?

$\frac{1}{2}(2m)v_A^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 \implies v_B^2 = 2v_A^2 \implies v_B = \sqrt{2} \cdot v_A$ [1]
Car B (lighter) moves $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41$ times faster [1].
Less mass needs more speed to carry the same energy.

Key Takeaways

  • $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ — scalar, always ≥ 0, depends on $v^2$.
  • Double speed → 4× KE. This is why high-speed impacts are so dangerous.
  • Work-Energy Theorem: $W_{net} = \Delta E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2$.
  • Positive net work → speeds up. Negative → slows down. Zero → constant speed.
  • Braking distance $\propto v^2$. Double the speed = 4× the stopping distance.
  • W-E theorem is a shortcut: find speed from forces + displacement without needing $a$ or $t$.
  • Energy lost to friction = $|W_{friction}|$ = converted to heat.
