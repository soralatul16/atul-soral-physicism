C.1.4

Damped Oscillations

When real oscillators lose energy

No real oscillation lasts forever. Friction, air resistance and other resistive forces continuously drain energy, shrinking the amplitude toward zero. Here we classify the three regimes of damping — light, critical and heavy — and see how each is used, from car suspensions to precision instruments.

What Is Damping?

Damping is the progressive loss of energy from an oscillating system to its surroundings, caused by a resistive (dissipative) force.

  • Cause: a resistive force — friction, air resistance, drag in a fluid — always opposes the velocity.
  • Effect: mechanical energy is converted to internal (thermal) energy, so the total energy of the oscillator falls.
  • Consequence: the amplitude decreases with each cycle; since $E_T \propto x_0^2$, energy falls even faster than amplitude.
Key subtlety: for light damping, the period (and frequency) stays almost unchanged — only the amplitude shrinks. The oscillator still swings at essentially its natural frequency; it just does so with ever-smaller swings.
Everyday examples: a plucked guitar string fading, a child's swing slowing after you stop pushing, a pendulum in air gradually coming to rest, the needle of an analogue meter settling.

Light (Underdamping) & the Decay Envelope

In light damping the system oscillates many times, with amplitude decaying exponentially — the peaks trace a smooth envelope:

Amplitude falls exponentially; period stays roughly constant.

The envelope follows $x_0(t) = x_0 e^{-\gamma t}$, where $\gamma$ is a damping constant. Equal fractions of amplitude are lost in equal times — a hallmark of exponential decay.

The Degrees of Damping

Light (under-)damping

Several oscillations occur, amplitude decays exponentially. Period ≈ natural period. E.g. a pendulum in air.

Critical damping

Returns to equilibrium in the shortest time without oscillating. No overshoot. E.g. car suspension, analogue meters, door closers.

Heavy (over-)damping

Returns to equilibrium slowly, without oscillating — even slower than critical. E.g. a mass in thick oil, heavy hydraulic dampers.

The comparison

Critical is the boundary case: any less and it overshoots and oscillates; any more and it crawls back. Fastest non-oscillating return.

Energy Loss & the Quality Factor

Since energy depends on the square of amplitude, an exponential amplitude decay means an even steeper energy decay: $E \propto x_0^2 \Rightarrow E(t) = E_0 e^{-2\gamma t}$.

Quality factor (Q)

$$Q = 2\pi \times \frac{\text{energy stored}}{\text{energy lost per cycle}}$$

A dimensionless measure of how lightly damped a system is.

  • High Q (≫1): very light damping — many oscillations before dying out (a struck tuning fork, Q ~ 1000).
  • Low Q (~1 or less): heavy damping — dies within a cycle or two.
  • Q ≈ ½ corresponds to critical damping — the fastest non-oscillatory return.
Design trade-off: you want high Q for a clock pendulum or a musical instrument (it should keep ringing), but low Q (near critical) for a car suspension or a measuring instrument (it should settle fast without wobbling).

Worked Examples

Example 1 — amplitude fraction

A lightly damped oscillator loses 10% of its amplitude each cycle. What fraction of its original amplitude remains after 3 cycles?

Each cycle keeps 90% = 0.90 of amplitude. After 3 cycles: $0.90^3$.

$= 0.729 \approx \mathbf{73\%}$ of the original amplitude.

Example 2 — energy from amplitude

If the amplitude in Example 1 falls to 73% of the original, what fraction of the original energy remains?

$E \propto x_0^2$, so fraction $= (0.73)^2$.

$= 0.53 \approx \mathbf{53\%}$ of the original energy.

Example 3 — choosing damping

A car goes over a bump. Which type of damping should the shock absorbers provide, and why?

Critical damping — it brings the car body back to equilibrium in the shortest time without oscillating up and down, giving a smooth, stable ride.

Define [2]

(a) Define damping and state the effect of light damping on the amplitude and period of an oscillation.

Damping is the loss of energy from an oscillating system to its surroundings due to a resistive force opposing the motion [1]. Under light damping the amplitude decreases exponentially with time, while the period remains approximately constant (unchanged) [1].
Distinguish [3]

(b) Distinguish between critical damping and heavy (over-)damping.

Critical damping returns the system to equilibrium in the shortest possible time without any oscillation (no overshoot) [1]. Heavy damping also returns the system to equilibrium without oscillating, but does so more slowly than critical damping [1]. Both differ from light damping, which oscillates several times before stopping [1].
Calculate [2]

(c) An oscillator loses 20% of its amplitude per cycle. Calculate the percentage of its original energy remaining after one cycle.

After one cycle amplitude = 80% = 0.80 of original. Since $E \propto x_0^2$, energy fraction $= (0.80)^2 = 0.64$ [1], so $\mathbf{64\%}$ of the original energy remains [1].
Apply [2]

(d) The needle of an analogue ammeter is designed to be critically damped. Explain why this is the best choice.

Critical damping lets the needle reach the correct reading in the shortest time without overshooting or oscillating about the value [1]. This allows the user to take an accurate reading quickly, whereas light damping would make the needle wobble and heavy damping would make it respond too slowly [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Damping: energy loss to surroundings via a resistive force opposing velocity.
  • Light damping: amplitude decays exponentially; period nearly unchanged.
  • Critical damping: fastest return to equilibrium with no oscillation (car suspension, meters).
  • Heavy damping: slow return, no oscillation (thick oil, heavy dampers).
  • Energy: $E \propto x_0^2$, so energy falls faster than amplitude ($E = E_0 e^{-2\gamma t}$).
  • Q factor: high Q = lightly damped (rings long); low Q ≈ critical (settles fast).
