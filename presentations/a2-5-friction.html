Friction

Static & Kinetic

Friction is the force that resists sliding. Without it, you couldn't walk, cars couldn't brake, and screws wouldn't hold. It's both the enemy of efficiency and the hero of everyday life.

Atul Soral Physicism

A.2 Forces & Momentum  |  A.2.5 FrictionDefinition

What is Friction?

Friction is a contact force that opposes the relative motion (or tendency of motion) between two surfaces in contact.

  • Always parallel to the contact surface
  • Always opposes the direction of motion (or intended motion)
  • Caused by microscopic interlocking of surface irregularities + intermolecular forces
  • Depends on surface roughness and normal force — NOT on contact area
Friction does NOT depend on contact area. A brick on its side or on its end has the same friction (same $\mu$ and $N$). This is counterintuitive but experimentally verified.

Two Types of Friction

Static Friction ($f_s$)

Acts when the object is not yet moving. Prevents motion from starting.

$$f_s \leq \mu_s N$$
  • Variable: matches the applied force up to a maximum
  • Push gently → $f_s$ = small. Push harder → $f_s$ increases
  • Maximum: $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$ — the "break-free" point
  • Beyond this, the object starts to slide

Kinetic Friction ($f_k$)

Acts when the object is already sliding.

$$f_k = \mu_k N$$
  • Constant: doesn't change with speed (approximately)
  • Always less than max static: $\mu_k < \mu_s$
  • This is why it's harder to start pushing a heavy box than to keep it moving
  • Direction: always opposite to the velocity of sliding
$\mu$ (mu) = coefficient of friction. Dimensionless (no units). Depends on the two materials in contact. Rubber on concrete: $\mu_s \approx 0.8$. Ice on ice: $\mu_k \approx 0.03$.

The Friction Formulas

Maximum Static Friction

$$f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$$

$\mu_s$ = coefficient of static friction
$N$ = normal force (NOT weight — equal to weight only on flat ground)

Kinetic Friction

$$f_k = \mu_k N$$

$\mu_k$ = coefficient of kinetic friction
Always: $\mu_k < \mu_s$ for the same surface pair

$N$ is NOT always $mg$.
• Flat surface: $N = mg$ ✓
• Incline: $N = mg\cos\theta$ (less than $mg$)
• Extra downward push: $N = mg + F_{push}$ (more than $mg$)
• Upward pull: $N = mg - F_{pull}$ (less than $mg$)
Always find $N$ from vertical equilibrium first, then use it in $f = \mu N$.
Typical $\mu$ values: Rubber on concrete: $\mu_s \approx 0.8$, $\mu_k \approx 0.6$. Wood on wood: $\mu_s \approx 0.5$, $\mu_k \approx 0.3$. Steel on ice: $\mu_k \approx 0.03$. Teflon: $\mu_k \approx 0.04$.

Worked Example 1 — Flat Surface 4/10

A 10 kg box on a horizontal floor has $\mu_s = 0.4$ and $\mu_k = 0.3$. (a) What is the minimum force to start it sliding? (b) What force keeps it sliding at constant speed? (c) If pushed with 40 N, what is the acceleration?

(a) Minimum force to start sliding:

→$N = mg = 10 \times 9.8 = 98$ N (flat surface)
$$F_{min} = f_{s,max} = \mu_s N = 0.4 \times 98 = \mathbf{39.2 \text{ N}}$$

(b) Force for constant speed (kinetic friction, $a = 0$):

$$F = f_k = \mu_k N = 0.3 \times 98 = \mathbf{29.4 \text{ N}}$$

(c) Pushed with 40 N (already sliding, so kinetic):

→$F_{net} = F - f_k = 40 - 29.4 = 10.6$ N
$$a = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \frac{10.6}{10} = \mathbf{1.06 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$$

Worked Example 2 — Friction on an Incline 7/10

A 5.0 kg block sits on a 35° incline. $\mu_s = 0.5$, $\mu_k = 0.35$. (a) Does the block slide? (b) If yes, find the acceleration.

(a) Will it slide? Compare driving force with max static friction:

1Force down slope: $mg\sin35° = 5(9.8)(0.574) = 28.1$ N
2Normal: $N = mg\cos35° = 5(9.8)(0.819) = 40.1$ N
3Max static friction: $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N = 0.5 \times 40.1 = 20.1$ N
$mg\sin35° = 28.1$ N $>$ $f_{s,max} = 20.1$ N → YES, it slides! The component down the slope exceeds the maximum friction can provide.

(b) Acceleration (use kinetic friction now):

4$f_k = \mu_k N = 0.35 \times 40.1 = 14.0$ N
5$F_{net} = mg\sin35° - f_k = 28.1 - 14.0 = 14.1$ N
$$a = \frac{14.1}{5.0} = \mathbf{2.82 \text{ m s}^{-2}} \text{ down the slope}$$

The Angle of Repose

The angle of repose is the steepest angle at which an object can rest on a surface without sliding. At this angle, $mg\sin\theta = \mu_s mg\cos\theta$.

$$\tan\theta_{max} = \mu_s \implies \theta_{max} = \tan^{-1}(\mu_s)$$
  • Below this angle: block stays (static friction is enough)
  • At this angle: block is on the verge of sliding
  • Above this angle: block slides (friction can't hold)
Derivation: At the critical angle, $f_s = f_{s,max}$:
$mg\sin\theta = \mu_s mg\cos\theta$ → $\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = \mu_s$ → $\tan\theta = \mu_s$
Mass cancels — the angle doesn't depend on mass!
Practical use: To measure $\mu_s$, slowly tilt a surface until the object starts sliding. Measure the angle. $\mu_s = \tan\theta$. This is used in engineering to test materials.
Example: If $\mu_s = 0.5$, then $\theta_{max} = \tan^{-1}(0.5) = 26.6°$. Any slope steeper than this and the object slides.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A 8.0 kg box is pulled along a horizontal floor by a rope at 25° above the horizontal with a force of 50 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction is $\mu_k = 0.30$.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the vertical and horizontal components of the pulling force.

$F_x = 50\cos25° = \mathbf{45.3 \text{ N}}$ [1]
$F_y = 50\sin25° = \mathbf{21.1 \text{ N}}$ (upward) [1]
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the normal force on the box.

Vertical: $N + F_y - mg = 0$
$N = mg - F_y = 8(9.8) - 21.1 = 78.4 - 21.1 = \mathbf{57.3 \text{ N}}$ [2]
N < mg because the rope pulls upward, partially lifting the box.
Calculate [2]

(c) Calculate the kinetic friction force.

$f_k = \mu_k N = 0.30 \times 57.3 = \mathbf{17.2 \text{ N}}$ [2]
Calculate [2]

(d) Calculate the acceleration of the box.

$F_{net,x} = F_x - f_k = 45.3 - 17.2 = 28.1$ N [1]
$a = F_{net}/m = 28.1/8.0 = \mathbf{3.51 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(e) If the rope angle increased to 45°, would the friction force increase or decrease? Explain.

Friction would decrease [1]. A steeper angle means a larger upward component $F_y$, which reduces $N$ ($N = mg - F_y$). Since $f_k = \mu_k N$, less $N$ means less friction [1].

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A 15 kg crate on a flat floor has $\mu_s = 0.45$. What horizontal force is needed to start it moving?

$F = \mu_s mg = 0.45 \times 15 \times 9.8 = \mathbf{66.2 \text{ N}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A block slides down a 20° incline at constant speed. Find $\mu_k$.

Constant speed → $a = 0$ → $f_k = mg\sin20°$ [1]
$\mu_k N = mg\sin20°$ and $N = mg\cos20°$ [1]
$\mu_k = \frac{\sin20°}{\cos20°} = \tan20° = \mathbf{0.364}$ [1]
(At constant speed on an incline, $\mu_k = \tan\theta$!)
Calculate [3] Hard

A 6.0 kg block is pushed against a vertical wall with a horizontal force of 80 N. The coefficient of static friction is 0.4. Does the block slide down?

Normal force (from wall) $N = 80$ N [1]
$f_{s,max} = \mu_s N = 0.4 \times 80 = 32$ N [1]
Weight = $6 \times 9.8 = 58.8$ N downward
$f_{s,max} (32) < W (58.8)$ → friction can't support weight → YES, it slides down [1]
Determine [3] Hard

Find the minimum horizontal force to keep a 4.0 kg block stationary against a vertical wall if $\mu_s = 0.6$.

Need $f_s = W$: $\mu_s N = mg$ [1]
$0.6 \times F = 4.0 \times 9.8$ (here $N = F$ since push is horizontal against wall) [1]
$F = 39.2 / 0.6 = \mathbf{65.3 \text{ N}}$ [1]

Key Takeaways

  • Static friction prevents motion: $f_s \leq \mu_s N$. Variable, up to a maximum.
  • Kinetic friction opposes sliding: $f_k = \mu_k N$. Constant. Always $\mu_k < \mu_s$.
  • $N$ is NOT always $mg$. Find $N$ from vertical equilibrium first.
  • Friction is independent of contact area and approximately independent of speed.
  • Angle of repose: $\theta_{max} = \tan^{-1}(\mu_s)$. At constant speed on incline: $\mu_k = \tan\theta$.
  • Angled pull: upward component reduces $N$, which reduces friction. Pulling at an angle can be easier than pushing horizontally.
  • The graph: static rises linearly, peaks at $\mu_s N$, drops to constant $\mu_k N$.
