CO₂ was 280 ppm for ten thousand years. In two centuries we've pushed it to 424 ppm — a 50% increase. The physics is clear: more GHGs trap more IR, the energy balance shifts, and the planet warms. The question isn't whether — it's how much and how fast.

How Human Activity Enhances the Greenhouse Effect

The enhanced greenhouse effect follows the same physics as the natural one — but with artificially increased GHG concentrations that shift the energy balance.

THE PHYSICS CHAIN (each step causes the next)

  1. Burning fossil fuels + deforestation → CO₂ concentration rises from 280 → 424 ppm
  2. Agriculture + fossil fuel extraction → CH₄ rises from 700 → 1900 ppb
  3. More GHG molecules → atmosphere absorbs a greater fraction of outgoing IR
  4. More IR re-emitted back to surface → surface receives more energy than it radiates away
  5. Energy imbalance: $P_{\text{in}} > P_{\text{out}}$ → Earth accumulates energy → temperature rises
  6. Warming continues until a new equilibrium is reached at a higher temperature where $P_{\text{out}}$ catches up (since $P_{\text{out}} \propto T^4$)
Current energy imbalance: Earth is absorbing ~1.0 W m⁻² more than it's radiating. This doesn't sound like much, but multiplied by Earth's surface area ($5.1 \times 10^{14}$ m²) it's $5.1 \times 10^{14}$ W — equivalent to 400,000 Hiroshima bombs per day. ~93% of this excess goes into the oceans.

Radiative Forcing — Quantifying the Imbalance

Radiative forcing (RF) is the change in net energy flux at the top of the atmosphere caused by a change in an external driver (like CO₂ concentration). Measured in W m⁻².

DriverRF (W m⁻²)Direction
CO₂ (280→424 ppm)+2.16Warming — the largest single forcing
CH₄ (700→1900 ppb)+0.54Warming
N₂O+0.21Warming
Halocarbons (CFCs, HFCs)+0.41Warming
Aerosols (sulphates, soot)−1.3 (uncertain)Cooling — reflects sunlight
Total anthropogenic+2.7 ± 1.0Net warming
CO₂ forcing formula: $\Delta F = 5.35 \ln\left(\frac{C}{C_0}\right)$ W m⁻². For doubling CO₂ (280→560): $\Delta F = 5.35 \ln(2) = 3.7$ W m⁻². This produces ~3°C warming (climate sensitivity). The logarithmic relationship means each doubling adds the same forcing — and the same temperature increase.
Aerosols partially mask warming. Without aerosol cooling (~−1.3 W m⁻²), the warming would already be ~50% greater. If we clean up air pollution (which we should, for health), the masking effect disappears and warming accelerates. This is a cruel paradox.

Climate Sensitivity — How Much Warming Per Doubling?

EQUILIBRIUM CLIMATE SENSITIVITY (ECS)

The eventual global mean temperature increase from doubling CO₂ (280 → 560 ppm), after all feedbacks have played out.

Best estimate: 3.0°C  (likely range: 2.5–4.0°C)

This number combines the direct radiative forcing (3.7 W m⁻² per doubling) with all feedback amplifications:

Positive feedbacks (amplify warming)

  • Water vapour: warming → more evaporation → more H₂O (a GHG) → more warming. Roughly doubles the CO₂-only warming.
  • Ice-albedo: warming → ice melts → darker surface → absorbs more solar → more warming.
  • Permafrost: warming → permafrost thaws → releases stored CH₄ and CO₂ → more warming.

Negative feedbacks (dampen warming)

  • Planck response: warmer surface radiates more ($T^4$) → increases outgoing IR → self-limiting.
  • Lapse rate: tropical upper atmosphere warms faster → radiates more efficiently → slight cooling effect.
  • Cloud changes: more low clouds → higher albedo → cooling (but high clouds warm — net effect uncertain).
Without feedbacks (Planck response only), doubling CO₂ would warm ~1.1°C. Feedbacks amplify this to ~3°C. The amplification factor is ~2.7. Water vapour feedback alone roughly doubles the warming.

Where the Extra CO₂ Comes From

Human sources (~40 Gt CO₂/year)

  • Energy/electricity (73%): coal, oil, gas power stations
  • Industry (5%): cement production (CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂), steel
  • Transport (16%): cars, ships, planes burn fossil fuels
  • Deforestation (6%): removes carbon sinks + releases stored C

Natural sinks (absorb ~50%)

  • Oceans (~25%): CO₂ dissolves in seawater → carbonic acid
  • Land biosphere (~25%): photosynthesis absorbs CO₂
  • Remaining ~50% stays in atmosphere → concentration rises

Sinks are weakening as oceans warm (hold less CO₂) and forests are destroyed.

The carbon cycle imbalance

Before industrialisation, the carbon cycle was balanced: natural emissions ≈ natural absorption (~750 Gt CO₂/year each way). Humans add ~40 Gt/year on top. Nature absorbs ~20 Gt of that. The other ~20 Gt accumulates → CO₂ rises by ~2.5 ppm/year.

At current rates, CO₂ will reach 560 ppm (double pre-industrial) by ~2060.

How we know it's human-caused: (1) CO₂ rise correlates with fossil fuel burning. (2) Isotope ratios (¹³C/¹²C) match fossil carbon, not volcanic. (3) O₂ is decreasing (combustion consumes O₂). (4) CO₂ rises faster than any natural process in the geological record.

How Much Has the Planet Warmed?

  • +1.2°C above pre-industrial (1850–1900 average) as of 2024
  • Rate: ~0.2°C per decade and accelerating
  • 2023 and 2024 were the hottest years in recorded history (and likely in 100,000+ years)

Tipping Points — When Feedbacks Become Irreversible

A tipping point is a threshold beyond which a change becomes self-reinforcing and irreversible on human timescales, even if emissions stop.

Arctic sea ice collapse (~1.5°C)

Ice-free summers → ice-albedo feedback accelerates. Arctic ocean absorbs heat year-round. May be crossed within a decade.

Greenland ice sheet (~1.5–2°C)

Irreversible melting. Contains enough ice to raise sea level by 7.4 m. Would take centuries but once started, cannot be stopped.

Permafrost thaw (~2°C)

Contains ~1,500 Gt of carbon (twice current atmospheric CO₂). Thawing releases CO₂ and CH₄, driving further warming independent of human emissions.

Amazon dieback (~3–4°C)

Rainforest dries, burns, converts to savanna. Releases stored carbon. Reduces rainfall. A carbon sink becomes a carbon source.

Cascade risk: Tipping points can trigger each other. Arctic ice loss → Greenland melting → ocean circulation weakens → Amazon dries → permafrost thaws → more warming → more tipping points. This is why climate scientists stress the urgency of staying below 1.5–2°C.

Calculating Temperature Change from Forcing

The relationship between radiative forcing and temperature change (without feedbacks):

$$\Delta T_0 = \frac{\Delta F}{\lambda_0}$$

where $\lambda_0 \approx 3.3$ W m⁻² K⁻¹ (Planck feedback parameter) → $\Delta T_0 \approx \frac{\Delta F}{3.3}$

Example: Warming from doubling CO₂

$\Delta F = 5.35 \ln(2) = 3.7$ W m⁻²

Without feedbacks: $\Delta T_0 = \frac{3.7}{3.3} = 1.1°C$

With feedbacks: Feedback factor $f \approx 2.7$, so $\Delta T = f \times \Delta T_0 = 2.7 \times 1.1 = 3.0°C$

This is the equilibrium climate sensitivity: ~3°C per doubling of CO₂.

Example: Current warming from all GHGs

Total anthropogenic forcing ≈ 2.7 W m⁻² (net, including aerosol cooling)

Expected equilibrium warming: $\Delta T = \frac{2.7}{3.3} \times 2.7 = 2.2°C$

We've only realised 1.2°C so far — meaning ~1°C of additional warming is "in the pipeline" even if all emissions stopped today. The oceans are still absorbing and will release this heat over decades.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Outline [3]

(a) Outline how the burning of fossil fuels leads to an increase in global average temperature.

Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ into the atmosphere, increasing its concentration beyond natural levels [1]. The additional CO₂ molecules absorb more outgoing infrared radiation that would otherwise escape to space [1]. This absorbed IR is re-emitted in all directions including back toward the surface, creating a positive energy imbalance ($P_{\text{in}} > P_{\text{out}}$) that raises the surface temperature until a new equilibrium is reached [1].
Explain [3]

(b) Explain the water vapour feedback and why it is classified as a feedback rather than a forcing.

As temperature increases (from CO₂ forcing), evaporation increases, adding more water vapour to the atmosphere [1]. Water vapour is itself a greenhouse gas, so more H₂O absorbs more IR radiation, causing additional warming beyond what CO₂ alone would produce [1]. It is classified as a feedback (not a forcing) because human activities don't directly control H₂O concentration — it responds to temperature. If CO₂ decreased, temperature would drop, H₂O would condense out, and the extra warming would disappear [1].
Calculate [2]

(c) The radiative forcing from CO₂ is given by $\Delta F = 5.35 \ln(C/C_0)$. Calculate the forcing when CO₂ reaches 560 ppm (from a pre-industrial baseline of 280 ppm).

$\Delta F = 5.35 \ln\left(\frac{560}{280}\right) = 5.35 \ln(2)$ [1]
$\Delta F = 5.35 \times 0.693 = \mathbf{3.71 \text{ W m}^{-2}}$ [1]
Explain [3]

(d) Explain what is meant by a "tipping point" in the context of climate change and give one example.

A tipping point is a critical threshold beyond which a change in the climate system becomes self-reinforcing and effectively irreversible on human timescales, even if the original forcing is removed [1]. Example: Greenland ice sheet collapse — once warming exceeds ~1.5–2°C, the ice sheet enters an irreversible decline because the exposed darker land surface absorbs more solar radiation (ice-albedo feedback), lowering the ice sheet's surface to warmer altitudes, accelerating melting further [1]. The full collapse would raise global sea levels by ~7.4 metres over centuries [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

Current CO₂ is 424 ppm. Using $\Delta F = 5.35 \ln(C/280)$, calculate the current radiative forcing from CO₂ alone. If climate sensitivity is 3°C per 3.7 W m⁻², estimate the equilibrium warming from CO₂ alone.

$\Delta F = 5.35 \ln(424/280) = 5.35 \ln(1.514) = 5.35 \times 0.415 = 2.22$ W m⁻² [1]
Climate sensitivity: 3°C per 3.7 W m⁻² → sensitivity parameter = 3/3.7 = 0.81 °C/(W m⁻²) [1]
$\Delta T = 0.81 \times 2.22 = \mathbf{1.8°C}$ equilibrium warming from CO₂ alone. We've realised ~1.2°C so far — the rest is "committed warming" [1].
Discuss [4]

Explain why the observed warming of 1.2°C is less than the equilibrium warming predicted by current GHG concentrations. Discuss the implications.

The oceans have a very high heat capacity (high specific heat, enormous mass) and absorb ~93% of excess energy, delaying atmospheric warming [1]. The climate system has thermal inertia — it takes decades to centuries to reach full equilibrium after a forcing change [1]. This means ~1°C of additional warming is "locked in" even if all emissions stopped immediately — the system will continue warming as the oceans slowly release stored heat [1]. Implication: even with immediate zero emissions, further warming, sea level rise, and ice loss are unavoidable — they are consequences of past emissions already in the atmosphere [1].
Explain [2]

Explain how scientists know that the increase in atmospheric CO₂ is caused by human activity rather than natural processes.

Fossil fuels contain carbon that has been underground for millions of years and is depleted in the isotope ¹³C (and has no ¹⁴C). As atmospheric CO₂ rises, the ratio of ¹³C/¹²C decreases — matching the isotopic signature of burned fossil fuels, not volcanic or oceanic sources [1]. Additionally, atmospheric O₂ is decreasing in proportion to CO₂ increase, consistent with combustion (C + O₂ → CO₂ consumes oxygen). Natural processes like volcanism release only ~1% of human emissions annually [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Same physics, more GHGs: Fossil fuel burning raises CO₂ (280→424 ppm) → more IR absorbed → $P_{\text{in}} > P_{\text{out}}$ → warming.
  • Energy imbalance: Earth absorbs ~1.0 W m⁻² more than it radiates. ~93% goes into oceans.
  • Radiative forcing: CO₂ contributes +2.16 W m⁻². Total anthropogenic = +2.7 W m⁻² (net).
  • CO₂ forcing is logarithmic: $\Delta F = 5.35 \ln(C/C_0)$. Each doubling adds 3.7 W m⁻² → ~3°C warming.
  • Feedbacks amplify: Water vapour (~2×), ice-albedo, permafrost. Without feedbacks = 1.1°C. With = 3.0°C per doubling.
  • Current warming: +1.2°C. Pipeline warming: ~+1°C more even with zero emissions.
  • Tipping points: Arctic ice, Greenland, permafrost, Amazon. May cascade. Critical thresholds near 1.5–2°C.
  • Attribution: Isotope ratios (¹³C), O₂ decline, correlation with fossil fuel use confirm human cause.
