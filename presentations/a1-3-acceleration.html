A.1.3

Acceleration

Uniform & Non-Uniform

Acceleration isn't just "speeding up." It's any change in velocity — getting faster, getting slower, or changing direction.

Atul Soral Physicism

A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.3 AccelerationDefinition

What is Acceleration?

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time.

$$\vec{a} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - u}{t}$$

Where $u$ = initial velocity, $v$ = final velocity, $t$ = time taken.

  • Vector quantity — has magnitude AND direction
  • SI unit: m s$^{-2}$ (metres per second per second)
  • An object accelerates when its velocity changes — in magnitude, direction, or both
FYI: "Metres per second per second" means: each second, the velocity changes by this many m/s. If $a = 3$ m s$^{-2}$, the object gains 3 m/s of speed every second.

$a = 3$ m s$^{-2}$ — velocity grows by 3 m/s every second

A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.3 AccelerationKey Concept

Three Ways an Object Can Accelerate

Acceleration = change in velocity. Velocity can change in three ways:

🏎

Speeding Up

Velocity magnitude increases

a is in the SAME direction as v

🛑

Slowing Down

Velocity magnitude decreases

a is OPPOSITE to v

🔄

Changing Direction

Speed same, direction changes

a is perpendicular to v

Critical point: "Deceleration" is NOT a separate quantity. It's just acceleration in the opposite direction to motion. In IB, always use "acceleration" and specify the direction.
FYI: A car going around a roundabout at constant 40 km/h IS accelerating — because the direction of velocity keeps changing. This is centripetal acceleration.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.3 AccelerationSign Convention

Positive vs Negative Acceleration

The sign of acceleration tells you its direction, not whether the object is speeding up or slowing down.

Rule: If $v$ and $a$ have the same sign → object speeds up. If opposite signs → object slows down. The sign alone doesn't tell you "speeding up" or "slowing down."
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.3 AccelerationUniform

Uniform (Constant) Acceleration

Uniform acceleration means the velocity changes by equal amounts in equal time intervals.

  • Acceleration value stays constant: $a = \text{constant}$
  • v-t graph: straight line (constant gradient)
  • s-t graph: parabola (curved, getting steeper)
  • a-t graph: horizontal line

The most important example:

Free fall near Earth's surface: $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$ downward. This is constant (ignoring air resistance) regardless of mass. Galileo proved this.
IB key point: The SUVAT equations (next subtopic) ONLY work for uniform acceleration. If acceleration varies, you need calculus or graphical methods.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.3 AccelerationNon-Uniform

Non-Uniform Acceleration

Non-uniform acceleration means the acceleration itself changes over time. The velocity doesn't change at a constant rate.

Real-world examples:

  • A car accelerating from traffic lights (driver presses pedal gradually)
  • A skydiver before terminal velocity (air resistance increases with speed)
  • A ball bouncing — acceleration changes at each bounce
  • A rocket — as fuel burns, mass decreases, so acceleration increases

On graphs:

  • v-t graph: curved line (changing gradient)
  • a-t graph: not horizontal (changes with time)
At SL: Most problems involve uniform acceleration. Non-uniform cases are handled graphically (read the gradient of the v-t graph at specific points).
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.3 AccelerationThe Big Misconception

The Ball Thrown Upward

This is the single most tested misconception in IB Physics.

THE MISCONCEPTION: "At the top, the ball stops, so acceleration must be zero."

THE TRUTH: At the top, velocity = 0 for an instant, but acceleration = 9.8 m s$^{-2}$ downward the ENTIRE time. Gravity doesn't pause. The ball stops only because it's been decelerating — and it immediately starts accelerating downward.
Gold arrows ($g$) in the diagram are the SAME length at every position — acceleration due to gravity is constant throughout the flight.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.3 AccelerationGraphs

Reading Acceleration from Graphs

On a v-t graph:

$$a = \text{gradient of v-t graph}$$
  • Positive gradient → positive acceleration
  • Negative gradient → negative acceleration
  • Zero gradient (horizontal) → zero acceleration (constant v)
  • Steeper line → greater acceleration

On a s-t graph:

  • Straight line → constant velocity → $a = 0$
  • Curve getting steeper → positive acceleration
  • Curve getting flatter → negative acceleration

Worked Example

Problem: A car is travelling at 20 m s$^{-1}$. The driver brakes and the car comes to rest in 8.0 seconds. Calculate the acceleration. Is the car uniformly accelerating?
1Identify: $u = 20$ m s$^{-1}$, $v = 0$ m s$^{-1}$, $t = 8.0$ s
2Apply formula: $a = \frac{v - u}{t}$
$$a = \frac{0 - 20}{8.0} = \frac{-20}{8.0} = \mathbf{-2.5 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$$
3Interpret: The negative sign means acceleration is opposite to motion → the car is slowing down.

If we assume constant braking force, yes — the acceleration is uniform at −2.5 m s$^{-2}$.

Follow-up: In reality, braking force isn't perfectly constant — the acceleration would be slightly non-uniform. But for IB calculations, we usually assume uniform braking.

Practice Questions

Click to reveal answers.

Calculate

A rocket accelerates from rest to 150 m s$^{-1}$ in 6.0 seconds. Calculate the acceleration.

$a = \frac{v-u}{t} = \frac{150-0}{6.0} = \mathbf{25 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$
Determine

A ball is thrown vertically upward at 15 m s$^{-1}$. What is its velocity after 2.0 seconds? (Take $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$)

$v = u + at = 15 + (-9.8)(2.0) = 15 - 19.6 = \mathbf{-4.6 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$
Negative = the ball has passed the top and is now falling back down.
Explain

A car moves at constant speed around a circular track. Is it accelerating? Explain using the definition of acceleration.

Yes. Acceleration = rate of change of velocity. Speed is constant but direction changes continuously. Since velocity = speed + direction, and direction changes, velocity changes, so the car IS accelerating (centripetal acceleration toward the center).
State and Explain

At what point during a ball's upward flight is the acceleration greatest?

The acceleration is the same at ALL points — it's $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$ downward throughout. Many students think it's greatest at the bottom or zero at the top. It's constant because gravity is constant near Earth's surface.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.3 AccelerationSummary

Key Takeaways

  • Acceleration = rate of change of velocity. Vector. Unit: m s$^{-2}$.
  • Three ways to accelerate: speed up, slow down, change direction.
  • Same signs ($v$ and $a$) → speeding up. Opposite signs → slowing down.
  • Uniform: constant $a$, straight v-t line. Non-uniform: changing $a$, curved v-t line.
  • At the top of a throw: $v = 0$ but $a = g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$ downward. Always.
  • Gradient of v-t graph = acceleration.
