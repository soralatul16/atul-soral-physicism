Forces & Free Body Diagrams

A force is a push or pull. A free body diagram strips away everything except the forces — and it's the single most important tool for solving any mechanics problem in IB Physics.

What is a Force?

A force is an interaction that can change an object's velocity — speed it up, slow it down, or change its direction.

$$\text{Force is a vector: magnitude + direction}$$
  • SI unit: Newton (N). 1 N = 1 kg m s$^{-2}$
  • A force needs an agent — something must exert it (Earth, a rope, a surface)
  • Forces come in two types: contact (touching) and non-contact (at a distance)

Contact Forces

  • Normal force — surface pushes perpendicular
  • Friction — opposes sliding
  • Tension — rope/string pulls
  • Applied force — direct push/pull
  • Drag / Air resistance — fluid opposes motion
  • Spring force — Hooke's law

Non-Contact Forces

  • Gravitational — mass attracts mass
  • Electromagnetic — charges attract/repel
  • Nuclear strong — holds nuclei together
  • Nuclear weak — radioactive decay
IB note: At SL, you mainly deal with gravity, normal, friction, tension, and applied forces.

The Forces You Must Know

Weight (W = mg): Always down, toward Earth's centre. Acts from the centre of mass.
Normal (N): Perpendicular to the contact surface. Pushes away from surface. NOT always equal to W.
Friction (f): Parallel to surface, opposes the motion or tendency of motion.
Applied (F): Any external push/pull you apply to the object.
Common error: "Normal force = weight." This is only true on a flat horizontal surface with no vertical acceleration. On an incline or in an elevator, $N \neq mg$.

Weight: The Force of Gravity

Weight is the gravitational force exerted on an object by a planet (usually Earth).

$$W = mg$$
  • $m$ = mass (kg) — a property of the object, doesn't change with location
  • $g$ = gravitational field strength = $9.8$ N kg$^{-1}$ (near Earth's surface)
  • $W$ = weight (N) — a force, changes with location (Moon: $g = 1.6$)
  • Direction: always toward the centre of the Earth (downward)
  • Acts from the centre of mass of the object
Mass ≠ Weight. Mass is scalar (kg). Weight is a vector force (N). A 70 kg person weighs 686 N on Earth but only 113 N on the Moon. Their mass is still 70 kg everywhere.
IB notation: $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$ (acceleration) = $9.8$ N kg$^{-1}$ (field strength). Same number, same symbol, two different interpretations. In dynamics, use N kg$^{-1}$.

What is a Free Body Diagram (FBD)?

A free body diagram shows a single object, isolated from everything else, with ALL forces acting on it drawn as arrows from the object.

The rules:
1. Draw the object as a simple dot or box
2. Draw ONLY forces acting ON the object (not forces the object exerts on others)
3. Each force arrow starts from the object
4. Arrow length = relative magnitude
5. Label every force with its name or symbol

What NOT to include:

  • Velocity arrows (velocity is NOT a force)
  • Acceleration arrows
  • Forces the object exerts on OTHER things
  • The surface, rope, or other objects (just the forces they exert)

What TO include:

  • Every force acting ON this object
  • Correct direction for each force
  • Correct relative lengths (bigger force = longer arrow)
  • Clear labels: $W$, $N$, $F$, $f$, $T$, etc.
IB marking: FBDs are worth 2-3 marks. Common losses: missing a force, wrong direction, including velocity, or including forces the object exerts on something else.

Building a FBD — Step by Step

A book sits on a table. Let's build its FBD one force at a time.

1Draw the object — a dot or simple box. Remove everything else.
2Weight (W = mg) — always present, always downward from centre of mass.
3Ask: what's touching it? Table touches it → Normal force $N$ perpendicular to surface (upward).
4Any other forces? No push, no rope, no friction (it's not sliding). Done.
5Check: Book is at rest → $\sum F = 0$ → $N$ must equal $W$. Arrows should be same length.
The "what's touching it?" question is the key to finding all contact forces. If nothing touches it except the ground, you only have $W$ and $N$.

FBD on an Inclined Plane

This is the most tested FBD in IB. The key: resolve weight into components along and perpendicular to the slope.

Resolving weight on a slope:

$$W_{\parallel} = mg\sin\theta \text{ (along slope, downhill)}$$
$$W_{\perp} = mg\cos\theta \text{ (into slope)}$$
Why sin and cos? Think of it this way: at $\theta = 0$ (flat), no component along the slope ($\sin 0 = 0$) and full weight into surface ($\cos 0 = 1$). At $\theta = 90$ (vertical), full weight along the "slope" and no normal force.

On the slope:

  • $N = mg\cos\theta$ (balances the perpendicular component)
  • $N < mg$ on any slope! (This is why $N \neq mg$ in general)

FBD Gallery — Common Scenarios

The projectile FBD is the most common mistake. Students add a "forward force" or a "force of motion." There is NO forward force on a projectile — only gravity acts. The ball keeps moving forward because of inertia, not a force.

Net Force (Resultant Force)

The net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object. It determines the acceleration.

$$\vec{F}_{net} = \sum \vec{F} = \vec{F}_1 + \vec{F}_2 + \vec{F}_3 + \ldots$$
  • If $F_{net} = 0$ → object is in equilibrium (constant velocity or at rest)
  • If $F_{net} \neq 0$ → object accelerates in the direction of $F_{net}$
  • In 2D: resolve all forces into x and y, then sum each direction separately
$$F_{net,x} = \sum F_x \qquad F_{net,y} = \sum F_y$$
$$F_{net} = \sqrt{F_{net,x}^2 + F_{net,y}^2}$$
This is Newton's Second Law preview: $F_{net} = ma$. The net force determines the acceleration. We'll derive this fully in A.2.3.
Worked example: A 5 kg box is pushed right with 30 N. Friction is 12 N left. Weight = 49 N down. Normal = 49 N up.
$F_{net,x} = 30 - 12 = 18$ N right. $F_{net,y} = 49 - 49 = 0$ N.
$F_{net} = 18$ N right. $a = 18/5 = 3.6$ m s$^{-2}$ right.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A 4.0 kg block rests on a rough surface inclined at 25° to the horizontal. A string attached to the block runs parallel to the slope and over a frictionless pulley at the top. A 2.5 kg mass hangs from the other end of the string. The system is in equilibrium.
Draw [3]

(a) Draw a free body diagram for the 4.0 kg block on the incline, labelling all forces.

Forces on block: W (39.2 N vertically down) [1], N (perpendicular to slope, away from surface) [1], T (tension, up the slope along the string) [1], f (friction, direction depends on tendency of motion). All arrows from the block, correct directions.
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the component of the block's weight parallel to the incline.

$W_{\parallel} = mg\sin\theta = 4.0 \times 9.8 \times \sin25° = 39.2 \times 0.423 = \mathbf{16.6 \text{ N}}$ down the slope [2]
Calculate [2]

(c) The tension in the string equals the weight of the hanging mass. Calculate the tension.

$T = m_2 g = 2.5 \times 9.8 = \mathbf{24.5 \text{ N}}$ [2]
Determine [3]

(d) Determine the magnitude and direction of the friction force on the block.

Along slope: $T - W_{\parallel} - f = 0$ (equilibrium)
$24.5 - 16.6 - f = 0 \implies f = \mathbf{7.9 \text{ N}}$ down the slope [2]
Friction acts DOWN because $T > W_{\parallel}$ — the block would slide UP without friction [1]

Practice Questions

Draw [2] Easy

Draw a FBD for a book at rest on a table.

Two forces: W = mg downward [1] and N upward [1]. Equal length arrows (equilibrium).
Draw & Calculate [3] Medium

A 3.0 kg object is pushed horizontally with 20 N on a surface with 8 N friction. Draw the FBD and calculate the acceleration.

FBD: W (29.4 N down), N (29.4 N up), F (20 N right), f (8 N left) [1]
$F_{net} = 20 - 8 = 12$ N right [1]
$a = F_{net}/m = 12/3.0 = \mathbf{4.0 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$ right [1]
Draw [3] Hard

A ball has just been thrown upward and is still rising. Draw the FBD. How many forces act on it?

Only ONE force: W = mg downward [2]. There is NO upward "throwing force" once the ball leaves the hand [1]. The ball moves upward due to inertia, not an upward force.
Calculate [3] Hard

A 10 kg block sits on a 30° incline. Calculate the normal force and the component of weight along the slope.

$W_{\parallel} = mg\sin30° = 10(9.8)(0.5) = \mathbf{49 \text{ N}}$ [1]
$N = mg\cos30° = 10(9.8)(0.866) = \mathbf{84.9 \text{ N}}$ [1]
Note: $N < mg$ (84.9 < 98) — normal force is less than weight on a slope [1]

Key Takeaways

  • Force = push/pull. Vector. Unit: Newton. Needs an agent.
  • Weight $W = mg$ always acts downward from centre of mass.
  • Normal force $N$ is perpendicular to the contact surface. $N = mg$ only on flat ground.
  • FBD shows ONE object with ALL forces ON it. No velocity. No forces it exerts on others.
  • On an incline: $W_{\parallel} = mg\sin\theta$, $W_{\perp} = mg\cos\theta$, $N = mg\cos\theta$.
  • Net force $= \sum F$. Zero → equilibrium. Non-zero → acceleration.
  • Projectile FBD: ONLY weight acts. No "force of motion." Ever.
