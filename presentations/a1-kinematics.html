A.1 – KinematicsFoundations

Position & Frame of Reference

DEFINITION

Position

The location of an object relative to a chosen origin (reference point) in a coordinate system.

Requires: a reference point, a direction convention, a unit.

KEY CONCEPT

Frame of Reference

The coordinate system from which an observer measures position and motion. All motion is relative to the chosen frame.

IB Exam: "Describe" questions on reference frames require you to state the observer's frame explicitly.

Every physical quantity is either a scalar or a vector.

A.1 – KinematicsCore Concept

Distance vs Displacement

SCALAR

Distance (d)

  • Total path length travelled
  • Always positive or zero
  • SI unit: metre (m)
  • Does NOT depend on direction
  • Cannot decrease during a journey
VECTOR

Displacement (s or $\Delta x$)

  • Straight-line distance from start to end
  • Has direction (e.g. "5 m East")
  • SI unit: metre (m)
  • Can be zero, positive, or negative
  • Depends only on initial and final position
$$|\text{Distance}| \geq |\text{Displacement}| \quad \textbf{ALWAYS}$$
IBO expects: Students must be able to distinguish between distance and displacement in all contexts.

A.1 – KinematicsSpecial Case

Closed Path: Displacement = 0

RESULT

One complete lap:

Distance = 400 m (full circumference)

Displacement = 0 m (back to start)

This applies to:

  • Runner completing a lap
  • Earth completing one orbit
  • Pendulum completing one full swing
  • Any round trip back to origin
IB Trap: "What is the displacement after one complete revolution?" → Always 0.
A.1 – KinematicsCore Concept

Speed vs Velocity

SCALAR

Speed

Rate of change of distance

$$\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}} = \frac{d}{t}$$
  • Always positive
  • Unit: m s$^{-1}$
  • Speedometer reading
VECTOR

Velocity

Rate of change of displacement

$$\vec{v} = \frac{\Delta \vec{s}}{\Delta t}$$
  • Can be positive or negative
  • Unit: m s$^{-1}$ + direction
  • Specifies how fast AND which way
FYI: A car going around a roundabout at constant speed has changing velocity (direction changes). This means it is accelerating!
A.1 – KinematicsDistinction

Average vs Instantaneous

AVERAGE

Over the entire journey

$$v_{avg} = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}$$
$$\vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\text{total displacement}}{\text{total time}}$$

Includes all stops, speed changes, rest periods.

INSTANTANEOUS

At a specific moment

$$v_{inst} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \text{ as } \Delta t \to 0$$

What the speedometer shows at any instant.

On a graph: the gradient of the tangent to the curve at that point.

HL ENRICHMENT In calculus: $v = ds/dt$
Common error: Average speed ≠ average of individual speeds! Always use total distance ÷ total time.
A.1 – KinematicsCore Concept

Acceleration

The rate of change of velocity

$$\vec{a} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - u}{t}$$
VECTOR

Has magnitude
AND direction

m s$^{-2}$

SI unit

Deceleration

a is opposite
to direction of v

COMMON MISTAKE

"At the highest point of a throw, acceleration = 0" WRONG!

Gravity always acts → $a = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$ downward, even when $v = 0$ at the top.

Steeper → faster | Flat → stationary | Negative → returning
Gradient = Acceleration

A.1 – KinematicsWorked Example

Worked Example

PROBLEM

A car travels 150 km in 2 hours, stops for 30 minutes, then travels 90 km in 1 hour. Calculate the average speed for the entire journey.

1Identify total distance: $d = 150 + 90 = 240$ km
2Identify total time: $t = 2 + 0.5 + 1 = 3.5$ hours

⚠ Include the 30 min stop — average speed uses ALL time!

3Apply formula: $v_{avg} = \frac{d}{t} = \frac{240}{3.5} = \mathbf{68.6 \text{ km h}^{-1}}$
FYI: If you averaged the individual speeds (75 and 90), you'd get 82.5 — which is wrong. Always use totals.
A.1 – KinematicsWorked Example

Worked Example — Pythagoras

PROBLEM

A person walks 3 km East, then 4 km North. Find: (a) distance (b) displacement magnitude (c) direction.

aDistance $= 3 + 4 = \mathbf{7 \text{ km}}$
b$s = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{25} = \mathbf{5 \text{ km}}$
c$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) = \mathbf{53.1\degree}$ N of E

Check: distance (7) > displacement (5) ✓

A.1 – KinematicsPractice

Practice Questions

Click each question to reveal the answer

Calculate

A runner completes 2 laps of a 400 m track in 3 minutes. Calculate (a) the average speed and (b) the average velocity.

(a) $v = 800/180 = \mathbf{4.44 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$  |  (b) $\vec{v} = 0/180 = \mathbf{0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ (returns to start, displacement = 0)
Determine

A ball is thrown vertically upward at 20 m s$^{-1}$. State the velocity and acceleration at the highest point.

Velocity = $\mathbf{0}$ m s$^{-1}$ (momentarily at rest) | Acceleration = $\mathbf{9.8}$ m s$^{-2}$ downward (gravity never stops!)
Calculate

A car accelerates uniformly from rest to 25 m s$^{-1}$ in 5.0 s. Calculate the acceleration.

$a = \frac{v-u}{t} = \frac{25-0}{5.0} = \mathbf{5.0 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$
Distinguish

Distinguish between average speed and instantaneous speed. Give an example of each.

Average speed: total distance / total time (e.g. "drove 200 km in 4 h = 50 km/h"). Instantaneous: speed at one moment (e.g. speedometer reads 60 km/h right now).

Theory of Knowledge

The Nature of Measurement & Motion

DISCUSSION

Is all motion truly relative?

  • If there is no "absolute rest," can we ever say an object is truly moving?
  • Newton assumed absolute space. Einstein proved all motion is relative to the observer.
  • What does this mean for objectivity in physics?
THOUGHT EXPERIMENT

"A passenger on a train drops a ball. The passenger says it fell straight down. A person on the platform says it followed a parabolic path."

Who is correct? → Both. Each describes the same event from a valid reference frame.
