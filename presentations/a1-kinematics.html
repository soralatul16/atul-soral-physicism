Kinematics

Motion, Position, Distance, Displacement, Speed, Velocity & Acceleration

IB DP Physics SL + HL 9 Hours

Are you moving right now?

You're sitting "still" — but Earth rotates at 1,670 km/h.
Earth orbits the Sun at 107,000 km/h.
The solar system moves through the galaxy at 828,000 km/h.

So... are you moving or not?
The answer depends on your reference frame.
This is why kinematics starts with defining position and frame of reference.

What is Motion?

🔑
Motion is a change in position of an object with respect to time and a reference point.
📍
Position — where an object is located relative to a chosen origin (reference point).
📐
Reference frame — the coordinate system from which motion is observed and measured.
IB Key Point: All motion is relative. The same object can be "moving" or "at rest" depending on the observer's frame of reference.

The fundamental classification of physical quantities

Memory trick: Vectors have an arrow → direction matters!

Distance vs Displacement

Distance (d)

SCALAR
  • Total path length travelled
  • Always positive
  • SI unit: metre (m)
  • Does NOT depend on direction

Displacement (s)

VECTOR
  • Shortest path from start → end
  • Has direction
  • SI unit: metre (m)
  • Can be zero even if distance ≠ 0
$$\text{Distance} \geq |\text{Displacement}| \quad \text{ALWAYS}$$

Key Insight
Dist > Disp!

When is Displacement = 0?

Complete loop: Distance = 400 m, but Displacement = 0
Object returns to starting point → net change in position is zero.
This applies to: laps on a track, orbiting satellites, round trips, pendulum swings (full cycle)

Speed vs Velocity

Speed (v)

SCALAR
$$v = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}} = \frac{d}{t}$$
  • Rate of change of distance
  • Always positive
  • Unit: m/s or km/h

Velocity (v⃗)

VECTOR
$$\vec{v} = \frac{\text{displacement}}{\text{time}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{s}}{\Delta t}$$
  • Rate of change of displacement
  • Can be negative (direction)
  • Unit: m/s + direction

Average vs Instantaneous

Average Speed / Velocity

$$v_{avg} = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}} \qquad \vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\text{total displacement}}{\text{total time}}$$

Covers the entire journey — includes stops, speed changes, everything.

Instantaneous Speed / Velocity

$v_{inst} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = \frac{ds}{dt}$
HL ENRICHMENT Calculus notation — SL students use the average formula only.

Speed/velocity at a specific moment — what the speedometer shows.

Acceleration

The rate of change of velocity

$$\vec{a} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - u}{t}$$
VECTOR

Has magnitude AND direction

Unit

m/s²

Deceleration

a opposite to v direction

Common mistake: "At the highest point of a throw, acceleration = 0" ❌
Gravity ALWAYS acts → a = 9.8 m/s² downward, even at the top!

Derivation: Average Speed

Calculate Average speed for a multi-stage journey

1Define: $$v_{avg} = \frac{d_{total}}{t_{total}}$$
2Total distance: $$d_{total} = d_1 + d_2 + d_3 + \ldots$$
3Total time: $$t_{total} = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 + \ldots$$
⚠️ Include ALL time — even when stopped!
4$$v_{avg} = \frac{d_1 + d_2 + d_3}{t_1 + t_2 + t_3}$$
⚠️ NOT the average of individual speeds!

Worked Example 1

Problem

A car travels 150 km in 2 hours, stops for 30 minutes, then travels 90 km in 1 hour. Find the average speed.

1Total distance: $$d = 150 + 90 = 240 \text{ km}$$
2Total time: $$t = 2 + 0.5 + 1 = 3.5 \text{ hours}$$
Include the 30 min stop!
3$$v_{avg} = \frac{240}{3.5} = 68.6 \text{ km/h}$$
Answer: 68.6 km/h

Worked Example 2

Problem

A person walks 3 km East, then 4 km North. Find: (a) total distance (b) displacement magnitude (c) direction of displacement.

aDistance: $$d = 3 + 4 = 7 \text{ km}$$
bDisplacement (Pythagoras): $$s = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{ km}$$
cDirection: $$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) = 53.1° \text{ N of E}$$
Answer: 5 km at 53.1° North of East
Notice: distance (7) > displacement (5) ✓

Motion Graphs — Reading the Story

Graphs are the language of kinematics. Learn to read them.

Gradient = Speed
Steeper = faster | Flat = stationary
Gradient = Acceleration
Area = Displacement
IB Exam Trap: Students lose marks confusing "gradient of d-t" (= speed) with "gradient of v-t" (= acceleration). Know which graph you're reading!

Practice Questions

Try these before checking the answer (click to reveal)

Calculate Q1: A runner completes 2 laps of a 400 m track in 3 minutes. Calculate the average speed and average velocity.

Speed = 800/180 = 4.44 m/s | Velocity = 0/180 = 0 m/s (back to start!)

Determine Q2: A ball is thrown up at 20 m/s. What is its velocity at the top? What is its acceleration at the top?

Velocity = 0 m/s (momentarily at rest) | Acceleration = 9.8 m/s² downward (gravity never stops!)

Calculate Q3: A car accelerates from rest to 25 m/s in 5 seconds. Calculate the acceleration.

a = (v-u)/t = (25-0)/5 = 5 m/s²

Key Takeaways

Distance ≥ Displacement

Always. Equal only for straight-line motion.

Speed vs Velocity

Speed = scalar. Velocity = speed + direction.

Acceleration ≠ 0 at top

g = 9.8 m/s² always acts downward.
