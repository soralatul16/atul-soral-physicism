A.1 – KinematicsMotivation

Are you moving right now?

You feel stationary — but:

  • Earth rotates → you move at 1,670 km/h
  • Earth orbits the Sun → 107,000 km/h
  • Solar system through the Milky Way → 828,000 km/h

So are you moving or at rest?

The answer depends entirely on the reference frame you choose. This is why kinematics begins with carefully defining position and frame of reference.

This links to TOK: Is there such a thing as "absolute motion"? Newton said yes. Einstein proved no.

A.1 – KinematicsFoundations

Position & Frame of Reference

Position is where an object is, relative to a chosen origin.

To describe position, you need:

  • A reference point (origin)
  • A direction convention (+x = right, +y = up)
  • A unit (metres)
FYI: Position is a vector — it has magnitude AND direction from the origin.

Without a reference frame, "the car is at 5 m" is meaningless. 5 m from what?

A.1 – KinematicsClassification

Scalars and Vectors

Every physical quantity in physics is classified as either:

Scalar — magnitude only

  • Distance
  • Speed
  • Time, Mass, Energy, Temperature

Added normally: 3 km + 4 km = 7 km

Vector — magnitude + direction

  • Displacement
  • Velocity
  • Acceleration, Force, Momentum

Added by vector rules: 3 km East + 4 km North = 5 km (not 7!)

Why does this matter? In IB Physics, confusing scalars and vectors is one of the most common reasons students lose marks. Direction must be stated for all vector answers.
A.1 – KinematicsCore Concept

Distance vs Displacement

Distance (scalar)

The total length of path actually travelled by the object. It accumulates — can never decrease during a journey. Always ≥ 0.

Displacement (vector)

The straight-line distance from start to end, with a specified direction. Depends only on initial and final positions — not the path taken.

$$|\text{distance}| \geq |\text{displacement}| \quad \text{always}$$
Equal only when motion is in a straight line without changing direction.
A.1 – KinematicsSpecial Case

Displacement = 0 (Closed Path)

Whenever an object returns to its starting point, displacement is zero regardless of distance travelled.

Example: 400 m track, 1 lap

  • Distance = 400 m (full circumference)
  • Displacement = 0 m (back to start)

Other examples:

  • Earth completing one orbit around the Sun
  • A pendulum completing one full cycle
  • Driving to work and back home
IB Exam Trap: "Calculate the displacement after 2 complete revolutions" → Answer is always 0.
A.1 – KinematicsCore Concept

Speed and Velocity

Just as distance and displacement are the scalar/vector pair for "how far," speed and velocity are the pair for "how fast."

Speed (scalar)

Rate of change of distance.

$$\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}$$

Always positive. Unit: m s⁻¹. A speedometer measures this.

Velocity (vector)

Rate of change of displacement.

$$\vec{v} = \frac{\Delta \vec{s}}{\Delta t}$$

Can be + or −. Unit: m s⁻¹ with direction. Tells you how fast AND which way.

FYI: A car turning a corner at constant speed has changing velocity (because direction changes). This means it is accelerating — even though the speedometer doesn't change!
A.1 – KinematicsImportant Distinction

Average vs Instantaneous

Average Speed/Velocity

Calculated over the entire journey:

$$v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}} \qquad\qquad \vec{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\text{total displacement}}{\text{total time}}$$
Common Error: Average speed ≠ average of individual speeds. You must always use $\frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}$. The rest periods are included in total time!

Instantaneous Speed/Velocity

The speed/velocity at a single moment in time. What the speedometer reads at that instant.

On a displacement-time graph, it is the gradient of the tangent at that point.

HL Enrichment: In calculus notation: $v = \frac{ds}{dt}$. This is the limit of $\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}$ as $\Delta t \to 0$. SL students use the average formula only.
A.1 – KinematicsCore Concept

Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity.

$$\vec{a} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - u}{t}$$
  • Vector quantity — has direction
  • SI unit: m s⁻² (metres per second per second)
  • Positive acceleration = speeding up (in the positive direction)
  • Negative acceleration = either slowing down OR speeding up in the negative direction
The #1 student misconception:

"At the highest point of a ball thrown upward, the acceleration is zero because it stops for an instant."

WRONG. Velocity = 0 at the top, but acceleration = 9.8 m s⁻² downward at all times. Gravity doesn't take a break. The ball stops momentarily because it's decelerating, not because there's no acceleration.
A.1 – KinematicsGraphs

Interpreting Motion Graphs

In IB Physics, you must read three relationships from graphs:

Displacement–Time (s-t)

  • Gradient = velocity
  • Straight line → constant velocity
  • Horizontal → at rest
  • Steeper → faster
  • Negative gradient → moving backward

Velocity–Time (v-t)

  • Gradient = acceleration
  • Area under curve = displacement
  • Straight line → constant acceleration
  • Horizontal → constant velocity (a = 0)
  • Below axis → moving in negative direction
Paper 2 common error: Students confuse the gradient of s-t (= velocity) with the gradient of v-t (= acceleration). Always identify which graph you are reading FIRST.
A.1 – KinematicsWorked Example

Worked Example — Average Speed

Problem: A car travels 150 km in 2 hours, stops for 30 minutes, then travels 90 km in 1 hour. Calculate the average speed for the entire journey.

Solution (step by step):

Step 1: Total distance = 150 + 90 = 240 km

Step 2: Total time = 2 + 0.5 + 1 = 3.5 hours

⚠ Include the 30 min rest — average speed uses ALL elapsed time.

Step 3: Apply formula:

$$v_{avg} = \frac{d_{total}}{t_{total}} = \frac{240}{3.5} = \mathbf{68.6 \text{ km h}^{-1}}$$
FYI: If you average the individual speeds (75 and 90 km/h), you get 82.5 — which is incorrect. Never average speeds; always use total distance / total time.
A.1 – KinematicsWorked Example

Worked Example — Displacement (Pythagoras)

Problem: A person walks 3 km East, then 4 km North. Find: (a) the total distance (b) the magnitude of displacement (c) the direction of displacement.

(a) Distance = 3 + 4 = 7 km (simply add path lengths)

(b) Displacement — use Pythagoras:

$$s = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9+16} = \sqrt{25} = \mathbf{5 \text{ km}}$$

(c) Direction — use trigonometry:

$$\theta = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) = \mathbf{53.1°} \text{ North of East}$$

Check: distance (7) > displacement (5) ✓

A.1 – KinematicsPractice

Practice Questions

Click each question to reveal the answer.

CALCULATE

A runner completes 2 full laps of a 400 m track in 3 minutes. Calculate (a) the average speed and (b) the average velocity.

(a) speed = 800 ÷ 180 = 4.44 m s⁻¹ | (b) velocity = 0 ÷ 180 = 0 m s⁻¹ (displacement = 0)
DETERMINE

A ball is thrown upward at 20 m s⁻¹. State the velocity and acceleration at the highest point.

v = 0 m s⁻¹ (momentarily at rest) | a = 9.8 m s⁻² downward (gravity is constant!)
CALCULATE

A car accelerates uniformly from rest to 25 m s⁻¹ in 5.0 s. Calculate the acceleration.

a = (v − u)/t = (25 − 0)/5.0 = 5.0 m s⁻²
DISTINGUISH

A cyclist rides 10 km in 20 minutes, rests 10 minutes, then rides 5 km in 10 minutes. What is the average speed?

Total distance = 15 km. Total time = 40 min = 2/3 h. Average speed = 15 ÷ (2/3) = 22.5 km h⁻¹. Include rest time!
A.1 – KinematicsSummary

Summary

  • Distance ≥ Displacement — always. Equal only for straight-line, one-direction motion.
  • Speed = scalar (how fast). Velocity = vector (how fast + which way).
  • Acceleration = rate of change of velocity. It's 9.8 m s⁻² down even when v = 0 at the top.
  • Average speed = total distance / total time (include rest periods!).
  • s-t gradient = v. v-t gradient = a. v-t area = displacement.
