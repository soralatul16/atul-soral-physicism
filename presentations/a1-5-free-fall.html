What is Free Fall?

Free fall is motion under the influence of gravity alone — no other forces acting.

  • The ONLY force on the object is its weight (gravitational pull)
  • No air resistance, no thrust, no tension — nothing else
  • Can be going up, down, or sideways — as long as only gravity acts, it's free fall
  • A ball thrown upward IS in free fall the moment it leaves your hand
Common misconception: "Free fall means falling downward." NO — a ball going UP after being thrown is still in free fall. Free fall = only gravity acting, regardless of direction of motion.
FYI: Astronauts on the ISS are in free fall — they're falling toward Earth but moving sideways fast enough to keep missing it. That's why they appear "weightless."

The Acceleration Due to Gravity: $g$

Near Earth's surface, all freely falling objects accelerate at the same rate:

$$g = 9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2} \approx 9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}$$
  • Always directed downward (toward centre of Earth)
  • Independent of mass — a feather and a bowling ball fall at the same rate in vacuum
  • Approximately constant near Earth's surface (varies slightly with altitude and latitude)
  • IB uses $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$ unless stated otherwise
Sign convention: If you define "up" as positive, then $a = -g = -9.8$ m s$^{-2}$. If "down" is positive, then $a = +9.8$ m s$^{-2}$. Be consistent — state your convention at the start.

Galileo vs Aristotle

Aristotle (384–322 BC)

"Heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones."

This seems obvious — drop a rock and a feather. The rock hits first. For 2,000 years, nobody questioned this.

The problem: Aristotle didn't account for air resistance. He confused the effect of drag with the effect of gravity.

Galileo (1564–1642)

"All objects fall at the same rate, regardless of mass."

Galileo used inclined planes (not the Tower of Pisa — that's a myth) to slow down falling and measure carefully. He showed that distance $\propto t^2$.

The proof: In 1971, astronaut David Scott dropped a hammer and a feather on the Moon (no air). Both hit the ground at the same time. Galileo was right.

SUVAT Applied to Free Fall

Free fall is uniform acceleration with $a = g$. The SUVAT equations become:

Convention: taking downward as positive

Dropping from rest ($u = 0$)

$$v = gt$$
$$s = \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$$
$$v^2 = 2gs$$

These are simplified because $u = 0$.

Thrown upward ($u > 0$, up = +)

$$v = u - gt$$
$$s = ut - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$$
$$v^2 = u^2 - 2gs$$

Note the minus signs — $g$ opposes upward motion.

At maximum height: $v = 0$ (momentarily). Time to max height: $t_{max} = \frac{u}{g}$. Max height: $h_{max} = \frac{u^2}{2g}$.
Symmetry: Time up = time down. Speed at launch = speed at return (same height). The trajectory is symmetric about the peak.

Worked Example 1 — Dropped Object 3/10

Problem: A stone is dropped from rest from a bridge 80 m above a river. Ignoring air resistance, find (a) the time to hit the water (b) the speed just before impact.

SUVAT (down = +)

s = 80 m, u = 0, v = ?, a = 9.8, t = ?

(a) Find $t$

→$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$: $80 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2$
$$t = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 80}{9.8}} = \sqrt{16.3} = \mathbf{4.04 \text{ s}}$$

(b) Find $v$

→$v = u + at = 0 + 9.8(4.04)$
$$v = \mathbf{39.6 \text{ m s}^{-1}} \text{ downward}$$

Check: $v^2 = 2(9.8)(80) = 1568 \implies v = 39.6$ m/s ✓

Worked Example 2 — Thrown Upward 6/10

Problem: A ball is thrown vertically upward at 25 m s$^{-1}$ from ground level. Find (a) the maximum height (b) the total time in the air (c) the speed when it returns to the ground.

SUVAT (up = +)

u = +25 m/s, a = -9.8 m/s², at top: v = 0

(a) Max height

→$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $0 = 625 + 2(-9.8)s$
$$s = \frac{625}{19.6} = \mathbf{31.9 \text{ m}}$$

(b) Total time

→Time up: $0 = 25 + (-9.8)t \implies t_{up} = 2.55$ s

By symmetry: $t_{total} = 2 \times 2.55 = \mathbf{5.10 \text{ s}}$

(c) Speed at return

By symmetry, the ball returns at the same speed it was launched — but in the opposite direction.

$$v = \mathbf{-25 \text{ m s}^{-1}} \text{ (downward)}$$

Speed = 25 m/s. Velocity = -25 m/s.

Verify: $v = 25 + (-9.8)(5.10) = 25 - 49.98 = -25.0$ m/s ✓

Worked Example 3 — From a Height 8/10

Problem: A ball is thrown upward at 12 m s$^{-1}$ from the edge of a cliff 50 m high. Find (a) the maximum height above the ground (b) the total time before hitting the ground below (c) the impact velocity.

SUVAT (up = +, launch point = origin)

u = +12 m/s, a = -9.8 m/s², ground is at s = -50 m (below launch)

(a) Max height above launch = $\frac{u^2}{2g} = \frac{144}{19.6} = 7.35$ m

Max height above ground = 50 + 7.35 = 57.35 m

(b) Time to ground: $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$

$$-50 = 12t - 4.9t^2 \quad \implies \quad 4.9t^2 - 12t - 50 = 0$$
$$t = \frac{12 \pm \sqrt{144 + 980}}{9.8} = \frac{12 \pm 33.5}{9.8}$$

$t = \frac{45.5}{9.8} = \mathbf{4.64 \text{ s}}$ (take positive root)

(c) Impact velocity: $v = u + at = 12 + (-9.8)(4.64)$

$$v = 12 - 45.5 = \mathbf{-33.5 \text{ m s}^{-1}} \text{ (downward, speed = 33.5 m/s)}$$

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A student drops a ball from a window 15 m above the ground. At the same instant, a second student throws a ball vertically upward from the ground directly below at 12 m s$^{-1}$. Ignore air resistance.
Show that [3]

(a) Show that the two balls meet after approximately 1.25 s.

Ball A: $s_A = \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 = 4.9t^2$ (down from top)
Ball B: $s_B = 12t - 4.9t^2$ (up from ground)
Meet when $s_A + s_B = 15$: $4.9t^2 + 12t - 4.9t^2 = 15$ → $12t = 15$ → $t = \mathbf{1.25}$ s ✓
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the height above the ground where they meet.

Height of Ball B: $s_B = 12(1.25) - 4.9(1.25)^2 = 15 - 7.66 = \mathbf{7.34 \text{ m}}$ above ground
Determine [3]

(c) Determine the velocity of each ball when they meet.

Ball A: $v_A = 0 + 9.8(1.25) = \mathbf{12.3 \text{ m/s downward}}$
Ball B: $v_B = 12 - 9.8(1.25) = 12 - 12.25 = \mathbf{-0.25 \text{ m/s}}$ (just past peak, starting to fall)
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why the $4.9t^2$ terms cancelled in part (a), and what this tells us about the meeting time.

Both balls have the same gravitational acceleration $g$ [1]. The $\frac{1}{2}gt^2$ represents gravity's effect — identical for both. So the meeting time depends only on the initial velocity of Ball B and the separation distance, not on $g$ [1]. They would meet at the same time even on the Moon!

When Free Fall Isn't Free: Air Resistance

In reality, air resistance (drag) opposes motion. As speed increases, drag increases until it equals weight.

What happens:

  • Object falls → speed increases → drag increases
  • Eventually drag = weight → net force = 0
  • Acceleration = 0 → terminal velocity reached
  • Object falls at constant speed from this point
Examples: Skydiver: ~55 m/s. Raindrop: ~9 m/s. Feather: ~0.5 m/s. Ping pong ball: ~9 m/s.
IB distinction: "Free fall" means no air resistance. Once air resistance is mentioned, it's NOT free fall — it's "falling with drag." IB will ask you to "explain" or "compare" these two cases.

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A coin is dropped from rest from a height of 20 m. How long does it take to reach the ground?

$s = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$: $20 = \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2$ → $t = \sqrt{\frac{40}{9.8}} = \mathbf{2.02 \text{ s}}$
Calculate [3] Medium

A ball is thrown vertically upward at 18 m s$^{-1}$. Find the maximum height and the time to return to the launch point.

Max height: $h = \frac{u^2}{2g} = \frac{324}{19.6} = \mathbf{16.5 \text{ m}}$
Total time: $t = \frac{2u}{g} = \frac{36}{9.8} = \mathbf{3.67 \text{ s}}$
Determine [3] Hard

An object is thrown downward at 5.0 m s$^{-1}$ from a 40 m tower. Find the time to reach the ground and the impact speed.

$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}gt^2$: $40 = 5t + 4.9t^2$ → $4.9t^2 + 5t - 40 = 0$
$t = \frac{-5+\sqrt{25+784}}{9.8} = \frac{-5+28.4}{9.8} = \mathbf{2.39 \text{ s}}$
$v = 5 + 9.8(2.39) = \mathbf{28.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ downward
Explain [3] Hard

Two identical balls are dropped from the same height. One falls in a vacuum, the other in air. Compare and explain their (i) accelerations (ii) final speeds (iii) times to reach the ground.

(i) Vacuum: constant $a = g$. Air: $a$ starts at $g$ then decreases to 0 at terminal velocity [1]
(ii) Vacuum: $v$ keeps increasing. Air: $v$ reaches terminal velocity and stays constant [1]
(iii) Vacuum ball arrives first — constant acceleration means faster average speed than the air ball which decelerates due to drag [1]

Key Takeaways

  • Free fall = motion under gravity only. No air resistance. Can be going up OR down.
  • $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$ near Earth. Constant. Independent of mass. Always downward.
  • Galileo was right — all objects fall at the same rate in vacuum. Aristotle was wrong.
  • SUVAT applies with $a = \pm g$. Define your sign convention first.
  • Symmetry: time up = time down. Launch speed = return speed (same height).
  • At the top: $v = 0$ but $a = g$ still. Gravity never stops.
  • Terminal velocity: when drag = weight, $a = 0$, speed is constant. Not free fall.
