A.3.1

Work Done by a Force

You push a box across a room — that's work. You hold a heavy bag stationary — that's NOT work. You carry the bag horizontally — still not work. In physics, "work" has a very precise meaning.

What is Work?

Work is done when a force causes displacement in the direction of the force.

$$W = Fs\cos\theta$$
  • $W$ = work done (Joules, J)
  • $F$ = magnitude of the force (N)
  • $s$ = displacement (m) — NOT distance
  • $\theta$ = angle between the force and the displacement direction
SI unit: Joule (J). $1 \text{ J} = 1 \text{ N} \times 1 \text{ m} = 1 \text{ kg m}^2 \text{ s}^{-2}$. One joule is the work done when a 1 N force moves an object 1 m in the direction of the force.
Work is a scalar — it has no direction. But it can be positive, negative, or zero depending on the angle $\theta$.

Understanding $\cos\theta$ — The Three Cases

$\theta = 0°$: Positive Work

$\cos 0° = 1$
$W = Fs$ (maximum)

Force and motion same direction

$\theta = 90°$: Zero Work

$\cos 90° = 0$
$W = 0$

Force perpendicular to motion

$\theta = 180°$: Negative Work

$\cos 180° = -1$
$W = -Fs$

Force opposes motion (friction)

When is work zero?
1. No displacement ($s = 0$) — holding a bag stationary
2. Force perpendicular to displacement ($\theta = 90°$) — carrying a bag horizontally (gravity is perpendicular to horizontal motion)
3. No force ($F = 0$)

When is Work NOT Done?

These are the most tested misconceptions in IB:

Zero work situations:

  • Holding a heavy suitcase — no displacement, $W = 0$
  • Carrying a bag horizontally — the lifting force is vertical, motion is horizontal → $\theta = 90°$, $W = 0$
  • Circular motion at constant speed — centripetal force is perpendicular to velocity → $W = 0$
  • Normal force on a flat surface — perpendicular to horizontal motion → $W = 0$

Work IS done:

  • Pushing a box across a floor — force and displacement in same direction
  • Lifting a bag vertically — force up, displacement up → $\theta = 0°$
  • Friction slowing a car — force backward, displacement forward → negative work
  • Gravity on a falling ball — force down, displacement down → positive work
Negative work means the force REMOVES energy from the object. Friction does negative work — it converts kinetic energy into heat. Gravity does negative work on a ball going UP (removes KE, adds PE).

Work Done by an Angled Force

When force is at angle $\theta$ to displacement, only the component along the displacement does work.

Physical meaning of $F\cos\theta$: It's the component of force in the direction of movement. Only this component transfers energy to the object. The perpendicular component ($F\sin\theta$) changes the normal force but does no work.
Why pulling at an angle is interesting: $F\sin\theta$ partially lifts the object, reducing $N$, which reduces friction. So even though not all of $F$ does work directly, the reduced friction means less energy is wasted.

Work from a Force-Displacement Graph

When force varies with displacement, work = area under the F-s graph.

Spring example: $F = kx$ (Hooke's law). The F-s graph is a straight line through the origin. Area = triangle = $\frac{1}{2} \times kx \times x = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$. This is the elastic PE stored in a spring.
IB exam: "Determine the work done from the graph." → Find the area. Use rectangles + triangles, or count grid squares for curved graphs.

Work Done by Specific Forces

Gravity (lifting at constant v)

$$W_{gravity} = mgh$$

Positive when falling (force & displacement both down). Negative when lifting (force down, displacement up — so work done BY YOU is $+mgh$, work done BY GRAVITY is $-mgh$).

Friction

$$W_{friction} = -f_k \cdot s = -\mu_k N s$$

Always negative — friction opposes motion ($\theta = 180°$). Removes KE, converts to heat.

Spring ($F = kx$)

$$W_{spring} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$

Work done to stretch/compress a spring by $x$ from natural length. Stored as elastic PE.

Normal force (flat surface)

$$W_{normal} = 0$$

Always perpendicular to horizontal motion → $\cos 90° = 0$ → zero work. The normal force never does work on a flat surface.

Worked Example 1 — Horizontal Push 3/10

A 200 N force pushes a box 5.0 m along a floor. Find the work done.
$$W = Fs\cos 0° = 200 \times 5.0 \times 1 = \mathbf{1000 \text{ J}} = 1.0 \text{ kJ}$$

Worked Example 2 — Angled Pull 5/10

A rope pulls a sled with 150 N at 35° above horizontal for 20 m. Find the work done by the rope.
$$W = Fs\cos\theta = 150 \times 20 \times \cos 35° = 3000 \times 0.819 = \mathbf{2457 \text{ J}}$$

Only the horizontal component ($150\cos35° = 123$ N) does work over the 20 m.

Worked Example 3 — Lifting 5/10

A 4.0 kg box is lifted 3.0 m vertically at constant speed. Find the work done by the person and by gravity.
→By person: $W = Fs = mgs = 4.0 \times 9.8 \times 3.0 = \mathbf{+117.6 \text{ J}}$ (force up, displacement up)
→By gravity: $W = Fs\cos180° = 39.2 \times 3.0 \times (-1) = \mathbf{-117.6 \text{ J}}$ (gravity down, displacement up)
Net work = 0 (constant speed → no change in KE). The person's work is stored as gravitational PE.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A 5.0 kg box is pulled 8.0 m along a horizontal surface by a rope at 30° above horizontal with a force of 40 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction is $\mu_k = 0.25$.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the work done by the pulling force.

$W = Fs\cos\theta = 40 \times 8.0 \times \cos30° = 320 \times 0.866 = \mathbf{277 \text{ J}}$ [2]
Calculate [3]

(b) Calculate the normal force and the work done by friction.

$N = mg - F\sin30° = 49 - 20 = 29$ N [1]
$f_k = \mu_k N = 0.25 \times 29 = 7.25$ N [1]
$W_{friction} = -f_k \times s = -7.25 \times 8.0 = \mathbf{-58 \text{ J}}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) Calculate the net work done on the box.

$W_{net} = W_{pull} + W_{friction} + W_{gravity} + W_{normal}$
$= 277 + (-58) + 0 + 0 = \mathbf{219 \text{ J}}$ [2]
(Gravity and normal do zero work on horizontal motion)
Determine [2]

(d) Using the work-energy theorem, determine the final speed of the box if it starts from rest.

$W_{net} = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - 0$
$219 = \frac{1}{2}(5.0)v^2 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{438}{5.0}} = \mathbf{9.36 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Explain [2]

(e) Explain why the work done by gravity and the normal force are both zero in this situation.

Both gravity (downward) and normal force (upward) are perpendicular to the horizontal displacement [1]. Since $W = Fs\cos90° = 0$, neither does work. Only forces with a component along the displacement can do work [1].

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A 50 N force pushes a trolley 12 m along a flat surface. Find the work done.

$W = Fs = 50 \times 12 = \mathbf{600 \text{ J}}$ [2]
Explain [2] Medium

A person carries a 10 kg bag horizontally for 50 m. How much work does the person do on the bag against gravity?

Zero. The carrying force is vertical (upward), but the displacement is horizontal [1]. $\theta = 90°$, so $W = Fs\cos90° = 0$ [1]. No work is done against gravity in horizontal motion.
Calculate [3] Medium

A spring with $k = 200$ N/m is compressed by 0.15 m. Calculate the work done to compress it.

$W = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.15)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.0225) = \mathbf{2.25 \text{ J}}$ [3]
Calculate [3] Hard

A 3.0 kg block slides 4.0 m down a 25° incline with $\mu_k = 0.2$. Find the net work done on the block.

$W_{gravity} = mgs\sin25° = 3(9.8)(4)(0.423) = 49.7$ J [1]
$N = mg\cos25° = 26.6$ N, $f = 0.2 \times 26.6 = 5.33$ N
$W_{friction} = -5.33 \times 4 = -21.3$ J [1]
$W_{net} = 49.7 - 21.3 = \mathbf{28.4 \text{ J}}$ [1]

Key Takeaways

  • $W = Fs\cos\theta$ — force × displacement × cosine of angle between them.
  • Positive work ($\theta < 90°$): force adds energy. Negative work ($\theta > 90°$): force removes energy.
  • Zero work when: no displacement, force perpendicular to motion, or no force.
  • Gravity: $W = mgh$ (positive falling, negative rising). Friction: $W = -f_k s$ (always negative).
  • Spring: $W = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$. Normal force: $W = 0$ on flat surface.
  • F-s graph: area under graph = work done.
  • Net work = $\Delta KE$ (Work-Energy Theorem — next subtopic).
