Newton's Second Law for Rotation

$F = ma$ tells you how forces cause linear acceleration. Its rotational twin, $\tau = I\alpha$, tells you how torques cause angular acceleration. Same idea — different domain.

Newton's Second Law — Rotational Form

Linear (you know this)

$$\sum F = ma$$

Net force = mass × linear acceleration

Rotational (the new one)

$$\sum \tau = I\alpha$$

Net torque = moment of inertia × angular acceleration

Reading the equation: Large I (heavy or spread-out mass) → small α for a given torque. Large τ → large α for a given I. Identical logic to $F = ma$.
α must be in rad s⁻². If angular velocity is given in rev/s or rpm, convert first: $1 \text{ rev} = 2\pi \text{ rad}$, $1 \text{ rpm} = \frac{2\pi}{60}$ rad s⁻¹.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.4 τ = IαThree Forms

Three Rearrangements

FIND TORQUE

$$\tau = I\alpha$$

"How much torque do I need to spin this at a given α?"

FIND ANGULAR ACCEL

$$\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I}$$

"How fast will this accelerate under a given torque?"

FIND MOMENT OF INERTIA

$$I = \frac{\tau}{\alpha}$$

"What's the rotational inertia if I know τ and α?"

Connecting linear and rotational (at the rim)

For a point on a rotating object at distance $r$ from the axis:

  • $v = r\omega$  (tangential speed)
  • $a_t = r\alpha$  (tangential acceleration)
  • $s = r\theta$  (arc length)

These bridge between the linear and rotational worlds. If a string wraps around a pulley of radius $R$, the string's linear acceleration = $R\alpha$.

Rotational kinematics: The SUVAT equations work for rotation too: $\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t$, $\theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2$, $\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta$. Same structure, different symbols.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.4 τ = IαWorked Example 1

Worked Example: Spinning Disc

A solid disc of mass 6.0 kg and radius 0.25 m is initially at rest. A constant torque of 3.0 N·m is applied. Find: (a) the angular acceleration, (b) the angular velocity after 4.0 s, (c) the number of revolutions in that time.

Step 1 — Find I: $I = \frac{1}{2}mR^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 6.0 \times 0.25^2 = 0.1875$ kg·m²

Step 2 — (a) Find α: $\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{3.0}{0.1875} = \mathbf{16 \text{ rad s}^{-2}}$

Step 3 — (b) Find ω: $\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t = 0 + 16 \times 4.0 = \mathbf{64 \text{ rad s}^{-1}}$

Step 4 — (c) Find θ: $\theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{2} \times 16 \times 16 = 128$ rad

Revolutions: $\frac{128}{2\pi} = \mathbf{20.4 \text{ revolutions}}$

Method: $\tau = I\alpha$ problems almost always follow this pattern: (1) Find I from shape formula, (2) Find α from $\tau/I$, (3) Use rotational kinematics for ω, θ, or t.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.4 τ = IαWorked Example 2

Worked Example: Pulley with Mass

A solid disc pulley (mass 2.0 kg, radius 0.10 m) has a string wrapped around it with a 5.0 kg mass hanging from the string. The system is released from rest. Find the linear acceleration of the hanging mass.

Mass: $mg - T = ma$ → $5.0 \times 9.81 - T = 5.0a$  …(1)

Pulley: $TR = \frac{1}{2}MR^2\alpha$ → $T = \frac{1}{2}Ma = \frac{1}{2} \times 2.0 \times a = a$  …(2)

Substitute (2) into (1): $49.05 - a = 5.0a$ → $6.0a = 49.05$ → $a = \mathbf{8.18 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$

The pulley's inertia slows the system down. Without the pulley (massless string over frictionless peg), $a = g = 9.81$ m s⁻². The pulley's rotational inertia absorbs some of the driving force, reducing $a$ to 8.18 m s⁻².

Braking & Friction Torque

When a frictional torque opposes motion, it causes angular deceleration (negative α).

Example: Grinding wheel slowing down

A grinding wheel ($I = 0.50$ kg·m²) spins at 3000 rpm. A brake applies a constant friction torque of 2.0 N·m. How long until it stops? How many revolutions?

Convert: $\omega_0 = 3000 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 314.2$ rad s⁻¹

Find α: $\alpha = \frac{-\tau}{I} = \frac{-2.0}{0.50} = -4.0$ rad s⁻² (negative = decelerating)

Time to stop: $\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t$ → $0 = 314.2 + (-4.0)t$ → $t = \mathbf{78.6 \text{ s}}$

Revolutions: $\theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 = 314.2 \times 78.6 + \frac{1}{2}(-4.0)(78.6)^2 = 12\,340$ rad $= \mathbf{1964 \text{ rev}}$

Alternative for θ: Use $\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta$ → $0 = 314.2^2 + 2(-4.0)\theta$ → $\theta = \frac{314.2^2}{8.0} = 12\,340$ rad. Same answer — choose whichever is faster.

Strategy for Combined Problems

Many IB problems involve both linear and rotational motion connected by a string, axle, or contact point.

The 4-Step Method

  1. Draw separate FBDs — one for each object (masses, pulleys, wheels)
  2. Write Newton's 2nd — $F = ma$ for translating objects, $\tau = I\alpha$ for rotating objects
  3. Write the constraint — usually $a = R\alpha$ (string doesn't slip)
  4. Solve simultaneously — substitute constraint into equations, eliminate unknowns

Common constraint equations

String on pulley:
$a = R\alpha$ (no-slip)
$v = R\omega$
Rolling without slipping:
$v_{\text{CM}} = R\omega$
$a_{\text{CM}} = R\alpha$
Common mistake: Forgetting that the tension on each side of a pulley with mass is different. For a massless pulley, $T_1 = T_2$. For a real pulley, $T_1 \neq T_2$ — the difference drives the pulley's rotation: $(T_1 - T_2)R = I\alpha$.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

State [1]

(a) State Newton's second law for rotational motion.

The net torque acting on a body equals the product of its moment of inertia and its angular acceleration: $\sum\tau = I\alpha$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A solid sphere of mass 4.0 kg and radius 0.15 m has a net torque of 0.60 N·m applied to it. Calculate the angular acceleration.

$I = \frac{2}{5}mR^2 = \frac{2}{5} \times 4.0 \times 0.15^2 = 0.036$ kg·m² [1]
$\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{0.60}{0.036}$ [1]
$\alpha = \mathbf{16.7 \text{ rad s}^{-2}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) A flywheel ($I = 2.5$ kg·m²) decelerates uniformly from 120 rad s⁻¹ to 40 rad s⁻¹ in 10 s. Calculate the braking torque.

$\alpha = \frac{\omega - \omega_0}{t} = \frac{40 - 120}{10} = -8.0$ rad s⁻² [1]
$|\tau| = I|\alpha| = 2.5 \times 8.0$ [1]
$\tau = \mathbf{20 \text{ N·m}}$ [1]
Calculate [4]

(d) A hoop of mass 3.0 kg and radius 0.20 m starts from rest. A constant tangential force of 15 N is applied at the rim. Find: (i) the torque, (ii) the angular velocity after 2.0 s.

(i) $\tau = FR = 15 \times 0.20 = \mathbf{3.0 \text{ N·m}}$ [1]
$I = mR^2 = 3.0 \times 0.04 = 0.12$ kg·m² [1]
$\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{3.0}{0.12} = 25$ rad s⁻² [1]
(ii) $\omega = 0 + 25 \times 2.0 = \mathbf{50 \text{ rad s}^{-1}}$ [1]

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [4]

A disc pulley ($M = 4.0$ kg, $R = 0.15$ m) has a string wrapped around it with a 8.0 kg mass hanging from it. The system is released. Find the acceleration of the hanging mass.

For the mass: $mg - T = ma$ → $78.5 - T = 8.0a$ …(1) [1]
For the pulley: $TR = \frac{1}{2}MR^2 \alpha$ and $a = R\alpha$ → $T = \frac{1}{2}Ma = \frac{1}{2}(4.0)a = 2.0a$ …(2) [1]
Substitute: $78.5 - 2.0a = 8.0a$ → $10.0a = 78.5$ [1]
$a = \mathbf{7.85 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

A merry-go-round ($I = 500$ kg·m²) is spinning at 0.40 rad s⁻¹. A child (40 kg) runs tangentially at 3.0 m s⁻¹ and jumps on at radius 2.0 m from the centre, applying a torque of 120 N·m for 0.50 s. Find the angular acceleration during the push.

New I = $500 + 40 \times 2.0^2 = 500 + 160 = 660$ kg·m² [1]
$\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{120}{660}$ [1]
$\alpha = \mathbf{0.182 \text{ rad s}^{-2}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

Two discs of identical mass and radius are accelerated from rest by the same torque. Disc A is solid; Disc B is a hoop. After 5 seconds, which has greater angular velocity and by what factor?

Disc A: $I_A = \frac{1}{2}mR^2$, $\alpha_A = \frac{\tau}{I_A}$. Disc B: $I_B = mR^2$, $\alpha_B = \frac{\tau}{I_B}$ [1].
$\frac{\alpha_A}{\alpha_B} = \frac{I_B}{I_A} = \frac{mR^2}{\frac{1}{2}mR^2} = 2$. After the same time, $\omega_A = 2\omega_B$. Disc A has twice the angular velocity [1].
Connection to A.4.5: $\tau = I\alpha$ can also be written as $\tau = \frac{dL}{dt}$ (rate of change of angular momentum), just like $F = \frac{dp}{dt}$. This leads directly to angular impulse and conservation of angular momentum.

Key Takeaways

  • $\tau = I\alpha$ — the rotational Newton's 2nd law. Net torque drives angular acceleration.
  • Three rearrangements: $\alpha = \tau/I$, $\tau = I\alpha$, $I = \tau/\alpha$.
  • Method: Find I (shape formula) → Find α from $\tau/I$ → Use rotational kinematics.
  • Rotational SUVAT: $\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t$, $\theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2$, $\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta$.
  • Combined problems: Write $F = ma$ for linear parts, $\tau = I\alpha$ for rotating parts, link with $a = R\alpha$.
  • Real pulleys have $T_1 \neq T_2$. The tension difference creates the net torque.
  • Friction torque causes angular deceleration. Use negative α.
