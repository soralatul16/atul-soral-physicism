SHM Graphs — Displacement, Velocity & Acceleration

Reading the motion off a graph

Displacement, velocity and acceleration in SHM are all sinusoids of the same frequency — but each is shifted from the next by a quarter of a cycle. Master these three curves and their phase relationships, and every SHM problem becomes a matter of reading the graph.

Displacement, Velocity & Acceleration

Start from a cosine displacement (motion released from maximum displacement at $t=0$). Differentiate once for velocity, again for acceleration:

$$x = x_0\cos(\omega t)$$
$$v = \frac{dx}{dt} = -\omega x_0\sin(\omega t)$$
$$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = -\omega^2 x_0\cos(\omega t) = -\omega^2 x$$
  • Velocity is the gradient of the displacement–time graph.
  • Acceleration is the gradient of the velocity–time graph.
  • The last line recovers the defining equation $a = -\omega^2 x$ — the acceleration graph is just the displacement graph flipped and scaled by $\omega^2$.
Amplitudes stack up in ω: displacement peaks at $x_0$, velocity peaks at $\omega x_0$, acceleration peaks at $\omega^2 x_0$. Each derivative multiplies the amplitude by another factor of $\omega$.

Phase Relationships

The three quantities are locked in fixed phase differences that never change:

  • Velocity leads displacement by 90° ($\pi/2$, a quarter period).
  • Acceleration leads velocity by 90° ($\pi/2$).
  • Acceleration is 180° ($\pi$) out of phase with displacement — exactly anti-phase (the minus sign in $a=-\omega^2x$).

At the centre (x = 0)

Velocity is maximum ($v_{max}=\omega x_0$); acceleration is zero (no restoring force at equilibrium).

At the extremes (x = ±x₀)

Velocity is zero (momentarily at rest); acceleration is maximum ($a_{max}=\omega^2 x_0$).

Common exam trap: "leads by 90°" means the velocity curve reaches its peak a quarter-period earlier in time than the displacement curve. On a sine-start graph, velocity is a cosine; on a cosine-start graph, velocity is a negative sine. Always sketch from the displacement curve's gradient.

Velocity as a Function of Displacement

Eliminating time from the sine and cosine gives velocity directly in terms of displacement — one of the most useful SHM equations:

$$v = \pm\,\omega\sqrt{x_0^2 - x^2}$$
  • At $x = 0$: $v = \pm\omega x_0 = v_{max}$ (fastest at the centre).
  • At $x = \pm x_0$: $v = 0$ (instantaneously at rest at the extremes).
  • The $\pm$ reflects that the object passes each point moving in both directions.

A v–x plot is an ellipse: widest v at the centre, zero v at the edges.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — max velocity & acceleration

An object oscillates with amplitude 0.040 m and period 0.20 s. Find $v_{max}$ and $a_{max}$.

$\omega = 2\pi/T = 2\pi/0.20 = 31.4$ rad s⁻¹.

$v_{max} = \omega x_0 = 31.4\times0.040 = \mathbf{1.26\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$

$a_{max} = \omega^2 x_0 = (31.4)^2\times0.040 = \mathbf{39.5\ \text{m s}^{-2}}$

Example 2 — velocity at a given displacement

The same object ($x_0=0.040$ m, $\omega=31.4$) is at $x = 0.020$ m. Find its speed.

$v = \omega\sqrt{x_0^2-x^2} = 31.4\sqrt{0.040^2-0.020^2}$

$= 31.4\sqrt{0.0016-0.0004} = 31.4\sqrt{0.0012}$

$= 31.4\times0.0346 = \mathbf{1.09\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$

Example 3 — reading a graph

A displacement–time graph is a positive sine curve starting at the origin. Describe the velocity at $t=0$.

At $t=0$ the displacement is zero (at centre) and the gradient is maximum positive → velocity is maximum, $v = +\omega x_0$. Acceleration is zero.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

State [2]

(a) An object undergoes SHM. State the phase relationship between (i) velocity and displacement, and (ii) acceleration and displacement.

(i) Velocity leads displacement by 90° ($\pi/2$ rad / a quarter of a period) [1]. (ii) Acceleration is 180° ($\pi$ rad) out of phase with displacement — they are in anti-phase [1].
Describe [3]

(b) The displacement of an oscillator is x = x₀cos(ωt). Describe the values of displacement, velocity and acceleration at t = 0.

Displacement is maximum, $x = x_0$ [1]. Velocity is zero — the object is momentarily at rest at the extreme [1]. Acceleration is maximum in magnitude and directed toward equilibrium, $a = -\omega^2 x_0$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) An oscillator has amplitude 0.060 m and angular frequency 15 rad s⁻¹. Calculate its speed when its displacement is 0.030 m.

$v = \omega\sqrt{x_0^2-x^2} = 15\sqrt{0.060^2-0.030^2}$ [1]
$= 15\sqrt{0.0036-0.0009} = 15\sqrt{0.0027}$ [1]
$= 15\times0.0520 = \mathbf{0.78\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why the velocity–time graph is the gradient of the displacement–time graph, and hence why they are a quarter-cycle out of step.

Velocity is the rate of change of displacement, $v = dx/dt$, so at each instant it equals the gradient of the x–t curve [1]. The gradient of a cosine is a negative sine (and of a sine is a cosine), which is the same curve shifted by a quarter period — hence velocity is displaced 90° from displacement [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Three sinusoids, one frequency: $x=x_0\cos\omega t$, $v=-\omega x_0\sin\omega t$, $a=-\omega^2 x_0\cos\omega t$.
  • Each is the gradient of the one above: v = gradient of x; a = gradient of v.
  • Peak values: $v_{max}=\omega x_0$ (at centre); $a_{max}=\omega^2 x_0$ (at extremes).
  • Phase: v leads x by 90°; a leads v by 90°; a is anti-phase (180°) with x.
  • Velocity–displacement: $v = \pm\omega\sqrt{x_0^2-x^2}$; a v–x plot is an ellipse.
  • Centre = fast & no acceleration; extremes = still & max acceleration.
