Entropy in Practice

Free expansion, mixing & phase changes

Now we compute real entropy changes. Free expansion shows entropy rising with no heat flow at all; phase changes give clean $\Delta S = mL/T$ calculations; and mixing reveals why unmixing never happens. Three concrete cases that turn the abstract second law into numbers.

Free (Joule) Expansion of a Gas

A gas expands into a vacuum when a valve opens — no piston, no resistance. It does no work and (for an ideal gas, insulated) exchanges no heat.

  • $W = 0$ — expanding into vacuum, no external pressure to push against.
  • $Q = 0$ — insulated container.
  • First law: $\Delta U = Q - W = 0$, so for an ideal gas $T$ is unchanged.
  • Yet the gas will never spontaneously crowd back into one half — the process is irreversible.
$$\Delta S = nR\ln\frac{V_2}{V_1} \; > 0$$
The striking lesson: entropy increases even though $Q = 0$. The naive $\Delta S = Q/T$ gives zero here — but that only applies to reversible heat transfer. For an irreversible process you compute $\Delta S$ along a reversible path with the same endpoints (here, isothermal expansion), giving $nR\ln(V_2/V_1)$.

Entropy Change with Volume (Isothermal)

For an ideal gas expanding isothermally (reversibly) from $V_1$ to $V_2$:

$$\Delta S = \frac{Q_{rev}}{T} = \frac{W}{T} = \frac{nRT\ln(V_2/V_1)}{T}$$
$$\Delta S = nR\ln\frac{V_2}{V_1}$$
  • Expansion ($V_2 > V_1$): $\ln > 0$ → entropy increases (more space, more microstates).
  • Compression ($V_2 < V_1$): $\ln < 0$ → the gas's entropy decreases (but work/heat raises the surroundings' entropy more).
  • Depends only on the ratio of volumes, not their absolute size.

Quick example

2.0 mol of ideal gas doubles its volume in a free expansion. Find $\Delta S$.

$\Delta S = nR\ln 2 = (2.0)(8.31)(0.693) = \mathbf{+11.5\ \text{J K}^{-1}}$

Entropy of a Phase Change

Melting and boiling happen at constant temperature, so the entropy change is simply the latent heat divided by that temperature:

$$\Delta S = \frac{Q}{T} = \frac{mL}{T}$$

$L$ = specific latent heat, $T$ = phase-change temperature (K)

  • Melting / boiling (solid→liquid→gas): heat absorbed, $\Delta S > 0$ — particles become more disordered.
  • Freezing / condensing: heat released, $\Delta S < 0$ for the substance — particles become more ordered.
  • The jump in entropy is largest for boiling (liquid→gas is a huge increase in disorder).

Worked example — melting ice

Find the entropy change when 0.50 kg of ice melts at 0 °C. ($L_f = 3.34\times10^{5}$ J kg⁻¹)

$T = 273$ K, $Q = mL = 0.50 \times 3.34\times10^{5} = 1.67\times10^{5}$ J.

$\Delta S = \dfrac{Q}{T} = \dfrac{1.67\times10^{5}}{273} = \mathbf{+612\ \text{J K}^{-1}}$

Mixing & Thermal Equilibration

Mixing two gases

Open a valve between two different gases and they interdiffuse. Each gas effectively expands into the whole volume, so total entropy rises — and they never spontaneously separate again.

Mixing hot & cold water

Heat flows from hot to cold until they reach a common temperature. The cold water's entropy gain exceeds the hot water's loss, so $\Delta S_{total} > 0$.

Worked example — mixing water

Estimate the entropy change when a small amount of heat, 4200 J, flows from water at 350 K to water at 290 K (treat reservoirs as roughly constant T).

Hot: $\Delta S = -4200/350 = -12.0$ J K⁻¹. Cold: $\Delta S = +4200/290 = +14.5$ J K⁻¹.

$\Delta S_{total} = -12.0 + 14.5 = \mathbf{+2.5\ \text{J K}^{-1}} > 0$ ✓

Why mixing is irreversible: the mixed / equilibrated state has vastly more microstates than the separated state. Reversing it (unmixing, or re-separating hot and cold) would require total entropy to fall — forbidden by the second law.

Reversible vs Irreversible — The Entropy Test

ReversibleIrreversible
$\Delta S_{total}$$= 0$$> 0$
Speedinfinitely slow (quasi-static)finite, real

Realityidealisation onlyall real processes
The arrow of time: every real process increases the entropy of the universe. This one-way increase is the only fundamental physical law that distinguishes past from future — everything else (Newton's laws, electromagnetism) works the same forwards and backwards.
Method for any ΔS problem: even if the actual process is irreversible, compute $\Delta S$ along an imagined reversible path between the same start and end states — entropy is a state function, so the path used to calculate it doesn't matter.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Explain [3]

(a) A gas undergoes free expansion into a vacuum in an insulated container. State the values of Q and W, and explain how the entropy can still increase.

$W = 0$ (no external pressure to push against) and $Q = 0$ (insulated) [1]. So $\Delta U = 0$ and, for an ideal gas, temperature is unchanged. Although $Q = 0$, the process is irreversible; entropy is a state function, so $\Delta S$ is found along a reversible isothermal path: $\Delta S = nR\ln(V_2/V_1) > 0$ [1]. The gas now occupies more volume with far more available microstates, so its entropy (and the universe's) has increased [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) Calculate the entropy change when 0.20 kg of water boils to steam at 100 °C. ($L_v = 2.26\times10^{6}$ J kg⁻¹)

$T = 373$ K; $Q = mL = 0.20 \times 2.26\times10^{6} = 4.52\times10^{5}$ J [1]
$\Delta S = Q/T = 4.52\times10^{5} / 373$ [1]
$= \mathbf{+1.21\times10^{3}\ \text{J K}^{-1}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) 3.0 mol of an ideal gas expands isothermally to three times its original volume. Determine its entropy change.

$\Delta S = nR\ln(V_2/V_1) = (3.0)(8.31)\ln 3$ [1]
$= (3.0)(8.31)(1.099)$ [1]
$= \mathbf{+27.4\ \text{J K}^{-1}}$ [1]
Discuss [2]

(d) Explain why the mixing of two different gases is an irreversible process, in terms of microstates and probability.

After mixing, each gas occupies the entire volume, giving an enormously larger number of possible microscopic arrangements (microstates) than the separated state [1]. Since $S = k_B\ln\Omega$, the mixed state has much higher entropy; spontaneous unmixing would require a jump to a vastly less probable, lower-entropy state, which is effectively impossible — so the process is irreversible [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Free expansion: $Q=0$, $W=0$, $\Delta U=0$, yet $\Delta S = nR\ln(V_2/V_1) > 0$ — irreversible.
  • Volume change (isothermal): $\Delta S = nR\ln(V_2/V_1)$ — depends only on the volume ratio.
  • Phase change: $\Delta S = mL/T$ at the (constant) transition temperature.
  • Mixing & equilibration: always raise total entropy; never spontaneously reverse.
  • State function: compute $\Delta S$ along any reversible path between the same endpoints.
  • Reversible: $\Delta S_{total}=0$; irreversible (all real): $\Delta S_{total} > 0$ — the arrow of time.
