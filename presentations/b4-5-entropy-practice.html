Free Expansion — Gas into a Vacuum

A gas expands into a vacuum (no external pressure). No work is done ($W = 0$, nothing to push against). No heat flows ($Q = 0$, instantaneous). By the first law: $\Delta U = 0$, so temperature is unchanged.

But entropy increases!

$Q = 0$, $W = 0$, $\Delta U = 0$, $\Delta T = 0$ — nothing seems to change. Yet this process is irreversible. The gas will never spontaneously collect back into one half.

Using Boltzmann: the number of microstates $\Omega$ increases enormously when the gas has twice the volume. $\Delta S = Nk_B \ln 2 = nR\ln 2$ (for doubling the volume).

Example

1.0 mol of gas free-expands to double its volume.

$\Delta S = nR\ln(V_2/V_1) = 1.0 \times 8.314 \times \ln 2 = \mathbf{5.76 \text{ J K}^{-1}}$

Free expansion proves entropy isn't just about heat. $\Delta S = Q/T$ only works for reversible processes. For irreversible processes like free expansion ($Q = 0$ but $\Delta S > 0$), we must use the statistical definition or calculate via a reversible path between the same states.

Entropy of Mixing & Phase Changes

Mixing of gases

When two different gases at the same T and P are allowed to mix, entropy increases — each gas expands into the volume previously occupied by the other. $\Delta S_{\text{mix}} > 0$ always. The process is irreversible — mixed gases never spontaneously unmix.

For equal volumes of two ideal gases: $\Delta S = 2nR\ln 2$.

Phase changes

$\Delta S_{\text{phase}} = \frac{Q}{T} = \frac{mL}{T}$ (at constant temperature)

  • Melting: $\Delta S = +mL_f / T_m$ — positive, entropy increases (more disorder)
  • Boiling: $\Delta S = +mL_v / T_b$ — positive and much larger (gas has far more microstates)
  • Freezing/condensing: $\Delta S < 0$ for the substance, but surroundings gain more entropy → $\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$

Example: Entropy of melting ice

0.50 kg of ice melts at 0°C. $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5$ J/kg.

$\Delta S = \frac{mL_f}{T} = \frac{0.50 \times 3.34 \times 10^5}{273} = \mathbf{612 \text{ J K}^{-1}}$

Boiling has much larger ΔS than melting because $L_v \gg L_f$ AND gas has enormously more microstates than liquid (molecules free to go anywhere in the container vs staying near each other).

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Explain [3]

(a) A gas undergoes free expansion into a vacuum. Explain why the temperature doesn't change but the process is irreversible.

No work is done ($P_{\text{ext}} = 0$, so $W = P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V = 0$) and the system is insulated ($Q = 0$). By the first law, $\Delta U = 0$, so temperature is unchanged [1]. However, the gas now occupies a larger volume — the number of microstates $\Omega$ has increased enormously [1]. The entropy increase ($\Delta S = nR\ln(V_2/V_1) > 0$) means the process is irreversible — the gas will never spontaneously return to the original half, as that would require a decrease in entropy [1].
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the entropy change when 2.0 kg of water boils at 100°C. ($L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6$ J/kg)

$\Delta S = \frac{mL_v}{T} = \frac{2.0 \times 2.26 \times 10^6}{373}$ [1]
$= \mathbf{12\,115 \text{ J K}^{-1} \approx 12.1 \text{ kJ K}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) When water freezes, its entropy decreases. Explain why this doesn't violate the second law.

The second law states that the total entropy of an isolated system cannot decrease — not the entropy of any individual part [1]. When water freezes, it releases latent heat ($Q = mL_f$) to the surroundings at a temperature $T_{\text{surr}} < T_{\text{freeze}}$ (otherwise freezing wouldn't occur). The surroundings gain entropy $Q/T_{\text{surr}}$, which exceeds the entropy lost by the water $Q/T_{\text{freeze}}$. So $\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$ [1].
Explain [2]

(d) Explain, using entropy, why a cup of hot coffee always cools to room temperature and never heats up spontaneously.

Heat flows from the hot coffee (high T) to the cooler surroundings (low T). The entropy decrease of the coffee ($-Q/T_{\text{hot}}$) is smaller than the entropy increase of the surroundings ($+Q/T_{\text{cold}}$), since $T_{\text{cold}} < T_{\text{hot}}$ [1]. So $\Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0$. The reverse (surroundings heating the coffee) would require $\Delta S_{\text{total}} < 0$, violating the second law — so it never happens spontaneously [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Free expansion: $Q=0$, $W=0$, $\Delta T=0$, but $\Delta S = nR\ln(V_2/V_1) > 0$. Irreversible.
  • Mixing: $\Delta S_{\text{mix}} > 0$ always. Gases never spontaneously unmix.
  • Phase changes: $\Delta S = mL/T$. Melting/boiling increase entropy. Freezing/condensing decrease substance's entropy but increase surroundings' more.
  • Boiling ΔS ≫ melting ΔS — gas has enormously more microstates than liquid.
  • Local entropy decrease is fine — as long as total (system + surroundings) increases.
  • $\Delta S = Q/T$ only for reversible. For irreversible processes, use Boltzmann or equivalent reversible path.
