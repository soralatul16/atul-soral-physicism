Turning theory into measurement

Measuring the Period Precisely

The period of one swing is short, so timing a single oscillation gives huge percentage uncertainty. The standard techniques:

  • Time many oscillations: measure the time for (say) 20 oscillations and divide by 20. The stopwatch's fixed reaction-time error is spread across all 20, cutting the percentage uncertainty in T by a factor of 20.
  • Use a fiducial marker: a fixed reference mark at the equilibrium position, not at an extreme. The oscillator moves fastest through the centre, so it's easier to judge the exact instant it passes — reducing timing error.
  • Start counting from zero: say "zero" as you start the clock, not "one", to avoid an off-by-one error.
  • Repeat and average: take several sets of readings and average to reduce random error.
Why the centre, not the end? At the extremes the object is momentarily at rest, so it lingers and it's hard to pinpoint the turning instant. At the centre its speed is maximum ($v_{max}=\omega x_0$), so it crosses the mark sharply — a far more reproducible trigger point.

The Two Classic Setups

Mass–spring

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$

Vary: the mass m on the spring.
Measure: T for each mass (time 20 swings).
Find: the spring constant k.

Simple pendulum

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

Vary: the length L of the string.
Measure: T for each length.
Find: the acceleration g.

Controls for a good pendulum: keep the swing angle small (< 10°) so the motion is genuinely SHM; use a small, dense bob (reduces air resistance and locates the centre of mass); measure L from the pivot to the centre of the bob; ensure the string is inextensible and the pivot rigid.
Systematic error alert: forgetting to add the bob's radius to the string length, or a swing angle that's too large, both shift every reading the same way — these are systematic, not random, and won't average out.

Linearising: Plot T², Not T

The relationships are square-root curves. Squaring both sides turns them into straight lines through the origin — the standard way to test a theory and extract a constant:

Spring: T² vs m

$$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{k}\,m$$

Straight line through origin. Gradient $= 4\pi^2/k$, so $k = 4\pi^2/\text{gradient}$.

Pendulum: T² vs L

$$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{g}\,L$$

Straight line through origin. Gradient $= 4\pi^2/g$, so $g = 4\pi^2/\text{gradient}$.

A straight line through the origin confirms $T^2 \propto L$ — i.e. genuine SHM behaviour.

Verifying SHM: the fact that the T²–L (or T²–m) graph is a straight line through the origin is itself the evidence that the system obeys the SHM period relationship. A non-zero intercept or a curve signals a systematic error or a breakdown of the model (e.g. large-angle swings).

Reducing Uncertainty & Modern Methods

  • Percentage uncertainty in T: timing N oscillations divides the human reaction uncertainty by N. Timing 20 swings instead of 1 cuts the % uncertainty roughly 20-fold.
  • Propagating to T²: since you plot T², its percentage uncertainty is double that of T ($T^2$ means %unc doubles) — worth stating in an IB lab.
  • Line of best fit + worst-fit lines: the spread of gradients between best and worst acceptable lines gives the uncertainty in k or g.

Data-logging alternatives

A light gate + timer at the equilibrium position removes human reaction time entirely — it triggers exactly as the bob passes. A motion sensor can record the full displacement–time trace, letting you read T directly and even see the exponential amplitude decay of damping.

Best practice summary: many oscillations, fiducial mark at the centre, small amplitude, repeat and average, plot the linearised graph, and quote uncertainties from the gradient. This is the recipe examiners reward.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — period from many swings

A student times 20 oscillations of a pendulum as 36.0 s. Find the period.

$T = 36.0/20 = \mathbf{1.80\ \text{s}}$

Example 2 — g from a gradient

A T²-vs-L graph for a pendulum has gradient 4.1 s² m⁻¹. Find g.

Gradient $= 4\pi^2/g$, so $g = 4\pi^2/\text{gradient} = 4\pi^2/4.1$

$= 39.5/4.1 = \mathbf{9.6\ \text{m s}^{-2}}$ (close to 9.81)

Example 3 — k from a gradient

A T²-vs-m graph for a mass–spring system has gradient 0.50 s² kg⁻¹. Find the spring constant.

Gradient $= 4\pi^2/k$, so $k = 4\pi^2/0.50 = 39.5/0.50$

$= \mathbf{79\ \text{N m}^{-1}}$

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Explain [2]

(a) Explain why a student times 20 oscillations rather than one when measuring the period of a pendulum.

The human reaction-time uncertainty in starting and stopping the clock is roughly fixed regardless of how many swings are timed [1]. Dividing the total time by 20 spreads this fixed uncertainty over 20 periods, reducing the percentage uncertainty in T by a factor of about 20 [1].
State [2]

(b) State where a fiducial marker should be placed when timing oscillations, and explain why.

It should be placed at the equilibrium (centre) position [1], because the oscillator moves fastest there ($v_{max}$), so the instant it passes the mark can be judged most sharply and reproducibly — reducing timing uncertainty [1].
Analyse [3]

(c) A student plots T² against L for a pendulum and obtains a straight line through the origin of gradient 4.0 s² m⁻¹. Determine g, and state what the shape of the graph confirms.

$T^2 = (4\pi^2/g)L$, so gradient $= 4\pi^2/g$ [1]. $g = 4\pi^2/4.0 = 39.5/4.0 = \mathbf{9.9\ \text{m s}^{-2}}$ [1]. A straight line through the origin confirms $T^2 \propto L$, i.e. the pendulum obeys the SHM period relationship [1].
Evaluate [2]

(d) A student's T²–L graph has a small positive intercept on the T² axis instead of passing through the origin. Suggest a likely cause.

A systematic error in measuring the length — most likely the length was measured to the top of the bob rather than to its centre of mass, so every L is systematically too small by the bob's radius [1]. This shifts all points and produces a non-zero intercept even though the gradient (and hence g) may still be reasonable [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Time many oscillations (e.g. 20) and divide — cuts % uncertainty in T dramatically.
  • Fiducial marker at the centre (fastest point) for the sharpest timing.
  • Small amplitude, dense bob, measure to centre of mass for a valid pendulum.
  • Linearise: plot T² vs L (or vs m) — a straight line through the origin verifies SHM.
  • Extract constants: $g = 4\pi^2/\text{gradient}$ (pendulum); $k = 4\pi^2/\text{gradient}$ (spring).
  • Reduce error further: light gates/motion sensors remove human reaction time.
