Faraday's Discovery: Movement Makes Voltage

Move a magnet in and out of a coil and a current flows — with no battery anywhere. This is electromagnetic induction.

The essential requirement: change

  • Magnet moving in → current one way. Moving out → current the other way.
  • Magnet held still inside → no current, even though the field is strong.
  • Faster movement → bigger induced current.
Key insight: it is not the field that induces a voltage — it is the change in field (more precisely, the change in magnetic flux linkage). No change, no EMF.

Magnetic Flux — the Key Quantity

Magnetic flux measures how much field passes through a loop of area A:

$$\Phi = BA\cos\theta$$

flux Φ in webers (Wb); θ = angle between B and the normal to the area

  • $\Phi$ — magnetic flux (weber, Wb). 1 Wb = 1 T m².
  • Maximum flux when the field is perpendicular to the loop ($\theta = 0$, $\cos\theta = 1$).
  • Zero flux when the field lies in the plane of the loop ($\theta = 90°$).
  • Flux linkage $= N\Phi$ for a coil of $N$ turns — each turn "links" the flux.
Three ways to change the flux (and so induce an EMF): change the field strength $B$, change the area $A$ inside the loop, or rotate the loop to change the angle $\theta$. Generators use the third; transformers use the first.

Faraday's Law of Induction

The induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage.

$$\varepsilon = -N\frac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t}$$
  • $N$ — number of turns; $\Delta\Phi/\Delta t$ — rate of change of flux (Wb s⁻¹).
  • Bigger induced EMF from: more turns, stronger field change, or faster change.
  • The minus sign is Lenz's law (next slide) — it fixes the direction, not the size.

Worked example

A 200-turn coil experiences a flux change of $3.0\times10^{-3}$ Wb in 0.20 s. Find the induced EMF.

$\varepsilon = N\dfrac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t} = 200\times\dfrac{3.0\times10^{-3}}{0.20} = \mathbf{3.0\ \text{V}}$

Lenz's Law — the Direction of the Induced Current

The induced current always flows in the direction that opposes the change producing it.

  • Push a north pole toward a coil → the coil's near face becomes a north pole to repel it (opposing the approach).
  • Pull the magnet away → the coil's near face becomes a south pole to attract it (opposing the retreat).
  • Either way, you must do work against the opposition — that work becomes the electrical energy induced.

Lenz's law is energy conservation

If the induced current helped the change instead of opposing it, you'd get free energy — a perpetual motion machine. The minus sign in Faraday's law guarantees the induced effect always resists its cause, conserving energy.

The negative sign matters. $\varepsilon = -N\Delta\Phi/\Delta t$: the magnitude comes from Faraday's law; the minus sign (Lenz) tells you the induced current opposes the flux change that created it.

Why It Matters: Generators & Transformers

Generator

A coil rotated in a magnetic field continually changes its flux ($\theta$ changes), inducing an alternating EMF. This is how nearly all electricity is generated — turbines (steam, water, wind) spin the coil.

Transformer

An alternating current in one coil creates a changing flux in a shared iron core, inducing an EMF in a second coil. Lets us step voltage up/down for efficient power transmission.

The motor–generator duality

A motor uses current + field to make motion (B.5.7). A generator uses motion + field to make current (B.5.8). They are the same device run in opposite directions — electricity and magnetism are two sides of one phenomenon.

Everyday induction: wireless phone charging, induction cooktops, electric guitar pickups, metal detectors, and regenerative braking in electric cars all rely on a changing flux inducing an EMF.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Explain [3]

(a) A bar magnet is moved into a coil connected to a galvanometer. Explain why a current is induced, and why no current flows when the magnet is held stationary inside the coil.

As the magnet moves in, the magnetic flux through the coil changes; by Faraday's law a changing flux induces an EMF, which drives a current [1]. The size of the EMF depends on the rate of change of flux [1]. When the magnet is stationary the flux is constant, so $\Delta\Phi/\Delta t = 0$ and no EMF (and no current) is induced [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A single loop of area 0.020 m² sits perpendicular to a field that changes from 0.10 T to 0.40 T in 0.15 s. Calculate the induced EMF.

$\Delta\Phi = A\,\Delta B = 0.020\times(0.40-0.10) = 6.0\times10^{-3}$ Wb [1]
$\varepsilon = N\dfrac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t} = 1\times\dfrac{6.0\times10^{-3}}{0.15}$ [1]
$= \mathbf{0.040\ \text{V}}$ [1]
State & Justify [2]

(c) State Lenz's law and explain how it is a consequence of the conservation of energy.

Lenz's law: the induced current flows in the direction that opposes the change in flux that produced it [1]. If it instead reinforced the change, the effect would grow without any energy input, creating energy from nothing; opposing the change means work must be done against the induced effect, and that work is converted into the electrical energy — consistent with conservation of energy [1].
Suggest [2]

(d) Suggest two ways to increase the EMF induced when a magnet is moved into a coil.

Move the magnet faster, increasing the rate of change of flux $\Delta\Phi/\Delta t$ [1]; and use a coil with more turns $N$ (or a stronger magnet / larger coil area), both of which increase the flux linkage change and hence the induced EMF [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Induction: a changing magnetic flux induces an EMF — no change, no EMF.
  • Magnetic flux: $\Phi = BA\cos\theta$ (webers); flux linkage $= N\Phi$.
  • Faraday's law: $\varepsilon = -N\dfrac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t}$ — EMF ∝ rate of change of flux linkage.
  • Lenz's law: the induced current opposes the change producing it (the minus sign) — energy conservation.
  • Change the flux via field, area, or angle — generators rotate the coil; transformers change the field.
  • Motor ↔ generator are the same device reversed: electricity and magnetism unified.
