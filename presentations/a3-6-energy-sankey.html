Forms of Energy

Energy exists in many forms. Every device converts energy from one form to another:

Kinetic

Moving objects

Gravitational PE

Height above ground

Elastic PE

Stretched/compressed

Thermal

Heat (internal energy)

Chemical

Food, fuel, batteries

Electrical

Current in circuits

Nuclear

Binding energy of nuclei

Radiant (Light)

Electromagnetic waves

Sound

Vibrations in medium

Energy transfer = energy moving between objects (e.g., heat from fire to pot). Energy transformation = energy changing form (e.g., chemical → thermal in combustion). IB uses both terms.

Energy Chains

Every device can be described as an energy chain — input → useful output + wasted output:

Light bulb (incandescent)

Electrical → Light (5%) + Heat (95%)

Car engine

Chemical (fuel) → Kinetic (25%) + Heat (65%) + Sound (10%)

Solar panel

Radiant (sunlight) → Electrical (20%) + Heat (80%)

Coal power station

Chemical → Thermal → Kinetic (turbine) → Electrical (35%) + Heat (65%)

Every chain: input energy = useful output + wasted output. The total ALWAYS balances — conservation of energy. The ratio of useful/total = efficiency.

What is a Sankey Diagram?

A Sankey diagram is a flow diagram where the width of each arrow is proportional to the energy it represents.

Rules for Sankey diagrams:
1. Input arrow enters from the left
2. Useful output continues to the right
3. Wasted energy branches off (usually downward)
4. Arrow WIDTH is proportional to energy amount
5. Total output widths = input width (conservation)

Drawing a Sankey Diagram — Step by Step

Example: A coal power station receives 1000 MJ of chemical energy. It produces 350 MJ of electrical energy. The rest is wasted as heat.
1Find all values: Input = 1000 MJ. Useful = 350 MJ. Wasted = 1000 - 350 = 650 MJ.
2Choose a scale: e.g., 1 cm width = 100 MJ. Input arrow = 10 cm wide.
3Draw input arrow from the left, 10 cm wide.
4Split into outputs: Useful arrow continues right (3.5 cm wide). Wasted branches down (6.5 cm wide).
5Label everything: Values, forms of energy, percentages.
IB exam: They may ask you to draw a Sankey diagram OR read one. For reading: measure arrow widths relative to each other to find percentages. The wider the waste arrow, the less efficient the device.

Reading a Sankey Diagram 5/10

From this Sankey diagram, determine: (a) the input energy (b) each output (c) the efficiency.
aInput = 200 J
bLight = 1/10 × 200 = 20 J. Heat = 7/10 × 200 = 140 J. Sound = 2/10 × 200 = 40 J.
cIf light is useful: $\eta = 20/200 = \mathbf{10\%}$ (like an incandescent bulb)
Check: 20 + 140 + 40 = 200 J ✓. Outputs always sum to input — if they don't, you've made an error.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Context: A hydroelectric power station converts GPE of water into electrical energy. 500 MJ of GPE is available per second. The turbine converts 90% of GPE to KE. The generator converts 95% of KE to electrical energy.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the KE output of the turbine per second.

$KE = 0.90 \times 500 = \mathbf{450 \text{ MJ}}$ [1]. Wasted = 50 MJ as heat/friction [1].
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the electrical power output of the generator.

$P_{elec} = 0.95 \times 450 = \mathbf{427.5 \text{ MW}}$ [1]. (per second = power) [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) Calculate the overall efficiency of the power station.

$\eta = \frac{427.5}{500} \times 100 = \mathbf{85.5\%}$ [1]
Or: $0.90 \times 0.95 = 0.855 = 85.5\%$ (multiply individual efficiencies) [1]
Draw [3]

(d) Draw a Sankey diagram for this power station showing all energy flows.

Input: 500 MJ (GPE) → Turbine → 450 MJ KE [1] + 50 MJ heat [1]
450 MJ KE → Generator → 427.5 MJ electrical + 22.5 MJ heat [1]
Total waste = 50 + 22.5 = 72.5 MJ. Useful = 427.5 MJ. Check: 427.5 + 72.5 = 500 ✓
Explain [2]

(e) Explain why the overall efficiency is the product of the individual efficiencies, not the average.

Each stage processes the OUTPUT of the previous stage, not the original input [1]. The second stage can only convert what the first stage gives it. So losses compound multiplicatively: $\eta_{total} = \eta_1 \times \eta_2$, not $(\eta_1 + \eta_2)/2$ [1].

Practice Questions

State [2] Easy

List the energy transformations in a battery-powered torch.

Chemical (battery) → Electrical (circuit) → Light (useful) + Heat (wasted in bulb and wires) [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A machine has three stages with efficiencies 80%, 90%, and 70%. If the input is 1000 J, how much useful energy comes out?

$\eta_{total} = 0.80 \times 0.90 \times 0.70 = 0.504 = 50.4\%$ [1]
$E_{out} = 0.504 \times 1000 = \mathbf{504 \text{ J}}$ [1]
496 J wasted across all three stages [1].
Draw [3] Medium

A 60 W incandescent bulb converts 5% of electrical energy to light. Draw a Sankey diagram and calculate the heat output per second.

Input: 60 J/s electrical. Light: $0.05 \times 60 = 3$ J/s [1].
Heat: $60 - 3 = 57$ J/s [1].
Sankey: 60 J input arrow → thin 3 J light arrow right + thick 57 J heat arrow down [1].
Explain [2] Hard

Why can't we ever achieve 100% efficiency in a thermal power station, even in theory?

The 2nd law of thermodynamics states that heat cannot be completely converted to work [1]. Some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir (cooling tower/environment). The maximum theoretical efficiency is the Carnot efficiency: $\eta = 1 - T_{cold}/T_{hot}$, which is always < 100% [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Energy forms: kinetic, GPE, elastic, thermal, chemical, electrical, nuclear, radiant, sound.
  • Every device: input → useful output + wasted output. Total always conserved.
  • Sankey diagrams: arrow width ∝ energy. Input from left, useful right, waste branches off.
  • Reading Sankeys: measure widths relative to input to find percentages.
  • Multi-stage efficiency: $\eta_{total} = \eta_1 \times \eta_2 \times \ldots$ (multiply, don't average).
  • Outputs must sum to input — if they don't, check your calculations.
  • 100% efficiency is impossible for heat engines (2nd law of thermodynamics).
