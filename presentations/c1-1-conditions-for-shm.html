C.1.1

Conditions for SHM & the Defining Equation

The physics of oscillation

From a swinging pendulum to a vibrating atom, an enormous range of systems oscillate in exactly the same way: simple harmonic motion. Here we pin down the two precise conditions that define SHM, the equation that captures them, and the meaning of angular frequency, period and amplitude.

Oscillations & Key Definitions

An oscillation is a repeated back-and-forth motion about an equilibrium position. Before SHM, fix the vocabulary:

  • Equilibrium position: where the object rests with no net force.
  • Displacement (x): distance from equilibrium, with direction. Zero at the centre.
  • Amplitude (x₀): the maximum displacement from equilibrium.
  • Period (T): time for one complete oscillation (seconds).
  • Frequency (f): oscillations per second (hertz). $f = 1/T$.
  • Angular frequency (ω): $\omega = 2\pi f = \dfrac{2\pi}{T}$ (rad s⁻¹).
Isochronous: a defining feature of SHM is that the period is independent of amplitude. A pendulum swinging through a small angle takes the same time whether the swing is large or small — this is what makes pendulum clocks keep time.

The Two Conditions for SHM

A system performs simple harmonic motion if and only if:

1. Acceleration is proportional to displacement

The bigger the displacement from equilibrium, the bigger the acceleration.

2. Acceleration is directed toward equilibrium

It always points back to the centre — a restoring acceleration (opposite to displacement).

The minus sign is everything. "Proportional to displacement and directed toward equilibrium" means acceleration and displacement always have opposite signs. Drop the restoring (toward-centre) condition and you don't have SHM.

The Defining Equation

Both conditions combine into a single equation — the mathematical definition of SHM:

$$a = -\omega^2 x$$
  • $a$ — acceleration; $x$ — displacement; $\omega$ — angular frequency (rad s⁻¹).
  • Proportionality ($a \propto x$) is shown by the constant $\omega^2$.
  • Restoring direction is shown by the minus sign ($a$ opposite to $x$).
  • The constant of proportionality is $\omega^2$ — so if you find it from a graph or force analysis, $\omega = \sqrt{|a/x|}$ and then $T = 2\pi/\omega$.

Where the restoring force comes from

Since $F = ma$, the condition is equivalently $F = -m\omega^2 x$ — a restoring force proportional to displacement. For a mass on a spring this is Hooke's law $F = -kx$, giving $\omega^2 = k/m$.

The Motion & Standard Systems

The equation $a = -\omega^2 x$ is solved by a sinusoid — displacement varies as sine or cosine of time:

$$x = x_0\sin(\omega t) \quad \text{or} \quad x = x_0\cos(\omega t)$$

Use sine when motion starts at equilibrium ($x = 0$ at $t = 0$); use cosine when it starts at maximum displacement.

Mass on a spring

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$

Larger mass → slower; stiffer spring → faster.

Simple pendulum

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

Depends on length & g only — not on mass or (small) amplitude.

Small-angle approximation: a pendulum is only truly SHM for small swings (roughly < 10°), where $\sin\theta \approx \theta$ makes the restoring force proportional to displacement. At large angles the motion is periodic but no longer simple harmonic.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — angular frequency and period

An object in SHM has an acceleration of 8.0 m s⁻² when its displacement is 0.020 m. Find ω and T.

$\omega^2 = |a/x| = 8.0/0.020 = 400$, so $\omega = 20$ rad s⁻¹.

$T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/20 = \mathbf{0.31\ \text{s}}$

Example 2 — spring system

A 0.25 kg mass on a spring of constant $k = 100$ N m⁻¹ oscillates in SHM. Find the period.

$T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.25/100} = 2\pi(0.050) = \mathbf{0.31\ \text{s}}$

Example 3 — max acceleration

A pendulum has amplitude 0.050 m and $\omega = 6.0$ rad s⁻¹. Find the maximum acceleration.

Max acceleration occurs at maximum displacement ($x = x_0$).

$a_{max} = \omega^2 x_0 = (6.0)^2\times0.050 = 36\times0.050 = \mathbf{1.8\ \text{m s}^{-2}}$

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

State [2]

(a) State the two conditions necessary for a body to undergo simple harmonic motion.

(1) The acceleration is directly proportional to the displacement from the equilibrium position [1]. (2) The acceleration is always directed toward the equilibrium position (i.e. it is a restoring acceleration, opposite in direction to the displacement) [1].
Explain [2]

(b) The defining equation for SHM is a = −ω²x. Explain the significance of the minus sign and the constant ω².

The minus sign shows that the acceleration is always in the opposite direction to the displacement — it is a restoring acceleration directed toward equilibrium [1]. The constant $\omega^2$ (always positive) shows the acceleration is directly proportional to the displacement, and its value fixes the angular frequency and hence the period $T = 2\pi/\omega$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A body moving with SHM has a period of 0.50 s. Calculate its angular frequency, and its acceleration when displaced 0.030 m from equilibrium.

$\omega = 2\pi/T = 2\pi/0.50 = 12.6$ rad s⁻¹ [1]
$a = \omega^2 x = (12.6)^2\times0.030$ [1]
$= 158\times0.030 = \mathbf{4.7\ \text{m s}^{-2}}$ (toward equilibrium) [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why a simple pendulum only performs SHM for small angular displacements.

SHM requires the restoring force (and acceleration) to be proportional to the displacement [1]. For a pendulum the restoring force is proportional to $\sin\theta$; only for small angles does $\sin\theta \approx \theta$, making the restoring force approximately proportional to displacement. At large angles this approximation fails, so the motion is periodic but no longer simple harmonic [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Two conditions: acceleration ∝ displacement, and directed toward equilibrium (restoring).
  • Defining equation: $a = -\omega^2 x$ — minus = restoring, $\omega^2$ = proportionality.
  • Angular frequency: $\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi/T$; solutions are $x = x_0\sin\omega t$ or $x_0\cos\omega t$.
  • Isochronous: period is independent of amplitude (for true SHM).
  • Spring: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$. Pendulum: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ (small angles only).
  • Max acceleration $= \omega^2 x_0$, occurring at maximum displacement.
