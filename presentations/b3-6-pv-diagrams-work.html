Work Done by a Gas

Reading Energy from Graphs

When a gas expands, it pushes a piston and does work on the surroundings. When compressed, work is done on the gas. A PV diagram captures the entire story — the area under the curve is the work done. This is where gas laws meet thermodynamics.

Work Done by an Expanding Gas

A gas at pressure $P$ pushing a piston of area $A$ through distance $\Delta x$:

$W = F \times \Delta x = PA \times \Delta x = P \times \Delta V$

$$W = P\Delta V$$

(for constant pressure processes)

Gas expands ($\Delta V > 0$)

$W > 0$ — gas does work ON surroundings

Gas pushes piston out

Gas compressed ($\Delta V < 0$)

$W < 0$ — work done ON the gas

Piston pushes gas in

For non-constant pressure: $W = \int P \, dV$ — the area under the P-V curve. This is why PV diagrams are so powerful: the work is always the area, regardless of the path shape.
First Law of Thermodynamics: $Q = \Delta U + W$. Heat added to gas = change in internal energy + work done by gas. This connects heat, internal energy, and work in one equation.

First Law of Thermodynamics on PV Diagrams

$$Q = \Delta U + W$$

Heat added = change in internal energy + work done by gas

Isobaric expansion

$W = P\Delta V$ (from graph area)

$\Delta U = \frac{3}{2}nR\Delta T$ (T rises → U rises)

$Q = \Delta U + W$ — heat goes into BOTH work and U

Isothermal expansion

$\Delta U = 0$ (T constant → KE constant)

$W =$ area under hyperbola

$Q = W$ — ALL heat becomes work

Isochoric heating

$W = 0$ (no volume change)

$\Delta U = \frac{3}{2}nR\Delta T$

$Q = \Delta U$ — ALL heat goes into internal energy

Adiabatic expansion

$Q = 0$ (no heat exchange)

$\Delta U = -W$ (gas cools as it does work)

$0 = \Delta U + W$ → work comes from internal energy → T drops

Adiabatic curves are steeper than isothermal on PV diagrams because the gas cools as it expands (losing internal energy), so pressure drops faster than it would at constant temperature.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Isobaric expansion

A gas at 200 kPa expands from 2.0 L to 5.0 L at constant pressure. Find the work done.

$W = P\Delta V = 200 \times 10^3 \times (5.0 - 2.0) \times 10^{-3} = 200000 \times 0.003 = \mathbf{600 \text{ J}}$

Example 2: First law application

In the above expansion, 1500 J of heat is added. Find the change in internal energy.

$Q = \Delta U + W$ → $1500 = \Delta U + 600$ → $\Delta U = \mathbf{900 \text{ J}}$

900 J goes into heating the gas (increasing molecular KE), 600 J goes into pushing the piston.

Example 3: Cyclic process

A gas undergoes a cycle returning to its initial state. $\Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0$ (returns to same T). Net work = enclosed area on PV diagram.

$Q_{\text{net}} = W_{\text{net}}$ — all net heat input becomes net work output. This is the principle behind heat engines.

Cycles: For any closed cycle on a PV diagram, $\Delta U = 0$ (same state). Net work = area enclosed by the loop. Clockwise = engine (gas does net work). Counterclockwise = refrigerator (work done on gas).

Thermodynamic Cycles

Example: Rectangular cycle A→B→C→D→A

$P_{\text{high}} = 300$ kPa, $P_{\text{low}} = 100$ kPa, $V_1 = 1.0$ L, $V_2 = 3.0$ L

$W_{\text{net}} = (P_H - P_L)(V_2 - V_1) = (300-100) \times 10^3 \times (3.0-1.0) \times 10^{-3} = \mathbf{400 \text{ J}}$

Heat engines (car engines, power plants) operate in clockwise cycles on PV diagrams. They absorb heat from a hot source, do work (enclosed area), and reject waste heat to a cold sink. Efficiency = $W_{\text{net}} / Q_{\text{in}}$. The Carnot efficiency $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H$ is the maximum possible.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Calculate [2]

(a) A gas expands at constant pressure of 150 kPa from 0.010 m³ to 0.030 m³. Calculate the work done by the gas.

$W = P\Delta V = 150 \times 10^3 \times (0.030 - 0.010)$ [1]
$W = 150000 \times 0.020 = \mathbf{3000 \text{ J} = 3.0 \text{ kJ}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(b) During the expansion in (a), 5.0 kJ of heat is added to the gas. Calculate the change in internal energy and state whether the temperature increases or decreases.

$Q = \Delta U + W$ → $5000 = \Delta U + 3000$ [1]
$\Delta U = \mathbf{2000 \text{ J} = 2.0 \text{ kJ}}$ [1]
Since $\Delta U > 0$, the internal energy increased, meaning the temperature increased [1].
Explain [2]

(c) An ideal gas undergoes an isothermal expansion. Explain why the internal energy does not change.

For an ideal gas, internal energy depends only on temperature: $U = \frac{3}{2}nRT$. In an isothermal process, T is constant, so $\Delta T = 0$ and therefore $\Delta U = 0$ [1]. All heat added goes entirely into work done by the gas: $Q = W$ [1].
Explain [2]

(d) On a PV diagram, explain what the enclosed area of a complete cycle represents. State the direction (clockwise or counterclockwise) for a heat engine.

The enclosed area represents the net work done by the gas during one complete cycle [1]. A clockwise cycle represents a heat engine — the gas does more work during expansion (at higher pressure) than is done on it during compression (at lower pressure), producing net work output [1].

Key Takeaways

  • $W = P\Delta V$ at constant pressure. General: $W =$ area under PV curve.
  • Expansion ($\Delta V > 0$): gas does positive work. Compression: work done on gas.
  • First Law: $Q = \Delta U + W$ — heat = internal energy change + work.
  • Isobaric: horizontal line, $W = P\Delta V$, both $\Delta U$ and $W$ nonzero.
  • Isochoric: vertical line, $W = 0$, all heat → $\Delta U$.
  • Isothermal: hyperbola, $\Delta U = 0$, all heat → work ($Q = W$).
  • Adiabatic: steeper than isothermal, $Q = 0$, work from internal energy ($\Delta U = -W$).
  • Cycles: enclosed area = net work. Clockwise = engine. $\Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0$.
