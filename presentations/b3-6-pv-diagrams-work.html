B.3.6

PV Diagrams & Work Done by a Gas

Reading energy off a graph

A PV diagram turns a gas process into a picture — and the area under the curve is the work done. Master this and you can read isobaric, isochoric, isothermal and adiabatic changes at a glance, and calculate the net work of a full thermodynamic cycle from its enclosed area.

Work Done When a Gas Expands

A gas pushing a piston out through distance $\Delta x$ does work. With force $F = PA$ and displacement $\Delta x$:

$$W = F\,\Delta x = (PA)\,\Delta x = P\,\Delta V$$
$$W = P\,\Delta V \quad (\text{constant } P)$$

Gas expands (ΔV > 0)

The gas does positive work on its surroundings. Energy leaves the gas. $W_{by\ gas} > 0$.

Gas compressed (ΔV < 0)

Work is done on the gas by the surroundings. Energy enters the gas. $W_{by\ gas} < 0$.

Sign convention matters. IB uses "work done by the gas." When you meet the first law $Q = \Delta U + W$, that $W$ is the work done by the gas — positive for expansion. Always state which convention you are using.
General case: if pressure varies, $W = \displaystyle\int P\,dV$ — which is exactly the area under the curve on a PV diagram.

The Area Under the Curve is the Work

  • Isobaric (horizontal line): the area is a simple rectangle, $W = P\Delta V$.
  • Any curve: $W$ = area between the curve and the V-axis. Count squares or integrate.
  • Direction matters: moving right (expansion) → $W$ positive; moving left (compression) → $W$ negative.
  • Isothermal work (HL, given if needed): $W = nRT\ln\!\dfrac{V_2}{V_1}$.
Exam skill: when a PV graph is drawn to scale on grid paper, "estimate the work done" simply means count the squares under the curve and multiply by the value of one square (in Pa × m³ = J).

Cycles: Net Work = Enclosed Area

A closed loop on a PV diagram is a cycle — the gas returns to its start. The net work per cycle equals the area enclosed by the loop.

Direction sets the sign

  • Clockwise loop → net work done by the gas is positive → this is a heat engine (heat in, work out).
  • Anticlockwise loop → net work is negative (work done on the gas) → a refrigerator / heat pump.
  • Over a full cycle $\Delta U = 0$ (gas returns to the same state), so by the first law $Q_{net} = W_{net}$.
Physical meaning: the enclosed area is the useful work an engine extracts each cycle. A "fatter" loop means more work per cycle.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — isobaric expansion

A gas at constant pressure 200 kPa expands from 2.0 L to 5.0 L. Find the work done by the gas.

$\Delta V = (5.0 - 2.0)\times10^{-3} = 3.0\times10^{-3}$ m³.

$W = P\Delta V = (2.0\times10^{5})(3.0\times10^{-3}) = \mathbf{600\ \text{J}}$ (done by the gas)

Example 2 — isochoric heating

A sealed rigid container of gas is heated, raising its pressure from 100 kPa to 180 kPa. Find the work done.

Volume is fixed → $\Delta V = 0$ → $W = P\Delta V = \mathbf{0\ \text{J}}$. All heat added goes to internal energy.

Example 3 — net work of a cycle

A rectangular cycle on a PV diagram spans ΔP = 150 kPa and ΔV = 4.0 L. Find the net work per cycle.

Net W = enclosed area $= \Delta P \times \Delta V = (1.5\times10^{5})(4.0\times10^{-3}) = \mathbf{600\ \text{J}}$ per cycle

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Define & State [2]

(a) State what the area under a curve on a pressure-volume diagram represents, and how its sign is determined.

The area under the curve (between the curve and the volume axis) represents the work done by the gas [1]. If the process moves to larger volume (expansion) the work done by the gas is positive; if it moves to smaller volume (compression) the work done by the gas is negative [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A gas expands isobarically at 250 kPa from 0.010 m³ to 0.030 m³. Calculate the work done by the gas and state the energy change if 6.0 kJ of heat is supplied. (Take W as work done by gas, Q = ΔU + W.)

$W = P\Delta V = (2.5\times10^{5})(0.030 - 0.010) = (2.5\times10^{5})(0.020) = 5.0\times10^{3}$ J = 5.0 kJ [1]
First law: $\Delta U = Q - W = 6.0 - 5.0 = \mathbf{1.0\ \text{kJ}}$ [1]
So internal energy increases by 1.0 kJ; the rest of the heat became work [1].
Explain [2]

(c) Explain why the work done during an isochoric (constant volume) process is zero, even when heat is supplied.

Work done by a gas requires a change in volume ($W = P\Delta V$ or the area under the PV curve) [1]. In an isochoric process $\Delta V = 0$, so no area is swept and $W = 0$; all the heat supplied goes into raising the internal energy (temperature) of the gas [1].
Analyse [3]

(d) A gas undergoes a clockwise cycle on a PV diagram enclosing an area of 450 J. State the net work per cycle, whether the device is an engine or a refrigerator, and the net heat exchanged per cycle.

Net work done by the gas per cycle = enclosed area = 450 J [1]. A clockwise loop means net positive work is done by the gas, so the device is a heat engine [1]. Over a complete cycle $\Delta U = 0$, so by the first law $Q_{net} = W_{net} = \mathbf{450\ \text{J}}$ (net heat input equals net work output) [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Work by a gas: $W = P\Delta V$ at constant pressure; in general $W = \int P\,dV$ = area under the PV curve.
  • Signs: expansion → gas does positive work; compression → work done on the gas (negative).
  • Isobaric: $W = P\Delta V$ (rectangle). Isochoric: $W = 0$ (vertical line, no area).
  • Isothermal: $\Delta U = 0$, so $Q = W$; area under a hyperbola. Adiabatic: $Q = 0$, so $W = -\Delta U$.
  • Cycles: net work per cycle = enclosed area. Clockwise = engine; anticlockwise = refrigerator.
  • Over a cycle $\Delta U = 0$, so $Q_{net} = W_{net}$.
