Torque

Moment of a Force

Every time you open a door, turn a wrench, or spin a wheel — you're applying torque. It's not just about how hard you push, but where and at what angle you push. Torque is the rotational equivalent of force.

What is Torque?

Torque (τ) is the turning effect of a force about a pivot point (axis of rotation).

$$\tau = rF\sin\theta$$

Only the perpendicular component of force creates torque. A force directed along the lever arm (through the pivot) produces zero torque, no matter how large it is.
Units: Torque is measured in N·m (newton-metres). Do NOT write "Nm" or confuse with joules — although both are kg·m²·s⁻², torque is not energy. IB expects "N m" or "N·m".

The Moment Arm (Lever Arm)

An equivalent way to think about torque: τ = F × d, where d is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force.

Two equivalent formulations:

1. $\tau = rF\sin\theta$ — resolve force into perpendicular component

2. $\tau = Fd$ — use perpendicular distance $d = r\sin\theta$ (moment arm)

Both give exactly the same answer. Use whichever is easier for the geometry.

Maximum torque occurs when $\theta = 90°$ (force perpendicular to lever arm), giving $\tau = rF$. This is why you push a door at right angles, far from the hinge.
Zero torque occurs when $\theta = 0°$ or $180°$ — force is parallel to lever arm, pushing through the pivot. Also zero when $r = 0$ (force applied at the pivot).
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.1 TorqueSign Convention

Direction of Torque

Torque is a vector quantity. In 2D problems, we assign signs to distinguish clockwise from counterclockwise.

IB convention: Counterclockwise (CCW) = positive, Clockwise (CW) = negative. Always state your convention clearly in exams.

Net Torque

When multiple forces act: $\sum\tau = \tau_1 + \tau_2 + \tau_3 + \ldots$

If $\sum\tau = 0$, the object is in rotational equilibrium (not rotating, or rotating at constant angular velocity). This leads directly to A.4.2.

3D torque: In 3D, torque is defined as a cross product: $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$. Direction is given by the right-hand rule. IB HL uses this at a basic level — curl fingers from $\vec{r}$ toward $\vec{F}$, thumb points along $\vec{\tau}$.

Couples

A couple is a pair of equal and opposite forces whose lines of action do not coincide. They produce pure rotation with no translational motion.

$$\tau_{\text{couple}} = Fd$$

where $d$ = perpendicular distance between the two lines of action

Key properties of a couple:

  • Net force = 0 → no translation, only rotation
  • Torque is the same about ANY point — you can choose any pivot
  • Examples: turning a steering wheel, turning a tap, winding a clock
IB exam: "Distinguish between a single force and a couple." A single force causes both translation and rotation. A couple causes pure rotation with zero net force.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.1 TorqueWorked Examples

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Example 1: Wrench on a bolt

A mechanic applies a 40 N force to a 0.30 m wrench at 60° to the handle. Calculate the torque.

Step 1: Identify: $r = 0.30$ m, $F = 40$ N, $\theta = 60°$

Step 2: $\tau = rF\sin\theta = 0.30 \times 40 \times \sin 60°$

Step 3: $\tau = 0.30 \times 40 \times 0.866 = \mathbf{10.4 \text{ N·m}}$

Example 2: Door

A 15 N force is applied perpendicular to a door, 0.80 m from the hinge. Another person pushes with 25 N perpendicular to the door, 0.50 m from the hinge, in the opposite direction. Find the net torque.

Step 1: Both forces are perpendicular ($\theta = 90°$, $\sin 90° = 1$)

Step 2: $\tau_1 = 0.80 \times 15 = +12.0$ N·m (CCW)  |  $\tau_2 = 0.50 \times 25 = -12.5$ N·m (CW)

Step 3: $\sum\tau = 12.0 + (-12.5) = \mathbf{-0.5 \text{ N·m}}$ → Net CW rotation (second person wins)

Exam tip: Always define your sign convention first, then apply it consistently. Show the direction (CW/CCW) of each torque for full marks.

Torque in the Real World

Why are door handles far from hinges?

Larger $r$ → more torque for the same force. A handle near the hinge would need enormous force to open the door.

Why do long wrenches loosen stuck bolts?

A longer wrench = larger $r$ = more torque with the same force. Mechanics use extension bars for stubborn bolts.

Why push perpendicular to the door?

$\sin 90° = 1$ maximises torque. Pushing at a shallow angle wastes most of your force — $\sin 10° = 0.17$, only 17% effective.

Engine torque specs

Car engines are rated in N·m. A sports car producing 500 N·m has twice the turning effect of one at 250 N·m on the crankshaft.

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

A uniform beam of length 2.0 m and weight 50 N is pivoted at its left end. A 30 N force acts downward at the free end, and a 20 N force acts upward at 0.8 m from the pivot.

Define [1]

(a) Define torque.

Torque is the product of a force and the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force. / $\tau = rF\sin\theta$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(b) Calculate the net torque about the pivot. Take counterclockwise as positive.

Weight acts at centre (1.0 m from pivot): $\tau_W = -(50 \times 1.0) = -50$ N·m (CW) [1]
30 N at 2.0 m: $\tau_1 = -(30 \times 2.0) = -60$ N·m (CW) [1]
20 N upward at 0.8 m: $\tau_2 = +(20 \times 0.8) = +16$ N·m (CCW) [1]
$\sum\tau = -50 + (-60) + 16 = \mathbf{-94 \text{ N·m}}$ (net clockwise)
Explain [2]

(c) Explain why a longer spanner requires less force to produce the same torque.

$\tau = rF\sin\theta$. For the same torque, if $r$ increases, $F$ must decrease proportionally [1]. A longer spanner increases the moment arm, so less force is needed to achieve the same turning effect [1].
Calculate [2]

(d) A couple consists of two 12 N forces. The perpendicular distance between them is 0.25 m. Calculate the torque of the couple.

$\tau = Fd = 12 \times 0.25$ [1]
$\tau = \mathbf{3.0 \text{ N·m}}$ [1]

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

A force of 60 N acts on a 0.45 m lever arm at 35° to the arm. Calculate the torque about the pivot.

$\tau = rF\sin\theta = 0.45 \times 60 \times \sin 35°$ [1]
$\tau = 0.45 \times 60 \times 0.574$ [1]
$\tau = \mathbf{15.5 \text{ N·m}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

A see-saw has a 40 kg child sitting 2.5 m from the pivot. How far from the pivot must a 60 kg child sit to balance the see-saw?

For balance: $\sum\tau = 0$, so $\tau_1 = \tau_2$ [1]
$m_1 g r_1 = m_2 g r_2$ → $g$ cancels: $40 \times 2.5 = 60 \times r_2$ [1]
$r_2 = \frac{100}{60} = \mathbf{1.67 \text{ m}}$ [1]
Explain [3]

A uniform metre rule is balanced at the 50 cm mark. A 2.0 N weight is hung at the 80 cm mark. Where must a 5.0 N weight be placed to restore balance?

Pivot is at 50 cm. The 2.0 N weight is at $r_1 = 80 - 50 = 30$ cm = 0.30 m from pivot [1].
For balance: $2.0 \times 0.30 = 5.0 \times r_2$ [1]
$r_2 = \frac{0.60}{5.0} = 0.12$ m = 12 cm from pivot → at the 38 cm mark (on the opposite side) [1].
Connection to A.4.2: When $\sum\tau = 0$ AND $\sum F = 0$, the object is in static equilibrium — no translation and no rotation. This is the foundation of structural engineering and every bridge, building, and crane.

Key Takeaways

  • Torque = turning effect of a force: $\tau = rF\sin\theta$ or $\tau = Fd$ (moment arm)
  • Units: N·m — not joules. Torque is a vector (direction matters).
  • Maximum torque when $\theta = 90°$. Zero when force acts through pivot or parallel to lever arm.
  • Sign convention: CCW = positive, CW = negative (state this in exams).
  • Couple: Two equal, opposite, non-coincident forces. $\tau = Fd$. Pure rotation, no translation.
  • Net torque: $\sum\tau = 0$ for rotational equilibrium.
  • Increase torque by: increasing force, increasing distance from pivot, or pushing at 90°.
