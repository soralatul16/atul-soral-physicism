Moment of Inertia

The Rotational Equivalent of Mass

Mass tells you how hard it is to accelerate an object in a straight line. Moment of inertia tells you how hard it is to spin it. Same mass, different shape? Completely different resistance to rotation.

A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.3 Moment of InertiaConcept

What is Moment of Inertia?

Moment of inertia (I) measures how much a body resists angular acceleration. It depends on both mass and how that mass is distributed relative to the axis of rotation.

$$I = \sum m_i r_i^2$$

Sum of each particle's mass × (distance from axis)²

The $r^2$ matters enormously. Double the distance from the axis → 4× the contribution to I. Mass far from the axis dominates the moment of inertia.
Units: $I$ is measured in kg·m². It is always positive and always depends on the chosen axis.

Linear ↔ Rotational Analogies

Every linear quantity has a rotational counterpart. Moment of inertia plays the role of mass:

LINEARROTATIONALSWAP
Displacement $s$↔Angular displacement $\theta$$s = r\theta$
Velocity $v$↔Angular velocity $\omega$$v = r\omega$
Acceleration $a$↔Angular acceleration $\alpha$$a = r\alpha$
Mass $m$↔Moment of inertia $I$$I = \sum mr^2$
Force $F$↔Torque $\tau$$\tau = rF\sin\theta$
$F = ma$↔$\tau = I\alpha$Newton's 2nd (rotation)
Momentum $p = mv$↔Angular momentum $L = I\omega$
KE $= \frac{1}{2}mv^2$↔Rotational KE $= \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$
Master this table. Every rotational equation is the linear equation with $m \to I$, $v \to \omega$, $a \to \alpha$, $F \to \tau$, $p \to L$. If you know the linear versions, you know the rotational ones.

Calculating I for Point Masses

For discrete masses, add up each contribution: $I = m_1 r_1^2 + m_2 r_2^2 + m_3 r_3^2 + \ldots$

Example 1: Two masses on a rod

A 2.0 kg mass and a 3.0 kg mass are connected by a light rod. The axis passes through the 2.0 kg mass. Distance between masses = 0.50 m.

$I = m_1 r_1^2 + m_2 r_2^2 = 2.0 \times 0^2 + 3.0 \times 0.50^2 = 0 + 0.75 = \mathbf{0.75 \text{ kg·m}^2}$

Example 2: Same system, axis at the centre

Now the axis passes through the midpoint (0.25 m from each mass).

$I = 2.0 \times 0.25^2 + 3.0 \times 0.25^2 = 0.125 + 0.1875 = \mathbf{0.3125 \text{ kg·m}^2}$

Same masses, different axis = different I. Axis through the centre gives lower I (easier to spin). Axis at one end gives higher I. This is why figure skaters spin faster when they pull their arms in — they reduce $r$, which reduces $I$.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.3 Moment of InertiaStandard Shapes

Moment of Inertia for Common Shapes

These are given in the IB data booklet. You do not need to derive them, but you must know how to use them.

Point mass

$I = mR^2$

Thin ring / hoop

$I = mR^2$

Solid disc / cylinder

$I = \frac{1}{2}mR^2$

Solid sphere

$I = \frac{2}{5}mR^2$

Hollow sphere (shell)

$I = \frac{2}{3}mR^2$

Thin rod (centre)

$I = \frac{1}{12}mL^2$

Pattern: Hollow shapes have higher I than solid shapes of the same mass and radius. All mass is at maximum r in a hollow shape. Hoop: $mR^2$ > Disc: $\frac{1}{2}mR^2$ > Sphere: $\frac{2}{5}mR^2$.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.3 Moment of InertiaParallel Axis Theorem

Parallel Axis Theorem

If you know I about the centre of mass, you can find I about any parallel axis:

$$I = I_{\text{CM}} + Md^2$$
$I_{\text{CM}}$ = moment of inertia about the centre-of-mass axis
$d$ = distance between the two parallel axes

Example: Rod about its end

For a thin rod: $I_{\text{CM}} = \frac{1}{12}mL^2$, and $d = \frac{L}{2}$ (centre to end).

$I_{\text{end}} = \frac{1}{12}mL^2 + m\left(\frac{L}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{12}mL^2 + \frac{1}{4}mL^2 = \mathbf{\frac{1}{3}mL^2}$

I about the end is 4× larger than I about the centre for a rod. Moving the axis away from the CM always increases I. The CM axis always gives the minimum moment of inertia.
IB note: The parallel axis theorem is in the data booklet and is often used in multi-part problems. You'll commonly combine it with standard shapes — e.g., a disc rotating about its edge.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.3 Moment of InertiaComposite Systems

Composite Bodies

For a system of rigid bodies sharing the same axis: add their individual moments of inertia.

$$I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2 + I_3 + \ldots$$

Example: Disc with point mass on rim

A solid disc (mass 4.0 kg, radius 0.30 m) has a 0.50 kg mass attached to its rim. Find I about the disc's centre.

Disc: $I_{\text{disc}} = \frac{1}{2}mR^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 4.0 \times 0.30^2 = 0.18$ kg·m²

Point mass at rim: $I_{\text{point}} = mR^2 = 0.50 \times 0.30^2 = 0.045$ kg·m²

Total: $I = 0.18 + 0.045 = \mathbf{0.225 \text{ kg·m}^2}$

Key rule: Moments of inertia always ADD (they're scalars, always positive). Unlike forces or torques, you never subtract them. If a system has multiple parts, find I for each about the same axis, then sum.

Real-world example: Why do figure skaters spin faster with arms pulled in?

Arms extended: large r → large I → slow spin. Arms pulled in: small r → small I → fast spin. Angular momentum $L = I\omega$ is conserved (no external torque), so when I decreases, $\omega$ must increase.

Define [1]

(a) Define moment of inertia.

Moment of inertia is the resistance of a body to angular acceleration / the sum of the products of each particle's mass and the square of its distance from the axis of rotation: $I = \sum m_i r_i^2$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) Three point masses are arranged on a light rod: 1.0 kg at the left end, 2.0 kg at the centre, and 3.0 kg at the right end. The rod is 1.0 m long. Calculate I about the left end.

$I = m_1 r_1^2 + m_2 r_2^2 + m_3 r_3^2$ [1]
$I = 1.0 \times 0^2 + 2.0 \times 0.50^2 + 3.0 \times 1.0^2$ [1]
$I = 0 + 0.50 + 3.0 = \mathbf{3.5 \text{ kg·m}^2}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) A solid disc of mass 5.0 kg and radius 0.20 m rotates about its centre. Calculate its moment of inertia.

$I = \frac{1}{2}mR^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 5.0 \times 0.20^2$ [1]
$I = \frac{1}{2} \times 5.0 \times 0.04 = \mathbf{0.10 \text{ kg·m}^2}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Two wheels have the same mass and radius. Wheel A is a solid disc and Wheel B is a hoop. Both start from rest and the same torque is applied. Explain which reaches a higher angular velocity after 5 seconds.

Wheel A (disc) has $I = \frac{1}{2}mR^2$ and Wheel B (hoop) has $I = mR^2$. Since $\tau = I\alpha$, the disc has a smaller I and therefore a larger angular acceleration for the same torque [1]. After 5 seconds, Wheel A has the higher angular velocity [1].
Calculate [3]

(e) Use the parallel axis theorem to find the moment of inertia of the disc in (c) about a point on its rim.

$I_{\text{rim}} = I_{\text{CM}} + Md^2 = \frac{1}{2}mR^2 + mR^2$ [1]
$I_{\text{rim}} = \frac{3}{2}mR^2 = \frac{3}{2} \times 5.0 \times 0.20^2$ [1]
$I_{\text{rim}} = \frac{3}{2} \times 0.20 = \mathbf{0.30 \text{ kg·m}^2}$ [1]

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

A solid sphere (mass 2.0 kg, radius 0.10 m) and a hollow sphere (mass 2.0 kg, radius 0.10 m) both rotate about their centres. Calculate the ratio $I_{\text{hollow}} : I_{\text{solid}}$.

$I_{\text{solid}} = \frac{2}{5}mR^2 = \frac{2}{5} \times 2.0 \times 0.01 = 0.008$ kg·m² [1]
$I_{\text{hollow}} = \frac{2}{3}mR^2 = \frac{2}{3} \times 2.0 \times 0.01 = 0.0133$ kg·m² [1]
Ratio = $\frac{2/3}{2/5} = \frac{5}{3} \approx \mathbf{1.67 : 1}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

A bicycle wheel can be modelled as a thin hoop of mass 1.2 kg and radius 0.35 m. It has 36 spokes, each of mass 0.005 kg and length 0.35 m (thin rods from centre). Calculate the total moment of inertia.

Hoop: $I = mR^2 = 1.2 \times 0.35^2 = 0.147$ kg·m² [1]
Each spoke (rod about end): $I = \frac{1}{3}mL^2 = \frac{1}{3} \times 0.005 \times 0.35^2 = 0.000204$ kg·m²
36 spokes: $36 \times 0.000204 = 0.00735$ kg·m² [1]
Total: $I = 0.147 + 0.00735 = \mathbf{0.154 \text{ kg·m}^2}$ [1]
Explain [2]

A ball rolls down a slope faster than a hollow cylinder of the same mass and radius. Explain why.

The solid ball has $I = \frac{2}{5}mR^2$ while the hollow cylinder has $I = mR^2$ — the ball has less rotational inertia [1]. For the same GPE, more energy goes into translational KE for the ball (less is "wasted" on rotation), so it has a higher linear speed at the bottom [1].
This connects to A.4.6: Objects with lower I (relative to $mR^2$) roll faster down inclines. Solid sphere beats solid cylinder beats hollow sphere beats hoop — always, regardless of mass or radius.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.3 Moment of InertiaSummary

Key Takeaways

  • $I = \sum mr^2$ — the rotational equivalent of mass. Depends on mass distribution, not just total mass.
  • $r^2$ dominance: mass far from the axis contributes disproportionately. Double r → 4× the contribution.
  • Standard shapes: Point/hoop: $mR^2$, Disc: $\frac{1}{2}mR^2$, Solid sphere: $\frac{2}{5}mR^2$, Shell: $\frac{2}{3}mR^2$, Rod (centre): $\frac{1}{12}mL^2$.
  • Hollow > Solid for same mass and radius — all mass is at maximum r.
  • Parallel axis theorem: $I = I_{\text{CM}} + Md^2$. Moving axis away from CM always increases I.
  • Composite bodies: I values add. Find I for each part about the same axis, then sum.
  • Analogy: $m \to I$ in every equation: $F=ma \to \tau=I\alpha$, $p=mv \to L=I\omega$, $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 \to \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$.
