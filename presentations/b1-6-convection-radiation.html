B.1.6

Convection & Radiation

The Other Two Ways Heat Moves

Conduction needs contact. Convection needs fluid. But radiation needs nothing at all — it crosses the vacuum of space to bring you sunlight from 150 million kilometres away. Together, these three mechanisms explain every thermal process in the universe.

Convection — Heat by Fluid Movement

Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the bulk movement of a fluid (liquid or gas). Hot fluid rises (less dense), cold fluid sinks (more dense), creating a circulation called a convection current.

Convection ONLY occurs in fluids (liquids and gases). It cannot happen in solids (particles can't move freely) or in a vacuum (no fluid). This is why there's no convection in space.
Natural vs Forced: Natural convection is driven by density differences (hot air rising). Forced convection uses a fan or pump (e.g., car radiator fan, hair dryer). Forced convection is faster.

Convection in Nature & Technology

Sea & Land Breezes

Day: Land heats faster → air rises over land → cooler air from sea flows in = sea breeze.

Night: Land cools faster → air rises over sea → land air flows out = land breeze.

Weather Systems

Sun heats equator more than poles. Warm air rises at equator, flows poleward, cools and sinks → global convection cells (Hadley, Ferrel, Polar) drive wind patterns.

Heating a Room

Radiators (misnamed!) heat by convection. Hot air rises from the radiator, circulates around the room, cools at windows/walls, sinks back down. Place radiators under windows to counter cold drafts.

Boiling Water

Water at the bottom heats, becomes less dense, rises. Cooler water sinks to take its place. This convection current distributes heat throughout the pot far faster than conduction alone.

Why heaters go at the bottom and AC at the top: Heaters heat air which rises — place them low so the current fills the room. AC cools air which sinks — place it high so cold air descends evenly.

Thermal Radiation — Heat Without Matter

Thermal radiation is the transfer of energy by electromagnetic waves (primarily infrared). It requires no medium — it works through a vacuum.

Key properties of thermal radiation

  • All objects above 0 K emit thermal radiation — you are radiating right now
  • Travels at the speed of light ($3 \times 10^8$ m/s)
  • No medium needed — how the Sun heats the Earth across 150 million km of vacuum
  • Hotter objects emit more radiation and at shorter wavelengths
  • Can be reflected, absorbed, or transmitted depending on surface properties

Good absorbers/emitters

Dark, matt (rough) surfaces. A black body is the ideal — absorbs all incident radiation.

Examples: black car bonnet, matte black paint, charcoal

Good reflectors (poor absorbers)

Light, shiny (smooth) surfaces. Reflect most radiation back — absorb very little.

Examples: aluminium foil, silver, white paint, mirror

Good absorbers = good emitters. A black body absorbs all radiation AND emits the most radiation at any temperature. A shiny surface reflects well but emits poorly. This is the same for both. (Kirchhoff's law of radiation.)

$T^4$ is extreme. Double the temperature → radiate 16× more power. Triple → 81×. A 6000 K star radiates $(6000/300)^4 = 160\,000\times$ more per unit area than a 300 K object. This is why hot objects glow.

Emissivity (e)

  • $e = 1$ — perfect black body (absorbs and emits maximally)
  • $e = 0$ — perfect reflector (emits nothing, reflects everything)
  • Real objects: $0 < e < 1$. Black paint ≈ 0.95, polished silver ≈ 0.02, human skin ≈ 0.98

Net Radiated Power

An object both emits AND absorbs radiation from its surroundings. The net power loss is:

$$P_{\text{net}} = e\sigma A(T^4 - T_{\text{env}}^4)$$

Only the temperature difference matters for net heat loss.

Example: Human body radiating

A person ($A = 1.7$ m², $e = 0.98$, skin $T = 33°C = 306$ K) stands in a room at 20°C (293 K).

$P_{\text{net}} = 0.98 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 1.7 \times (306^4 - 293^4)$

$306^4 = 8.76 \times 10^9$, $293^4 = 7.37 \times 10^9$, difference $= 1.39 \times 10^9$

$P_{\text{net}} = 0.98 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 1.7 \times 1.39 \times 10^9 = \mathbf{131 \text{ W}}$

You radiate ~130 W just by existing — roughly the power of a bright light bulb. This is why crowded rooms get warm.

If $T = T_{\text{env}}$: $P_{\text{net}} = 0$. The object emits exactly as much as it absorbs — thermal equilibrium via radiation. This is also why objects eventually reach room temperature.

In most real situations, all three act simultaneously. A hot cup of tea loses heat by: conduction (through the cup wall), convection (hot air rises from the surface), and radiation (infrared from the hot surface). The dominant mechanism depends on the situation.

Vacuum flask — defeats all three

Double wall with vacuum: no conduction or convection (no medium). Silver coating: reflects radiation, low emissivity. Stopper: prevents convection through the top. Result: keeps drinks hot (or cold) for hours.

Explain [2]

(a) Explain how a convection current is established when water is heated from below.

Water near the heat source gains energy, expands, and becomes less dense, so it rises due to buoyancy [1]. Cooler, denser water from above sinks to replace it. This continuous cycle of rising warm fluid and sinking cool fluid creates a convection current [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A black sphere ($e = 1.0$, $A = 0.050$ m²) is heated to 500°C. Calculate the power it radiates. ($\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}$ W m⁻² K⁻⁴)

$T = 500 + 273 = 773$ K [1]
$P = e\sigma AT^4 = 1.0 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 0.050 \times 773^4$ [1]
$773^4 = 3.57 \times 10^{11}$, $P = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 0.050 \times 3.57 \times 10^{11} = \mathbf{1012 \text{ W} \approx 1.0 \text{ kW}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) Explain why a vacuum flask is effective at keeping liquids hot, referring to all three heat transfer mechanisms.

The vacuum between the double walls eliminates conduction and convection (no medium for particles to transfer energy through) [1]. The silvered inner surfaces have very low emissivity, so they emit minimal thermal radiation and reflect most incident radiation back, reducing radiative heat loss [1].
Distinguish [2]

(d) State two differences between convection and radiation as methods of heat transfer.

Convection requires a fluid medium (liquid or gas), while radiation can travel through a vacuum [1]. Convection involves bulk movement of matter, while radiation transfers energy via electromagnetic waves without any matter movement [1].

Explain [3]

On a clear night, car windscreens sometimes frost over even when air temperature is above 0°C. Explain this using radiation.

The windscreen radiates thermal energy (infrared) to the cold, clear night sky [1]. On a clear night, there are no clouds to absorb and re-radiate this energy back, so the windscreen loses energy rapidly [1]. The windscreen's temperature drops below the surrounding air temperature and below 0°C, causing water vapour to freeze on its surface as frost [1].
Explain [2]

Explain why sea breezes blow from sea to land during the day.

During the day, land heats up faster than the sea (lower specific heat capacity). The air above the land becomes warmer, expands, becomes less dense, and rises [1]. Cooler, denser air above the sea flows inland to replace it, creating a sea breeze from sea to land [1].
Your body loses heat by all three: Conduction (~3%) through contact with chairs, floors. Convection (~15%) as warm air rises from your skin. Radiation (~40%) as infrared from your skin. Evaporation (~22%) from sweating. Breathing (~20%) exhaling warm, moist air.

Key Takeaways

  • Convection: bulk fluid movement. Hot rises (less dense), cold sinks. Only in fluids.
  • Radiation: electromagnetic waves (infrared). No medium needed. Works through vacuum.
  • Stefan-Boltzmann: $P = e\sigma AT^4$. Power ∝ $T^4$ — doubling T gives 16× the power.
  • Net power: $P_{\text{net}} = e\sigma A(T^4 - T_{\text{env}}^4)$. Zero at thermal equilibrium.
  • Emissivity: $e = 1$ (black body), $e \approx 0$ (shiny mirror). Good absorbers = good emitters.
  • Dark matt surfaces absorb/emit well. Shiny surfaces reflect/emit poorly.
  • Vacuum flask: defeats all three — vacuum (no conduction/convection), silver (no radiation).
  • All three usually act together in real situations. Dominant one depends on context.
