B.3.3

The Gas Laws

Boyle's, Charles's & Gay-Lussac's

Three scientists, three experiments, three laws — each holding one variable constant and watching how the other two relate. Together they form the foundation of gas behaviour, and all three are special cases of one master equation: PV = nRT.

Atul Soral Physicism

Boyle's Law (1662) — P vs V at Constant T

BOYLE'S LAW

At constant temperature and fixed amount of gas: pressure is inversely proportional to volume.

$$PV = \text{constant} \quad \Rightarrow \quad P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$$

Molecular explanation

Halving the volume means molecules travel half the distance between wall collisions → they hit the walls twice as often. Same speed, double the collision rate → double the pressure. $P \propto 1/V$.

Worked example

A gas at 100 kPa occupies 3.0 L. It is compressed to 1.0 L at constant T. Find the new pressure.

$P_2 = \frac{P_1V_1}{V_2} = \frac{100 \times 3.0}{1.0} = \mathbf{300 \text{ kPa}}$

Charles's Law (1787) — V vs T at Constant P

CHARLES'S LAW

At constant pressure and fixed amount of gas: volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature.

$$\frac{V}{T} = \text{constant} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}$$

Molecular explanation

Higher T → faster molecules → harder wall collisions → pressure would increase. But since P is held constant, the gas expands until the collision rate per unit area drops back to give the original pressure. More speed → more volume needed → $V \propto T$.

T must be in kelvin. If you use °C, the graph doesn't pass through the origin and the proportionality breaks. $V \propto T$ only works with absolute temperature. This is how absolute zero was first estimated — extrapolating V-T graphs to V = 0.

Worked example

A gas occupies 2.0 L at 300 K. It is heated to 600 K at constant P. Find the new volume.

$V_2 = V_1 \times \frac{T_2}{T_1} = 2.0 \times \frac{600}{300} = \mathbf{4.0 \text{ L}}$ — double T, double V

Gay-Lussac's Law (1809) — P vs T at Constant V

GAY-LUSSAC'S LAW (PRESSURE LAW)

At constant volume and fixed amount of gas: pressure is directly proportional to absolute temperature.

$$\frac{P}{T} = \text{constant} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2}$$

Molecular explanation

Higher T → faster molecules → hit walls harder ($\Delta p = 2mv$, larger $v$) AND more frequently. Volume is fixed, so the extra force goes directly into higher pressure. $P \propto T$.

Worked example

A sealed container holds gas at 200 kPa and 27°C. It is heated to 127°C. Find the new pressure.

$T_1 = 300$ K, $T_2 = 400$ K

$P_2 = P_1 \times \frac{T_2}{T_1} = 200 \times \frac{400}{300} = \mathbf{267 \text{ kPa}}$

This is why aerosol cans warn "do not heat." A sealed container at constant volume with rising temperature → rising pressure. Beyond the can's structural limit → explosion. The physics is Gay-Lussac's law.

The Combined Gas Law

All three laws are special cases of one equation:

$$\frac{P_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2V_2}{T_2}$$
LawConstantRelationshipGraph shapeMolecular reason
BoyleT, n$PV = k$HyperbolaLess space → more frequent hits
CharlesP, n$V/T = k$Straight line through originFaster hits → expand to maintain P
Gay-LussacV, n$P/T = k$Straight line through originFaster + harder hits → more P

Combined example

A gas at 100 kPa, 2.0 L, 300 K is compressed to 0.50 L and heated to 600 K. Find the new pressure.

$P_2 = P_1 \times \frac{V_1}{V_2} \times \frac{T_2}{T_1} = 100 \times \frac{2.0}{0.50} \times \frac{600}{300} = 100 \times 4 \times 2 = \mathbf{800 \text{ kPa}}$

IB exam tip: Always identify what's constant first. If the problem says "sealed container" → V constant (Gay-Lussac). "Piston moves freely" → P constant (Charles). "Isothermal" → T constant (Boyle). If two things change, use the combined law.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

State [2]

(a) State Boyle's law and the condition under which it applies.

At constant temperature (and for a fixed amount of gas), the pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume: $PV = \text{constant}$ or $P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$ [1]. This applies when the temperature and the amount of gas remain unchanged [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A balloon contains 5.0 L of helium at 20°C and 101 kPa. It rises to an altitude where the temperature is −30°C and the pressure is 40 kPa. Calculate the new volume.

$T_1 = 293$ K, $T_2 = 243$ K, $P_1 = 101$ kPa, $P_2 = 40$ kPa [1]
$V_2 = V_1 \times \frac{P_1}{P_2} \times \frac{T_2}{T_1} = 5.0 \times \frac{101}{40} \times \frac{243}{293}$ [1]
$= 5.0 \times 2.525 \times 0.829 = \mathbf{10.5 \text{ L}}$ [1]
Explain [3]

(c) Using kinetic theory, explain why the pressure of a gas increases when its temperature increases at constant volume.

Increasing temperature increases the average kinetic energy and speed of gas molecules [1]. At constant volume, faster molecules collide with the walls more frequently (shorter time between collisions) [1]. Each collision also transfers more momentum ($\Delta p = 2mv$, with larger $v$), increasing the average force per unit area on the walls — hence higher pressure [1].
Sketch [2]

(d) Sketch a graph of P vs 1/V for an ideal gas at constant temperature. Describe the expected shape.

Since $P = k/V$ (Boyle's law), a graph of P vs $1/V$ is a straight line through the origin with gradient $k = nRT$ [1]. The straight line confirms the inverse proportionality between P and V. A steeper gradient indicates a higher temperature or more moles of gas [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Boyle's law: $P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$ at constant T. P vs V = hyperbola. PV vs P = horizontal line.
  • Charles's law: $V_1/T_1 = V_2/T_2$ at constant P. V vs T(K) = straight line through origin.
  • Gay-Lussac's law: $P_1/T_1 = P_2/T_2$ at constant V. P vs T(K) = straight line through origin.
  • Combined: $P_1V_1/T_1 = P_2V_2/T_2$ — use when two variables change.
  • T must be in kelvin for Charles and Gay-Lussac. Boyle doesn't use T directly.
  • Molecular explanations: all three laws follow from molecules hitting walls — frequency and force of impacts.
  • Identify the constant: sealed = V constant, free piston = P constant, isothermal = T constant.
