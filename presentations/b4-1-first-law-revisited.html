The First Law of Thermodynamics

Revisited

Energy is conserved — but for a gas it can flow in as heat, out as work, or be stored as internal energy. The first law is the exact bookkeeping of these three. Here we sharpen the sign convention, define internal energy for an ideal gas, and apply the law to every process on a PV diagram.

Statement of the First Law

The first law is conservation of energy applied to a thermodynamic system:

$$Q = \Delta U + W$$

heat supplied to gas = change in internal energy + work done by gas

  • $Q$ = thermal energy (heat) added to the gas.
  • $\Delta U$ = change in the internal energy of the gas.
  • $W$ = work done by the gas on its surroundings.
Read it in words: heat you put into a gas either raises its internal energy (it gets hotter) or lets it do work (it expands and pushes something) — or a mix of both. Nothing is lost.
Convention alert. IB writes $Q = \Delta U + W$ with $W$ = work done by the gas. Some textbooks write $\Delta U = Q - W$ (same thing) or $\Delta U = Q + W$ with $W$ = work done on the gas. Always state which $W$ you mean, or you'll drop sign marks.

Getting the Signs Right

QuantityPositive when…Negative when…
$Q$heat flows INTO gasheat flows OUT of gas
$W$gas EXPANDS (does work)gas COMPRESSED (work done on it)
$\Delta U$temperature RISEStemperature FALLS

A quick sanity check

Heat a gas in a rigid box: $W = 0$ (no expansion), so $Q = \Delta U$ — all heat becomes internal energy, temperature rises. Let the same gas expand while heated: some $Q$ becomes $W$, so $\Delta U$ (and the temperature rise) is smaller.

Tip: decide the sign of each term from the physical situation first, then substitute. Never blindly plug numbers — a compression with $\Delta V < 0$ makes $W$ negative.

Internal Energy of an Ideal Gas

For an ideal gas there are no intermolecular forces, so there is no potential energy — internal energy is purely the total random kinetic energy of the molecules.

$$U = N\cdot\bar{E}_k = N\left(\tfrac{3}{2}k_BT\right)$$
$$U = \tfrac{3}{2}nRT \qquad \Delta U = \tfrac{3}{2}nR\,\Delta T$$
  • $U$ depends only on temperature (for a fixed amount of ideal gas). Change T and you change U; hold T constant and $\Delta U = 0$.
  • This is why isothermal → $\Delta U = 0$: same temperature, same internal energy.
  • The factor $\tfrac{3}{2}$ is for a monatomic ideal gas (3 translational degrees of freedom). It links straight back to B.3.5.
Common trap: $\Delta U$ tracks temperature, not heat. A gas can absorb heat and yet have $\Delta U = 0$ (isothermal expansion — all the heat leaves again as work). Don't equate "heat added" with "hotter."

The First Law in Each Process

ProcessConditionFirst law becomes
Isochoric$\Delta V = 0,\ W = 0$$Q = \Delta U$
Isobaric$P$ constant$Q = \Delta U + P\Delta V$
Isothermal$\Delta T = 0,\ \Delta U = 0$$Q = W$
Adiabatic$Q = 0$$\Delta U = -W$

Isothermal expansion

$\Delta U = 0$, so every joule of heat in comes straight out as work: $Q = W$. Temperature unchanged.

Adiabatic expansion

No heat exchange ($Q=0$). The gas does work using its own internal energy, so it cools: $\Delta U = -W < 0$.

Why adiabatic curves are steeper than isotherms: during adiabatic expansion the gas also cools, so pressure drops faster than in an isothermal expansion at the same volume change.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — heat, work and ΔU

2.0 kJ of heat is supplied to a gas, which expands and does 800 J of work. Find the change in internal energy.

$\Delta U = Q - W = 2000 - 800 = \mathbf{+1200\ \text{J}}$ (internal energy rises, gas warms)

Example 2 — compression

A gas is compressed, with 500 J of work done on it, while it loses 300 J of heat. Find ΔU.

Work done on gas → $W_{by\ gas} = -500$ J. Heat lost → $Q = -300$ J.

$\Delta U = Q - W = (-300) - (-500) = \mathbf{+200\ \text{J}}$ (still warms, compression wins)

Example 3 — monatomic ΔU

0.50 mol of monatomic ideal gas is heated from 300 K to 400 K at constant volume. Find Q.

$W = 0$, so $Q = \Delta U = \tfrac{3}{2}nR\Delta T = \tfrac{3}{2}(0.50)(8.31)(100) = \mathbf{623\ \text{J}}$

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

State & Define [3]

(a) State the first law of thermodynamics and define each term in the equation Q = ΔU + W.

The first law states that the heat supplied to a system equals the increase in its internal energy plus the work done by the system: $Q = \Delta U + W$ [1]. $Q$ is the thermal energy transferred to the gas; $\Delta U$ is the change in internal energy (total random KE of molecules for an ideal gas) [1]; $W$ is the work done by the gas on its surroundings ($= P\Delta V$ at constant pressure) [1].
Apply [3]

(b) A gas absorbs 1500 J of heat and its internal energy increases by 900 J. Determine the work done, and state whether the gas expanded or was compressed.

$W = Q - \Delta U = 1500 - 900 = 600$ J [1]. Since $W$ (work done by the gas) is positive, the gas did work on its surroundings [1], so the gas expanded [1].
Explain [3]

(c) Explain why the internal energy of an ideal gas does not change during an isothermal process, and state what happens to any heat supplied.

The internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on its absolute temperature ($U = \tfrac{3}{2}nRT$) [1]. In an isothermal process the temperature is constant, so $\Delta U = 0$ [1]. By the first law $Q = \Delta U + W = W$, so all the heat supplied is converted entirely into work done by the gas as it expands [1].
Reason [2]

(d) A gas expands adiabatically. State the value of Q and explain what happens to the temperature of the gas.

In an adiabatic process there is no heat exchange, so $Q = 0$ [1]. The first law gives $\Delta U = -W$; as the gas expands it does positive work, so $\Delta U$ is negative — the internal energy and hence the temperature of the gas decrease (the gas cools) [1].

Key Takeaways

  • First law: $Q = \Delta U + W$ — energy conservation for a gas ($W$ = work done by the gas).
  • Signs: $Q>0$ heat in; $W>0$ expansion; $\Delta U>0$ temperature rises.
  • Internal energy: $U = \tfrac{3}{2}nRT$ (monatomic ideal gas) — depends only on temperature.
  • Isochoric: $Q=\Delta U$. Isobaric: $Q=\Delta U + P\Delta V$.
  • Isothermal: $\Delta U=0$, $Q=W$. Adiabatic: $Q=0$, $\Delta U=-W$ (expansion cools the gas).
  • Watch the trap: heat added ≠ hotter; $\Delta U$ follows temperature, not $Q$.
