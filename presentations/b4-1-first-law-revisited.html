The First Law — Precise Statement

$$Q = \Delta U + W$$
Q

the gas (J)
+Q = heat in, −Q = heat out

energy (J)
+ΔU = T rises, −ΔU = T drops

Work done BY
the gas (J)
+W = expansion, −W = compression
Sign convention is critical. IB uses: Q positive = heat added to gas. W positive = work done BY gas (expansion). Some textbooks use $\Delta U = Q - W$; IB uses $Q = \Delta U + W$. Same physics, different arrangement. Know which your exam uses.
What the first law says: Energy is conserved. Every joule of heat you put into a gas must either (a) increase its internal energy (raise its temperature) or (b) do work (push a piston). There's nowhere else for it to go. $Q = \Delta U + W$ is conservation of energy for thermal systems.

For an ideal monatomic gas

$\Delta U = \frac{3}{2}nR\Delta T$ (always, regardless of process)

$W = P\Delta V$ (constant pressure) or area under PV curve (general)

These plus $PV = nRT$ give you everything you need.

The key insight for each process

  • Isochoric: easiest — no work, so $Q = \Delta U$. Directly heats the gas.
  • Isobaric: most common — gas expands as it heats. Part of Q does work, part heats gas.
  • Isothermal: subtle — gas expands and does work, but T stays constant because heat flows in to replace the energy lost as work.
  • Adiabatic: fast processes (sound waves, rapid compression). No time for heat exchange. T changes because work draws from or adds to internal energy.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Isobaric expansion

2.0 mol of monatomic ideal gas at 300 K expands at constant P = 100 kPa. Temperature rises to 500 K. Find Q, ΔU, and W.

ΔU: $\frac{3}{2}nR\Delta T = \frac{3}{2} \times 2.0 \times 8.314 \times 200 = \mathbf{4988 \text{ J}}$

W: $P\Delta V = nR\Delta T = 2.0 \times 8.314 \times 200 = \mathbf{3326 \text{ J}}$ (from $PV = nRT$ at constant P)

Q: $\Delta U + W = 4988 + 3326 = \mathbf{8314 \text{ J}} = \frac{5}{2}nR\Delta T$

For isobaric process: $Q = \frac{5}{2}nR\Delta T$ (monatomic). 60% goes to ΔU, 40% goes to work. The gas "wastes" 40% of heat input on expansion — it takes more heat to raise the temperature at constant P than at constant V.

Example 2: Adiabatic compression

1.0 mol of gas is compressed adiabatically. 500 J of work is done ON the gas. Find ΔU and the temperature change.

$Q = 0$ (adiabatic). Work done ON gas → $W = -500$ J (negative = compression)

$0 = \Delta U + (-500)$ → $\Delta U = +500$ J. Gas gets hotter!

$\Delta T = \frac{\Delta U}{\frac{3}{2}nR} = \frac{500}{1.5 \times 8.314} = \mathbf{40.1 \text{ K}}$

Adiabatic compression heats a gas. This is why diesel engines work — compressing air adiabatically raises its temperature enough to ignite fuel without a spark plug. It's also why bicycle pumps get hot.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

State [2]

(a) State the first law of thermodynamics and explain the meaning of each term.

$Q = \Delta U + W$. Q is the heat added to the system, ΔU is the change in internal energy, and W is the work done by the system on the surroundings [1]. It is a statement of conservation of energy applied to thermal processes: energy input as heat equals the sum of the increase in internal energy and the work output [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) 3.0 mol of monatomic ideal gas is heated at constant volume from 200 K to 400 K. Calculate Q, ΔU, and W.

Isochoric: $W = 0$ (constant V) [1]
$\Delta U = \frac{3}{2}nR\Delta T = 1.5 \times 3.0 \times 8.314 \times 200 = 7483$ J [1]
$Q = \Delta U + W = 7483 + 0 = \mathbf{7483 \text{ J}}$ [1]
Explain [3]

(c) Explain why more heat is needed to raise the temperature of a gas by 1 K at constant pressure than at constant volume.

At constant volume, $W = 0$, so all heat goes into increasing internal energy: $Q_V = \Delta U = \frac{3}{2}nR\Delta T$ [1]. At constant pressure, the gas also expands, doing work $W = nR\Delta T$, so $Q_P = \Delta U + W = \frac{3}{2}nR\Delta T + nR\Delta T = \frac{5}{2}nR\Delta T$ [1]. $Q_P > Q_V$ because at constant pressure, extra heat must be supplied to do the work of expansion against the external pressure [1].
Explain [2]

(d) In an adiabatic expansion, the temperature of a gas decreases. Explain this using the first law.

In an adiabatic process, $Q = 0$ (no heat exchange). The first law gives $0 = \Delta U + W$, so $\Delta U = -W$ [1]. Since the gas expands ($W > 0$), $\Delta U < 0$ — the internal energy decreases. For an ideal gas, $U \propto T$, so the temperature must decrease [1].
B.4 Thermodynamics  |  B.4.1 The First LawSummary

Key Takeaways

  • $Q = \Delta U + W$ — heat in = internal energy rise + work out. Conservation of energy.
  • Isochoric (V const): $W = 0$, $Q = \Delta U = \frac{3}{2}nR\Delta T$. All heat → temperature.
  • Isobaric (P const): $Q = \frac{5}{2}nR\Delta T$. Heat splits: 60% → ΔU, 40% → W (monatomic).
  • Isothermal (T const): $\Delta U = 0$, $Q = W$. All heat → work.
  • Adiabatic (Q = 0): $\Delta U = -W$. Expansion cools, compression heats.
  • $C_P > C_V$ because constant-P heating requires extra energy for expansion work.
  • Diesel engines use adiabatic compression to ignite fuel. Bicycle pumps get hot for the same reason.
