Two Conditions for Static Equilibrium

For a rigid body to be in complete static equilibrium, both conditions must be satisfied simultaneously:

Condition 1: Translational

$$\sum \vec{F} = 0$$

Net force is zero in every direction.
No linear acceleration.

In components: $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$

Condition 2: Rotational

$$\sum \tau = 0$$

Net torque about any point is zero.
No angular acceleration.

CW torques = CCW torques

Both conditions are needed. A couple has $\sum F = 0$ but $\sum\tau \neq 0$ — it satisfies Condition 1 but not 2, so it spins. A single force through the centre of mass has $\sum\tau = 0$ about the CM but $\sum F \neq 0$, so it translates.
Key insight: If $\sum\tau = 0$ about one point, it's zero about every point (when $\sum F = 0$ also holds). So you can choose any convenient pivot for your torque equation.

The Art of Choosing a Pivot

You can take torques about any point. A smart choice eliminates unknowns:

Strategy: Put the pivot where an unknown force acts.

That force has $r = 0$, so its torque vanishes. You eliminate one unknown from the equation without ever solving for it.

Pivot at A → $R_A$ drops out. Torque equation gives $R_B$ directly:

$R_B \times L = W \times d_1 + F \times (d_1 + d_2)$

Then use $\sum F_y = 0$: $R_A + R_B = W + F$ → solve for $R_A$.

Exam technique: In IB papers, always (1) draw a clear diagram with all forces, (2) state your pivot, (3) write $\sum\tau = 0$, (4) solve. Then check with $\sum F = 0$.

Worked Example: Beam on Two Supports

A uniform beam of length 4.0 m and weight 200 N rests on two supports A (left end) and B (right end). A 300 N load is placed 1.0 m from A. Find the reaction forces $R_A$ and $R_B$.

Step 1: Take pivot at A (eliminates $R_A$).

Step 2: $\sum\tau_A = 0$:   $R_B \times 4.0 = 300 \times 1.0 + 200 \times 2.0$

Step 3: $4.0\,R_B = 300 + 400 = 700$ → $R_B = \mathbf{175 \text{ N}}$

Step 4: $\sum F_y = 0$:   $R_A + 175 = 300 + 200$ → $R_A = \mathbf{325 \text{ N}}$

Check: $R_A + R_B = 325 + 175 = 500 = W + F$ ✓   Load is closer to A, so $R_A > R_B$ ✓

Sanity check: The support closer to the load always bears more weight. If the load were exactly at the centre, both reactions would equal 250 N.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.2 Rotational EquilibriumWorked Example 2

Worked Example: Hinged Beam with Cable

A uniform beam of weight 120 N and length 3.0 m is hinged at a wall. A horizontal cable holds the free end. A 200 N load hangs from the free end. The beam makes 30° with the horizontal. Find the tension in the cable.

Pivot at hinge (eliminates hinge reaction forces).

$\sum\tau = 0$:   $T \times 3.0\sin 30° = 120 \times 1.5\cos 30° + 200 \times 3.0\cos 30°$

$T \times 1.5 = 120 \times 1.299 + 200 \times 2.598 = 155.9 + 519.6 = 675.5$

$T = \frac{675.5}{1.5} = \mathbf{450 \text{ N}}$

Why sin and cos? The perpendicular distance from the hinge to the cable's line of action is $L\sin\theta$. The perpendicular distance from the hinge to each weight's line of action is the horizontal distance $= r\cos\theta$. Drawing the moment arms helps.

Classic Problem: Ladder Against a Wall

A uniform ladder of weight 150 N and length 5.0 m leans against a smooth (frictionless) wall at 60° to the ground. Find the friction force at the base and the normal reaction from the wall.

Pivot at base: $N_w \times 5.0\sin 60° = 150 \times 2.5\cos 60°$

$N_w \times 4.33 = 150 \times 1.25 = 187.5$ → $N_w = \mathbf{43.3 \text{ N}}$

$\sum F_x = 0$: $f = N_w = \mathbf{43.3 \text{ N}}$  |  $\sum F_y = 0$: $N = 150$ N ✓

IB favourite: Ladder problems test all three equations ($\sum F_x$, $\sum F_y$, $\sum\tau$) in one setup. Always identify which surface is smooth (no friction) and which is rough.

Centre of Mass & Stability

The centre of mass (CM) is the point where the entire weight can be considered to act. For a uniform object, CM is at the geometric centre.

Stable Equilibrium

CM is low, base is wide. If tilted, weight creates a restoring torque.

Unstable Equilibrium

CM is high, base is narrow. Small tilt → weight line falls outside base → topples.

Toppling condition: An object topples when the vertical line through its CM falls outside the base of support. Race cars are low and wide. Double-decker buses have heavy chassis below.
Non-uniform objects: CM is not at the geometric centre. It's closer to the heavier end. You can find it experimentally by suspending the object from two different points — the CM is where the plumb lines intersect.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

A uniform plank of weight 80 N and length 6.0 m is supported at its left end (P) and at a point 4.0 m from P (Q). A child of weight 400 N stands at the right end.

Calculate [3]

(a) By taking moments about P, calculate the reaction force at Q.

$\sum\tau_P = 0$: $R_Q \times 4.0 = 80 \times 3.0 + 400 \times 6.0$ [1]
$4.0\,R_Q = 240 + 2400 = 2640$ [1]
$R_Q = \mathbf{660 \text{ N}}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the reaction force at P.

$\sum F_y = 0$: $R_P + R_Q = 80 + 400$ [1]
$R_P = 480 - 660 = -180$ N. The negative sign means the force at P acts downward — the plank tends to lift off at P, so P must be clamped or pinned [1].
Explain [2]

(c) Explain the physical significance of the negative value of $R_P$.

The plank would rotate clockwise about Q due to the child's weight [1]. P must exert a downward force (or be clamped) to prevent the plank from tipping. The plank is not simply resting on P — it requires a fixing [1].
Determine [2]

(d) Determine the maximum distance from P at which the child can stand without the plank tipping about Q.

At the tipping point, $R_P = 0$. Taking moments about Q:
$400 \times (x - 4.0) = 80 \times (4.0 - 3.0) = 80$ [1]
$x - 4.0 = 0.2$ → $x = \mathbf{4.2 \text{ m}}$ from P [1]

Calculate [4]

A shop sign of weight 60 N hangs from the end of a uniform horizontal rod of weight 40 N and length 1.2 m. The rod is hinged to a wall, and a wire at 45° connects the free end to the wall above. Find the tension in the wire.

Pivot at hinge (eliminates hinge reactions):
$T\sin 45° \times 1.2 = 40 \times 0.6 + 60 \times 1.2$ [1]
$T \times 0.849 = 24 + 72 = 96$ [1]
$T = \frac{96}{0.849} = \mathbf{113 \text{ N}}$ [1] [1]
Calculate [3]

A non-uniform beam of length 2.0 m and weight 100 N is supported at both ends. When a 50 N force is applied at the right end, the left support reads 90 N. Find the position of the centre of mass from the left end.

$\sum F_y = 0$: $R_{\text{right}} = 100 + 50 - 90 = 60$ N [1]
Pivot at left: $60 \times 2.0 = 100 \times d + 50 \times 2.0$ → $120 = 100d + 100$ [1]
$d = \frac{20}{100} = \mathbf{0.20 \text{ m}}$ from the left end [1]
Explain [2]

A tall thin vase and a short wide bowl both weigh the same. Explain which is more stable and why.

The bowl is more stable [1]. It has a lower centre of mass and a wider base, so a larger angle of tilt is needed before the vertical line through the CM falls outside the base of support. The vase has a high CM and narrow base, so it topples with a small perturbation [1].
Engineering link: Structural engineers use these exact principles. Every beam, truss, and cantilever in a building is designed so that all forces and torques balance — with safety factors of 2× or more.

Key Takeaways

  • Static equilibrium requires both $\sum F = 0$ (no translation) and $\sum\tau = 0$ (no rotation).
  • Choose pivot wisely — place it where an unknown force acts to eliminate that unknown.
  • Uniform beam: weight acts at the geometric centre (midpoint).
  • Method: Draw diagram → label all forces → choose pivot → write $\sum\tau = 0$ → use $\sum F = 0$ to find remaining unknowns.
  • Negative reaction means the assumed direction was wrong — the force acts the other way (e.g., support needs to pull down, not push up).
  • Stability: low CM + wide base = stable. Object topples when weight line exits the base.
  • Classic problems: beam on two supports, hinged beam with cable, ladder against wall.
