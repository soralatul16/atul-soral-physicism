A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.6 Projectile MotionCore Principle

The Independence Principle

The single most important idea in projectile motion:

Horizontal and vertical motions are completely independent. They don't affect each other. You solve them separately, then combine.

Horizontal (x-direction)

  • No horizontal force (ignoring air resistance)
  • Therefore $a_x = 0$
  • Velocity is constant: $v_x = u_x$ always
  • Displacement: $x = u_x \cdot t$

Vertical (y-direction)

  • Gravity acts downward
  • Therefore $a_y = -g = -9.8$ m s$^{-2}$
  • Velocity changes: $v_y = u_y - gt$
  • SUVAT applies in y-direction
Why this matters: A bullet fired horizontally and a bullet dropped from the same height hit the ground at the same time. The horizontal speed doesn't affect how fast it falls.

Building the Projectile Diagram — Step 1: The Launch

A projectile is launched at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal with initial speed $u$.

Before doing ANY calculation, you must resolve the initial velocity into horizontal and vertical components. This is the foundation of every projectile problem.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.6 Projectile MotionDiagram Step 2

Step 2: Resolving the Initial Velocity

Split $u$ into two perpendicular components using trigonometry:

Horizontal component

$$u_x = u \cos\theta$$

This is the "adjacent" side. It stays constant throughout the flight (no horizontal force).

Vertical component

$$u_y = u \sin\theta$$

This is the "opposite" side. It changes due to gravity: decreases going up, increases going down.

Memory trick: "$\cos$ goes with horizontal" — both start with a hard sound. "$\sin$ goes with vertical" — both go up.

A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.6 Projectile MotionEquations

The Complete Set of Projectile Equations

Horizontal ($a_x = 0$)

$$u_x = u\cos\theta$$
$$v_x = u_x = u\cos\theta \text{ (constant)}$$
$$x = u_x \cdot t = (u\cos\theta) \cdot t$$

No acceleration → simple: distance = speed × time

Vertical ($a_y = -g$)

$$u_y = u\sin\theta$$
$$v_y = u\sin\theta - gt$$
$$y = (u\sin\theta)t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$$
$$v_y^2 = (u\sin\theta)^2 - 2gy$$

Full SUVAT applies vertically

Time is the link. $t$ is the same in both directions. This is how you connect horizontal and vertical: find $t$ from one direction, use it in the other.

Deriving Time of Flight

For a projectile launched from and landing at the same height ($y = 0$ at landing):

1Start with: $y = u_y t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$
2At landing, $y = 0$: $\quad 0 = u_y t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$
3Factor out $t$: $\quad 0 = t\left(u_y - \frac{1}{2}gt\right)$
4Two solutions: $t = 0$ (launch) or $u_y - \frac{1}{2}gt = 0$
5Solve: $t = \frac{2u_y}{g} = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$
$$\boxed{T = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}}$$
Physical meaning: Time of flight depends on the vertical component of velocity ($u\sin\theta$) only. A steeper angle means more time in the air. At $\theta = 90°$: $T = 2u/g$ (straight up and down).
Symmetry: Time to reach max height = $T/2 = \frac{u\sin\theta}{g}$. The trajectory is symmetric about the peak.

Deriving Maximum Height

At the highest point, $v_y = 0$. Use the vertical SUVAT equation:

1Start with: $v_y^2 = u_y^2 - 2gH$
2At max height: $v_y = 0$: $\quad 0 = u_y^2 - 2gH$
3Solve for $H$: $\quad H = \frac{u_y^2}{2g} = \frac{(u\sin\theta)^2}{2g}$
$$\boxed{H = \frac{u^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}}$$
At $\theta = 90°$ (straight up): $H = u^2/(2g)$ — maximum possible height for given $u$.
At $\theta = 45°$: $H = u^2/(4g)$ — half the maximum height but maximum range (next slide).

Deriving Range

Range $R$ = horizontal distance when the projectile returns to launch height.

1Horizontal: $R = u_x \cdot T = (u\cos\theta) \cdot T$
2Substitute $T = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$:
$$R = u\cos\theta \cdot \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g} = \frac{2u^2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{g}$$
3Use identity $2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin 2\theta$:
$$\boxed{R = \frac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g}}$$
Maximum range occurs when $\sin 2\theta = 1$, i.e., $2\theta = 90°$, so $\theta = 45°$.
Complementary angles give equal range: $30°$ and $60°$, $20°$ and $70°$ — any pair adding to $90°$ gives the same range. ($\sin 2\theta$ is the same for both.) Different trajectories, same landing point.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.6 Projectile MotionResultant Velocity

Step 4: Finding Velocity at Any Point

At any time $t$, you know $v_x$ (constant) and $v_y$ (from SUVAT). Combine them:

Speed (magnitude):

$$v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$$

Direction (angle below horizontal):

$$\alpha = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{|v_y|}{v_x}\right)$$

This works at any point during flight. At the top, $v_y = 0$ so $v = v_x$ and $\alpha = 0$.

IB marking point: Always state the direction of velocity, not just the magnitude. "The velocity is 25 m/s at 40° below the horizontal."

Special Case: Horizontal Launch ($\theta = 0$)

When an object is launched horizontally from a height (e.g., kicked off a cliff), $\theta = 0$ so $u_y = 0$.

Simplified equations:

Since $u_y = 0$ and $u_x = u$:

$$x = ut$$
$$y = \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$$
$$v_y = gt$$

To find time to ground:

$$h = \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}$$

Then range:

$$R = u \cdot t = u\sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}$$

Worked Example 1 — Horizontal Launch 5/10

Problem: A ball is kicked horizontally at 12 m s$^{-1}$ off a cliff 45 m high. Find (a) time to reach the ground (b) horizontal distance (range) (c) velocity just before impact.

SUVAT — separate horizontal and vertical

Horizontal: uₓ=12, aₓ=0  |  Vertical: uᵧ=0, aᵧ=9.8, sᵧ=45
a$h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$: $45 = \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{90}{9.8}} = \mathbf{3.03 \text{ s}}$
b$R = u_x \cdot t = 12 \times 3.03 = \mathbf{36.4 \text{ m}}$

(c) Impact velocity:

→$v_x = 12$ m/s (unchanged)  |  $v_y = gt = 9.8 \times 3.03 = 29.7$ m/s
$$v = \sqrt{12^2 + 29.7^2} = \sqrt{144 + 882} = \mathbf{32.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$
$$\alpha = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{29.7}{12}\right) = \mathbf{68.0°} \text{ below horizontal}$$

Worked Example 2 — Angled Launch 7/10

Problem: A football is kicked at 20 m s$^{-1}$ at 30° to the horizontal on level ground. Find (a) time of flight (b) maximum height (c) range.

Resolve first:

uₓ = 20cos30° = 17.3 m/s  |  uᵧ = 20sin30° = 10.0 m/s
a$T = \frac{2u_y}{g} = \frac{2(10.0)}{9.8} = \mathbf{2.04 \text{ s}}$
b$H = \frac{u_y^2}{2g} = \frac{100}{19.6} = \mathbf{5.10 \text{ m}}$
c$R = u_x \times T = 17.3 \times 2.04 = \mathbf{35.3 \text{ m}}$

Check with range formula: $R = \frac{u^2\sin 60°}{9.8} = \frac{400 \times 0.866}{9.8} = 35.3$ m ✓

What if the angle were 60°? $u_x = 10$, $u_y = 17.3$. Range = same 35.3 m! But $H = 15.3$ m (higher arc). Complementary angles → same range, different heights.

IB-Style Question Set 9/10

Context: A ball is launched at 25 m s$^{-1}$ at an angle of 40° above the horizontal from the edge of a 30 m high building. It lands on the ground below. Ignore air resistance. $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the horizontal and vertical components of the initial velocity.

$u_x = 25\cos40° = \mathbf{19.2}$ m/s [1]
$u_y = 25\sin40° = \mathbf{16.1}$ m/s [1]
Determine [3]

(b) Determine the maximum height above the ground reached by the ball.

Max above launch: $H = \frac{u_y^2}{2g} = \frac{259}{19.6} = 13.2$ m [2]
Above ground: $30 + 13.2 = \mathbf{43.2}$ m [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) Calculate the time for the ball to reach the ground.

Taking up as +, ground is $y = -30$ m from launch:
$-30 = 16.1t - 4.9t^2$ → $4.9t^2 - 16.1t - 30 = 0$
$t = \frac{16.1 + \sqrt{259 + 588}}{9.8} = \frac{16.1 + 29.1}{9.8} = \mathbf{4.61}$ s [3]
Determine [3]

(d) Determine the speed and direction of the ball just before it hits the ground.

$v_x = 19.2$ m/s (unchanged)
$v_y = 16.1 - 9.8(4.61) = 16.1 - 45.2 = -29.1$ m/s (down)
$v = \sqrt{19.2^2 + 29.1^2} = \sqrt{369 + 847} = \mathbf{34.9}$ m/s [2]
$\alpha = \tan^{-1}(29.1/19.2) = \mathbf{56.6°}$ below horizontal [1]

Practice Questions

Calculate [3] Medium

A stone is thrown horizontally at 8.0 m s$^{-1}$ from a 20 m cliff. Find the time, range, and impact speed.

$t = \sqrt{2(20)/9.8} = 2.02$ s | $R = 8.0 \times 2.02 = 16.2$ m
$v_y = 9.8 \times 2.02 = 19.8$ m/s | $v = \sqrt{64+392} = \mathbf{21.4}$ m/s at 68° below horiz.
Determine [3] Hard

A ball is launched at 30 m s$^{-1}$ at 50° on level ground. Find the range and compare with a 40° launch at the same speed.

50°: $R = \frac{900\sin100°}{9.8} = \frac{900 \times 0.985}{9.8} = 90.4$ m
40°: $R = \frac{900\sin80°}{9.8} = \frac{900 \times 0.985}{9.8} = 90.4$ m
Same range! $\sin100° = \sin80°$. Complementary angles (40+50=90).
Explain [2] Hard

A monkey hangs from a branch. A hunter aims directly at the monkey and fires. The monkey drops at the instant the gun fires. Does the dart hit the monkey? Explain.

Yes. Both the dart and the monkey experience the same $g$. The dart falls below its aimed line by $\frac{1}{2}gt^2$. The monkey falls the same $\frac{1}{2}gt^2$. They meet at the point where both have "fallen" equally from where they would have been without gravity.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.6 Projectile MotionSummary

Key Takeaways

  • Horizontal and vertical are independent. Solve separately, connect through time $t$.
  • Resolve first: $u_x = u\cos\theta$, $u_y = u\sin\theta$.
  • Horizontal: $a = 0$, constant speed, $x = u_x t$.
  • Vertical: $a = g$, full SUVAT applies.
  • Time of flight: $T = 2u\sin\theta / g$. Max height: $H = u^2\sin^2\theta / 2g$. Range: $R = u^2\sin2\theta / g$.
  • Max range at 45°. Complementary angles give same range.
  • At the peak: $v_y = 0$ but $v_x$ is still there. The ball is NOT stationary at the top.
  • Impact velocity: Use Pythagoras: $v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$ + state direction.
