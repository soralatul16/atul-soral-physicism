C.2.2

Wave Speed in Different Media

What sets how fast a wave travels

A wave's speed is not set by its source but by the medium it moves through. Understand the two competing properties — a restoring "stiffness" and an "inertia" — and you can predict why sound races through steel, crawls through air, and why light slows down in glass.

What Determines Wave Speed?

Wave speed is a property of the medium, not the wave. It is set by a balance of two competing factors:

Restoring property (↑ speed)

A stronger "spring back" force transmits the disturbance faster: tension in a string, stiffness/elasticity of a solid, pressure in a gas.

Inertial property (↓ speed)

More mass to move slows the disturbance: mass per length of a string, density of a solid/liquid/gas.

$$v \sim \sqrt{\frac{\text{restoring (stiffness) property}}{\text{inertial (mass) property}}}$$
Crucial point: changing the frequency of the source does not change the wave speed in a given medium — it changes the wavelength instead (since $v = f\lambda$ with v fixed). Speed is decided by the medium; f is decided by the source.

Waves on a String

The clearest example of the stiffness/inertia balance is a transverse wave on a stretched string:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}$$
  • T — the tension in the string (N), the restoring property. Tighter string → faster wave.
  • μ — the mass per unit length (kg m⁻¹), $\mu = m/L$, the inertial property. Heavier/thicker string → slower wave.
  • Because of the square root, to double the speed you must quadruple the tension.
Musical strings: this is why a guitar's thick low strings (large μ) vibrate slowly and sound low-pitched, while tightening a string (raising T) raises the wave speed, raises the frequency of its standing waves, and sharpens the note.

Speed of Sound: Solids > Liquids > Gases

Sound is longitudinal and needs a medium. Its speed rises with the medium's stiffness — solids are far stiffer than gases, so sound is fastest in solids:

MediumApprox. speed of sound
Air (20 °C)≈ 340 m s⁻¹
Water≈ 1500 m s⁻¹
Steel≈ 5000 m s⁻¹
  • In a gas, speed also rises with temperature: hotter air → faster-moving molecules → sound travels faster ($v \propto \sqrt{T_{\text{abs}}}$).
  • Light is the opposite of sound here — light travels fastest in a vacuum and slows in denser media; sound cannot travel in a vacuum at all.
Why you hear the train through the rail first: put your ear to a steel rail and the sound of an approaching train arrives long before it reaches you through the air — because it travels ~15× faster in steel.

Electromagnetic Waves & Refractive Index

All EM waves travel at $c = 3.0\times10^8$ m s⁻¹ in a vacuum. In a transparent medium they slow down, described by the refractive index n:

$$n = \frac{c}{v}$$
  • n ≥ 1 always, since v ≤ c. Vacuum n = 1; air ≈ 1.00; water ≈ 1.33; glass ≈ 1.5.
  • Higher n → slower light in that medium ($v = c/n$).
  • When light enters a new medium its frequency stays the same; the speed changes, so the wavelength changes: $\lambda_{\text{medium}} = \lambda_{\text{vacuum}}/n$.

The frequency rule (both sound and light)

At any boundary, frequency is conserved — set by the source. Speed is set by the medium. Whatever's left, the wavelength, must adjust: $\lambda = v/f$. This single rule underlies refraction (next comes up in C.2.7).

Worked Examples

Example 1 — speed on a string

A string of mass per unit length 0.020 kg m⁻¹ is under a tension of 80 N. Find the wave speed.

$v = \sqrt{T/\mu} = \sqrt{80/0.020} = \sqrt{4000}$

$= \mathbf{63\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$

Example 2 — speed of light in glass

Glass has refractive index n = 1.5. Find the speed of light in the glass.

$v = c/n = (3.0\times10^8)/1.5$

$= \mathbf{2.0\times10^8\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$

Example 3 — wavelength change in water

Light of vacuum wavelength 600 nm enters water (n = 1.33). Find its wavelength in water. Does its colour change?

$\lambda_{\text{water}} = \lambda_0/n = 600/1.33 = \mathbf{451\ \text{nm}}$

Colour does not change — frequency (which we perceive as colour) is unchanged; only the wavelength shortens.

(a) State the two types of property of a medium that determine the speed of a mechanical wave, and how each affects the speed.

A restoring/elastic (stiffness) property — a stronger restoring force increases the wave speed [1]. An inertial (mass/density) property — greater mass per length or density decreases the wave speed [1].

(b) A wire of length 1.2 m and mass 24 g is under a tension of 50 N. Calculate the speed of a transverse wave on it.

$\mu = m/L = 0.024/1.2 = 0.020$ kg m⁻¹ [1]
$v = \sqrt{T/\mu} = \sqrt{50/0.020} = \sqrt{2500}$ [1]
$= \mathbf{50\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) The refractive index of a diamond is 2.4. Calculate the speed of light inside the diamond.

$v = c/n = (3.0\times10^8)/2.4$ [1] $= \mathbf{1.25\times10^8\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why, when a sound wave passes from air into water, its wavelength increases even though its frequency is unchanged.

Frequency is fixed by the source and is conserved across the boundary [1]. Water is much stiffer than air so the wave speed is far greater; since $v = f\lambda$ with f constant, a larger v means a larger wavelength [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Speed is a property of the medium, not the source: $v \sim \sqrt{\text{stiffness}/\text{inertia}}$.
  • String: $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$ — higher tension faster, higher mass/length slower.
  • Sound: solids > liquids > gases; in a gas $v$ rises with temperature; no sound in vacuum.
  • Light: $c = 3.0\times10^8$ m s⁻¹ in vacuum; slows in media; $n = c/v \ge 1$.
  • Wavelength in a medium: $\lambda_{\text{medium}} = \lambda_{\text{vacuum}}/n$.
  • At a boundary: frequency is conserved; speed set by medium; wavelength adjusts.
