What Does SUVAT Stand For?

Five letters, five variables that describe any uniformly accelerated motion:

S
Displacement
s (metres)
U
Initial velocity
u (m/s)
V
Final velocity
v (m/s)
A
Acceleration
a (m/s²)
T
Time
t (seconds)
CRITICAL: SUVAT equations ONLY work when acceleration is constant (uniform). If $a$ is changing, you cannot use them.
Strategy: In every problem, list what you know (3 variables) and what you want. Then pick the equation that contains exactly those 4 variables.

The Four SUVAT Equations

These are given in the IB Data Booklet. You don't need to memorise them — but you must know when to use each one.

Equation 1 — no $s$

$$v = u + at$$

Use when you don't need displacement

Equation 2 — no $v$

$$s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$

Use when you don't know final velocity

Equation 3 — no $t$

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

Use when time is not given or needed

Equation 4 — no $a$

$$s = \tfrac{(u + v)}{2} \cdot t$$

Use when acceleration isn't needed

FYI: Each equation is "missing" one variable. Identify which variable you don't know AND don't need — that tells you which equation to pick.

Deriving $v = u + at$

Start from the definition of acceleration:

1Acceleration = rate of change of velocity: $\quad a = \frac{v - u}{t}$
2Multiply both sides by $t$: $\quad at = v - u$
3Rearrange for $v$: $\quad v = u + at$
$$\boxed{v = u + at}$$
Physical meaning: The final velocity equals the initial velocity plus however much the velocity changed ($a \times t$). If $a$ is negative, the object slows down.

Deriving $s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$ and $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$

Equation 2

1Displacement = area under v-t graph
2Area = rectangle + triangle
3$s = u \cdot t + \tfrac{1}{2}(v-u) \cdot t$
4Since $v - u = at$:
$$\boxed{s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2}$$

Equation 3

1From Eq 1: $t = \frac{v - u}{a}$
2Substitute into Eq 4: $s = \frac{(u+v)}{2} \cdot \frac{(v-u)}{a}$
3$s = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a}$
4Rearrange:
$$\boxed{v^2 = u^2 + 2as}$$
You don't need to derive these in the exam — but understanding where they come from helps you remember which one to use and catch sign errors.

The 5-Step SUVAT Method

Use this every single time. It prevents silly mistakes and gets full marks for working.

1Draw a diagram. Label direction as positive (+). Show the object, start, end.
2List SUVAT. Write $s = ?, \; u = ?, \; v = ?, \; a = ?, \; t = ?$. Fill in what you know.
3Identify the unknown you want and the variable you don't have.
4Pick the equation that contains your 3 knowns + 1 unknown (and is missing the variable you don't have).
5Substitute, solve, state units and direction. Check the sign of your answer makes physical sense.
IB marking: Examiners award marks for: correct identification of variables [1], correct equation [1], correct substitution [1], correct answer with units [1]. Skipping steps loses marks even if the final answer is right.

Worked Example 1 3/10

Problem: A car accelerates uniformly from rest to 25 m s$^{-1}$ in 10 s. Find (a) the acceleration (b) the distance travelled.

Step 1–2: List SUVAT

s = ?
u = 0 m/s (from rest)
v = 25 m/s
a = ?
t = 10 s

(a) Find $a$ — don't have $s$

→Use $v = u + at$ (no $s$)
$$25 = 0 + a(10) \implies a = \mathbf{2.5 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$$

(b) Find $s$ — have all 5 now

→Use $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$
$$s = 0(10) + \tfrac{1}{2}(2.5)(10^2) = \mathbf{125 \text{ m}}$$

Worked Example 2 5/10

Problem: A ball is thrown vertically upward at 20 m s$^{-1}$ from the top of a 45 m building. Find (a) the maximum height above the ground (b) the time to reach the ground (c) the velocity just before hitting the ground.

SUVAT (taking up as +)

u = +20 m/s (up)
a = −9.8 m/s² (gravity down)
At top: v = 0

(a) Height above launch point

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as \implies 0 = 400 + 2(-9.8)s$$
$$s = \frac{400}{19.6} = 20.4 \text{ m above launch}$$

Max height above ground = 45 + 20.4 = 65.4 m

(b) Total time to ground

Total displacement from launch to ground = $-45$ m (downward)

$$-45 = 20t + \tfrac{1}{2}(-9.8)t^2$$
$$4.9t^2 - 20t - 45 = 0$$

Quadratic: $t = \frac{20 \pm \sqrt{400 + 882}}{9.8} = \frac{20 \pm 35.8}{9.8}$

$t = 5.69$ s (take positive root)

(c) Velocity at ground

$$v^2 = 20^2 + 2(-9.8)(-45) = 400 + 882 = 1282 \implies v = -35.8 \text{ m s}^{-1} \text{ (downward)}$$

Worked Example 3 — Two-Stage Problem 7/10

Problem: A car travelling at 30 m s$^{-1}$ approaches a red light. The driver takes 0.8 s to react (constant speed), then brakes at $-5.0$ m s$^{-2}$. Find the total stopping distance.

Stage 1: Reaction (constant speed)

1$s_1 = v \times t = 30 \times 0.8 = \mathbf{24 \text{ m}}$ (thinking distance)

Stage 2: Braking (uniform deceleration)

u = 30 m/s, v = 0, a = −5.0 m/s², s₂ = ?
2Use $v^2 = u^2 + 2as_2$: $\quad 0 = 900 + 2(-5.0)s_2$
$$s_2 = \frac{900}{10} = \mathbf{90 \text{ m}} \text{ (braking distance)}$$

Total

$$s_{total} = s_1 + s_2 = 24 + 90 = \mathbf{114 \text{ m}}$$
Real-world link: This is exactly how stopping distances are calculated for road safety. Faster speed → dramatically longer stopping distance (because $s_2 \propto u^2$).

IB-Style Question Set 1 6/10

Context: A stone is dropped from rest from the top of a cliff. Air resistance is negligible. Take $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the velocity of the stone after 3.0 seconds.

$v = u + at = 0 + 9.8(3.0) = \mathbf{29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ downward [1 for formula, 1 for answer]
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the distance fallen in this time.

$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(3.0)^2 = \mathbf{44.1 \text{ m}}$ [1 for correct equation, 1 for answer]
Determine [3]

(c) The stone hits the sea 4.5 s after release. Determine the height of the cliff.

$s = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(4.5)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(20.25) = \mathbf{99.2 \text{ m}}$ [1 eq, 1 sub, 1 ans]
Explain [2]

(d) In reality, the stone would take slightly longer than 4.5 s. Explain why.

Air resistance acts upward [1], opposing motion, reducing the net downward acceleration [1]. With lower effective acceleration, the stone takes longer to fall the same distance.

IB-Style Question Set 2 8/10

Context: A car travelling at 25 m s$^{-1}$ on a straight road begins to decelerate uniformly. After travelling 50 m, its speed is 15 m s$^{-1}$.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the deceleration of the car.

$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $15^2 = 25^2 + 2a(50)$ → $225 = 625 + 100a$ → $a = \frac{-400}{100} = \mathbf{-4.0 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the time taken for the speed to decrease from 25 m s$^{-1}$ to 15 m s$^{-1}$.

$v = u + at$: $15 = 25 + (-4.0)t$ → $t = \frac{-10}{-4.0} = \mathbf{2.5 \text{ s}}$
Determine [3]

(c) The car continues to decelerate at the same rate until it stops. Determine the total distance from the point where braking began to where the car stops.

For the full stop: $v^2 = u^2 + 2as_{total}$: $0 = 625 + 2(-4.0)s$ → $s = \frac{625}{8.0} = \mathbf{78.1 \text{ m}}$
(Not 50 + something — recalculate from original speed to rest)
Explain [2]

(d) The driver claims the braking distance would halve if the initial speed were halved. Comment on this claim using your answer to (c).

Since $s \propto u^2$ (from $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ with $v=0$), halving $u$ gives $s' = \frac{(12.5)^2}{8.0} = 19.5$ m. This is roughly one quarter, not half [1]. The claim is incorrect — braking distance is proportional to the square of speed [1].

IB-Style Question Set 3 — Two Objects 9/10

Context: Ball A is thrown vertically upward at 15 m s$^{-1}$ from ground level. At the same instant, Ball B is dropped from rest from a height of 20 m directly above. Ignore air resistance. $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$.
Show that [3]

(a) Show that the two balls meet after approximately 1.3 s.

Ball A height: $h_A = 15t - 4.9t^2$
Ball B height: $h_B = 20 - 4.9t^2$
Meet when $h_A = h_B$: $15t - 4.9t^2 = 20 - 4.9t^2$
$15t = 20 \implies t = 20/15 = \mathbf{1.33 \text{ s}}$ ✓
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the height at which they meet.

$h = 20 - 4.9(1.33)^2 = 20 - 4.9(1.78) = 20 - 8.7 = \mathbf{11.3 \text{ m}}$
Determine [3]

(c) Determine the velocity of each ball at the moment they meet.

Ball A: $v_A = 15 + (-9.8)(1.33) = 15 - 13.0 = \mathbf{+2.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ (still going up)
Ball B: $v_B = 0 + (-9.8)(1.33) = \mathbf{-13.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ (going down)
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why the $4.9t^2$ terms cancel in part (a).

Both balls experience the same gravitational acceleration ($g$) [1]. The $\frac{1}{2}gt^2$ term represents the effect of gravity, which is identical for both — so when equating heights, gravity cancels, and only the initial conditions determine when they meet [1].

Quick Fire — Which Equation?

For each problem, identify which SUVAT equation you would use. Click to check.

1. Know $u$, $a$, $t$. Find $v$. Easy

$v = u + at$ (Eq 1 — missing $s$)

2. Know $u$, $v$, $a$. Find $s$. No time given. Easy

$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ (Eq 3 — missing $t$)

3. Object dropped from rest, find distance after time $t$. Medium

$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$. Since $u=0$: $s = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$ (Eq 2 — missing $v$)

4. Object thrown up at $u$, find max height. Medium

At max height, $v = 0$. Use $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $0 = u^2 - 2gs \implies s = \frac{u^2}{2g}$ (Eq 3)

5. Car brakes from $u$ to rest over distance $s$. Find time. Hard

Use Eq 4: $s = \frac{(u+v)}{2} \times t = \frac{u}{2} \times t \implies t = \frac{2s}{u}$ (since $v=0$)

Key Takeaways

  • SUVAT = 5 variables: $s$, $u$, $v$, $a$, $t$. Know 3, find the rest.
  • Only for uniform acceleration — if $a$ varies, use graphs or calculus.
  • 4 equations, each missing one variable. Pick the one that matches your knowns + unknown.
  • 5-step method: Diagram → List SUVAT → Identify unknowns → Pick equation → Solve with units.
  • Signs matter: Define + direction first. "Up = +" means $g = -9.8$. Be consistent.
  • Multi-stage problems: Split into stages. The $v$ of stage 1 becomes the $u$ of stage 2.
  • $s \propto u^2$: Double the speed → quadruple the braking distance.
