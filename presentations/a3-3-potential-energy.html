Potential energy is stored energy — energy waiting to be converted. Lift a ball and it gains gravitational PE. Compress a spring and it stores elastic PE. Release either one and that stored energy transforms into motion.

Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational PE is the energy stored in an object due to its height above a reference level.

$$E_p = mgh$$
  • $m$ = mass (kg), $g$ = 9.8 N/kg, $h$ = height above reference (m)
  • Scalar — always measured relative to a chosen reference level
  • The choice of $h = 0$ is arbitrary — only CHANGES in PE matter
  • Derived from work done against gravity: $W = Fs = (mg)(h) = mgh$
$h$ is the VERTICAL height, not the path length. Whether you climb straight up or take a winding staircase, the PE gained is the same — it only depends on the vertical change in height. This is because gravity is a conservative force.
Path independence: GPE depends only on initial and final heights, not on the path taken. This is the defining property of a conservative force. Friction is NOT conservative — energy lost depends on path length.

Elastic Potential Energy

Elastic PE is energy stored in a deformed elastic object (stretched or compressed spring, bent bow, rubber band).

$$E_{elastic} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$
  • $k$ = spring constant (N m$^{-1}$) — stiffness of the spring
  • $x$ = extension or compression from natural length (m)
  • Always positive — whether stretched or compressed
  • Derived from area under F-x graph: triangle = $\frac{1}{2} \times kx \times x$

Hooke's Law (prerequisite):

$$F = kx$$

Force is proportional to extension — the spring's F-x graph is a straight line through the origin with gradient $k$.

Hooke's Law has a limit. Beyond the elastic limit, the spring deforms permanently and $F \neq kx$. The elastic PE formula only works within the elastic limit.

GPE vs Elastic PE — Side by Side

Gravitational PE

$$E_p = mgh$$
  • Due to position in a gravitational field
  • Proportional to $h$ (linear)
  • Reference level is arbitrary
  • Only vertical height matters (path independent)
  • Gravity is a conservative force

Elastic PE

$$E_{el} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$
  • Due to deformation of an elastic object
  • Proportional to $x^2$ (quadratic)
  • Reference is natural length ($x = 0$)
  • Same energy for stretch or compression
  • Spring force is conservative (within elastic limit)
Both are forms of stored energy that can be converted to kinetic energy. A ball at height $h$ has GPE that converts to KE as it falls. A compressed spring has elastic PE that converts to KE when released.
The $x^2$ in elastic PE matters: Double the compression → 4× the stored energy. This is why springs launch objects much faster with a small extra compression — the energy scales quadratically.

Worked Example 1 — GPE 3/10

A 2.0 kg ball is lifted 5.0 m above the floor. Find its GPE relative to the floor.
$$E_p = mgh = 2.0 \times 9.8 \times 5.0 = \mathbf{98 \text{ J}}$$

Worked Example 2 — Spring 5/10

A spring ($k = 400$ N/m) is compressed by 0.20 m. Find the elastic PE stored. If released, what speed does a 0.50 kg ball reach?
1$E_{el} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}(400)(0.04) = \mathbf{8.0 \text{ J}}$
2All elastic PE → KE: $\frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{kx^2}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{400 \times 0.04}{0.50}} = \sqrt{32} = \mathbf{5.66 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$

Worked Example 3 — Height from incline 6/10

A ball slides (frictionless) down a 4.0 m incline at 30°. Find its speed at the bottom using energy.
1Vertical height: $h = 4.0 \sin 30° = 2.0$ m
2$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ → mass cancels → $v = \sqrt{2gh}$
$$v = \sqrt{2(9.8)(2.0)} = \sqrt{39.2} = \mathbf{6.26 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$
Energy method is faster than SUVAT here — no need to find acceleration or resolve forces. Just use vertical height.

Spring Problems — Finding $k$ 6/10

A 0.30 kg mass hangs from a spring and stretches it by 0.12 m. Find (a) the spring constant (b) the elastic PE stored.

(a) At equilibrium: spring force = weight

→$kx = mg$: $k(0.12) = 0.30(9.8) = 2.94$
$$k = \frac{2.94}{0.12} = \mathbf{24.5 \text{ N m}^{-1}}$$

(b) Elastic PE:

$$E_{el} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}(24.5)(0.0144) = \mathbf{0.176 \text{ J}}$$

Worked Example — Spring Launcher 8/10

A vertical spring ($k = 500$ N/m) is compressed by 0.10 m. A 0.20 kg ball sits on top. When released, how high does the ball go above the compressed position?
1$E_{elastic} = \frac{1}{2}(500)(0.01) = 2.5$ J
2At max height: all energy → GPE: $2.5 = mgh$
$$h = \frac{2.5}{0.20 \times 9.8} = \frac{2.5}{1.96} = \mathbf{1.28 \text{ m}}$$
Careful: This height is above the compressed position, not above the natural length. For precision, you'd subtract the compression distance (0.10 m) if measuring from natural length. IB usually specifies which reference.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A roller coaster car of mass 500 kg starts from rest at the top of a 40 m hill. The track is frictionless.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the GPE of the car at the top of the hill (relative to the bottom).

$E_p = mgh = 500 \times 9.8 \times 40 = \mathbf{196{,}000 \text{ J}} = 196$ kJ [2]
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the speed of the car at the bottom of the hill.

$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ → $v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(40)} = \sqrt{784} = \mathbf{28.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Calculate [2]

(c) The car then goes over a second hill of height 25 m. Find its speed at the top of this hill.

$\frac{1}{2}mv_1^2 = mgh_2 + \frac{1}{2}mv_2^2$ → $v_2 = \sqrt{2g(h_1 - h_2)} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(15)} = \mathbf{17.1 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Determine [2]

(d) What is the maximum height of a second hill that the car can just reach (speed = 0 at top)?

All KE → GPE: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mgh_{max}$. Since it started at 40 m with $v = 0$: $h_{max} = 40$ m [1]. The car can never go higher than the starting height (no friction — energy conservation) [1].
Explain [2]

(e) In reality, the car could not reach 40 m on the second hill. Explain why.

Friction and air resistance do negative work on the car [1]. Some energy is converted to heat, so less is available as GPE. The car reaches a height less than 40 m [1].

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A 0.50 kg ball is 10 m above the ground. Find its GPE.

$E_p = mgh = 0.50 \times 9.8 \times 10 = \mathbf{49 \text{ J}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A spring ($k = 300$ N/m) launches a 0.10 kg ball from compression of 0.08 m. Find the launch speed.

$\frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$: $\frac{1}{2}(300)(0.0064) = \frac{1}{2}(0.10)v^2$ [1]
$0.96 = 0.05v^2$ → $v = \sqrt{19.2} = \mathbf{4.38 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Hard

A 2.0 kg block slides from rest down a 5.0 m frictionless ramp at 35° and compresses a spring ($k = 800$ N/m) at the bottom. Find the maximum compression.

$h = 5\sin35° = 2.87$ m. GPE → elastic PE:
$mgh = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$: $2(9.8)(2.87) = \frac{1}{2}(800)x^2$ [1]
$56.3 = 400x^2$ → $x = \sqrt{0.141} = \mathbf{0.375 \text{ m}}$ [2]
Explain [2] Hard

Two paths lead from A (top of a hill) to B (bottom): a straight slide and a curved slide. Both are frictionless. Which gives a higher speed at B?

Same speed. On a frictionless surface, only vertical height matters [1]. Both paths have the same $\Delta h$, so the same GPE is converted to KE. $v = \sqrt{2g\Delta h}$ regardless of path shape — this is what "conservative force" means [1].

Key Takeaways

  • GPE = $mgh$ — depends on vertical height only. Path doesn't matter (conservative).
  • Elastic PE = $\frac{1}{2}kx^2$ — energy stored in deformed springs. Scales with $x^2$.
  • Hooke's Law: $F = kx$. F-x graph is straight line. Area = elastic PE.
  • Reference level for GPE is arbitrary — only changes matter.
  • Energy conversions: GPE ↔ KE (falling/rising). Elastic PE ↔ KE (spring release).
  • Frictionless incline: speed at bottom = $\sqrt{2gh}$ regardless of incline angle or path.
  • Spring launcher: $\frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ (horizontal) or $= mgh$ (vertical).
