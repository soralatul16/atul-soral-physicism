Aristotle vs Newton

Aristotle (Wrong)

"An object in motion needs a continuous force to keep it moving. Remove the force and it stops."

This seems intuitive — push a box and it stops when you stop pushing. But Aristotle was confused by friction.

Newton (Correct)

"An object doesn't stop because you removed the force — it stops because friction is a force opposing motion. Remove friction, and it keeps going forever."

Real example: A hockey puck on ice barely slows down — very little friction. In space, objects travel forever with zero force. Voyager 1 is still moving 47 years after its engines stopped.
TOK: Why did Aristotle's incorrect theory survive 2,000 years? Because everyday experience confirms it — we always see objects slow down. It took Newton's genius to realize that friction was the hidden variable.

Newton's First Law — The Law of Inertia

An object remains at rest, or continues to move in a straight line at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.

This has two parts:

Part 1: At rest → stays at rest

A book on a table doesn't spontaneously start moving. It stays at rest until something pushes it.

$\sum F = 0 \implies$ object stays at rest.

Part 2: Moving → keeps moving

An object in motion continues at the SAME speed in the SAME direction. No force is needed to maintain constant velocity.

$\sum F = 0 \implies$ constant velocity (not necessarily zero).

$$\sum \vec{F} = 0 \iff \vec{a} = 0 \iff \text{constant velocity (or rest)}$$
The most misunderstood law: "No force means no motion" is WRONG. No NET force means no CHANGE in motion. An object can be moving at 1000 km/h with zero net force — it just won't speed up, slow down, or turn.

Inertia — Resistance to Change

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. The more mass an object has, the more inertia it has.

  • Mass is the measure of inertia — heavier objects are harder to accelerate
  • A bowling ball has more inertia than a tennis ball — harder to start, harder to stop
  • Inertia is NOT a force — it's a property of matter

Everyday Examples of Inertia:

  • Seatbelts: Car stops suddenly, your body wants to keep moving forward (inertia)
  • Tablecloth trick: Pull quickly — plates stay put because their inertia resists the brief force
  • Wet dog shake: Dog's body changes direction, water droplets keep going straight (inertia)
  • Bus braking: You lurch forward — your body was moving and wants to continue

Translational Equilibrium

An object is in translational equilibrium when the net force on it is zero.

$$\sum \vec{F} = 0 \implies \vec{a} = 0$$

This means either:

  • Static equilibrium: object is at rest and stays at rest ($v = 0$)
  • Dynamic equilibrium: object moves at constant velocity ($v \neq 0$ but $a = 0$)

Static Equilibrium

Book on table. Bridge. Building.

$v = 0$, $a = 0$, $\sum F = 0$

Dynamic Equilibrium

Car at constant speed. Plane cruising. Skydiver at terminal velocity.

$v \neq 0$, $a = 0$, $\sum F = 0$

Key insight: "Equilibrium" does NOT mean "at rest." A car moving at 100 km/h on a straight road with engine force = friction is in dynamic equilibrium. $\sum F = 0$, so $a = 0$.
In 2D equilibrium: $\sum F_x = 0$ AND $\sum F_y = 0$ must BOTH be true. Solve each direction separately.

Solving Equilibrium Problems

If an object is in equilibrium ($a = 0$), set $\sum F = 0$ in each direction.

1Draw FBD — identify ALL forces on the object
2Choose axes — usually horizontal (x) and vertical (y)
3Resolve any angled forces into x and y components
4Apply $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$ separately
5Solve the simultaneous equations for unknowns

Worked Example: 5/10

A 200 N sign hangs from two wires, each at 30° to the horizontal. Find the tension in each wire.
y$\sum F_y = 0$: $T\sin30° + T\sin30° - 200 = 0 \implies 2T(0.5) = 200$
$$T = \frac{200}{2 \times 0.5} = \frac{200}{1} = \mathbf{200 \text{ N}}$$
Notice: Each wire has tension GREATER than $W/2$! At 30°, $T = W$ (same as the weight). At smaller angles, $T$ gets even larger. This is why washing lines sag — supporting weight at small angles requires enormous tension.

Worked Example — Angled Strings 7/10

A 50 N lamp hangs from two strings. String A makes 40° with the ceiling. String B makes 55° with the ceiling. Find $T_A$ and $T_B$.

Resolve and set $\sum F = 0$:

x-direction:

$$T_A\cos50° - T_B\cos35° = 0$$

(Angles from vertical: 90-40=50°, 90-55=35°)

y-direction:

$$T_A\sin50° + T_B\sin35° - 50 = 0$$

From x-eq:

$$T_A = T_B \cdot \frac{\cos35°}{\cos50°} = 1.274 \cdot T_B$$

Substitute into y-eq:

$$1.274T_B(0.766) + T_B(0.574) = 50$$
$$T_B(0.976 + 0.574) = 50 \implies T_B = \mathbf{32.3 \text{ N}}$$
$$T_A = 1.274 \times 32.3 = \mathbf{41.1 \text{ N}}$$

Equilibrium with Connected Objects 8/10

A 3.0 kg mass hangs from a string attached to the ceiling. A second string connects it to a 2.0 kg mass below. Find the tension in each string.

Start from the BOTTOM mass:

FBD of 2 kg: $T_2$ up, $W_2 = 19.6$ N down.

1$\sum F = 0$: $T_2 - 19.6 = 0 \implies T_2 = \mathbf{19.6 \text{ N}}$

Then the TOP mass:

FBD of 3 kg: $T_1$ up, $W_1 = 29.4$ N down, $T_2 = 19.6$ N down (string pulls it).

2$T_1 - 29.4 - 19.6 = 0 \implies T_1 = \mathbf{49.0 \text{ N}}$
Key insight: $T_1$ supports BOTH masses. $T_1 = (3+2)(9.8) = 49.0$ N. The top string always has the highest tension. This is why chains break at the top link.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A 1500 kg car travels at constant velocity of 25 m s$^{-1}$ on a straight, level road. The total resistive force on the car is 800 N.
State [1]

(a) State Newton's first law of motion.

An object remains at rest or continues to move at constant velocity unless acted upon by a net (unbalanced/resultant) external force [1].
Explain [2]

(b) Explain, with reference to Newton's first law, how the car can be moving at constant velocity while forces act on it.

The driving force equals the resistive force [1], so the net force is zero. By Newton's first law, zero net force means zero acceleration, so the car continues at constant velocity [1].
Calculate [1]

(c) Calculate the driving force of the engine.

Constant velocity → $\sum F = 0$ → driving force = resistive force = $\mathbf{800 \text{ N}}$ [1]
Explain [3]

(d) The driver now increases the engine force to 1200 N. Describe and explain the subsequent motion of the car.

Net force = 1200 - 800 = 400 N forward [1]. The car accelerates ($a = 400/1500 = 0.267$ m s$^{-2}$) [1]. As speed increases, resistive force increases until it equals 1200 N, at which point net force = 0 again and the car reaches a new, higher constant velocity [1].
Explain [2]

(e) The car suddenly drives onto an icy patch where friction drops to zero. The driver removes their foot from the accelerator (engine force = 0). Describe the motion.

With no friction and no engine force, $\sum F = 0$ [1]. By Newton's first law, the car continues at constant velocity in a straight line — it does NOT slow down without friction [1]. This is why cars slide on ice.

Practice Questions

Explain [2] Easy

A person pushes a box across the floor at constant speed. Is the box in equilibrium? Explain.

Yes — constant speed means $a = 0$, so $\sum F = 0$ [1]. The push force equals the friction force. This is dynamic equilibrium [1].
Calculate [3] Medium

A 20 N weight hangs from two strings, both at 45° to the horizontal. Find the tension in each string.

$\sum F_y = 0$: $2T\sin45° = 20 \implies T = \frac{20}{2 \times 0.707} = \mathbf{14.1 \text{ N}}$ [3]
Check: $T > W/2$ (14.1 > 10) because the strings are angled.
Explain [2] Hard

A ball is thrown horizontally from a cliff. After leaving the hand, is it in equilibrium? What force(s) act on it?

No — only weight acts (downward) [1]. There is no horizontal force but the ball decelerates vertically (accelerates downward). $\sum F \neq 0$, so it is NOT in equilibrium. It accelerates at $g$ downward [1].
Explain [2] Hard

A skydiver reaches terminal velocity. Is this an example of Newton's first law? Explain.

Yes [1]. At terminal velocity, drag = weight, so $\sum F = 0$. The skydiver continues at constant velocity (not accelerating). This is dynamic equilibrium — Newton's first law in action [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Newton's 1st Law: No net force → no change in velocity (rest or constant $v$).
  • Inertia = resistance to change. Mass measures inertia.
  • Equilibrium: $\sum F = 0$. Static (at rest) or dynamic (constant $v$).
  • "No force = no motion" is WRONG. No net force = no CHANGE in motion.
  • Solving equilibrium: Draw FBD, resolve forces, set $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$.
  • Connected objects: Start from the bottom/simplest mass, work upward.
  • Angled strings: $T > W/2$ always. Smaller angle → greater tension.
