Electrical Power — Three Equivalent Formulas

Power is the rate of energy transfer. Starting from $P = \dfrac{W}{t} = \dfrac{VIt}{t}$:

$$P = VI = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$
  • $P = VI$ — the base definition; works for any component.
  • $P = I^2R$ — substitute $V = IR$. Best when you know the current and resistance.
  • $P = V^2/R$ — substitute $I = V/R$. Best when you know the voltage and resistance.
  • All three are the same equation combined with Ohm's law — pick whichever matches your known quantities.
Units: power in watts (W); 1 W = 1 J s⁻¹ = 1 volt-ampere. A 60 W bulb transfers 60 joules of energy every second.

Quick example

A 3.0 A current flows through a 20 Ω heater. Find the power dissipated.

$P = I^2R = (3.0)^2\times20 = 9.0\times20 = \mathbf{180\ \text{W}}$

A Subtle Point: Series vs Parallel Power

The formula you pick must match what's actually held constant in the circuit — this catches many students out.

Same current (series)

Use $P = I^2R$. With current fixed, power ∝ R — the bigger resistor dissipates more power.

Same voltage (parallel)

Use $P = V^2/R$. With voltage fixed, power ∝ 1/R — the smaller resistor dissipates more power.

Classic exam trap. "Which resistor gets hottest?" depends on the wiring. In series it's the largest R; in parallel it's the smallest R. Always identify whether current or voltage is the shared quantity first.

Where does the energy go?

In a resistor, electrical energy becomes heat (electrons colliding with the lattice). In a motor, most becomes mechanical work; in a lamp, light + heat. The power formulas give the total rate of energy conversion.

Energy and the Kilowatt-Hour

$$W = Pt = VIt$$

Electrical energy in joules is power × time. But joules are tiny for household use, so energy companies bill in kilowatt-hours (kWh).

The kilowatt-hour

1 kWh = the energy used by a 1 kW device running for 1 hour.

$$1\ \text{kWh} = 1000\ \text{W} \times 3600\ \text{s} = 3.6\times10^{6}\ \text{J}$$

Energy in kWh = power (in kW) × time (in hours). This is the "unit" on your electricity bill.

Worked example — the bill

A 2.0 kW heater runs for 3.0 hours. If electricity costs ₹8 per kWh, find the energy used and the cost.

Energy $= 2.0\ \text{kW} \times 3.0\ \text{h} = 6.0$ kWh.

Cost $= 6.0 \times 8 = \mathbf{₹48}$ (and $6.0\ \text{kWh} = 2.16\times10^{7}$ J)

Household Circuits: Live, Neutral & Earth

  • Live wire: carries the alternating supply voltage (~230 V in India/UK). Dangerous — the one that shocks you.
  • Neutral wire: completes the circuit, kept near 0 V (earth potential).
  • Earth wire: a safety path connected to the appliance's metal casing and to ground.
Appliances connect across live and neutral (in parallel with each other), so each receives the full mains voltage and can be switched independently.

Safety Devices: Fuses & Earthing

The fuse

A thin wire in the live line that melts if the current exceeds a rating, breaking the circuit before wires overheat and cause a fire. Choose a rating just above the appliance's normal current.

The earth wire

Connects the metal casing to ground. If a live wire touches the casing, a large current flows to earth, blowing the fuse — so the casing never stays live and dangerous.

Choosing a fuse — worked example

A 920 W appliance runs on 230 V mains. What is its normal current, and which fuse (3 A, 5 A, or 13 A) is suitable?

$I = P/V = 920/230 = 4.0$ A.

Choose the next rating above 4.0 A → the 5 A fuse.

Double-insulated appliances (plastic casing, e.g. phone chargers, hair dryers) don't need an earth wire — no exposed metal can become live, so the plastic itself provides the protection.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Derive & Calculate [3]

(a) Show that electrical power can be written as I²R, and calculate the power dissipated in a 15 Ω resistor carrying 0.80 A.

Starting from $P = VI$ and substituting $V = IR$: $P = (IR)I = I^2R$ [1]. $P = (0.80)^2\times15$ [1] $= 0.64\times15 = \mathbf{9.6\ \text{W}}$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A household uses a 1.5 kW iron for 40 minutes and a 100 W lamp for 5.0 hours. Calculate the total energy in kWh and the cost at ₹7 per kWh.

Iron: $1.5\ \text{kW}\times(40/60)\ \text{h} = 1.0$ kWh. Lamp: $0.10\ \text{kW}\times5.0\ \text{h} = 0.50$ kWh [1]. Total $= 1.5$ kWh [1]. Cost $= 1.5\times7 = \mathbf{₹10.50}$ [1].
Explain [3]

(c) Explain the function of (i) the fuse and (ii) the earth wire in a household appliance with a metal casing.

(i) The fuse is a thin wire in the live line that melts and breaks the circuit if the current exceeds its rating, preventing overheating and fire [1]. (ii) The earth wire connects the metal casing to ground; if a fault makes the casing live, a large current flows through the low-resistance earth path [1], which blows the fuse and disconnects the supply, so the casing cannot remain at a dangerous voltage [1].
Apply [2]

(d) Two resistors, 10 Ω and 40 Ω, are connected in parallel across a 12 V supply. State which dissipates more power and justify using a formula.

In parallel the voltage is the same across both, so use $P = V^2/R$ [1]. Since $P \propto 1/R$, the smaller resistance (10 Ω) dissipates more power: $P_{10} = 12^2/10 = 14.4$ W versus $P_{40} = 144/40 = 3.6$ W [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Power: $P = VI = I^2R = V^2/R$ — same equation via Ohm's law; pick to match known quantities.
  • Series: use $P = I^2R$ (largest R hottest); parallel: use $P = V^2/R$ (smallest R hottest).
  • Energy: $W = Pt = VIt$; $1\ \text{kWh} = 3.6\times10^{6}$ J = one billed "unit".
  • Household wiring: live (dangerous), neutral (~0 V), earth (safety); appliances in parallel.
  • Fuse: melts on excess current (in the live wire). Earth: makes a fault blow the fuse.
  • Fuse rating: just above the appliance's normal current ($I = P/V$).
