C.2.1

Transverse & Longitudinal Waves

Two ways to carry a disturbance

Every wave transfers energy without transferring matter — but they split into two families by how the medium oscillates relative to the direction of travel. Getting this distinction crystal-clear, along with the shared vocabulary of amplitude, wavelength and frequency, is the foundation for everything else in wave physics.

What Is a Wave?

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy and momentum from one place to another without any net transfer of matter. The medium's particles oscillate about fixed positions (in SHM) while the disturbance travels on.

  • Mechanical waves need a medium to travel through (sound, water, seismic, waves on a string).
  • Electromagnetic waves need no medium and travel through vacuum (light, radio, X-rays) at $c = 3.0\times10^8$ m s⁻¹.
  • Pulse vs continuous: a single disturbance is a pulse; a repeating disturbance from an oscillating source is a continuous (periodic) wave.
The key test — put a cork on a pond: as ripples pass, the cork bobs up and down but does not travel across the pond. Energy moves outward; the water (and the cork) stays put. This is the signature of a wave.

Transverse Waves

In a transverse wave, the particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular (at 90°) to the direction in which the wave transfers energy.

  • Crest: the highest point (maximum positive displacement).
  • Trough: the lowest point (maximum negative displacement).
  • Examples: all electromagnetic waves (light, radio), waves on a string, water surface waves, S-waves in earthquakes.
  • Can be polarised — because the oscillation has a definable direction in the plane perpendicular to travel (covered in C.2.6).

Longitudinal Waves

In a longitudinal wave, the particles of the medium oscillate parallel to the direction in which the wave transfers energy — creating regions of compression and rarefaction.

  • Compression: region where particles are bunched together (high pressure/density).
  • Rarefaction: region where particles are spread apart (low pressure/density).
  • Wavelength = distance between adjacent compressions (or adjacent rarefactions).
  • Examples: sound waves, P-waves in earthquakes, ultrasound, a pushed slinky.
  • Cannot be polarised — the oscillation is along the travel direction, with no perpendicular direction to filter.

The Shared Wave Quantities

Both types share the same defining quantities:

  • Amplitude (x₀ or A): maximum displacement from equilibrium; sets the energy carried ($E \propto A^2$).
  • Wavelength (λ): the shortest distance between two points oscillating in phase (crest→crest, or compression→compression). Unit: metres.
  • Period (T): time for one complete oscillation of a particle. Unit: seconds.
  • Frequency (f): oscillations per second; $f = 1/T$. Unit: hertz.
  • Wave speed (v): speed the energy/pattern travels.
$$v = f\lambda = \frac{\lambda}{T}$$
Phase: two points are "in phase" if they move identically (both at a crest, moving the same way). Points exactly one wavelength apart are always in phase; points half a wavelength apart are in anti-phase.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — wave speed

A water wave has wavelength 2.5 m and frequency 0.40 Hz. Find its speed.

$v = f\lambda = 0.40\times2.5 = \mathbf{1.0\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$

Example 2 — wavelength of sound

A sound wave (longitudinal) of frequency 440 Hz travels at 340 m s⁻¹. Find its wavelength.

$\lambda = v/f = 340/440$

$= \mathbf{0.77\ \text{m}}$

Example 3 — classifying

Classify each as transverse or longitudinal: (i) light, (ii) sound, (iii) a wave on a rope, (iv) ultrasound.

(i) transverse (EM wave); (ii) longitudinal; (iii) transverse; (iv) longitudinal.

Distinguish [2]

(a) Distinguish between a transverse wave and a longitudinal wave.

In a transverse wave the particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer [1]. In a longitudinal wave the particles oscillate parallel to the direction of energy transfer, producing compressions and rarefactions [1].

(b) Define wavelength and state how it is identified on a longitudinal wave.

Wavelength is the shortest distance between two points on a wave that are oscillating in phase [1]. On a longitudinal wave it is the distance between two adjacent compressions (or two adjacent rarefactions) [1].

(c) A wave on a string has frequency 25 Hz and wavelength 0.12 m. Calculate the wave speed.

(d) A cork floats on water as waves pass. Explain what its motion demonstrates about wave energy transfer.

The cork oscillates up and down about a fixed position but does not travel along with the wave [1]. This shows that the wave transfers energy through the medium without any net transfer of matter — the water itself is not carried forward [1].

Key Takeaways

  • A wave transfers energy, not matter; particles oscillate in SHM about fixed points.
  • Transverse: particle motion ⊥ to travel; has crests and troughs; can be polarised (light, string, water, S-waves).
  • Longitudinal: particle motion ∥ to travel; has compressions and rarefactions; cannot be polarised (sound, P-waves, ultrasound).
  • Shared quantities: amplitude, wavelength λ, period T, frequency f = 1/T, speed v.
  • Wave equation: $v = f\lambda = \lambda/T$.
  • In phase = one wavelength apart; anti-phase = half a wavelength apart.
