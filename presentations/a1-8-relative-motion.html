A.1.8

Relative Motion

in 1D and 2D

How fast is a car moving? It depends on who's watching. A passenger says "zero." A person on the road says "100 km/h." Both are correct — in their own reference frame.

Atul Soral Physicism

A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative MotionCore Principle

Velocity is Always Relative

There is no such thing as "absolute velocity." All velocity is measured relative to something else — a reference frame.

Relative velocity is the velocity of object A as observed from object B. Notation: $\vec{v}_{A \text{ rel } B}$ or $\vec{v}_{AB}$.
$$\vec{v}_{AB} = \vec{v}_A - \vec{v}_B$$

Velocity of A relative to B = velocity of A minus velocity of B (both relative to ground)

Everyday example: You're on a train at 100 km/h. A car on a parallel road goes 80 km/h in the same direction. From your window, the car appears to move backward at 20 km/h. That's relative velocity.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative Motion1D — Same Direction

1D: Objects Moving in the Same Direction

When two objects move in the same direction, subtract their speeds to get relative velocity.

$$v_{AB} = v_A - v_B = 80 - 50 = +30 \text{ km/h}$$

Interpretation: From B's perspective, A is pulling ahead at 30 km/h. From A's perspective, B is falling behind at 30 km/h.

Note: $v_{AB} = -v_{BA}$. If A sees B going backward at 30, then B sees A going forward at 30. Always opposite.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative Motion1D — Opposite Direction

1D: Objects Moving in Opposite Directions

When two objects move toward each other, their relative speed is the sum of their individual speeds.

$$v_{AB} = v_A - v_B = 60 - (-40) = +100 \text{ km/h}$$
This is why head-on collisions are devastating. Two cars at 60 km/h approaching head-on: relative speed = 120 km/h. The impact is as if one car hits a wall at 120 km/h.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative Motion1D Summary

1D Relative Velocity — Summary

The formula is always the same: $v_{AB} = v_A - v_B$. The sign handles the direction.

FYI: Two cars driving side by side at the same speed: relative velocity = 0. From inside either car, the other appears completely stationary. This is why overtaking on a highway feels slow — relative speed is small.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative Motion2D

2D Relative Velocity — Vector Subtraction

In 2D, velocities are vectors. To find relative velocity, subtract vectors:

$$\vec{v}_{AB} = \vec{v}_A - \vec{v}_B$$

This means: draw $\vec{v}_A$, then draw $-\vec{v}_B$ (reverse B's arrow), and add them tip-to-tail.

Key step: Don't forget to REVERSE $\vec{v}_B$ before adding. Subtraction = add the negative. Many students draw $\vec{v}_B$ in its original direction and get the wrong answer.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative MotionClassic Problem

Classic Problem: River Crossing

A boat tries to cross a river. The boat has velocity relative to water, and the water has velocity relative to ground.

The equation:

$$\vec{v}_{BG} = \vec{v}_{BW} + \vec{v}_{WG}$$

Velocity of Boat relative to Ground = Velocity of Boat relative to Water + Velocity of Water relative to Ground

  • $\vec{v}_{BW}$ = what the boat engine produces (across river)
  • $\vec{v}_{WG}$ = river current (downstream)
  • $\vec{v}_{BG}$ = actual path of boat (diagonal)
If perpendicular: $v_{BG} = \sqrt{v_{BW}^2 + v_{WG}^2}$ and the boat drifts downstream while crossing.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative MotionWorked Example

Worked Example 1 — 1D 3/10

Problem: Car A travels East at 90 km/h. Car B travels East at 70 km/h in the same lane ahead. Find (a) velocity of A relative to B (b) velocity of B relative to A.
a$v_{AB} = v_A - v_B = 90 - 70 = \mathbf{+20 \text{ km/h East}}$

A sees B moving backward (or A approaches B at 20 km/h)

b$v_{BA} = v_B - v_A = 70 - 90 = \mathbf{-20 \text{ km/h}} = 20 \text{ km/h West}$

B sees A approaching from behind at 20 km/h

Check: $v_{AB} = -v_{BA}$ ✓ — always true. If A sees B going left, B sees A going right at the same speed.

Worked Example 2 — Head-on 5/10

Problem: Train A: 120 km/h East. Train B: 80 km/h West. Find relative velocity.
→$v_{AB} = v_A - v_B = 120 - (-80) = \mathbf{+200 \text{ km/h}}$

They approach each other at 200 km/h. From A's window, B flies past at 200 km/h.

Worked Example 3 — 2D River 7/10

Problem: A boat can travel at 5.0 m/s in still water. It aims directly across a river that flows at 3.0 m/s downstream. The river is 40 m wide. Find (a) the resultant speed (b) the direction of travel (c) the time to cross (d) how far downstream the boat lands.
a$v = \sqrt{5.0^2 + 3.0^2} = \sqrt{34} = \mathbf{5.83 \text{ m/s}}$
b$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{3.0}{5.0}\right) = \mathbf{31.0°}$ downstream of directly across
cTime depends on crossing (perpendicular) component only:
$$t = \frac{d}{v_{BW}} = \frac{40}{5.0} = \mathbf{8.0 \text{ s}}$$

The river current doesn't affect crossing time — only the across-river component matters.

dDrift: $x = v_R \times t = 3.0 \times 8.0 = \mathbf{24 \text{ m}}$ downstream
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative MotionReal World

Real-World: Rain and a Moving Person

Rain falls vertically at 8 m/s. You run horizontally at 6 m/s. From your perspective, the rain seems to come at an angle.

From your frame:

$$v_{rain, you} = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{100} = \mathbf{10 \text{ m/s}}$$
$$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{6}{8}\right) = \mathbf{36.9°} \text{ from vertical}$$

This is why you tilt your umbrella forward when running — the rain appears to come from ahead, not straight down.

The faster you run, the more you tilt. At very high speed, the rain would seem almost horizontal. This is the same principle behind stellar aberration in astronomy.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A plane has an airspeed (speed relative to air) of 250 km/h and needs to fly due North. A wind blows from West to East at 60 km/h.
Calculate [2]

(a) If the pilot aims directly North, calculate the resultant velocity (magnitude and direction).

$v = \sqrt{250^2 + 60^2} = \sqrt{66100} = \mathbf{257 \text{ km/h}}$
$\theta = \tan^{-1}(60/250) = 13.5°$ East of North [2]
The plane drifts eastward.
Determine [3]

(b) To fly due North (no drift), at what angle West of North must the pilot aim?

The pilot must aim into the wind to cancel it. $\sin\alpha = 60/250$ → $\alpha = \sin^{-1}(0.24) = \mathbf{13.9°}$ West of North [2]
Ground speed: $v = \sqrt{250^2 - 60^2} = \sqrt{58900} = \mathbf{243 \text{ km/h}}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) The destination is 500 km due North. How long does the flight take with the correction from (b)?

$t = \frac{500}{243} = \mathbf{2.06 \text{ hours}}$ (about 2 hours 4 minutes) [2]
Explain [2]

(d) The pilot takes longer than if there were no wind. Explain why, even though the wind is perpendicular to the intended direction.

The pilot must aim partly into the wind [1], which reduces the northward component of velocity. Ground speed (243) is less than airspeed (250) because some of the engine's effort is spent counteracting the crosswind [1].
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative MotionPractice

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A bus travels at 40 km/h. A passenger walks toward the front at 5 km/h (relative to the bus). What is the passenger's velocity relative to the ground?

$v_{PG} = v_{PB} + v_{BG} = 5 + 40 = \mathbf{45 \text{ km/h}}$ (same direction as bus)
Calculate [3] Medium

Two ships: Ship A moves North at 15 km/h, Ship B moves East at 20 km/h. Find the velocity of A relative to B.

$\vec{v}_{AB} = \vec{v}_A - \vec{v}_B$. Components: $v_{AB,x} = 0 - 20 = -20$, $v_{AB,y} = 15 - 0 = +15$
$v_{AB} = \sqrt{20^2+15^2} = \mathbf{25 \text{ km/h}}$ at $\tan^{-1}(15/20) = 36.9°$ N of W (i.e., from B, A appears to move NW)
Determine [3] Hard

A swimmer can swim at 2.0 m/s in still water. The river current is 1.5 m/s. At what angle upstream must the swimmer aim to cross the 30 m wide river in minimum time?

For minimum time, aim straight across (perpendicular). $t = 30/2.0 = \mathbf{15 \text{ s}}$
The swimmer will drift downstream: $d = 1.5 \times 15 = 22.5$ m.
Note: aiming upstream reduces drift but increases crossing time. Straight across minimizes time.
A.1 Kinematics  |  A.1.8 Relative MotionSummary

Key Takeaways

  • All velocity is relative. $\vec{v}_{AB} = \vec{v}_A - \vec{v}_B$.
  • $v_{AB} = -v_{BA}$ — always opposite.
  • Same direction: subtract speeds. Opposite: add speeds.
  • 2D: subtract vectors. Reverse $\vec{v}_B$, then add tip-to-tail. Use Pythagoras if perpendicular.
  • River/wind problems: $\vec{v}_{BG} = \vec{v}_{BW} + \vec{v}_{WG}$. Crossing time depends only on the perpendicular component.
  • Rain problem: running makes vertical rain appear diagonal. Tilt = $\tan^{-1}(v_{you}/v_{rain})$.
