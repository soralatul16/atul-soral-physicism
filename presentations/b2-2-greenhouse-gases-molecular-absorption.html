Why Some Molecules Absorb IR and Others Don't

N₂ and O₂ make up 99% of the atmosphere but are invisible to infrared. CO₂ at just 0.04% traps enough heat to warm the planet by 7°C. The reason lies in molecular structure — how atoms vibrate, bend, and stretch when photons hit them. This is where climate physics meets quantum mechanics.

The Fundamental Rule of IR Absorption

THE RULE

A molecule can absorb infrared radiation only if the vibration causes a change in its electric dipole moment.

Every molecule has bonds that vibrate at specific natural frequencies. When an IR photon arrives with a frequency matching one of these vibrations, the molecule absorbs the photon — but only if that particular vibration mode changes the molecule's charge distribution (dipole moment).

N₂ and O₂ — NOT greenhouse gases

Symmetric diatomic molecules. When they vibrate (stretch), both atoms move equally — the charge distribution stays perfectly symmetric. No dipole change → no IR absorption.

N≡N stretches but charge stays centered. Invisible to IR.

CO₂, H₂O, CH₄ — ARE greenhouse gases

3+ atoms with asymmetric vibrations. Bending and asymmetric stretching create temporary dipole moments. Each vibration mode absorbs at a specific IR wavelength.

CO₂ bends and the charge shifts — absorbs at 15 μm.

Even CO₂ is symmetric overall (O=C=O is linear, no permanent dipole). But its bending mode creates a temporary dipole. It doesn't need a permanent dipole — just a dipole change during vibration. This distinction catches many students.

Vibrational Modes of CO₂

CO₂ (O=C=O) is a linear triatomic molecule with 4 vibrational modes. Only some absorb IR:

① Symmetric stretch

Both O atoms move out equally. No dipole change. Does NOT absorb IR.

② Asymmetric stretch (4.26 μm)

One O moves in, other moves out. Charge shifts. ABSORBS IR at 4.26 μm.

③④ Bending modes (15 μm) — THE MOST IMPORTANT FOR CLIMATE

The 15 μm bending mode is why CO₂ matters for climate. Earth's emission peaks near 10 μm, but CO₂'s 15 μm band sits on the long-wavelength side, absorbing a significant fraction of outgoing IR. As CO₂ concentration rises, this band widens (pressure broadening), absorbing even more.

Vibrational Modes of H₂O

Water is a bent triatomic molecule (104.5° bond angle). Unlike CO₂, it has a permanent dipole. All three vibration modes change the dipole — so all three absorb IR.

Symmetric stretch

Both O–H bonds stretch out simultaneously.

Absorbs at ~2.73 μm

Dipole changes because molecule is bent (not linear)

Asymmetric stretch

One O–H bond stretches while other contracts.

Absorbs at ~2.66 μm

Strong dipole change

Bending (scissor)

Bond angle changes (opens and closes).

Absorbs at ~6.27 μm

Plus broad rotational bands

H₂O absorbs across a much wider range than CO₂ because: (1) all three modes are IR-active (permanent dipole), (2) it also has rotational absorption bands (liquid and gas), and (3) each vibrational line is broadened by rotation into a wide band. H₂O absorbs from ~2.5 μm to beyond 20 μm with multiple strong bands.
Why H₂O is the dominant greenhouse gas by warming effect (~21 K of the 33 K): Its absorption spans a huge range of IR wavelengths, it's abundant (0–4% of atmosphere), and it has a permanent dipole giving strong absorption at every mode. But we can't control its concentration — it's set by temperature (Clausius-Clapeyron).

Methane, N₂O, and CFCs

Methane (CH₄) — 80× CO₂ per molecule (20-year GWP)

Tetrahedral molecule with 4 C–H bonds. It has 9 vibration modes, several of which change the dipole. Its main absorption band is at ~3.3 μm and 7.7 μm.

The 7.7 μm band falls in the atmospheric window — where almost nothing else absorbs. This is why CH₄ is so potent: it plugs a gap in the atmosphere's IR absorption.

Nitrous Oxide (N₂O) — 273× CO₂ per molecule (100-year GWP)

Linear triatomic (N=N=O) but asymmetric — it has a permanent dipole unlike CO₂. Absorbs at ~4.5 μm and 7.8 μm. Also absorbs in the window region. Atmospheric lifetime: ~114 years.

CFCs (e.g., CFC-12: CCl₂F₂) — up to 14,000× CO₂

Large molecules with many vibrational modes, many in the 8–13 μm window. Extremely potent per molecule because they absorb where nothing else does. Also destroy ozone. Now banned under Montreal Protocol (1987) but persist for decades.

Global Warming Potential (GWP): Compares the total warming effect of 1 kg of a gas vs 1 kg of CO₂ over a specified time (usually 100 years). Higher GWP = more potent per molecule. CO₂ has GWP = 1 by definition. CH₄ = 28 (100-yr) or 80 (20-yr). SF₆ = 23,500 (the most potent known GHG).

Why N₂ and O₂ Don't Absorb IR — The Deep Explanation

The physics is precise: a homonuclear diatomic molecule (two identical atoms) can only vibrate symmetrically — both atoms are identical, so the electron cloud stays centred. No vibration can shift the charge centre. Therefore, no IR photon can be absorbed or emitted.

Contrast with CO (carbon monoxide) — IS IR-active

CO is also diatomic, but heteronuclear (C≠O). The bond is polar — C and O have different electronegativities. When the bond stretches, the dipole changes. So CO does absorb IR (at 4.7 μm). It's the asymmetry between atoms that matters, not the number.

Summary rule for IB: Homonuclear diatomics (N₂, O₂, H₂) → never absorb IR. Heteronuclear molecules with 2+ atoms (CO₂, H₂O, CH₄, N₂O, O₃, CO) → absorb IR at specific wavelengths determined by their vibrational mode frequencies.

Why Absorption Bands Are Wide, Not Sharp Lines

You might expect a molecule to absorb at exactly one wavelength. In reality, absorption bands are broad. Three mechanisms widen them:

Pressure broadening

Collisions between molecules slightly shift vibrational frequencies. Higher pressure → more collisions → wider bands. This is the dominant broadening in the lower atmosphere.

Doppler broadening

Molecules move at different speeds (thermal motion). Those moving toward the photon see a higher frequency (blue shift); those moving away see lower (red shift). Higher T → wider bands.

Rotational fine structure

Molecules rotate simultaneously with vibrating. Each vibrational transition splits into many closely-spaced rotational lines that merge into a broad band.

Why this matters for climate: As CO₂ concentration increases, its absorption band doesn't just get "deeper" (it's already nearly saturated at the centre). Instead, the wings of the band absorb more — the band effectively widens. This is why each doubling of CO₂ adds approximately the same amount of warming (~3°C) — logarithmic, not linear.
Saturation effect: At the centre of CO₂'s 15 μm band, the atmosphere is already virtually opaque — adding more CO₂ makes little difference there. The additional warming comes from the band edges eating into the atmospheric window. This is why the relationship between CO₂ concentration and temperature is approximately logarithmic: $\Delta T \approx \lambda \ln(C/C_0)$.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Explain [3]

(a) Explain at the molecular level why CO₂ absorbs infrared radiation but N₂ does not.

CO₂ is a triatomic molecule (O=C=O) that has vibrational modes — particularly bending — which cause a change in the molecule's electric dipole moment [1]. When IR photons with the matching frequency (~15 μm) strike CO₂, the molecule absorbs the photon energy and increases its vibrational energy [1]. N₂ is a homonuclear diatomic molecule — both atoms are identical, so no vibration can create a dipole change. Without a dipole change, N₂ cannot interact with IR photons and does not absorb [1].
Explain [2]

(b) Explain why methane has a higher global warming potential per molecule than CO₂, despite being present in much lower concentrations.

Methane absorbs IR radiation at wavelengths (~7.7 μm) that fall within the atmospheric window — a region where CO₂ and H₂O do not absorb significantly [1]. Each CH₄ molecule therefore absorbs radiation that would otherwise escape to space. Additionally, CH₄ has more IR-active vibrational modes and absorbs more strongly per molecule than CO₂ [1].
Explain [3]

(c) State the condition for a molecule to absorb infrared radiation. Explain why all three vibrational modes of water absorb IR, while only two of the four modes of CO₂ are IR-active.

A molecule absorbs IR when a vibration mode produces a change in the molecule's electric dipole moment [1]. Water is a bent molecule with a permanent dipole — all three vibration modes (symmetric stretch, asymmetric stretch, and bending) change the dipole because of the non-linear geometry [1]. CO₂ is linear and symmetric. Its symmetric stretch doesn't change the dipole (both O atoms move equally outward), but its asymmetric stretch and bending modes do create temporary dipoles, making only those modes IR-active [1].
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why the relationship between CO₂ concentration and temperature increase is approximately logarithmic rather than linear.

At the centre of CO₂'s 15 μm absorption band, the atmosphere is already nearly saturated — almost all radiation at that wavelength is already absorbed [1]. Adding more CO₂ extends absorption at the wings/edges of the band rather than increasing absorption at the centre. Each doubling of concentration extends the wings by a similar amount, producing a roughly equal temperature increase — giving a logarithmic relationship: $\Delta T \propto \ln(C/C_0)$ [1].

Explain [3]

Carbon monoxide (CO) is a diatomic molecule. Explain whether you would expect it to absorb infrared radiation, and compare it to nitrogen (N₂).

CO is a heteronuclear diatomic molecule (C≠O) with different electronegativities, creating a polar bond with a permanent dipole [1]. When the C–O bond stretches, the dipole changes — satisfying the condition for IR absorption. CO absorbs at ~4.7 μm [1]. N₂ is homonuclear — both atoms are identical, the bond is non-polar, and stretching produces no dipole change. N₂ does not absorb IR [1].
Discuss [3]

SF₆ has a GWP of 23,500 and an atmospheric lifetime of 3,200 years. Discuss why even trace amounts of SF₆ are a significant concern for climate change.

SF₆ absorbs at 10.6 μm — precisely in the centre of the atmospheric window where no other gas absorbs, meaning each molecule intercepts radiation that would otherwise escape to space entirely [1]. Its GWP of 23,500 means one molecule traps as much heat as 23,500 CO₂ molecules over 100 years [1]. Its 3,200-year atmospheric lifetime means once released, it remains active for millennia — the warming effect is essentially permanent on human timescales. Even at parts-per-trillion concentrations, the cumulative warming impact over centuries is substantial [1].
Compare [2]

Ozone (O₃) is a bent triatomic molecule made of three identical oxygen atoms. Explain why O₃ absorbs IR radiation at 9.6 μm even though O₂ does not absorb IR at all.

O₃ is bent (not linear), so it has a non-symmetric geometry. Even though all three atoms are oxygen, the bent structure means that vibrations (particularly the asymmetric stretch and bending) cause changes in the distribution of electron density — creating a temporary dipole change [1]. O₂ is a homonuclear diatomic with perfect symmetry — no vibration can create a dipole. The key difference is molecular geometry: bent triatomic vs linear diatomic [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Rule: A molecule absorbs IR only if the vibration changes its electric dipole moment.
  • N₂, O₂ (homonuclear diatomics): symmetric vibrations, no dipole change → invisible to IR.
  • CO₂: bending mode (15 μm) and asymmetric stretch (4.3 μm) create temporary dipoles → absorbs IR. Symmetric stretch does NOT absorb.
  • H₂O: bent, permanent dipole → ALL three modes absorb IR. Plus broad rotational bands. Dominant GHG by warming effect.
  • CH₄: absorbs at 7.7 μm — in the atmospheric window. 28× CO₂ per molecule (100-yr GWP).
  • GWP matters: SF₆ = 23,500, N₂O = 273, CH₄ = 28. Gases absorbing in the window are disproportionately potent.
  • CO₂ saturation: Centre of 15 μm band is already opaque. More CO₂ widens band wings → logarithmic T-vs-CO₂ relationship.
  • Band broadening: Pressure, Doppler, and rotational effects widen absorption lines into broad bands.
