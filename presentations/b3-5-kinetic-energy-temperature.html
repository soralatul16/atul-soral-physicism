The Molecular Connection

Temperature isn't just a number on a thermometer — it's a direct measure of how fast molecules move. This subtopic derives the exact relationship and shows that temperature is nothing more than average molecular kinetic energy.

B.3 Gas Laws  |  B.3.5 Kinetic Energy & TemperatureThe Derivation

Deriving $\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$

From kinetic theory (B.3.2): $P = \frac{1}{3}\frac{Nm\overline{v^2}}{V}$ → $PV = \frac{1}{3}Nm\overline{v^2}$

From ideal gas law: $PV = Nk_BT$

Equating: $Nk_BT = \frac{1}{3}Nm\overline{v^2}$

Cancel N: $k_BT = \frac{1}{3}m\overline{v^2}$

Since $\bar{E}_k = \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2}$, multiply both sides by $\frac{3}{2}$:

$$\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$$
This is one of the most important results in physics. Temperature IS average molecular KE. Double the kelvin temperature → double the average KE. At 0 K, KE = 0 (classically). The $\frac{3}{2}$ comes from 3 translational degrees of freedom ($\frac{1}{2}k_BT$ per degree).
Molar form: $\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}\frac{R}{N_A}T = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$. Total KE of n moles: $E_{\text{total}} = \frac{3}{2}nRT$.

Internal Energy of an Ideal Gas

For an ideal gas (no intermolecular forces), internal energy IS the total kinetic energy:

$$U = \frac{3}{2}nRT = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$$

Key implications

  • U depends only on T for an ideal gas — not on P or V separately
  • Isothermal process (constant T): $\Delta U = 0$ — no change in internal energy
  • For a real gas: U also includes intermolecular PE, so U can depend on V
  • Monatomic gas (He, Ne, Ar): only translational KE → $U = \frac{3}{2}nRT$ exactly
  • Diatomic gas (N₂, O₂): also rotational KE → $U = \frac{5}{2}nRT$ at room temperature

Example

Total KE of 2.0 mol of helium at 300 K:

$U = \frac{3}{2}nRT = \frac{3}{2} \times 2.0 \times 8.314 \times 300 = \mathbf{7483 \text{ J} \approx 7.5 \text{ kJ}}$

Degrees of freedom ($f$): General formula: $U = \frac{f}{2}nRT$. Monatomic: $f = 3$ (translate x,y,z). Diatomic at room T: $f = 5$ (translate + rotate). At high T: $f = 7$ (+ vibrate). IB usually uses monatomic ($\frac{3}{2}$).

Maxwell-Boltzmann Speed Distribution

Not all molecules move at the same speed. At any temperature, there's a distribution of speeds:

Three characteristic speeds

Most probable ($v_p$)
Peak of distribution
$v_p = \sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{m}}$
Mean ($\bar{v}$)
Average speed
$\bar{v} = \sqrt{\frac{8k_BT}{\pi m}}$
RMS ($v_{\text{rms}}$)
Root mean square
$v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_BT}{m}}$

Always: $v_p < \bar{v} < v_{\text{rms}}$

IB uses $v_{\text{rms}}$ because it connects directly to KE: $\bar{E}_k = \frac{1}{2}m v_{\text{rms}}^2$. The RMS speed is the "energetically correct" average — it gives the right average KE.

(a) Calculate the average translational KE of a gas molecule at 400 K.

$= \mathbf{8.29 \times 10^{-21} \text{ J}}$ [1]

(b) Calculate the total internal energy of 3.0 mol of an ideal monatomic gas at 500 K.

$U = \frac{3}{2}nRT = \frac{3}{2} \times 3.0 \times 8.314 \times 500$ [1]
$= \frac{3}{2} \times 12471 = \mathbf{18707 \text{ J} \approx 18.7 \text{ kJ}}$ [1] [1]
Explain [2]

(c) Two containers hold equal amounts of helium (monatomic) and nitrogen (diatomic) at the same temperature. Compare their average molecular KE and their total internal energies.

Average translational KE is the same for both: $\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$ depends only on temperature, not on the type of gas [1]. However, N₂ (diatomic) has additional rotational degrees of freedom, so its total internal energy is $\frac{5}{2}nRT$ vs $\frac{3}{2}nRT$ for He — N₂ has higher total internal energy at the same temperature [1].
Explain [2]

(d) Describe how the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution changes when a gas is heated at constant volume.

The peak shifts to the right (higher most probable speed) and decreases in height [1]. The distribution becomes wider and flatter, indicating a broader range of molecular speeds. The total area under the curve remains constant (same number of molecules) [1].

Key Takeaways

  • $\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$ — temperature IS average molecular KE. Derived from equating kinetic theory with ideal gas law.
  • $U = \frac{3}{2}nRT$ — total internal energy of ideal monatomic gas. Depends only on T.
  • Degrees of freedom: monatomic $f=3$, diatomic $f=5$ (room T). $U = \frac{f}{2}nRT$.
  • Maxwell-Boltzmann: distribution of speeds. Higher T → wider, flatter, shifted right. $v_p < \bar{v} < v_{\text{rms}}$.
  • $v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{3k_BT/m}$ — lighter molecules move faster at same T.
  • Isothermal: $\Delta U = 0$ (ideal gas). KE unchanged. All heat → work.
