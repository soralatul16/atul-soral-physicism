Kinetic Energy & Temperature

The Molecular Connection

What is temperature, physically? Here we derive pressure from molecules bouncing off walls, then link it to PV = nRT to reveal the deepest idea in thermal physics: absolute temperature is a direct measure of the average kinetic energy of particles.

Where Pressure Comes From

Kinetic theory treats a gas as many tiny molecules in constant random motion. Pressure is the average force per unit area from countless molecular impacts on the walls.

The logic chain

  • A molecule hitting a wall head-on and bouncing back reverses its momentum: $\Delta p = 2mv_x$.
  • Between hits on the same wall it travels $2L$, taking time $\Delta t = 2L/v_x$.
  • Average force from one molecule $= \dfrac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{2mv_x}{2L/v_x} = \dfrac{mv_x^2}{L}$.
  • Sum over all $N$ molecules and use $\langle v_x^2\rangle = \tfrac{1}{3}\langle v^2\rangle$ (motion shared over 3 axes).

The Kinetic Theory Pressure Equation

$$P = \frac{1}{3}\,\frac{Nm}{V}\,\langle v^2 \rangle = \frac{1}{3}\rho\langle v^2\rangle$$
  • $N$ = number of molecules, $m$ = mass of one molecule, $V$ = volume.
  • $\langle v^2\rangle$ = mean square speed (average of the squares of all molecular speeds).
  • $\rho = Nm/V$ = gas density, giving the compact form $P = \tfrac{1}{3}\rho\langle v^2\rangle$.
  • The root-mean-square speed is $v_{rms} = \sqrt{\langle v^2\rangle}$ — the "typical" molecular speed.
Read the physics: pressure rises if there are more molecules ($N$), if they are heavier ($m$), or if they move faster ($\langle v^2\rangle$) — and falls if the container is larger ($V$). Every term matches intuition.
Why the factor ⅓? Molecular motion is random in 3D, so on average the kinetic energy is split equally among the x, y, z directions. Only the component toward a given wall creates pressure on it, giving $\langle v_x^2\rangle = \tfrac{1}{3}\langle v^2\rangle$.

Temperature IS Average Kinetic Energy

Set the kinetic-theory pressure equal to the ideal gas law and the meaning of temperature falls out.

From kinetic theory: $PV = \tfrac{1}{3}Nm\langle v^2\rangle$. From the gas law: $PV = Nk_BT$. Equate them:

$\tfrac{1}{3}Nm\langle v^2\rangle = Nk_BT \;\Rightarrow\; \tfrac{1}{3}m\langle v^2\rangle = k_BT$

Multiply by $\tfrac{3}{2}$ to build the kinetic energy $\tfrac{1}{2}m\langle v^2\rangle$:

$$\bar{E}_k = \tfrac{1}{2}m\langle v^2 \rangle = \tfrac{3}{2}k_BT$$
This is the headline result of the whole topic. The average translational kinetic energy of a gas molecule depends only on absolute temperature — not on the type of gas, its mass, or its pressure. Temperature is, quite literally, a measure of molecular kinetic energy.
Per mole: multiply by $N_A$ to get the average KE of a mole: $\bar{E}_k^{(mol)} = \tfrac{3}{2}RT$.

What This Equation Tells Us

KE ∝ absolute temperature

Double T (in kelvin) → double the average KE. At $T = 0$ K, $\bar{E}_k = 0$: molecular motion ceases. This is the true meaning of absolute zero.

Same T → same KE

At the same temperature, H₂ and O₂ molecules have the same average KE. But since KE $=\tfrac12 mv^2$, lighter H₂ molecules must move much faster.

Comparing molecular speeds

Rearranging for rms speed: $v_{rms} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3k_BT}{m}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}}$. At fixed T, $v_{rms} \propto 1/\sqrt{m}$. This is why light gases like helium and hydrogen escape Earth's atmosphere — they move fast enough to exceed escape velocity.

Real-world link: uranium enrichment (gaseous diffusion) works because ²³⁵UF₆ molecules move very slightly faster than ²³⁸UF₆ at the same T — the mass difference gives a tiny speed difference, exploited over thousands of stages.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — average KE at room temperature

Find the average translational KE of a gas molecule at 27 °C.

$T = 300$ K.

$\bar{E}_k = \tfrac{3}{2}k_BT = \tfrac{3}{2}(1.38\times10^{-23})(300) = \mathbf{6.2\times10^{-21}\ \text{J}}$

Example 2 — rms speed of nitrogen

Find $v_{rms}$ for N₂ ($M = 0.028$ kg mol⁻¹) at 300 K.

$v_{rms} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3(8.31)(300)}{0.028}} = \sqrt{2.67\times10^{5}} = \mathbf{517\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$

Example 3 — speed ratio

At the same temperature, how much faster do helium (M = 4) molecules move than oxygen (M = 32)?

$\dfrac{v_{He}}{v_{O_2}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{M_{O_2}}{M_{He}}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{32}{4}} = \sqrt{8} = \mathbf{2.8\times\ \text{faster}}$

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

State & Explain [3]

(a) State what is meant by the absolute (kelvin) temperature of a gas in terms of molecular motion.

Absolute temperature is a measure of the average translational kinetic energy of the molecules of the gas [1]. Specifically $\bar{E}_k = \tfrac{3}{2}k_BT$, so KE is directly proportional to T [1]. At $T = 0$ K the average kinetic energy is zero — molecules have (classically) no translational motion [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) Calculate the rms speed of helium atoms (m = 6.6 × 10⁻²⁷ kg) at 290 K.

$v_{rms} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3k_BT}{m}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3(1.38\times10^{-23})(290)}{6.6\times10^{-27}}}$ [1]
$= \sqrt{1.82\times10^{6}}$ [1]
$= \mathbf{1.35\times10^{3}\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) A container holds a mixture of helium and argon at thermal equilibrium. Compare the average kinetic energies and the average speeds of the two gases.

At thermal equilibrium both gases are at the same temperature, so their average translational kinetic energies are equal ($\bar{E}_k = \tfrac{3}{2}k_BT$ depends only on T) [1]. However, argon atoms are more massive than helium, so since $\tfrac12 m v^2$ is equal, argon must have a lower average speed — helium atoms move faster [1].
Deduce [2]

(d) The absolute temperature of an ideal gas is doubled. Deduce the factor by which the rms speed of its molecules changes.

Since $v_{rms} = \sqrt{3k_BT/m}$, $v_{rms} \propto \sqrt{T}$ [1]. Doubling T multiplies $v_{rms}$ by $\sqrt{2} \approx \mathbf{1.41}$ [1]. (Note the average KE doubles, but speed rises only by √2.)

Key Takeaways

  • Pressure from kinetic theory: $P = \tfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{Nm}{V}\langle v^2\rangle = \tfrac{1}{3}\rho\langle v^2\rangle$.
  • Central result: $\bar{E}_k = \tfrac{1}{2}m\langle v^2\rangle = \tfrac{3}{2}k_BT$ — average KE depends only on absolute T.
  • Temperature = molecular KE. Same T means same average KE for any gas.
  • rms speed: $v_{rms} = \sqrt{3k_BT/m} = \sqrt{3RT/M}$; at fixed T, lighter molecules move faster ($v \propto 1/\sqrt{m}$).
  • $v_{rms} \propto \sqrt{T}$: doubling T raises rms speed by √2, but doubles the KE.
  • Absolute zero: the temperature at which molecular translational KE would fall to zero.
