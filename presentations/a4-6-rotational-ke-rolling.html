Rotational Kinetic Energy

A spinning object has kinetic energy due to its rotation, even if its centre of mass is stationary.

Translational KE

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

Energy of linear motion

Rotational KE

$$E_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$$

Energy of spinning

Example: Flywheel energy storage

A flywheel ($I = 0.80$ kg·m²) spins at 6000 rpm. How much energy does it store?

$\omega = 6000 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 628.3$ rad s⁻¹

$E = \frac{1}{2}(0.80)(628.3)^2 = \mathbf{158\,000 \text{ J} = 158 \text{ kJ}}$

Work-energy theorem for rotation: The work done by a torque equals the change in rotational KE: $W = \tau\theta = \Delta(\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2)$. Analogous to $W = Fs = \Delta(\frac{1}{2}mv^2)$.

Total KE of a Rolling Object

A rolling object translates (centre of mass moves) AND rotates (spins about its centre). Its total KE is the sum of both:

$$E_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$$
Rolling without slipping constraint: $v = R\omega$. This links the two motions. The contact point has zero velocity relative to the ground — it's an instantaneous pivot.
Substituting $\omega = v/R$ into the total KE: $E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\frac{v^2}{R^2} = \frac{1}{2}v^2\left(m + \frac{I}{R^2}\right)$. This form is very useful for energy conservation problems.

Rolling Down an Incline

Use energy conservation: GPE at top → translational KE + rotational KE at bottom.

$$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$$

Substituting $I = cmR^2$ (where $c$ is the shape constant) and $\omega = v/R$:

$$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}(cmR^2)\frac{v^2}{R^2} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2(1 + c)$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1+c}}$$

Worked Example: Solid Sphere Rolling Down

A solid sphere of mass 2.0 kg and radius 0.10 m rolls from rest down a 1.5 m high ramp without slipping. Find: (a) the speed at the bottom, (b) the translational KE, (c) the rotational KE, (d) the fraction of total KE that is rotational.

(a) Speed: $v = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1+c}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(9.81)(1.5)}{1 + 2/5}} = \sqrt{\frac{29.43}{1.4}} = \sqrt{21.02} = \mathbf{4.59 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$

(b) Translational KE: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(21.02) = \mathbf{21.0 \text{ J}}$

(c) Rotational KE: Total KE $= mgh = 2.0 \times 9.81 \times 1.5 = 29.4$ J. So $E_{\text{rot}} = 29.4 - 21.0 = \mathbf{8.4 \text{ J}}$

(d) Fraction: $\frac{E_{\text{rot}}}{E_{\text{total}}} = \frac{8.4}{29.4} = \frac{c}{1+c} = \frac{2/5}{7/5} = \mathbf{\frac{2}{7} = 28.6\%}$

Shortcut: The rotational fraction is always $\frac{c}{1+c}$ regardless of mass, radius, or height. For a solid sphere it's always $2/7$. For a hoop it's $1/2$ — half the energy goes to spinning!

Sliding vs Rolling

Sliding (no rotation)

$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$

$v = \sqrt{2gh}$

ALL energy → translation. Fastest possible.

Rolling (no slipping)

$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2(1+c)$

$v = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1+c}}$

Energy split between translation and rotation. Always slower.

A rolling object is always slower than a sliding one (for the same height). Some GPE must go into rotation, leaving less for translation. The hoop is slowest ($c = 1$, speed = $\sqrt{gh}$ vs $\sqrt{2gh}$ for sliding).

Role of friction in rolling

  • Static friction provides the torque that makes rolling happen — without it, the object would just slide.
  • Static friction does no work — the contact point has zero velocity. No energy is lost.
  • If the surface is too smooth, the object slides instead of rolls. It arrives faster but without rotation.
IB exam trap: "A block slides and a ball rolls down identical ramps. Which arrives first?" The block — it doesn't waste energy on rotation. Students often think rolling = faster because wheels are "efficient."

Energy Conservation with Rolling

The General Method

  1. Identify the shape → look up $I$ (or $c = I/mR^2$)
  2. Write energy conservation: $E_{\text{top}} = E_{\text{bottom}}$
  3. Use constraint $v = R\omega$ to eliminate $\omega$
  4. Solve for $v$, $\omega$, $h$, or whatever is asked

Example: How high does a rolling hoop climb?

A hoop rolls at 4.0 m s⁻¹ on flat ground, then up a ramp. How high does it reach?

Hoop: $c = 1$, so $E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2(1 + 1) = mv^2$

All KE → GPE: $mv^2 = mgh$ → $h = \frac{v^2}{g} = \frac{16}{9.81} = \mathbf{1.63 \text{ m}}$

Compare: A sliding block at 4.0 m s⁻¹ would only reach $h = \frac{v^2}{2g} = 0.82$ m. The hoop climbs twice as high because it has twice the KE (half translational + half rotational).

Key insight: When rolling uphill, rotational KE converts back to GPE. A rolling object carries "hidden" energy in its spin that gets released when going uphill or decelerating.

(a) Write the expression for the total kinetic energy of an object that rolls without slipping.

Calculate [3]

(b) A solid cylinder ($m = 3.0$ kg, $R = 0.08$ m) rolls at 5.0 m s⁻¹. Calculate its total kinetic energy.

$E_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2}(3.0)(25) = 37.5$ J [1]
$I = \frac{1}{2}mR^2 = \frac{1}{2}(3.0)(0.0064) = 0.0096$ kg·m², $\omega = v/R = 62.5$ rad s⁻¹
$E_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}(0.0096)(3906) = 18.75$ J [1]
$E_{\text{total}} = 37.5 + 18.75 = \mathbf{56.3 \text{ J}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) A hollow sphere rolls from rest down a 2.0 m high ramp. Calculate the speed at the bottom.

Hollow sphere: $c = 2/3$, so $1 + c = 5/3$ [1]
$v = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1+c}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(9.81)(2.0)}{5/3}} = \sqrt{\frac{39.24}{1.667}}$ [1]
$v = \sqrt{23.54} = \mathbf{4.85 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) A solid sphere and a hoop of equal mass and radius are released from the same height. Explain which reaches the bottom first.

The solid sphere reaches the bottom first [1]. The sphere has $c = 2/5$ (lower fraction of energy in rotation) while the hoop has $c = 1$ (half its energy in rotation). With more energy available for translation, the sphere has a higher linear speed at every point on the ramp [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [4]

A solid cylinder rolls without slipping at 6.0 m s⁻¹ on flat ground, then rolls up a frictionless ramp (no rolling, pure sliding on the ramp). How high does it reach?

On the flat: total KE $= \frac{1}{2}mv^2(1 + 1/2) = \frac{3}{4}mv^2$ [1]
On the frictionless ramp: no torque, so the cylinder slides but keeps spinning. Only translational KE converts to GPE [1].
$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mgh$ → $h = \frac{v^2}{2g} = \frac{36}{19.62} = \mathbf{1.83 \text{ m}}$ [1]
The rotational KE ($\frac{1}{4}mv^2$) is retained as spin — it cannot convert to height without friction [1].
Calculate [3]

A flywheel ($I = 1.5$ kg·m²) is spinning at 200 rad s⁻¹. A brake applies a constant friction torque until the flywheel stops after 50 revolutions. Calculate the braking torque.

$\theta = 50 \times 2\pi = 314.2$ rad
$W = \tau\theta = \Delta E_{\text{rot}}$
$\tau \times 314.2 = \frac{1}{2}(1.5)(200)^2 = 30\,000$ J [1] [1]
$\tau = \frac{30000}{314.2} = \mathbf{95.5 \text{ N·m}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

Two identical-looking balls roll down the same ramp. Ball A arrives first. What can you deduce about their internal structure?

Ball A has a lower value of $c = I/mR^2$, meaning its mass is more concentrated near the centre [1]. Ball A is likely solid while Ball B is likely hollow. A solid sphere ($c = 2/5$) always beats a hollow sphere ($c = 2/3$) regardless of mass or radius [1].
Classic IB experiment: Roll a solid ball and a hollow ball (same size and mass) down a ramp. The solid one always wins. This demonstrates that internal mass distribution — not total mass — determines rolling speed.

Key Takeaways

  • Rotational KE: $E_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$ — energy stored in spinning.
  • Rolling = two KEs: $E_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$ with $v = R\omega$.
  • Speed down ramp: $v = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1+c}}$ where $c = I/mR^2$. Independent of mass and radius.
  • Rolling race: Solid sphere > solid cylinder > hollow sphere > hoop. Lower $c$ wins.
  • Rotational fraction: $\frac{c}{1+c}$ of total KE is rotational. Hoop: 50%. Solid sphere: 29%.
  • Sliding is always faster than rolling — no energy "wasted" on rotation.
  • Static friction enables rolling but does no work. Without it, objects slide.
  • Work-energy: $W = \tau\theta = \Delta(\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2)$.
