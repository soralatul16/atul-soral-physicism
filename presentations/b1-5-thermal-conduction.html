Thermal Conduction

Heat Transfer Through Matter

How Conduction Works

Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by particle vibrations and collisions, without bulk movement of the material.

In non-metals (insulators)

Hot particles vibrate faster → collide with cooler neighbours → transfer KE along the chain. Energy passes from particle to particle through the lattice.

Slow — bonds transmit vibrations weakly.

In metals (conductors)

Same lattice vibration mechanism PLUS free electrons that move rapidly through the metal, carrying energy from hot to cold regions far faster.

Fast — free electrons are the dominant carriers.

Why metals feel cold: Metal conducts heat away from your hand quickly (high thermal conductivity). Wood does it slowly. Both are at the same temperature, but metal removes your hand's heat faster — making it feel colder.

Fourier's Law of Conduction

The rate of heat flow through a material depends on four factors:

$$\frac{Q}{t} = \frac{kA\Delta T}{d}$$
Q/t
Rate of heat flow
(W = J/s)
k
Thermal conductivity
(W m⁻¹ K⁻¹)
A
Cross-sectional area
(m²)
ΔT/d
Temperature gradient
(K/m)
Temperature gradient $= \Delta T / d$ is the "steepness" of the temperature drop across the material. Higher gradient = faster conduction. This is why thin walls lose heat faster than thick ones.

Copper is ~16,000× better than air at conducting heat. The ratio spans four orders of magnitude. Insulation works by trapping still air — fibre, foam, fur, feathers all create pockets of trapped air.
Why metals are good conductors and good electrical conductors: Same reason — free electrons. Free electrons carry both thermal energy and electric charge. High thermal k ↔ high electrical conductivity. This is the Wiedemann-Franz law.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Heat loss through a window

A glass window ($k = 1.0$) is 1.5 m × 1.0 m and 5.0 mm thick. Inside = 22°C, outside = 2°C. Calculate the rate of heat loss.

$A = 1.5 \times 1.0 = 1.5$ m², $d = 0.005$ m, $\Delta T = 22 - 2 = 20$ K

$\frac{Q}{t} = \frac{kA\Delta T}{d} = \frac{1.0 \times 1.5 \times 20}{0.005} = \mathbf{6000 \text{ W} = 6.0 \text{ kW}}$

That's equivalent to six kettles running — just through one window! This is why double glazing matters.

Example 2: Double glazing comparison

The same window is replaced with double glazing: two 4 mm glass panes with a 12 mm air gap ($k_{\text{air}} = 0.025$). Estimate the new heat loss rate.

The air gap dominates (lowest k): $\frac{Q}{t} \approx \frac{k_{\text{air}} A \Delta T}{d_{\text{air}}} = \frac{0.025 \times 1.5 \times 20}{0.012} = \mathbf{62.5 \text{ W}}$

Heat loss drops from 6000 W to ~63 W — a 99% reduction! The trapped air layer is the key insulator.

In practice, you must add all layers in series: $\frac{Q}{t} = \frac{A\Delta T}{d_1/k_1 + d_2/k_2 + d_3/k_3}$ (thermal resistance in series). But for IB, the air gap dominates so heavily that approximating with just the air layer gives a reasonable answer.

Applications of Conduction

Want HIGH conduction

  • Cooking pans — copper/aluminium bottom transfers heat to food quickly
  • Heat sinks — aluminium fins remove heat from CPUs/LEDs
  • Radiators — metal fins spread heat into the room
  • Thermal paste — fills air gaps between CPU and heat sink

Want LOW conduction (insulation)

  • Double glazing — trapped air between glass panes
  • Cavity walls — air/foam gap in building walls
  • Polystyrene cups — foam traps air, keeps drinks hot
  • Fur/feathers — trap still air close to skin
  • Fibreglass loft insulation — air pockets in glass fibres
The common principle: Almost all insulation works by trapping still air. Air itself has very low k (0.025), but it must be kept still — otherwise convection takes over and transfers heat anyway. Foam, fur, fibres, and double glazing all prevent air from circulating.
Cooking pan design: Copper bottom (high k → heats evenly), stainless steel interior (durable, non-reactive), plastic/wood handle (low k → doesn't burn your hand). Three materials chosen for three different thermal jobs.

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

Explain [2]

(a) Explain the mechanism of thermal conduction in metals.

In metals, free (delocalised) electrons gain kinetic energy at the hot end and move rapidly to the cold end, transferring energy through collisions with the lattice ions [1]. Additionally, lattice ions at the hot end vibrate more vigorously and pass vibrations to neighbouring ions, though this is a slower mechanism [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A copper rod ($k = 401$) is 0.50 m long and has a cross-sectional area of $2.0 \times 10^{-4}$ m². One end is at 200°C and the other at 20°C. Calculate the rate of heat conduction.

$\frac{Q}{t} = \frac{kA\Delta T}{d} = \frac{401 \times 2.0 \times 10^{-4} \times 180}{0.50}$ [1]
$= \frac{401 \times 2.0 \times 10^{-4} \times 180}{0.50}$ [1]
$= \mathbf{28.9 \text{ W}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) A metal spoon and a wooden spoon are both placed in boiling water. Explain why the metal spoon handle becomes hot while the wooden one does not.

Metal has a much higher thermal conductivity than wood (e.g. steel k ≈ 50 vs wood k ≈ 0.15) [1]. Heat is conducted rapidly along the metal spoon from the hot water to the handle. Wood conducts heat so slowly that the handle remains cool, even though the end in water is at 100°C [1].
Suggest [2]

(d) Suggest two ways to reduce heat loss through a solid wall, explaining the physics behind each.

1. Add cavity wall insulation (foam/fibreglass) — traps still air which has very low thermal conductivity (k = 0.025), reducing the temperature gradient across the structure [1].
2. Increase wall thickness — increases $d$ in Fourier's law, reducing the rate of heat flow $Q/t = kA\Delta T/d$ [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

A steel pipe ($k = 50$) has outer radius 5.0 cm, wall thickness 3.0 mm, and length 2.0 m. Hot water at 80°C flows inside; outside temperature is 20°C. Estimate the rate of heat loss. (Use $A = 2\pi rL$ for the outer surface.)

$A = 2\pi \times 0.05 \times 2.0 = 0.628$ m², $\Delta T = 60$ K, $d = 0.003$ m [1]
$\frac{Q}{t} = \frac{50 \times 0.628 \times 60}{0.003}$ [1]
$= \mathbf{628\,000 \text{ W} \approx 628 \text{ kW}}$ [1]
This huge value shows why hot water pipes need insulation!
Compare [3]

Two rods of equal length and cross-section are made of copper ($k = 401$) and glass ($k = 1.0$). Both have one end at 100°C and the other at 0°C. Calculate the ratio of heat flow rates.

Since $A$, $\Delta T$, and $d$ are identical, $\frac{Q/t_{\text{Cu}}}{Q/t_{\text{glass}}} = \frac{k_{\text{Cu}}}{k_{\text{glass}}}$ [1]
$= \frac{401}{1.0} = \mathbf{401 : 1}$ [1]
Copper conducts heat 400× faster than glass [1].
Explain [2]

On a cold day, a tile floor feels colder than a carpeted floor at the same temperature. Explain this observation in terms of thermal conductivity.

Tile has a higher thermal conductivity than carpet [1]. When your foot touches tile, heat is conducted away from your skin more rapidly, making it feel colder. Carpet (with trapped air in its fibres) has very low k, so it conducts heat away from your foot slowly. Both are at the same temperature — the difference is the rate of heat removal [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Conduction: heat transfer through particle vibrations (all solids) + free electrons (metals).
  • Fourier's Law: $Q/t = kA\Delta T/d$. More heat flows through high-k, large-area, thin materials with big ΔT.
  • Thermal conductivity (k): metals ~50–400, glass ~1, wood ~0.15, air ~0.025 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹.
  • Metals conduct well because of free electrons — same reason they conduct electricity.
  • Insulation = trapped still air. Foam, fur, fibreglass, double glazing all work this way.
  • "Feels cold" is about conduction rate, not temperature. High-k materials remove heat from your skin faster.
  • Double glazing reduces heat loss by ~99% — the air gap (k = 0.025) dominates the resistance.
