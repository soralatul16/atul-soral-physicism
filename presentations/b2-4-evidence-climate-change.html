What We Measure, What We Know

Climate change isn't a prediction — it's an observation. Temperature records, ice cores, sea levels, glacier retreat, satellite data, and ocean measurements all tell the same story. The evidence comes from multiple independent lines, and they all converge on one conclusion.

B.2 The Greenhouse Effect  |  B.2.4 Evidence for Climate ChangeTemperature Records

Direct Temperature Measurements (1850–present)

  • Global average temperature has risen ~1.2°C since 1850–1900 (pre-industrial baseline)
  • Rate of warming is accelerating: 0.08°C/decade (1900–1980) → 0.18°C/decade (1980–2024)
  • Each of the last 4 decades has been warmer than any preceding decade since 1850
  • The 10 warmest years on record have all occurred since 2010
  • 2023–2024 shattered previous records — likely the warmest in at least 100,000 years

How temperature is measured

  • Land stations: ~10,000 weather stations worldwide, recording since mid-1800s
  • Ocean buoys & ships: sea surface temperatures from ship intakes and Argo floats (3,800+ autonomous buoys measuring to 2 km depth)
  • Satellites (since 1979): measure atmospheric temperature via microwave emissions — independent confirmation of surface records
  • Multiple independent datasets (NASA GISS, NOAA, HadCRUT, Berkeley Earth, JMA) all agree within ~0.05°C
Urban heat island objection: Some argue cities inflate temperature readings. This has been extensively studied — rural-only stations show the same trend. Satellite data (no urban bias) confirms the warming. The urban heat island effect is real but accounts for less than 0.01°C/decade — negligible compared to the 0.18°C/decade observed.

Ice Core Records — 800,000 Years of Climate History

Ice cores from Antarctica and Greenland trap ancient air bubbles, allowing us to measure past CO₂ concentrations and temperatures going back 800,000 years.

What ice cores reveal

  • CO₂ has oscillated between 180 ppm (ice ages) and 280 ppm (warm periods) for 800,000 years — always within this range
  • Current CO₂ (424 ppm) is 50% above the highest level in 800,000 years
  • Temperature and CO₂ are tightly correlated — when one rises, so does the other
  • The rate of CO₂ increase is ~100× faster than any natural change in the ice core record

How ice core temperature is measured

Isotope ratio method: Water molecules contain either ¹⁶O (light) or ¹⁸O (heavy). Warmer temperatures → more ¹⁸O evaporates → higher ¹⁸O/¹⁶O ratio in precipitation → in the ice. The ratio $\delta^{18}$O directly indicates temperature when the ice formed.

Similarly, the deuterium/hydrogen ratio ($\delta$D) provides an independent temperature proxy. Both methods agree.

Cause vs effect: In past ice age cycles, warming often started before CO₂ rose (triggered by orbital changes). This doesn't disprove CO₂'s role — the initial orbital warming released CO₂ from oceans, which then amplified the warming by 2–3×. CO₂ acted as a feedback, turning a small orbital nudge into a full ice age cycle. Today, CO₂ is the initial forcing — we're adding it directly.

Sea Level Rise — Two Physical Causes

1. Thermal expansion (~50%)

Warmer water expands — it's less dense, so the same mass occupies more volume. The ocean has absorbed 93% of excess heat.

Physics: Volume change = $\beta V \Delta T$, where $\beta$ is the volumetric thermal expansion coefficient ($\beta_{\text{seawater}} \approx 2.6 \times 10^{-4}$ K⁻¹).

Contributes ~2.0 mm/year to sea level rise.

2. Ice melt (~50%)

Melting of land-based ice (glaciers, ice sheets) adds water to the ocean. Sea ice melting does NOT raise sea level (already floating — Archimedes).

Sources: Greenland (7.4 m if fully melted), Antarctica (58 m), mountain glaciers (~0.3 m).

Contributes ~1.8 mm/year and accelerating.

The numbers

  • Total rise since 1900: ~21 cm
  • Current rate: ~3.7 mm/year (2006–2018) — doubled from 1.4 mm/year in the early 20th century
  • Projections by 2100: 0.3–1.0 m (depending on emissions scenario)
  • If all ice melts: ~65 m rise — but this would take centuries to millennia
IB distinction: Sea ice melting (Arctic) doesn't raise sea level (it's already displacing water). Land ice melting (Greenland, Antarctica, glaciers) does. Thermal expansion doesn't involve any ice at all — it's purely about water density changing with temperature.

Glacier Retreat & Polar Ice Loss

Arctic Sea Ice

  • September minimum has declined by ~13% per decade since 1979
  • ~40% less ice area than 1979
  • Ice is also thinner — volume decline is ~50%
  • Ice-free Arctic summers predicted by 2040s

Greenland Ice Sheet

  • Losing ~270 Gt of ice per year (2006–2018)
  • Rate has increased 6× since 1990s
  • Contains 7.4 m of sea level equivalent
  • Approaching irreversible tipping point

Antarctic Ice Sheet

  • West Antarctic losing ~150 Gt/year
  • Marine ice sheet instability (MISI) may be triggered
  • Contains 58 m of sea level equivalent
  • East Antarctic still relatively stable (for now)

Mountain Glaciers

  • ~90% of monitored glaciers are retreating
  • Alps have lost ~60% of ice since 1850
  • Himalayan glaciers supply water to 1.5 billion people
  • Many small glaciers will disappear by 2100
Satellite gravity measurements (GRACE/GRACE-FO) directly measure ice mass loss from space by detecting tiny changes in Earth's gravitational field. This is independent of all other methods and confirms the accelerating ice loss from Greenland and Antarctica.

Ocean Evidence — The Heat Sink

Ocean warming

The ocean has absorbed 93% of excess heat since 1970. The top 700 m has warmed by ~0.6°C. Even the deep ocean (2000 m) is warming — measured by the Argo float network.

Ocean heat content is the best single measure of Earth's energy imbalance — it integrates over the entire planet, smoothing out weather noise.

Ocean acidification

CO₂ dissolves in seawater: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid). Ocean pH has dropped from 8.21 to 8.10 since 1750 — a 26% increase in hydrogen ion concentration (pH is logarithmic).

This threatens coral reefs (can't build CaCO₃ skeletons), shellfish, and the entire marine food chain. It's the "other CO₂ problem."

Coral bleaching

Corals expel their symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) when water is too warm, turning white. If heat stress persists, the coral dies. Mass bleaching events occurred in 1998, 2010, 2016, 2017, 2020, 2024. The Great Barrier Reef has experienced six mass bleaching events since 1998 — five since 2016.

The ocean's role is double-edged: By absorbing heat and CO₂, oceans slow atmospheric warming and CO₂ buildup — but at the cost of warming and acidifying the ocean itself. As the ocean warms, it absorbs less CO₂ (warm water holds less dissolved gas), weakening this carbon sink over time.

Biological, Atmospheric & Paleoclimate Evidence

Biological changes

  • Species ranges shifting poleward (~17 km/decade)
  • Spring events (blooming, breeding) occurring 2–5 days earlier per decade
  • Growing seasons lengthened by ~2 weeks since 1950
  • Coral bleaching frequency tripled since 1980s
  • Insect populations declining (timing mismatch with food sources)

Extreme weather trends

  • Heatwave frequency has increased 5× since 1950
  • Heavy rainfall events increasing (~7%/°C, Clausius-Clapeyron)
  • Stronger tropical cyclones (more energy from warmer oceans)
  • Drought severity increasing in subtropical regions
  • Wildfire season lengthened, area burned increasing

Attribution science — "Is event X caused by climate change?"

Modern attribution studies compare the probability of an extreme event in a world with vs without human-caused warming (using climate models). Results show:

  • 2021 Pacific NW heatwave: "virtually impossible" without climate change
  • 2022 Pakistan floods: rainfall 75% more intense due to warming
  • Every heatwave since ~2000 has been made more likely by human-caused warming
Permafrost records: Permafrost that has been frozen continuously for thousands of years is now thawing. In Siberia, permafrost temperatures have risen 0.5–2°C since the 1970s. Ancient mammoth carcasses preserved for 40,000+ years are being exposed — direct evidence that current temperatures exceed anything in tens of millennia.

The Keeling Curve — The Most Important Graph in Science

Charles David Keeling began measuring atmospheric CO₂ at Mauna Loa, Hawaii in 1958. The resulting graph is the clearest single piece of evidence for human impact on the atmosphere.

The sawtooth pattern — Earth breathing

CO₂ drops ~6 ppm each Northern Hemisphere summer (plants absorb CO₂ via photosynthesis) and rises each winter (decomposition releases CO₂). Most land vegetation is in the Northern Hemisphere, so it dominates the seasonal cycle. This "breathing" is superimposed on the relentless upward trend from fossil fuel emissions.

The rate is unprecedented. CO₂ rose from 315 ppm (1958) to 424 ppm (2024) — a 35% increase in 66 years. In the ice core record, natural CO₂ changes of similar magnitude took 5,000–10,000 years. We've compressed millennia of change into decades.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Outline [3]

(a) Outline three different types of evidence that support the conclusion that global temperatures are rising.

1. Direct thermometer records from ~10,000 weather stations, ship measurements, and satellites show a ~1.2°C increase since 1850 [1].
2. Glaciers and ice sheets are retreating globally — ~90% of monitored glaciers are shrinking, Arctic sea ice has declined ~40% since 1979 [1].
3. Global mean sea level has risen ~21 cm since 1900 due to thermal expansion and ice melt, with the rate doubling in recent decades [1].
Explain [3]

(b) Explain how ice core data provides evidence for both past temperatures and past CO₂ concentrations.

Air bubbles trapped in ice layers preserve samples of ancient atmosphere — the CO₂ concentration can be directly measured from these bubbles [1]. Past temperatures are determined from the ratio of oxygen isotopes (¹⁸O/¹⁶O) in the ice — higher ¹⁸O/¹⁶O ratios indicate warmer conditions when the ice formed [1]. These measurements show that CO₂ and temperature have been closely correlated over 800,000 years, with current CO₂ levels 50% above anything in that record [1].
Explain [3]

(c) Explain the two physical mechanisms by which global warming causes sea level to rise.

1. Thermal expansion: as ocean water warms, it expands (lower density → larger volume). The ocean has absorbed 93% of excess heat, causing significant volumetric expansion contributing ~2.0 mm/year to sea level rise [1].
2. Melting of land-based ice: warming causes glaciers, and the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets to lose mass. This meltwater flows into the ocean, adding water volume [1]. Note: melting of floating sea ice does NOT raise sea level (Archimedes' principle — it already displaces its weight of water) [1].
Explain [2]

(d) The Keeling Curve shows both a long-term upward trend and a seasonal oscillation. Explain each feature.

The long-term upward trend (315→424 ppm since 1958) is caused by human emissions from burning fossil fuels and deforestation, which release CO₂ faster than natural sinks (oceans, plants) can absorb it [1]. The seasonal oscillation (~6 ppm amplitude) reflects Northern Hemisphere vegetation cycles: CO₂ drops in summer (photosynthesis absorbs CO₂) and rises in winter (decomposition releases it), since most land vegetation is in the Northern Hemisphere [1].

More Practice 8/10

Explain [3]

A sceptic argues: "In ice core records, temperature rises before CO₂, so CO₂ doesn't cause warming." Evaluate this argument using physics.

The initial warming in ice age cycles was triggered by orbital (Milankovitch) changes, not CO₂ — this is correct [1]. However, the initial warming caused oceans to release dissolved CO₂ (warm water holds less gas). This additional CO₂ then amplified the warming through the greenhouse effect — acting as a powerful positive feedback that turned a small orbital nudge into a full 5–6°C global temperature change [1]. Today's situation is different: humans are directly adding CO₂ as the initial forcing, not waiting for orbital warming. The physics of CO₂ absorption is identical regardless of whether CO₂ leads or lags temperature — each molecule absorbs IR the same way [1].
Calculate [3]

The ocean surface area is $3.6 \times 10^{14}$ m² and average depth is 3700 m. If the top 100 m warms by 0.5°C, estimate the thermal expansion and resulting sea level rise. ($\beta = 2.6 \times 10^{-4}$ K⁻¹)

Volume of top 100 m: $V = 3.6 \times 10^{14} \times 100 = 3.6 \times 10^{16}$ m³ [1]
$\Delta V = \beta V \Delta T = 2.6 \times 10^{-4} \times 3.6 \times 10^{16} \times 0.5 = 4.68 \times 10^{12}$ m³ [1]
Sea level rise: $\Delta h = \frac{\Delta V}{A} = \frac{4.68 \times 10^{12}}{3.6 \times 10^{14}} = \mathbf{0.013 \text{ m} = 1.3 \text{ cm}}$ [1]
This is a rough estimate — actual thermal expansion is the largest single contributor to observed sea level rise.
Discuss [3]

Explain why ocean acidification is sometimes called "the other CO₂ problem" and why it is a concern independent of global warming.

About 25% of emitted CO₂ dissolves in the ocean, forming carbonic acid (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃), which dissociates and lowers pH [1]. Ocean pH has already dropped 0.1 units (26% more H⁺ ions) since pre-industrial times. This reduces carbonate ion concentration, making it harder for organisms (corals, shellfish, plankton) to build calcium carbonate shells and skeletons [1]. Even if warming were somehow prevented, continued CO₂ emissions would still acidify oceans, threatening marine ecosystems and the food chains that depend on them. It is an independent consequence of CO₂ emissions, not of temperature rise [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Temperature records: +1.2°C since 1850. Rate accelerating (0.18°C/decade). Multiple independent datasets agree.
  • Ice cores: 800,000 years of CO₂ data. Current 424 ppm is 50% above any level in the record. Rate 100× faster than natural.
  • Keeling Curve: 315→424 ppm since 1958. Seasonal sawtooth (biosphere breathing) + relentless upward trend (fossil fuels).
  • Sea level: +21 cm since 1900. Rate doubled. Two causes: thermal expansion (~50%) + land ice melt (~50%). Sea ice melt ≠ sea level rise.
  • Glaciers/ice sheets: ~90% retreating. Arctic −40% since 1979. Greenland losing 270 Gt/year. Antarctic accelerating.
  • Ocean: absorbed 93% of excess heat. Warming to 2 km depth. pH dropped 0.1 (26% more acidic). Coral bleaching events intensifying.
  • Biology: species shifting poleward, seasons changing, extreme weather intensifying. Attribution science links specific events to warming.
  • Isotope fingerprint: ¹³C/¹²C ratio confirms fossil carbon. O₂ declining from combustion. Human cause is unambiguous.
