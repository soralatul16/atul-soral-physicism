Drag Force (Air Resistance)

Drag is a resistive force exerted by a fluid (air, water) on an object moving through it.

  • Always opposes the direction of motion
  • Increases with speed — faster you go, more drag you feel
  • Depends on: speed, cross-sectional area, shape, fluid density
  • At low speeds: $F_{drag} \propto v$. At high speeds: $F_{drag} \propto v^2$
$$F_{drag} \propto v^2 \quad \text{(at high speeds, IB level)}$$
Key factors affecting drag:
• Speed: double the speed → roughly 4× the drag ($v^2$)
• Cross-section: larger area → more drag (parachute vs bullet)
• Shape: streamlined → less drag (teardrop vs flat plate)
• Fluid density: water drag >> air drag
IB simplification: At SL, you don't need to calculate drag numerically. You need to understand qualitatively: drag increases with speed, opposes motion, and eventually leads to terminal velocity.

Terminal Velocity — Step by Step

When an object falls through a fluid, here's what happens:

1Object released from rest. Only weight acts. $a = g$. Free fall.
2Speed increases → drag increases (because $F_{drag} \propto v^2$).
3Net force decreases → $F_{net} = mg - F_{drag}$ gets smaller → acceleration decreases.
4Eventually $F_{drag} = mg$ → $F_{net} = 0$ → $a = 0$.
5Terminal velocity reached. Object falls at constant speed. No more acceleration.
$$\text{At terminal velocity: } F_{drag} = mg \implies a = 0$$
Terminal velocity values: Skydiver (spread): ~55 m/s (200 km/h). Skydiver (dive): ~90 m/s. Raindrop: ~9 m/s. Ping pong ball: ~9 m/s. Golf ball: ~32 m/s. Feather: ~0.5 m/s.

Buoyancy — Archimedes' Principle

Buoyancy is the upward force exerted by a fluid on an object immersed in it.

Archimedes' Principle: The buoyant force equals the weight of fluid displaced by the object.
$$F_b = \rho_{fluid} \cdot V_{submerged} \cdot g$$
  • $\rho_{fluid}$ = density of the fluid (kg m$^{-3}$)
  • $V_{submerged}$ = volume of object below the fluid surface
  • $g$ = gravitational field strength
  • Direction: always upward

Floating vs Sinking:

  • $F_b > W$: object rises (accelerates up)
  • $F_b = W$: object floats (equilibrium)
  • $F_b < W$: object sinks
Why ships float: A steel ship has a hollow hull that displaces a huge volume of water. The weight of displaced water ($F_b$) equals the ship's weight → floats. A solid steel ball of the same mass would sink (displaces less water).

Forces on Objects in Fluids — Three Stages

A ball dropped into water experiences three stages. Each has a different FBD:

Stage 1: Just released

$v \approx 0$, drag ≈ 0
$a$ is maximum

Stage 2: Speeding up

$v$ increasing, drag growing
$a$ decreasing

Stage 3: Terminal velocity

$v = v_T$, constant
$a = 0$

In a fluid: both buoyancy AND drag act upward. Terminal velocity is reached when $W = F_b + F_{drag}$. In air, buoyancy is negligible (air is light), so $W \approx F_{drag}$ at terminal velocity.

Worked Example — Buoyancy 6/10

A block of wood has volume $0.002$ m³ and mass 1.2 kg. It is placed in water ($\rho = 1000$ kg/m³). (a) Does it float or sink? (b) What fraction is submerged? (c) Find the buoyant force.

(a) Float or sink?

1$\rho_{wood} = m/V = 1.2/0.002 = 600$ kg/m³
2$\rho_{wood} (600) < \rho_{water} (1000)$ → it floats

(b) Fraction submerged:

3At equilibrium: $F_b = W$: $\rho_w V_{sub} g = mg$
$$\frac{V_{sub}}{V_{total}} = \frac{\rho_{object}}{\rho_{fluid}} = \frac{600}{1000} = \mathbf{0.6 = 60\%}$$

(c) Buoyant force (at equilibrium):

$$F_b = W = mg = 1.2 \times 9.8 = \mathbf{11.76 \text{ N}}$$
The fraction submerged rule: $\frac{V_{sub}}{V_{total}} = \frac{\rho_{object}}{\rho_{fluid}}$. Ice ($\rho = 917$) in water: 91.7% submerged — only 8.3% above water (the tip of the iceberg!).

Context: A skydiver of mass 80 kg jumps from a plane. After reaching terminal velocity, she opens her parachute.
Calculate [1]

(a) Calculate the drag force on the skydiver at terminal velocity (before the parachute opens).

At terminal velocity: $F_{drag} = W = mg = 80 \times 9.8 = \mathbf{784 \text{ N}}$ [1]
Explain [3]

(b) Describe and explain what happens to the skydiver's velocity and acceleration from the moment she jumps to reaching terminal velocity.

Initially: $v = 0$, drag = 0, only weight acts, so $a = g$ (maximum) [1].
As $v$ increases: drag increases ($\propto v^2$), net downward force decreases, so $a$ decreases [1].
Eventually: drag = weight, $F_{net} = 0$, $a = 0$, constant velocity = terminal velocity [1].
Explain [3]

(c) At the instant the parachute opens, describe and explain the changes in velocity, drag force, and acceleration.

Parachute increases cross-sectional area enormously [1]. At the current high speed, drag is now MUCH greater than weight → strong net upward force → deceleration [1]. Speed decreases → drag decreases → until drag = weight again at a new, much lower terminal velocity [1].
Sketch [3]

(d) Sketch the acceleration-time graph for the entire fall (before and after parachute opens).

Before chute: starts at $g$ (9.8), decreases to 0 as terminal velocity is reached [1].
At chute opening: sudden large negative $a$ (strong deceleration) [1].
After: $a$ returns to 0 as new terminal velocity is reached [1].

Practice Questions

Explain [2] Easy

Why does a feather fall slower than a coin in air, but at the same rate in a vacuum?

In air: the feather has a large area relative to its mass → reaches terminal velocity quickly at a low speed [1]. In vacuum: no drag → both experience only gravity → same $a = g$ regardless of mass [1].
Calculate [2] Medium

A steel ball ($\rho = 7800$ kg/m³, volume $= 0.0001$ m³) is submerged in water. Find the buoyant force and the net downward force.

$F_b = \rho_w V g = 1000 \times 0.0001 \times 9.8 = \mathbf{0.98 \text{ N}}$ [1]
$W = \rho_s V g = 7800 \times 0.0001 \times 9.8 = 7.64$ N
Net = $W - F_b = 7.64 - 0.98 = \mathbf{6.66 \text{ N}}$ downward [1]
Explain [3] Hard

Two balls of the same size but different masses are dropped from the same height in air. Which hits the ground first? Explain in terms of terminal velocity.

The heavier ball hits first [1]. Both have the same drag (same size/shape/speed), but the heavier ball has greater weight [1]. It needs a higher speed for drag to equal weight → higher terminal velocity → falls faster overall [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Drag opposes motion through a fluid. $F_{drag} \propto v^2$ at high speeds.
  • Terminal velocity: $F_{drag} = W \implies a = 0 \implies$ constant speed.
  • v-t graph: steep start → curve flattening → horizontal at $v_T$.
  • Parachute: increases area → drag >> weight → decelerates → new lower $v_T$.
  • Buoyancy: $F_b = \rho_{fluid} V_{sub} g$. Equals weight of fluid displaced.
  • Float if $\rho_{object} < \rho_{fluid}$. Fraction submerged = $\rho_{object}/\rho_{fluid}$.
  • In fluids: terminal velocity when $W = F_b + F_{drag}$.
