B.1.4

Heating & Cooling Curves

Reading the Story of Energy Transfer

A heating curve is a biography of energy. Every slope, plateau, and kink tells you what's happening at the molecular level — which phase you're in, how much energy is being absorbed, and where it's going. Learn to read these graphs and you can answer almost any thermal physics question.

What Slopes Tell You

If the heater delivers energy at a constant rate ($P$ = constant), the slope of a heating curve gives information about specific heat capacity:

$$\text{slope} = \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta t} = \frac{P}{mc}$$

Steep slope

Small $c$ → substance heats quickly.

OR small mass $m$.

Examples: metals, small samples

Gentle slope

Large $c$ → substance heats slowly.

OR large mass $m$.

Examples: water, large samples

Comparing slopes on the same graph: For water, the liquid segment (C) has a gentler slope than the solid (A) or gas (E) segments because $c_{\text{water}} = 4186 > c_{\text{ice}} = 2090 > c_{\text{steam}} = 2010$. The liquid is hardest to heat.
IB exam technique: "From the graph, determine the ratio of specific heat capacities of the solid and liquid phases." → Measure the slopes of A and C. Since $\text{slope} \propto 1/c$ (same $m$ and $P$), $c_{\text{solid}}/c_{\text{liquid}} = \text{slope}_C / \text{slope}_A$.

Cooling Curves — The Reverse

A cooling curve is a heating curve in reverse. The substance loses energy to its surroundings.

Key differences from heating curves

  • Plateaus go the same way — constant temperature during phase change
  • Energy is released during condensing and freezing (bonds form → PE decreases)
  • Slopes aren't uniform in real cooling — rate of heat loss depends on $\Delta T$ with surroundings (Newton's cooling law), so curves are exponential, not straight
Supercooling: Sometimes a liquid cools below its freezing point before suddenly crystallising. The cooling curve dips below $T_m$ then jumps back up to $T_m$ for the plateau. IB occasionally tests this.

Extracting Values from a Heating Curve

If you know the heater power $P$, you can extract $c$ and $L$ from the graph:

From a sloped region (finding c)

$Q = Pt = mc\Delta T$ → $c = \frac{Pt}{m\Delta T}$

Read $t$ (duration of slope) and $\Delta T$ (temperature change) from the graph.

From a plateau region (finding L)

$Q = Pt = mL$ → $L = \frac{Pt}{m}$

Read $t$ (duration of plateau) from the graph. Temperature doesn't change.

Example: Reading a graph

A 200 W heater heats 0.50 kg of a substance. The melting plateau lasts 250 s.

$L_f = \frac{Pt}{m} = \frac{200 \times 250}{0.50} = \mathbf{1.0 \times 10^5 \text{ J kg}^{-1}}$

The liquid phase heats from 50°C to 110°C in 300 s.

$c_l = \frac{Pt}{m\Delta T} = \frac{200 \times 300}{0.50 \times 60} = \mathbf{2000 \text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}}$

IB exam: You're often given a heating curve and asked to determine $c$ or $L$. The key is identifying which region corresponds to which formula and reading time/temperature values carefully from the graph.

Real vs Ideal Curves

Ideal (textbook)

  • Sharp transitions between slopes and plateaus
  • Perfectly flat plateaus
  • Constant-gradient slopes
  • Assumes no heat loss, pure substance, uniform heating

Real (experimental)

  • Rounded transitions — phase change starts gradually
  • Plateaus may slope slightly (impurities, heat loss)
  • Cooling curves are exponential (Newton's cooling)
  • Supercooling dip possible before freezing
Impurities affect the curve: An impure substance (e.g. salt water) has a lower freezing point and a less defined plateau. The plateau may slope because different components freeze/boil at different temperatures. Pure substances give the sharpest plateaus.
Determining melting/boiling points: The temperature at which the plateau occurs gives the melting or boiling point. For a cooling curve, the freezing point is read from the plateau where the liquid solidifies. If there's supercooling, take the plateau temperature, not the dip.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Explain [2]

(a) A heating curve shows a horizontal plateau at 80°C. Explain what is occurring at the molecular level during this plateau.

The substance is undergoing a phase change (boiling/melting) at 80°C [1]. Energy supplied is being used to break intermolecular bonds (increase PE), not to increase the kinetic energy of molecules, so temperature remains constant [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A 150 W heater heats 0.40 kg of solid. The temperature rises from 30°C to 60°C in 200 s. Calculate c for the solid.

$Q = Pt = 150 \times 200 = 30\,000$ J [1]
$c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta T} = \frac{30000}{0.40 \times 30}$ [1]
$c = \mathbf{2500 \text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}}$ [1]
Determine [3]

(c) On the same graph, the melting plateau lasts 400 s. Determine the specific latent heat of fusion.

$Q = Pt = 150 \times 400 = 60\,000$ J [1]
$L_f = \frac{Q}{m} = \frac{60000}{0.40}$ [1]
$L_f = \mathbf{1.5 \times 10^5 \text{ J kg}^{-1}}$ [1]
Compare [2]

(d) On a heating curve, the slope of the solid region is steeper than that of the liquid region. State what this tells you about the specific heat capacities.

Since slope $= P/(mc)$ and $P$ and $m$ are the same for both regions, a steeper slope means a smaller specific heat capacity [1]. Therefore the solid has a lower specific heat capacity than the liquid — the solid heats up faster per unit energy [1].

Sketch [3]

Sketch a cooling curve for steam at 120°C cooled to ice at −10°C. Label: (i) condensing plateau with its temperature, (ii) freezing plateau with its temperature, (iii) the three sloped regions with the states of matter.

The curve should show: steam cooling (120→100°C, slope), condensing plateau at 100°C (flat), water cooling (100→0°C, slope with gentler gradient than steam/ice), freezing plateau at 0°C (flat), ice cooling (0→−10°C, slope) [1] per correct feature, max [3].
Calculate [3]

A heating curve shows a substance heated by a 100 W heater. The solid phase heats from 20°C to 60°C in 120 s. The liquid phase heats from 60°C to 100°C in 300 s. Mass = 0.25 kg. Find the ratio $c_{\text{liquid}} / c_{\text{solid}}$.

$c_s = \frac{Pt}{m\Delta T} = \frac{100 \times 120}{0.25 \times 40} = 1200$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ [1]
$c_l = \frac{100 \times 300}{0.25 \times 40} = 3000$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ [1]
Ratio: $c_l/c_s = 3000/1200 = \mathbf{2.5}$ [1]
Explain [2]

A cooling curve for a liquid shows a slight dip below the freezing point before the temperature rises back to the plateau and remains constant. Explain this observation.

This is supercooling — the liquid cooled below its freezing point without crystallising because nucleation sites were absent [1]. When crystallisation suddenly begins, latent heat is released, raising the temperature back to the true freezing point where it remains constant during the phase change [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Heating curve: 5 regions — 3 slopes (temperature changes, $Q = mc\Delta T$) + 2 plateaus (phase changes, $Q = mL$).
  • Slopes tell you c: slope $= P/(mc)$. Steeper = smaller c. Compare slopes to compare heat capacities.
  • Plateaus tell you L: $L = Pt/m$. Longer plateau = larger latent heat. Boiling plateau > melting plateau.
  • Cooling curves are the reverse. Energy is released during condensing and freezing.
  • Real curves: rounded transitions, sloping plateaus (impurities/heat loss), exponential cooling, possible supercooling dip.
  • Reading graphs: identify the region → pick the correct formula → read time and temperature from axes → calculate.
