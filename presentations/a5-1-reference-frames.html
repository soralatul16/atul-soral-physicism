Reference Frames

& Galilean Relativity

You're sitting "still" right now — but you're spinning with the Earth at 1,670 km/h, orbiting the Sun at 107,000 km/h, and hurtling through the galaxy at 800,000 km/h. Motion is always relative. The question is: relative to what?

What is a Reference Frame?

A reference frame (or frame of reference) is a coordinate system attached to an observer, used to measure positions, velocities, and times of events.

Frame S — Ground observer

Standing on a platform, watching a train pass. The train moves at velocity $v$ relative to you.

Frame S′ — Train passenger

Sitting on the train. To you, the platform moves backward at $-v$. Your coffee cup is stationary.

Key idea: Neither frame is "more correct." The laws of physics work the same in both. This is the principle at the heart of all relativity — Galilean and Einsteinian.
IB terminology: S is typically the "lab frame" or "rest frame." S′ is the "moving frame." S′ moves at velocity $v$ relative to S along the x-axis.

Inertial vs Non-Inertial Frames

Inertial Frame

  • Moving at constant velocity (including rest)
  • Newton's laws hold without modification
  • No fictitious forces needed

Examples: a train at constant speed, a spacecraft drifting, the ground (approximately)

Non-Inertial Frame

  • Accelerating (speeding up, slowing down, or turning)
  • Newton's laws need fictitious (pseudo) forces
  • Objects appear to accelerate without real forces

Examples: a braking car, a spinning merry-go-round, a rocket during thrust

Special relativity only applies to inertial frames. Einstein's 1905 theory deals exclusively with frames in uniform motion. General relativity (1915) extends this to accelerating frames and gravity — but that's beyond IB.

The bus test

On a smooth bus at constant speed, you can't tell you're moving — pour coffee, toss a ball, everything works normally. The moment the bus brakes, you lurch forward. You've entered a non-inertial frame. No real force pushed you forward — it's a fictitious force from the acceleration.

Galilean Transformations

To convert measurements between two inertial frames S and S′ (where S′ moves at velocity $v$ along x relative to S):

Position

$$x' = x - vt$$

$y' = y$, $z' = z$

Time

$$t' = t$$

Time is absolute — same for everyone

Galilean Velocity Addition

$$u' = u - v$$

Velocity in S′ = velocity in S minus frame velocity

This is "common sense": If you walk at 5 km/h in a train going 100 km/h, a ground observer sees you at 105 km/h (same direction) or 95 km/h (opposite). Galilean addition works perfectly at everyday speeds.
The hidden assumption: $t' = t$ — time is the same for all observers. This seems obviously true. Einstein showed it's wrong at high speeds. This is where special relativity begins.

Galilean Velocity Addition — Examples

Example 1: Walking on a train

Train moves at 30 m/s east. Person walks at 2 m/s east (relative to train).

Ground sees: $u = u' + v = 2 + 30 = \mathbf{32 \text{ m/s east}}$

Example 2: Ball thrown backward

Car moves at 20 m/s. Passenger throws ball at 20 m/s backward (relative to car).

Ground sees: $u = -20 + 20 = \mathbf{0 \text{ m/s}}$ — ball drops straight down!

Example 3: The problem with light

Spaceship at 0.5c shines a headlight. Galilean prediction: ground sees light at $c + 0.5c = 1.5c$.

Experiment says: light is ALWAYS $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s. Galilean addition fails!

This failure is the seed of special relativity. Maxwell's equations predict light travels at $c$ in all frames. Galilean transforms say it shouldn't. Something had to give — and it was our assumptions about space and time.

Galilean Principle of Relativity

GALILEO'S PRINCIPLE (1632)

"The laws of mechanics are the same in all inertial reference frames."

This means no mechanical experiment can distinguish between being at rest and being in uniform motion. Locked in a windowless train, you cannot determine if you're moving or stationary by any physics experiment.

What is invariant under Galilean transforms?

Invariant (same in all frames):
• Acceleration ($a' = a$)
• Forces ($F' = F$)
• Mass ($m' = m$)
• Time intervals ($\Delta t' = \Delta t$)
• Distances ($\Delta x' = \Delta x$ for simultaneous events)
NOT invariant (differ between frames):
• Position ($x' \neq x$)
• Velocity ($u' \neq u$)
• Kinetic energy
• Momentum
Einstein's extension (1905): Not just mechanics — ALL laws of physics (including electromagnetism and optics) are the same in all inertial frames. This seemingly small upgrade has dramatic consequences.

Why Galilean Relativity Fails at High Speed

  • Maxwell's equations (1860s) predict light travels at $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s — but they don't specify "relative to what."
  • Galilean transforms predict different light speeds in different frames — contradicting Maxwell.
  • Michelson-Morley experiment (1887) tried to detect Earth's motion through the "luminiferous aether" — the supposed medium light travels through. Result: no difference detected. Light speed was the same in all directions.
  • Conclusion: Either Maxwell is wrong (unlikely — his equations work perfectly) or Galilean transforms are wrong at high speeds.

The resolution: Einstein (1905)

Einstein kept Maxwell's equations and upgraded the transformation equations. The Galilean transforms $x' = x - vt$, $t' = t$ get replaced by the Lorentz transforms, which preserve the speed of light in all frames — at the cost of absolute time and absolute length.

Historical note: The Michelson-Morley experiment is often called "the most famous failed experiment in physics." Its null result — the absence of any aether wind — directly paved the way for Einstein's special relativity.

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

Define [1]

(a) Define an inertial reference frame.

An inertial reference frame is one in which Newton's first law holds / a frame that is not accelerating (moves at constant velocity or is at rest) [1].
Calculate [2]

(b) A boat travels at 12 m/s east relative to the river. The river flows at 5.0 m/s east relative to the bank. Calculate the velocity of the boat relative to the bank.

$u = u' + v = 12 + 5.0$ [1]
$u = \mathbf{17 \text{ m/s east}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) State the Galilean principle of relativity and explain one of its consequences.

The laws of mechanics are the same in all inertial reference frames [1]. Consequence: No mechanical experiment can distinguish between a state of rest and a state of uniform motion — e.g., dropping a ball in a smooth train gives the same result as dropping it on the ground [1].
Explain [3]

(d) Explain why Galilean velocity addition is inconsistent with Maxwell's electromagnetic theory and describe the experimental evidence that supports Maxwell over Galileo.

Maxwell's equations predict light travels at $c$ in all frames, but Galilean velocity addition predicts different light speeds in different frames [1]. The Michelson-Morley experiment measured the speed of light in different directions (parallel and perpendicular to Earth's motion) and found no difference [1]. This null result confirmed that light speed is invariant, supporting Maxwell and contradicting Galilean velocity addition at high speeds [1].

More Practice 7/10

Calculate [2]

Spaceship A moves at 0.6c relative to Earth. Spaceship B moves at 0.8c relative to Earth in the same direction. Using Galilean velocity addition, calculate the speed of B relative to A. State why this answer is problematic.

Galilean: $u' = u - v = 0.8c - 0.6c = 0.2c$ [1].
This particular answer seems fine, but if B moved at 0.6c relative to A, the Galilean prediction for B relative to Earth would be $0.6c + 0.6c = 1.2c$, which exceeds the speed of light. Nothing can travel faster than $c$ — Galilean addition gives unphysical results at relativistic speeds [1].
Explain [2]

A passenger on a train drops a ball. Describe the trajectory of the ball as seen by: (i) the passenger, (ii) a ground observer.

(i) The passenger sees the ball fall straight down, accelerating due to gravity [1].
(ii) The ground observer sees the ball follow a parabolic path — it has the train's horizontal velocity plus downward gravitational acceleration, identical to projectile motion [1].
Distinguish [2]

Distinguish between an inertial and a non-inertial reference frame. Give one example of each.

An inertial frame moves at constant velocity (or is at rest) and Newton's first law holds without modification — e.g., a spacecraft drifting in deep space [1]. A non-inertial frame is accelerating and requires fictitious forces to explain observations — e.g., a car going around a bend where passengers feel pushed outward [1].
Preview: In A.5.2, Einstein takes Galileo's principle and makes it universal — not just mechanics but ALL physics, including light. Two simple postulates will reshape our understanding of space and time.

Key Takeaways

  • Reference frame = coordinate system attached to an observer for measuring motion.
  • Inertial frame: constant velocity, Newton's laws hold. Non-inertial: accelerating, needs fictitious forces.
  • Galilean transforms: $x' = x - vt$, $t' = t$, $u' = u - v$. Work perfectly at low speeds.
  • Galilean principle: Laws of mechanics are the same in all inertial frames.
  • Galilean velocity addition fails for light — predicts different speeds of light in different frames.
  • Michelson-Morley (1887): Found no evidence of aether. Light speed is the same in all directions.
  • The conflict between Galilean transforms and Maxwell's equations led Einstein to special relativity (1905).
