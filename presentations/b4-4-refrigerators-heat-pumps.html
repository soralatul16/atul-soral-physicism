Refrigerators, Heat Pumps & the COP

Running an engine in reverse

A heat engine run backwards uses work to move heat from cold to hot — that's a refrigerator or a heat pump. Since heat won't flow uphill on its own, this needs a work input. Here we define the coefficient of performance (COP) for both, and find its Carnot ceiling.

A Heat Engine Run Backwards

Reverse the arrows of a heat engine: supply work $W$, extract heat $Q_C$ from the cold space, and deliver $Q_H$ to the hot surroundings.

$$Q_H = Q_C + W$$

heat delivered to hot = heat drawn from cold + work supplied

Same device, two names. A refrigerator cares about $Q_C$ (heat removed from the cold interior). A heat pump cares about $Q_H$ (heat delivered to the warm room). The machine is identical — only the useful output differs.

Coefficient of Performance (COP)

COP = (useful heat transferred) ÷ (work input). Because the useful output is heat, COP is usually greater than 1 — that's why these devices are so efficient at moving heat.

Refrigerator

$$COP_{ref} = \frac{Q_C}{W} = \frac{Q_C}{Q_H - Q_C}$$

Useful effect = heat removed from the cold interior.

Heat pump

$$COP_{hp} = \frac{Q_H}{W} = \frac{Q_H}{Q_H - Q_C}$$

Useful effect = heat delivered to the warm room.

Neat relationship

Since $Q_H = Q_C + W$, dividing by $W$ gives $\dfrac{Q_H}{W} = \dfrac{Q_C}{W} + 1$, so $COP_{hp} = COP_{ref} + 1$. A heat pump's COP is always exactly one more than the fridge COP for the same machine.

COP > 1 is normal, not a violation. You are not creating energy — you are moving heat. Delivering 4 J of heat for 1 J of work (COP = 4) just means 3 J came free from the cold reservoir. Perfectly consistent with the first law.

The Maximum (Carnot) COP

For an ideal reversible device, $Q_C/Q_H = T_C/T_H$, so the COP can be written entirely in terms of temperatures:

Refrigerator (max)

$$COP_{ref}^{max} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C}$$

Heat pump (max)

$$COP_{hp}^{max} = \frac{T_H}{T_H - T_C}$$
  • T in kelvin, and $(T_H - T_C)$ is the temperature gap the device works across.
  • Small temperature gap → huge COP. Moving heat across a tiny difference costs very little work.
  • Large gap → COP drops. A freezer on a hot day (big $T_H - T_C$) works harder per joule removed.
  • Real devices always have COP below these Carnot maxima due to irreversibilities.
Why heat pumps beat electric heaters: a resistive heater turns 1 J of electricity into 1 J of heat (COP = 1). A heat pump with COP = 4 delivers 4 J of heat per 1 J of electricity — pulling the extra 3 J from the cold outside air. That's the physics behind modern efficient home heating.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — fridge COP

A refrigerator removes 500 J from its interior using 125 J of work per cycle. Find its COP and the heat rejected to the room.

$COP_{ref} = Q_C/W = 500/125 = \mathbf{4.0}$; $Q_H = Q_C + W = 500 + 125 = \mathbf{625\ \text{J}}$

Example 2 — Carnot heat pump

A heat pump keeps a room at 295 K by drawing heat from outside air at 275 K. Find the maximum possible COP.

$COP_{hp}^{max} = \dfrac{T_H}{T_H - T_C} = \dfrac{295}{295 - 275} = \dfrac{295}{20} = \mathbf{14.75}$

Example 3 — work needed

A Carnot fridge keeps food at 273 K in a 303 K kitchen. How much work is needed to remove 900 J?

$COP_{ref}^{max} = 273/(303-273) = 273/30 = 9.1$.

$W = Q_C / COP = 900 / 9.1 = \mathbf{99\ \text{J}}$

The unifying picture

Engines, fridges, and heat pumps are all the same cycle read differently:

  • Engine: heat flows hot→cold, producing work (clockwise loop).
  • Fridge / heat pump: work forces heat cold→hot (anticlockwise loop).
Second law in action: the fridge doesn't break the second law — the entropy dumped into the warm room exceeds the entropy removed from the cold interior, so total entropy still rises.

(a) Define the coefficient of performance for (i) a refrigerator and (ii) a heat pump.

(i) Refrigerator: $COP_{ref} = Q_C/W$ — the heat extracted from the cold space per unit of work input [1]. (ii) Heat pump: $COP_{hp} = Q_H/W$ — the heat delivered to the hot space per unit of work input [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A refrigerator extracts 1200 J of heat from its interior and rejects 1600 J to the room per cycle. Determine the work input and the coefficient of performance.

$W = Q_H - Q_C = 1600 - 1200 = 400$ J [1]
$COP_{ref} = Q_C/W = 1200/400$ [1]
$= \mathbf{3.0}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) A Carnot heat pump warms a house at 293 K by extracting heat from the ground at 283 K. Calculate the maximum COP and comment on why real heat pumps perform worse in very cold weather.

$COP_{hp}^{max} = T_H/(T_H - T_C) = 293/(293-283) = 293/10 = \mathbf{29.3}$ [1]. In very cold weather $T_C$ falls, so $(T_H - T_C)$ increases, reducing the COP — the pump must do more work per joule delivered, so real-world efficiency drops in winter [1].
Explain [3]

(d) Explain how a refrigerator is consistent with the second law of thermodynamics, given that it makes a cold region colder.

The second law forbids heat flowing spontaneously from cold to hot, but the refrigerator does work (uses electrical energy) to force this transfer, so it is not spontaneous [1]. Considering the whole system: the entropy decrease of the cold interior is smaller than the entropy increase of the warm surroundings (which receives $Q_H = Q_C + W$ at a higher... lower T ratio) [1]. Therefore the total entropy of the universe still increases, satisfying $\Delta S_{total} > 0$ [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Reverse cycle: work input moves heat cold→hot; $Q_H = Q_C + W$.
  • Refrigerator COP: $COP_{ref} = Q_C/W = \dfrac{T_C}{T_H - T_C}$ (Carnot max).
  • Heat pump COP: $COP_{hp} = Q_H/W = \dfrac{T_H}{T_H - T_C}$ (Carnot max).
  • Relationship: $COP_{hp} = COP_{ref} + 1$ for the same machine.
  • COP > 1 is normal — you move heat, not create it. Smaller temperature gap → higher COP.
  • Second law: the extra entropy dumped to the hot side keeps $\Delta S_{total} > 0$.
