B.5.5

Kirchhoff's Laws

Conservation, applied to circuits

When circuits get too tangled for simple series/parallel rules, Kirchhoff's two laws always work. They are conservation of charge and conservation of energy in disguise, and together they let you write equations to solve any network, however complex.

B.5 Current & Circuits  |  B.5.5 Kirchhoff's LawsFirst law (junction)

First Law — The Junction Rule

The sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum of currents leaving it.

$$\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$$
This is conservation of charge. Charge cannot pile up or vanish at a junction, so whatever flows in must flow out. It's why the current splitting into parallel branches always adds back up to the total.
B.5 Current & Circuits  |  B.5.5 Kirchhoff's LawsSecond law (loop)

Second Law — The Loop Rule

Around any closed loop, the sum of the EMFs equals the sum of the potential drops (IR terms).

$$\sum \varepsilon = \sum IR$$
  • Equivalently, the sum of all potential changes around a complete loop is zero: $\sum V = 0$.
  • Going around a loop, you rise in potential across a source (EMF) and drop across each resistor ($IR$).
  • Return to your starting point at the same potential — so the rises and drops must balance.
This is conservation of energy. Each coulomb gains energy from the sources and loses exactly that much to the resistors over one full loop. Energy per charge (voltage) around a closed loop nets to zero.

Simple single loop

A 9 V battery drives current through 2 Ω and 4 Ω in series. Loop rule: $9 = I(2) + I(4) = 6I$, so $I = 1.5$ A — the same answer series rules give, but now from first principles.

Getting the Signs Right

The whole difficulty of Kirchhoff problems is signs. Pick a direction to travel around the loop and apply these rules consistently.

ElementPotential rises (+) when…Potential drops (−) when…
EMF (cell)travel − to + terminaltravel + to − terminal
Resistortravel against the currenttravel with the current

Method for any network

  1. Assign a current (with a guessed direction) to each branch.
  2. Apply the junction rule at enough junctions to relate the currents.
  3. Apply the loop rule around enough independent loops to get one equation per unknown.
  4. Solve the simultaneous equations. A negative answer just means that current actually flows opposite to your guess.
Don't panic over a wrong guess. If you assume a current direction and the algebra returns a negative value, the magnitude is still correct — the real current simply flows the other way. No need to redo the problem.

Worked Example: A Two-Loop Circuit

Setup: Two cells drive a shared middle branch. Left loop: 6.0 V cell with a 2.0 Ω resistor. Right loop: 4.0 V cell with a 1.0 Ω resistor. Shared middle resistor: 3.0 Ω. Let $I_1$ (left), $I_2$ (right) flow into the top junction, and $I_3$ flow down through the middle 3.0 Ω.

Junction rule: $I_1 + I_2 = I_3$

Left loop: $6.0 = 2.0\,I_1 + 3.0\,I_3$

Right loop: $4.0 = 1.0\,I_2 + 3.0\,I_3$

Solving

Substitute $I_3 = I_1 + I_2$ into both loop equations:

Left: $6.0 = 5.0\,I_1 + 3.0\,I_2$. Right: $4.0 = 3.0\,I_1 + 4.0\,I_2$.

Solving: $I_1 \approx 1.09$ A, $I_2 \approx 0.18$ A, $I_3 = I_1+I_2 \approx \mathbf{1.27\ \text{A}}$

The takeaway: two junction/loop equations per unknown always give a solvable system. This method never fails, even where series/parallel shortcuts break down (e.g. multiple sources, bridge circuits).

Worked Examples

Example 1 — junction rule

At a junction, 4.0 A and 1.5 A flow in, and two branches carry current out; one carries 2.0 A. Find the other.

$\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$: $4.0 + 1.5 = 2.0 + I$, so $I = \mathbf{3.5\ \text{A}}$

Example 2 — loop rule with internal resistance

A single loop: EMF 12 V, internal resistance 0.5 Ω, external 5.5 Ω. Apply the loop rule to find the current.

$\sum\varepsilon = \sum IR$: $12 = I(0.5) + I(5.5) = 6.0\,I$, so $I = \mathbf{2.0\ \text{A}}$

Example 3 — opposing cells

Two cells, 9.0 V and 3.0 V, are connected in a loop with their EMFs opposing, plus a 6.0 Ω resistor. Find the current.

Net EMF $= 9.0 - 3.0 = 6.0$ V.

$I = \dfrac{\sum\varepsilon}{\sum R} = \dfrac{6.0}{6.0} = \mathbf{1.0\ \text{A}}$

State & Link [3]

(a) State Kirchhoff's two laws and identify the conservation principle each represents.

First law (junction rule): the sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum leaving it, $\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$ — this is conservation of charge [1]. Second law (loop rule): around any closed loop, the sum of EMFs equals the sum of IR drops, $\sum\varepsilon = \sum IR$ (equivalently $\sum V = 0$) [1] — this is conservation of energy [1].
Apply [2]

(b) At a junction, currents of 3.0 A and 2.0 A enter, while 1.5 A leaves along one branch. Determine the current in the remaining branch and state its direction.

$\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$: $3.0 + 2.0 = 1.5 + I$ [1], so $I = 3.5$ A leaving the junction (outward) [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A single loop contains a 15 V battery (internal resistance 1.0 Ω), a 2.0 Ω and a 3.0 Ω resistor in series. Using the loop rule, find the current and the terminal PD.

Loop rule: $15 = I(1.0 + 2.0 + 3.0) = 6.0\,I$, so $I = 2.5$ A [1]. Terminal PD = EMF − lost volts $= 15 - I(1.0) = 15 - 2.5$ [1] $= \mathbf{12.5\ \text{V}}$ [1].
Reason [2]

(d) A student solves a Kirchhoff problem and obtains a current of −0.40 A for one branch. Explain what this negative value means.

The magnitude of the current is 0.40 A [1]. The negative sign means the actual direction of current flow in that branch is opposite to the direction the student initially assumed when assigning the current — the answer is still valid, only the assumed direction was reversed [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Junction rule: $\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$ — conservation of charge.
  • Loop rule: $\sum\varepsilon = \sum IR$ (or $\sum V = 0$) — conservation of energy.
  • Signs: potential rises through a source (− to +); drops across a resistor in the current's direction.
  • Method: assign currents → junction equations → loop equations → solve simultaneously.
  • A negative current just means the real direction is opposite to your guess.
  • Always works — even for multi-source and bridge circuits where series/parallel shortcuts fail.
