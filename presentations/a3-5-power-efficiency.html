Power & Efficiency

Two engines do the same work. One takes 10 seconds, the other takes 2 seconds. Same work, different power. Power tells you how FAST energy is transferred — and efficiency tells you how much is wasted.

Power — The Rate of Energy Transfer

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred.

$$P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{\Delta E}{t}$$
  • Scalar quantity
  • SI unit: Watt (W). $1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ J s}^{-1}$
  • 1 kW = 1000 W. 1 MW = $10^6$ W. 1 horsepower $\approx$ 746 W
  • A 100 W light bulb converts 100 joules of electrical energy per second

The velocity form of power:

When a constant force $F$ moves an object at constant velocity $v$:

$$P = Fv$$
Derivation: $P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{Fs}{t} = F \cdot \frac{s}{t} = Fv$. This is extremely useful for problems involving engines, cars, and constant speed motion.
$P = Fv$ only works for constant force at constant velocity. If the object is accelerating, $v$ is changing and you need to be careful — the instantaneous power is $P = Fv$ at that instant, but average power uses $P = W/t$.

Using $P = Fv$ — Engine Problems

When a car moves at constant speed, the engine force equals the total resistive force. This gives us powerful relationships:

At constant speed:

  • $F_{engine} = F_{resistance}$ (equilibrium)
  • $P = F_{engine} \times v$
  • Maximum speed: $v_{max} = \frac{P_{max}}{F_{resistance}}$
Why cars have a top speed: As speed increases, drag increases ($\propto v^2$). At some speed, all engine power goes to overcoming drag. $P_{engine} = F_{drag} \times v$ → can't go faster.

Typical values:

  • Human walking: ~75 W
  • Cyclist: ~200 W
  • Small car: ~75 kW (100 hp)
  • Sports car: ~300 kW (400 hp)
  • Power station: ~500 MW
IB favourite: "A car engine has power 60 kW. At constant speed, the resistive force is 800 N. Find the maximum speed."
$v = P/F = 60000/800 = 75$ m/s (270 km/h).

Efficiency

Efficiency is the ratio of useful energy output to total energy input.

$$\eta = \frac{\text{useful energy output}}{\text{total energy input}} \times 100\%$$
$$\eta = \frac{\text{useful power output}}{\text{total power input}} \times 100\%$$
  • Dimensionless — expressed as a percentage (0–100%)
  • Always less than 100% — energy is always lost to heat, sound, etc.
  • $\eta = 100\%$ is impossible in practice (2nd law of thermodynamics)

LED bulb

~40%

Car engine

~25%

Electric motor

~90%

"Energy lost" doesn't mean it disappears. It transforms into less useful forms (heat, sound). A car engine converts 25% of fuel energy to motion — the other 75% becomes heat in the exhaust and engine block.

Worked Example 1 — Lifting 3/10

A crane lifts a 500 kg load 20 m in 8.0 s at constant speed. Find the power.
→$W = mgh = 500 \times 9.8 \times 20 = 98000$ J
$$P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{98000}{8.0} = \mathbf{12250 \text{ W}} = 12.25 \text{ kW}$$

Worked Example 2 — Car at constant speed 5/10

A car engine delivers 45 kW. At constant speed on a flat road, the total resistance is 900 N. Find (a) the speed (b) the fuel consumption if efficiency is 28%.
a$P = Fv$: $v = P/F = 45000/900 = \mathbf{50 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ (180 km/h)
b$\eta = P_{out}/P_{in}$: $P_{in} = 45000/0.28 = 160714$ W

The engine burns fuel at 160.7 kW but only 45 kW reaches the wheels. The rest (115.7 kW) is wasted as heat.

Worked Example 3 — Efficiency 6/10

A motor uses 2400 J of electrical energy to lift a 30 kg mass 6.0 m. Find the efficiency.
→Useful output = $mgh = 30 \times 9.8 \times 6.0 = 1764$ J
$$\eta = \frac{1764}{2400} \times 100 = \mathbf{73.5\%}$$

636 J was wasted as heat in the motor windings and friction.

IB-Style Question Set 8/10

Context: A 1200 kg car has a maximum engine power of 80 kW. The resistive force on the car is given by $F_R = 0.40v^2$ (in N, where $v$ is in m/s).
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the maximum speed of the car on a flat road.

At max speed: $P = F_R v$: $80000 = 0.40v^2 \times v = 0.40v^3$
$v^3 = 200000 \implies v = \sqrt[3]{200000} = \mathbf{58.5 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$ (211 km/h) [2]
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the resistive force at 30 m/s and the acceleration at this speed using full power.

$F_R = 0.40(30)^2 = 360$ N. $F_{engine} = P/v = 80000/30 = 2667$ N [1]
$F_{net} = 2667 - 360 = 2307$ N. $a = 2307/1200 = \mathbf{1.92 \text{ m s}^{-2}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) Explain why the car's acceleration decreases as it speeds up, even though engine power is constant.

As $v$ increases, $F_R = 0.40v^2$ increases rapidly [1]. The engine force $F = P/v$ decreases with speed. So $F_{net} = F_{engine} - F_R$ decreases, giving less acceleration. At $v_{max}$: $F_{net} = 0$, $a = 0$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(d) The car drives up a hill at 25° at constant speed of 20 m/s. Find the required engine power.

At constant speed: $F_{engine} = F_R + mg\sin25°$
$F_R = 0.40(400) = 160$ N. $mg\sin25° = 1200(9.8)(0.423) = 4972$ N [1]
$F_{engine} = 160 + 4972 = 5132$ N [1]
$P = Fv = 5132 \times 20 = \mathbf{102640 \text{ W}} = 102.6$ kW [1]
(Exceeds max power of 80 kW — the car can't maintain this speed up this hill!)
Determine [2]

(e) If the engine efficiency is 30%, calculate the rate of fuel energy consumption at maximum speed.

$P_{input} = P_{output}/\eta = 80000/0.30 = \mathbf{267 \text{ kW}}$ [1]
The engine burns fuel at 267 kW. Only 80 kW reaches the wheels. 187 kW is wasted as heat [1].

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A person does 3000 J of work climbing stairs in 20 s. Find their power output.

$P = W/t = 3000/20 = \mathbf{150 \text{ W}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A 60 kg student runs up a 4.0 m flight of stairs in 3.0 s. Find the power output against gravity.

$W = mgh = 60(9.8)(4.0) = 2352$ J [1]
$P = 2352/3.0 = \mathbf{784 \text{ W}}$ [1]
(About 1 horsepower — running upstairs is hard work!) [1]
Calculate [3] Hard

A pump lifts 200 kg of water per minute to a height of 15 m. The pump efficiency is 60%. Find the electrical power input.

Useful power: $P_{out} = mgh/t = 200(9.8)(15)/60 = 490$ W [1]
$P_{input} = P_{out}/\eta = 490/0.60 = \mathbf{817 \text{ W}}$ [1]
327 W is wasted as heat in the pump [1].
Explain [2] Hard

Two identical cars race. Car A has 200 hp, Car B has 100 hp. Both reach a top speed where $P = F_{drag} \times v$ and $F_{drag} = cv^2$. By what factor is Car A's top speed greater?

$P = cv^3 \implies v = (P/c)^{1/3}$. Car A has 2× the power [1].
$v_A/v_B = (2P/c)^{1/3} / (P/c)^{1/3} = 2^{1/3} = \mathbf{1.26}$ [1].
Only 26% faster with double the power — diminishing returns due to $v^3$ dependence.

Key Takeaways

  • $P = W/t = \Delta E/t$ — rate of energy transfer. Unit: Watt (J/s).
  • $P = Fv$ — for constant force at constant (or instantaneous) velocity.
  • Max speed: $v_{max} = P_{max}/F_{resistance}$. All engine power overcomes drag.
  • Efficiency: $\eta = E_{useful}/E_{input} \times 100\% = P_{out}/P_{in} \times 100\%$.
  • Always $< 100\%$ — energy is always lost to heat, friction, sound.
  • With drag $\propto v^2$: $P = cv^3$ at max speed. Double power → only $2^{1/3} = 1.26×$ faster.
  • Uphill at constant speed: $P = (F_R + mg\sin\theta) \times v$.
