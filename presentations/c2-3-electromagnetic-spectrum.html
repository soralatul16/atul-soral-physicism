What All EM Waves Share

Every electromagnetic wave is a transverse wave of oscillating electric and magnetic fields, at right angles to each other and to the direction of travel. They all share these properties:

  • Travel at the same speed in a vacuum: $c = 3.0\times10^8$ m s⁻¹.
  • Are transverse — and therefore can all be polarised.
  • Need no medium — they propagate through empty space.
  • Are unaffected by electric or magnetic fields (they carry no charge).
  • Obey $c = f\lambda$ and can reflect, refract, diffract and interfere.
  • Transfer energy; photon energy is $E = hf$, so higher frequency = more energetic.
$$c = f\lambda \qquad E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$
The single variable: the only physical difference between radio and gamma is frequency (and therefore wavelength and photon energy). Everything else — behaviour, danger, use — flows from that.

The Seven Bands, In Order

From longest wavelength (lowest frequency, lowest energy) to shortest:

  • Radio → Micro → Infrared → Visible → Ultraviolet → X-ray → Gamma
  • As you move right: frequency ↑, wavelength ↓, photon energy ↑, penetrating power / danger ↑.
Mnemonic: "Roman Men Invented Very Unusual X-ray Guns" — Radio, Micro, Infrared, Visible, Ultraviolet, X-ray, Gamma.

Visible Light — The Narrow Window

Visible light is a tiny slice of the spectrum, roughly 400 nm to 700 nm. Within it, wavelength sets colour:

  • Violet has the shortest wavelength (~400 nm) → highest frequency → highest photon energy.
  • Red has the longest wavelength (~700 nm) → lowest frequency → lowest photon energy.
  • Order (short→long λ): Violet, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red.
Beyond the edges: just past violet lies ultraviolet (invisible, more energetic); just past red lies infrared (invisible, felt as heat). Bees see into the UV; snakes "see" infrared.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — frequency of green light

Green light has wavelength 550 nm. Find its frequency ($c = 3.0\times10^8$).

$f = c/\lambda = (3.0\times10^8)/(550\times10^{-9})$

$= \mathbf{5.5\times10^{14}\ \text{Hz}}$

Example 2 — wavelength of a radio station

An FM station broadcasts at 98.0 MHz. Find the wavelength.

$\lambda = c/f = (3.0\times10^8)/(98.0\times10^6)$

$= \mathbf{3.06\ \text{m}}$

Example 3 — photon energy of an X-ray

Find the photon energy of an X-ray of frequency $3.0\times10^{18}$ Hz ($h = 6.63\times10^{-34}$ J s).

$E = hf = (6.63\times10^{-34})(3.0\times10^{18})$

$= \mathbf{2.0\times10^{-15}\ \text{J}}$ (far more than a visible photon, ~$10^{-19}$ J)

IB-Style Question Set 5/10

State [3]

(a) State three properties that are common to all electromagnetic waves.

Any three of: they all travel at $3.0\times10^8$ m s⁻¹ in a vacuum [1]; they are all transverse (and can be polarised) [1]; they require no medium / travel through a vacuum [1]; they obey $c = f\lambda$; they transfer energy; they are not deflected by electric or magnetic fields.
List [2]

(b) List the seven regions of the EM spectrum in order of increasing frequency.

Radio, microwave, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma [2] (all seven correct and in order for full marks).
Calculate [2]

(c) A microwave has wavelength 0.12 m. Calculate its frequency.

$f = c/\lambda = (3.0\times10^8)/0.12$ [1] $= \mathbf{2.5\times10^9\ \text{Hz}}$ (2.5 GHz) [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why gamma rays are far more hazardous to living tissue than radio waves.

Gamma rays have a much higher frequency, so each photon carries far more energy ($E = hf$) [1]. This energy is high enough to ionise atoms and molecules — damaging DNA and cells — whereas the low-energy photons of radio waves are non-ionising and cause no such damage [1].

Key Takeaways

  • All EM waves are transverse, travel at $c=3.0\times10^8$ m s⁻¹ in vacuum, need no medium, obey $c=f\lambda$.
  • Order (↑ frequency): Radio, Micro, Infrared, Visible, Ultraviolet, X-ray, Gamma.
  • Moving up the spectrum: f ↑, λ ↓, photon energy $E=hf$ ↑, danger ↑.
  • Ionising (UV, X-ray, gamma) can damage DNA; radio–visible are non-ionising.
  • Visible light: ~400 nm (violet) to ~700 nm (red); wavelength sets colour.
  • Each band's use follows its energy: radio for signals → gamma for therapy/imaging.
