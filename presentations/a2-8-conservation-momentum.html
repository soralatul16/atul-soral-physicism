Conservation of
Linear Momentum

Momentum can't be created or destroyed — only transferred. This makes it one of the most fundamental conservation laws in all of physics, from subatomic collisions to galaxy mergers.

The Law of Conservation of Momentum

The total momentum of a system remains constant, provided no external net force acts on it.
$$\sum \vec{p}_{before} = \sum \vec{p}_{after}$$
$$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$$
  • System = all objects involved in the interaction
  • No external net force = isolated system (internal forces don't count)
  • Momentum is transferred between objects but the total stays the same
  • Works in every direction independently (x and y separately in 2D)
Why internal forces don't matter: By Newton's Third Law, internal forces come in equal and opposite pairs. They cancel within the system. Only EXTERNAL forces can change the system's total momentum.
Derivation from Newton's 3rd Law: $F_{A\text{ on }B} = -F_{B\text{ on }A}$ → $\frac{\Delta p_B}{\Delta t} = -\frac{\Delta p_A}{\Delta t}$ → $\Delta p_A + \Delta p_B = 0$ → total momentum unchanged.

When Can We Use Conservation of Momentum?

YES — momentum is conserved:

  • Collisions (any type — elastic, inelastic)
  • Explosions (object splits apart)
  • Recoil (gun firing, rocket launching)
  • Any interaction where external forces are negligible during the event

CAREFUL — external forces present:

  • Friction during the collision → still approximately conserved if collision time is very short
  • Gravity during the collision → usually negligible for short-duration events
  • After a collision (objects sliding to stop) → NOT conserved (friction acts)
The key insight: Even when energy is lost (heat, sound, deformation), momentum is ALWAYS conserved in an isolated system. Energy and momentum are different — you can lose one while keeping the other.
IB exam trap: "Is momentum conserved in an inelastic collision?" YES — always, in all collision types. Kinetic energy is NOT conserved in inelastic collisions, but momentum IS. Don't confuse them.

Worked Example 1 — Head-on Collision 5/10

A 3.0 kg ball moving at 4.0 m/s East collides with a 2.0 kg ball moving at 3.0 m/s West. After collision, the 3.0 kg ball moves at 1.0 m/s East. Find the velocity of the 2.0 kg ball after collision.
1Define East = positive. $u_1 = +4$, $u_2 = -3$, $v_1 = +1$
2$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$
$$3(4) + 2(-3) = 3(1) + 2v_2$$
$$12 - 6 = 3 + 2v_2 \implies 6 = 3 + 2v_2 \implies v_2 = \mathbf{+1.5 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$

Positive → East. The 2 kg ball reverses direction and moves East at 1.5 m/s.

Check: Total $p_{before} = 12 - 6 = 6$. Total $p_{after} = 3 + 3 = 6$ ✓ Momentum conserved.

Explosions & Recoil

In an explosion, total momentum before = 0 (at rest). So total momentum after must also = 0.

$$0 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 \implies m_1 v_1 = -m_2 v_2$$

The pieces fly off in opposite directions with momenta that are equal in magnitude.

Worked Example — Gun Recoil: 5/10

A 4.0 kg rifle fires a 0.010 kg bullet at 400 m/s. Find the recoil velocity of the rifle.
→$0 = m_b v_b + m_r v_r$: $0 = 0.010(400) + 4.0(v_r)$
$$v_r = \frac{-4.0}{4.0} = \mathbf{-1.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$

Negative = backward. The rifle recoils at 1.0 m/s — slow because it's 400× heavier than the bullet.

Rocket propulsion is continuous recoil: exhaust goes backward, rocket goes forward. Total momentum of (rocket + exhaust) stays constant.

Conservation in 2D

Momentum is conserved independently in each direction:

$$\sum p_x \text{ (before)} = \sum p_x \text{ (after)}$$
$$\sum p_y \text{ (before)} = \sum p_y \text{ (after)}$$

This gives you two equations — one for x, one for y. Use them simultaneously to find unknowns.

Worked Example — 2D Collision: 8/10

A 2.0 kg ball moving East at 5.0 m/s collides with a stationary 3.0 kg ball. After collision, the 2.0 kg ball moves at 3.0 m/s at 30° North of East. Find the velocity of the 3.0 kg ball.
x$2(5) + 0 = 2(3\cos30°) + 3v_x$: $10 = 5.20 + 3v_x$ → $v_x = 1.60$ m/s
y$0 + 0 = 2(3\sin30°) + 3v_y$: $0 = 3.0 + 3v_y$ → $v_y = -1.0$ m/s
→$v = \sqrt{1.60^2 + 1.0^2} = \sqrt{3.56} = \mathbf{1.89 \text{ m/s}}$ at $\theta = \tan^{-1}(1.0/1.6) = 32°$ South of East
The 3 kg ball goes South-East — makes sense because momentum in the y-direction must cancel (it was zero before, so the y-momenta of both balls must be equal and opposite after).

IB-Style Question Set 9/10

Context: A 1500 kg car travelling East at 20 m/s collides with a 1000 kg car travelling North at 15 m/s at a junction. The cars lock together after the collision.
Calculate [2]

(a) Calculate the total momentum before the collision in each direction.

$p_x = 1500 \times 20 = 30{,}000$ kg m/s (East) [1]
$p_y = 1000 \times 15 = 15{,}000$ kg m/s (North) [1]
Calculate [3]

(b) Calculate the speed and direction of the wreckage immediately after the collision.

Combined mass $= 2500$ kg.
$v_x = 30000/2500 = 12$ m/s [1]
$v_y = 15000/2500 = 6$ m/s [1]
$v = \sqrt{12^2+6^2} = \sqrt{180} = \mathbf{13.4 \text{ m/s}}$ at $\theta = \tan^{-1}(6/12) = \mathbf{26.6°}$ North of East [1]
Calculate [2]

(c) Calculate the kinetic energy before and after the collision.

$KE_{before} = \frac{1}{2}(1500)(20^2) + \frac{1}{2}(1000)(15^2) = 300000 + 112500 = \mathbf{412{,}500 \text{ J}}$ [1]
$KE_{after} = \frac{1}{2}(2500)(13.4^2) = \frac{1}{2}(2500)(180) = \mathbf{225{,}000 \text{ J}}$ [1]
Determine [2]

(d) Determine the energy lost and state what happened to it.

$\Delta KE = 412500 - 225000 = \mathbf{187{,}500 \text{ J}}$ lost [1].
Converted to: heat, sound, deformation of car bodies [1]. This confirms it's a perfectly inelastic collision (objects stick together).
State [1]

(e) Is momentum conserved in this collision? Is kinetic energy conserved?

Momentum: YES — always conserved in any collision (no external net force). KE: NO — 45% was lost. This is an inelastic collision [1].

Practice Questions

Calculate [2] Easy

A 5 kg ball at 6 m/s hits a stationary 3 kg ball. They stick together. Find the velocity after.

$5(6) + 3(0) = (5+3)v$ → $30 = 8v$ → $v = \mathbf{3.75 \text{ m/s}}$ [2]
Calculate [3] Medium

A 60 kg person standing on ice throws a 5.0 kg ball at 12 m/s. Find their recoil velocity.

$0 = 5(12) + 60v$ → $v = -60/60 = \mathbf{-1.0 \text{ m/s}}$ [2]
(Person moves backward at 1.0 m/s) [1]
Calculate [3] Hard

A 10 kg bomb at rest explodes into two pieces. A 3 kg piece flies East at 20 m/s. What is the velocity of the 7 kg piece?

$0 = 3(20) + 7v$ → $7v = -60$ → $v = \mathbf{-8.57 \text{ m/s}}$ (West) [2]
Check: $|p_1| = 60$, $|p_2| = 60$ ✓ Equal and opposite [1].
Explain [2] Hard

In a perfectly inelastic collision, objects stick together and maximum KE is lost. Yet momentum is still conserved. Explain why these two facts are not contradictory.

Momentum and kinetic energy are different quantities [1]. Momentum is a vector conserved by Newton's 3rd Law (internal forces cancel). KE is a scalar that can be converted to other energy forms (heat, sound, deformation). Conservation of momentum is a law; conservation of KE is only true for elastic collisions [1].

Key Takeaways

  • $\sum p_{before} = \sum p_{after}$ — always, in any collision/explosion, if no external net force.
  • Momentum is ALWAYS conserved in collisions — elastic, inelastic, perfectly inelastic.
  • KE is NOT always conserved — only in elastic collisions.
  • Explosions: total $p = 0$ before and after. Pieces fly in opposite directions.
  • Recoil: $m_1 v_1 = -m_2 v_2$. Heavy object moves slowly, light object moves fast.
  • 2D: conserve $p_x$ and $p_y$ separately. Two equations, solve simultaneously.
  • Perfectly inelastic: objects stick. Maximum KE loss. Use $m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1+m_2)v$.
