A.5.7

E = mc²

Mass-Energy Equivalence

The most famous equation in physics. Five symbols that say: mass is frozen energy, and energy has mass. A 1 kg object contains 90 quadrillion joules — enough to power a city for years. This is why the Sun shines and why nuclear weapons are devastating.

Atul Soral Physicism

A.5 Galilean & Special Relativity  |  A.5.7 Mass-Energy EquivalenceThe Meaning

What E = mc² Really Means

$$E_0 = mc^2$$

Mass → Energy

A small amount of mass can be converted into an enormous amount of energy.

$c^2 = 9 \times 10^{16}$ m² s⁻² — a colossal multiplier.

1 kg of mass = 9 × 10¹⁶ J

Energy → Mass

Energy has mass. A hot object is (very slightly) heavier than a cold one.

A compressed spring has more mass than a relaxed one.

$\Delta m = \Delta E / c^2$

Mass and energy are two forms of the same thing. They are interconvertible. When mass "disappears" in nuclear reactions, it hasn't been destroyed — it's been converted to energy (kinetic energy of particles, photons, etc.).
IB precision: $E_0 = mc^2$ is the rest energy. The total energy is $E = \gamma mc^2$. When people say "E equals mc squared," they usually mean rest energy — the energy locked inside mass even when it's stationary.

Why we don't notice in daily life

Chemical reactions convert a tiny fraction of mass to energy. Burning 1 kg of petrol releases $\sim$47 MJ but only converts $5 \times 10^{-10}$ kg of mass — far too little to measure on any scale. Nuclear reactions convert ~0.7% of mass (fission) or ~0.7% (fusion) — millions of times more than chemical.

The Sun converts 620 million tonnes of hydrogen into 616 million tonnes of helium every second. The missing 4 million tonnes become 3.8 × 10²⁶ watts of light and heat. It has been doing this for 4.6 billion years — and has enough fuel for 5 billion more.

Mass Defect & Binding Energy

The mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of its individual protons and neutrons. The "missing" mass is called the mass defect (Δm).

$$\Delta m = Zm_p + Nm_n - m_{\text{nucleus}}$$

Z proton masses + N neutron masses − actual nuclear mass

$$E_b = \Delta m \cdot c^2$$

Binding energy = mass defect × c²

Where did the mass go? When nucleons bind together, they release energy (as photons, kinetic energy of products). That released energy had mass ($\Delta m = E/c^2$). The bound nucleus has less mass because energy was radiated away during formation.
To break the nucleus apart you must supply exactly this binding energy — putting the mass back. Higher binding energy per nucleon = more stable nucleus. Iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon — the most stable nucleus.

Nuclear Fission: Splitting Heavy Nuclei

A heavy nucleus (e.g. U-235) splits into lighter fragments. The products have more binding energy per nucleon, so the total mass decreases and energy is released.

Calculation: Energy from U-235 fission

$\Delta m = 0.215$ u $= 0.215 \times 1.661 \times 10^{-27} = 3.57 \times 10^{-28}$ kg

$E = \Delta mc^2 = 3.57 \times 10^{-28} \times 9 \times 10^{16} = 3.21 \times 10^{-11}$ J $= \mathbf{200 \text{ MeV}}$

Chain reaction: Each fission releases 2-3 neutrons, which can trigger more fissions. Controlled = nuclear power plant. Uncontrolled = nuclear weapon.

Nuclear Fusion: Joining Light Nuclei

Light nuclei fuse into heavier ones, moving up the binding energy curve. The products are more tightly bound — mass decreases, energy is released.

Why fusion is better (in principle)

  • Fuel (hydrogen) is virtually unlimited — from seawater
  • No radioactive waste (helium is inert)
  • Higher energy per kg than fission
  • No chain reaction risk — fusion stops if conditions aren't maintained
Challenge: Nuclei are positive, so they repel. You need temperatures of ~100 million K to force them close enough. Achieving and containing this is why fusion power is still under development (ITER, NIF).

Annihilation & Pair Production

The most dramatic demonstrations of mass-energy equivalence: mass converting entirely to energy, and energy creating mass from nothing.

Annihilation (mass → energy)

A particle meets its antiparticle. Both masses are completely destroyed. Two photons are produced.

$$e^- + e^+ \to 2\gamma$$

Each photon carries $E = m_e c^2 = 0.511$ MeV.

Two photons needed to conserve momentum.

Pair Production (energy → mass)

A high-energy photon near a nucleus creates a particle-antiparticle pair from pure energy.

$$\gamma \to e^- + e^+$$

Minimum photon energy: $2m_e c^2 = 1.022$ MeV.

Nucleus needed to conserve momentum.

In annihilation, 100% of mass becomes energy. Compare with fission (~0.09%) and fusion (~0.38%). Antimatter is the most energy-dense "fuel" conceivable — but we can't store it.
Conservation laws still hold: charge ($-1 + 1 = 0$), lepton number, momentum (that's why 2 photons in opposite directions), and total energy. $E = mc^2$ doesn't override conservation — it's consistent with it.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Energy from 1 u of mass

Calculate the energy equivalent of 1 unified atomic mass unit.

$1$ u $= 1.661 \times 10^{-27}$ kg

$E = mc^2 = 1.661 \times 10^{-27} \times (3 \times 10^8)^2 = 1.494 \times 10^{-10}$ J

In MeV: $\frac{1.494 \times 10^{-10}}{1.602 \times 10^{-13}} = \mathbf{931.5 \text{ MeV}}$

Key conversion: 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c². This is in the IB data booklet and is used constantly in nuclear physics problems.

Example 2: Helium-4 binding energy

He-4 has 2 protons + 2 neutrons. $m_p = 1.00728$ u, $m_n = 1.00867$ u, $m_{\text{He}} = 4.00260$ u.

$\Delta m = 2(1.00728) + 2(1.00867) - 4.00260 = 4.03190 - 4.00260 = 0.02930$ u

$E_b = 0.02930 \times 931.5 = \mathbf{27.3 \text{ MeV}}$

Per nucleon: $\frac{27.3}{4} = \mathbf{6.82 \text{ MeV/nucleon}}$

(a) Calculate the rest energy of an electron in joules and in MeV. ($m_e = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg)

(b) In a nuclear reaction, the total mass of the products is 0.0035 u less than the total mass of the reactants. Calculate the energy released in MeV.

$\Delta m = 0.0035$ u [1]
$E = \Delta m \times 931.5 = 0.0035 \times 931.5$ [1]
$E = \mathbf{3.26 \text{ MeV}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) A photon produces an electron-positron pair. The electron has kinetic energy 0.30 MeV and the positron has kinetic energy 0.20 MeV. Calculate the minimum energy of the photon.

Minimum to create both rest masses: $2 \times 0.511 = 1.022$ MeV [1]
Plus kinetic energies: $0.30 + 0.20 = 0.50$ MeV [1]
Total photon energy: $1.022 + 0.50 = \mathbf{1.52 \text{ MeV}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why nuclear fusion of light elements releases energy, while nuclear fission of heavy elements also releases energy.

Both processes move nuclei toward iron-56, which has the highest binding energy per nucleon [1]. Fusion of light nuclei produces more tightly bound products (moving up the BE curve). Fission of heavy nuclei also produces more tightly bound products (moving down the BE curve from the heavy side). In both cases, mass defect increases and energy is released via $E = \Delta mc^2$ [1].

Calculate [3]

The Sun radiates energy at a rate of $3.85 \times 10^{26}$ W. Calculate the mass the Sun loses per second.

$P = 3.85 \times 10^{26}$ W = 3.85 × 10²⁶ J/s [1]
$\Delta m = \frac{E}{c^2} = \frac{3.85 \times 10^{26}}{(3 \times 10^8)^2} = \frac{3.85 \times 10^{26}}{9 \times 10^{16}}$ [1]
$\Delta m = \mathbf{4.28 \times 10^{9} \text{ kg/s}} \approx 4.3$ million tonnes per second [1]
Calculate [3]

A proton-antiproton pair annihilates at rest. Calculate the total energy released and the wavelength of each photon produced. ($m_p = 938.3$ MeV/c²)

Total energy: $2 \times 938.3 = 1876.6$ MeV [1]
Each photon: $E = 938.3$ MeV $= 938.3 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-13} = 1.503 \times 10^{-10}$ J
$\lambda = \frac{hc}{E} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{1.503 \times 10^{-10}}$ [1]
$\lambda = \mathbf{1.32 \times 10^{-15} \text{ m}}$ (gamma ray — nuclear scale) [1]
Explain [2]

A spring is compressed and locked in place. According to special relativity, does the compressed spring have more, less, or equal mass compared to the relaxed spring? Explain.

The compressed spring has more mass [1]. The elastic potential energy stored in the spring has mass equivalent $\Delta m = E_{\text{PE}}/c^2$. Since energy was added to compress the spring, the system's total mass increased — though by an immeasurably tiny amount (e.g. ~1 J of PE adds ~$10^{-17}$ kg) [1].
PET scans (Positron Emission Tomography) use annihilation. A radioactive tracer emits positrons inside the body. Each positron annihilates with a nearby electron, producing two 0.511 MeV gamma rays in opposite directions. Detectors locate the annihilation point — mapping metabolic activity in real time.

Key Takeaways

  • $E_0 = mc^2$ — mass and energy are equivalent and interconvertible.
  • $c^2 = 9 \times 10^{16}$ — tiny mass = enormous energy. 1 kg = 9 × 10¹⁶ J.
  • 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c² — the essential conversion for nuclear calculations.
  • Mass defect: $\Delta m = \sum m_{\text{parts}} - m_{\text{whole}}$. Released as binding energy $E_b = \Delta mc^2$.
  • Fission: heavy nucleus splits → products closer to Fe-56 → energy released (~200 MeV).
  • Fusion: light nuclei join → product closer to Fe-56 → energy released (~17.6 MeV per D-T).
  • Annihilation: particle + antiparticle → photons. 100% mass-to-energy conversion.
  • Pair production: photon → particle + antiparticle. Minimum photon energy = $2mc^2$.
