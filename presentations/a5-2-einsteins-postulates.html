A.5.2

Einstein's Two Postulates

of Special Relativity

In 1905, a 26-year-old patent clerk published a paper that rewrote the rules of the universe. Two simple assumptions — and everything we thought we knew about space and time had to change.

A.5 Galilean & Special Relativity  |  A.5.2 Einstein's Two PostulatesThe Postulates

The Two Postulates (1905)

POSTULATE 1 — THE PRINCIPLE OF RELATIVITY

"The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames."

This extends Galileo's principle from mechanics to all of physics — including electromagnetism and optics. No experiment of any kind can detect absolute motion.

POSTULATE 2 — THE INVARIANCE OF THE SPEED OF LIGHT

"The speed of light in a vacuum is the same for all inertial observers, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer."

$c = 3.00 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹ — always, for everyone, no exceptions.

Learn these word-for-word. IB exams regularly ask "State the two postulates of special relativity" for 2 marks. The exact phrasing matters.
A.5 Galilean & Special Relativity  |  A.5.2 Einstein's Two PostulatesPostulate 1 Deep Dive

Postulate 1: No Preferred Frame

There is no experiment — mechanical, electromagnetic, optical, or any other — that can tell you whether you are "truly" moving or "truly" at rest.

What Galileo said (1632)

The laws of mechanics are the same in all inertial frames.

Drop a ball, throw a ball, swing a pendulum — same result on a smooth ship as on land.

What Einstein added (1905)

All laws of physics — including Maxwell's equations, light, electricity, magnetism.

Shine a light, measure magnetic fields, do any experiment — same result in any inertial frame.

The upgrade seems small but is revolutionary. Galileo left room for light to behave differently in different frames. Einstein closed that door. The speed of light must be the same everywhere — which demands a complete rethink of space and time.

The death of the aether

Before Einstein, physicists believed light needed a medium — the "luminiferous aether." Postulate 1 kills it. If no experiment can detect motion through the aether, the aether is physically meaningless. Einstein discarded it entirely.

Postulate 2: Light is Universal

No matter how fast you move, you always measure light travelling at $c$. This defies all intuition.

This contradicts Galilean velocity addition. If you run toward a light beam at 0.9c, Galileo says you'd measure 1.9c. Einstein says you measure exactly c. Something about space and time must bend to make this work.
What bends? Time and length. Moving clocks run slow (time dilation). Moving objects shrink (length contraction). These are not illusions — they are real, measurable physical effects. That's Topics A.5.3 and A.5.4.

What the Postulates Demand

If the speed of light is the same for everyone, then space and time must be flexible. Here's what follows:

Time Dilation (A.5.3)

$\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0$

Moving clocks tick slower. A muon travelling at 0.995c lives 10× longer than one at rest.

Length Contraction (A.5.4)

$L = \frac{L_0}{\gamma}$

Moving objects are shorter in the direction of motion. At 0.9c, a 10 m rod measures 4.36 m.

Velocity Addition (A.5.5)

$u' = \frac{u - v}{1 - \frac{uv}{c^2}}$

Velocities don't simply add. Even $0.9c + 0.9c = 0.994c$, never exceeding $c$.

Mass-Energy (A.5.7)

$E = mc^2$

Mass and energy are interchangeable. A tiny mass contains enormous energy.

All of these follow logically from just the two postulates. Einstein didn't invent new physics — he followed the logical consequences of two simple assumptions to their radical conclusions.

The Relativity of Simultaneity

Two events that are simultaneous in one frame may NOT be simultaneous in another. This is the first and deepest consequence of the postulates.

M′ is moving toward the front strike and away from the rear. Since light from both travels at $c$ (postulate 2), M′ receives the front flash first. M′ concludes the front strike happened earlier. Neither observer is wrong — simultaneity depends on the frame.

This is not an illusion. It's not about light travel time. Even after correcting for signal delay, the two observers genuinely disagree about the order of events. Time itself is frame-dependent.

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

State [2]

(a) State the two postulates of special relativity.

1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames [1].
2. The speed of light in a vacuum is the same for all inertial observers, regardless of the motion of the source or observer [1].
Calculate [2]

(b) Calculate the Lorentz factor γ for a particle travelling at 0.80c.

$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.64}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.36}}$ [1]
$\gamma = \frac{1}{0.60} = \mathbf{1.667}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) Explain how the second postulate contradicts Galilean velocity addition.

Galilean velocity addition predicts that if a spaceship at speed $v$ emits light, a stationary observer would measure the light at $c + v$ [1]. The second postulate states that the observer always measures $c$, regardless of the source's motion — this directly contradicts the Galilean prediction [1].
Explain [3]

(d) Two events occur simultaneously in the reference frame of an observer A. Explain why a second observer B, moving relative to A, may not consider the events to be simultaneous.

The two postulates require that the speed of light is the same in both frames [1]. Observer B is moving toward one event and away from the other, so light signals from the two events reach B at different times [1]. After correcting for light travel time, B still concludes the events occurred at different times — simultaneity is relative, not absolute [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [2]

Calculate γ for a proton in the Large Hadron Collider travelling at 0.999999991c.

$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (0.999999991)^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.999999982}}$ [1]
$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1.8 \times 10^{-8}}} = \frac{1}{1.342 \times 10^{-4}} = \mathbf{7454}$ [1]
Time runs 7454× slower for this proton than for a lab observer.
Explain [2]

A spaceship shines a laser forward while travelling at 0.99c. Explain what speed the laser beam has according to (i) the pilot, (ii) a stationary observer.

(i) The pilot measures the laser beam at exactly $c = 3.0 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹ — by the second postulate, light travels at $c$ in all inertial frames [1].
(ii) The stationary observer also measures exactly $c$ — not $1.99c$. The second postulate guarantees the speed of light is invariant regardless of the source's motion [1].
Discuss [3]

Discuss why the Michelson-Morley experiment supports the postulates of special relativity.

The experiment attempted to measure the speed of light in two perpendicular directions — parallel and transverse to Earth's orbital motion [1]. If an aether existed, the speed of light would differ in these directions. The null result showed no difference — light speed is the same in all directions [1]. This supports the second postulate (c is invariant) and the first postulate (no experiment can detect absolute motion) [1].
GPS satellites carry atomic clocks that run faster than ground clocks (less gravity) but slower due to their orbital speed. Both effects must be corrected using relativity, or GPS would drift by ~10 km per day.

Key Takeaways

  • Postulate 1: The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames (extends Galileo to ALL physics).
  • Postulate 2: The speed of light is $c$ for all inertial observers, regardless of source/observer motion.
  • Lorentz factor: $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$. Always $\geq 1$. Approaches $\infty$ as $v \to c$.
  • Consequences: time dilation, length contraction, relativistic velocity addition, $E = mc^2$.
  • Simultaneity is relative — two events simultaneous in one frame may not be in another.
  • The aether doesn't exist. Michelson-Morley confirmed it; Einstein made it unnecessary.
  • At everyday speeds ($v \ll c$), $\gamma \approx 1$ and relativity reduces to Newtonian physics.
