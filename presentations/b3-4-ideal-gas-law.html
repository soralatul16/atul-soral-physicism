B.3.4

The Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

One equation unites Boyle, Charles, and Gay-Lussac into a single law linking all four state variables — pressure, volume, amount, and temperature. It defines the "ideal gas" model, tells us exactly when real gases obey it, and unlocks quantitative gas problems across chemistry and physics.

Combining the Three Laws into One

Each empirical law fixes two variables and relates the third. Merge them and every relationship survives in a single expression.

LawHeld constantRelation
BoyleT, n$P \propto 1/V$
CharlesP, n$V \propto T$
Gay-LussacV, n$P \propto T$
AvogadroP, T$V \propto n$

Combining all four proportionalities: $V \propto \dfrac{nT}{P}$. Introduce the proportionality constant $R$ (the same for all gases) and rearrange:

$$PV = nRT$$
Why one constant works for every gas: because "ideal" gases are defined by molecules that have negligible volume and no intermolecular forces, their identity drops out — only the number of molecules matters, not what they are. That is why $R$ is universal.

Every Term, Every Unit

$$PV = nRT$$
  • $P$ — pressure, in pascals (Pa). 1 kPa = 10³ Pa; 1 atm = 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa. Always convert to Pa before substituting.
  • $V$ — volume, in cubic metres (m³). 1 L = 10⁻³ m³; 1 cm³ = 10⁻⁶ m³. This is the single most common source of error.
  • $n$ — amount of substance, in moles (mol). Recall $n = N/N_A = m/M$, linking back to B.3.1.
  • $T$ — absolute temperature, in kelvin (K). $T(\text{K}) = \theta(°\text{C}) + 273$. Never use Celsius here.
  • $R$ — universal (molar) gas constant $= 8.31\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$. Given in the IB data booklet.
Unit discipline wins marks. $R = 8.31$ only works in strict SI: Pa, m³, mol, K. If any input is in kPa, litres, or °C and you forget to convert, your answer is wrong by orders of magnitude.
Where does $R$ come from? $R = N_A k_B$ — the gas constant is Avogadro's number times the Boltzmann constant. It is the "per-mole" version of the molecular constant $k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{J K}^{-1}$.

Two Equivalent Forms: Moles vs Molecules

Molar form

$$PV = nRT$$

$n$ = moles, $R = 8.31\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$. Use when the amount is given in moles or mass.

Molecular form

$$PV = Nk_BT$$

$N$ = number of molecules, $k_B = 1.38\times10^{-23}\ \text{J K}^{-1}$. Use when given a count of molecules.

They are the same equation

Since $n = N/N_A$ and $R = N_A k_B$: $nRT = \dfrac{N}{N_A}(N_A k_B)T = N k_B T$. Choose the form that matches the data you're given.

IB tip: if a question hands you a raw number of molecules (e.g. $3.0\times10^{24}$), reach for $PV = Nk_BT$ — no need to convert to moles first.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — find the moles

A container of volume 0.020 m³ holds gas at 150 kPa and 27 °C. How many moles are present?

Convert: $P = 1.50\times10^{5}$ Pa, $V = 0.020$ m³, $T = 300$ K.

$n = \dfrac{PV}{RT} = \dfrac{(1.50\times10^{5})(0.020)}{(8.31)(300)} = \mathbf{1.20\ \text{mol}}$

Example 2 — find the volume

Find the volume occupied by 2.0 mol of an ideal gas at 100 kPa and 350 K.

$V = \dfrac{nRT}{P} = \dfrac{(2.0)(8.31)(350)}{1.00\times10^{5}} = \mathbf{0.058\ \text{m}^3} = 58\ \text{L}$

Example 3 — using molecules

$5.0\times10^{23}$ molecules of gas occupy 2.0 L at 300 K. Find the pressure.

$V = 2.0\times10^{-3}$ m³.

$P = \dfrac{Nk_BT}{V} = \dfrac{(5.0\times10^{23})(1.38\times10^{-23})(300)}{2.0\times10^{-3}} = \mathbf{1.04\times10^{6}\ \text{Pa}}$

When Does a Real Gas Behave Ideally?

THE IDEAL GAS ASSUMPTIONS

  • Molecules are point particles — their own volume is negligible vs the container.
  • No intermolecular forces except during collisions.
  • Collisions are perfectly elastic; no kinetic energy lost.
  • Motion is random; average KE depends only on temperature.
Real gases deviate at high pressure and low temperature. At high P, molecules are squeezed close — their own volume and mutual attractions stop being negligible. At low T, slow molecules feel intermolecular forces strongly. Real gases behave most ideally at low pressure and high temperature.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Calculate [3]

(a) A gas cylinder of volume 0.050 m³ contains gas at a pressure of 2.0 MPa and temperature 290 K. Determine the number of moles of gas.

$P = 2.0\times10^{6}$ Pa, $V = 0.050$ m³, $T = 290$ K [1]
$n = \dfrac{PV}{RT} = \dfrac{(2.0\times10^{6})(0.050)}{(8.31)(290)}$ [1]
$= \dfrac{1.0\times10^{5}}{2410} = \mathbf{41.5\ \text{mol}}$ [1]
Calculate [2]

(b) The gas in (a) leaks until the pressure falls to 1.2 MPa at the same temperature. How many moles have escaped?

At same V and T, $n \propto P$: $n_2 = 41.5 \times \dfrac{1.2}{2.0} = 24.9$ mol [1]
Moles escaped $= 41.5 - 24.9 = \mathbf{16.6\ \text{mol}}$ [1]
Explain [3]

(c) Explain, in terms of molecular behaviour, two conditions under which a real gas approximates an ideal gas.

Low pressure: molecules are far apart, so their own volume is negligible compared with the container and intermolecular forces are weak [1]. High temperature: molecules move fast with high kinetic energy, so the brief attractive forces during near-passes have negligible effect on their motion [1]. Under both conditions the ideal-gas assumptions (point particles, no forces) hold well, so $PV = nRT$ is accurate [1].
Calculate [3]

(d) A fixed mass of ideal gas has volume 3.0 L at 300 K and 100 kPa. It is heated to 450 K and compressed to 1.5 L. Find the new pressure and confirm using PV = nRT that n is unchanged.

Combined law: $P_2 = P_1\dfrac{V_1}{V_2}\dfrac{T_2}{T_1} = 100 \times \dfrac{3.0}{1.5} \times \dfrac{450}{300} = \mathbf{300\ \text{kPa}}$ [1]
Check $n = PV/RT$ before: $\dfrac{(1.0\times10^{5})(3.0\times10^{-3})}{(8.31)(300)} = 0.120$ mol [1]
After: $\dfrac{(3.0\times10^{5})(1.5\times10^{-3})}{(8.31)(450)} = 0.120$ mol — unchanged ✓ [1]

Key Takeaways

  • Ideal gas law: $PV = nRT$ unites Boyle, Charles, Gay-Lussac and Avogadro in one equation.
  • Molecular form: $PV = Nk_BT$, with $R = N_A k_B$. Pick the form matching your data.
  • Strict SI units: P in Pa, V in m³, n in mol, T in K, $R = 8.31\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$.
  • Most common errors: litres not converted to m³, and °C not converted to K.
  • Ideal assumptions: point particles, no intermolecular forces, elastic collisions, random motion.
  • Real gases deviate at high P and low T; they behave most ideally at low P and high T.
