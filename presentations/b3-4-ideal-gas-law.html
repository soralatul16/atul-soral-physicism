PV = nRT

The Ideal Gas Law

Five letters, one equation — it unifies Boyle's, Charles's, and Gay-Lussac's laws into a single master equation. Know this, and you can solve any gas problem. It connects pressure, volume, temperature, and the amount of gas in one elegant relationship.

The Ideal Gas Law — Two Forms

MOLAR FORM

$$PV = nRT$$

n = number of moles
R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ (gas constant)

MOLECULAR FORM

$$PV = Nk_BT$$

N = number of molecules
$k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}$ J K⁻¹

Connection: $R = N_A k_B$. So $nR = \frac{N}{N_A} \times N_A k_B = Nk_B$. Both forms say the same thing — one counts in moles, the other in individual molecules.

Unit trap — the #1 source of errors: P in Pa (not kPa!), V in m³ (not litres!), T in K (not °C!). Convert: 1 kPa = 1000 Pa. 1 L = $10^{-3}$ m³. °C + 273 = K. If you use consistent SI units, R = 8.314 works perfectly.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Find the volume

2.0 mol of gas at 300 K and 200 kPa. Find V.

$V = \frac{nRT}{P} = \frac{2.0 \times 8.314 \times 300}{200 \times 10^3} = \frac{4988.4}{200000} = \mathbf{0.0249 \text{ m}^3 = 24.9 \text{ L}}$

Example 2: Find the number of molecules

A 500 mL flask at 27°C and 101.3 kPa. How many molecules?

$V = 500 \times 10^{-6}$ m³, $T = 300$ K, $P = 101300$ Pa

$N = \frac{PV}{k_BT} = \frac{101300 \times 500 \times 10^{-6}}{1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 300} = \frac{50.65}{4.143 \times 10^{-21}} = \mathbf{1.22 \times 10^{22}}$

Example 3: Find the temperature

0.50 mol of gas in a 10 L container at 400 kPa. Find T.

$T = \frac{PV}{nR} = \frac{400 \times 10^3 \times 10 \times 10^{-3}}{0.50 \times 8.314} = \frac{4000}{4.157} = \mathbf{962 \text{ K} = 689°C}$

Molar volume at STP: At 0°C (273 K) and 1 atm (101325 Pa): $V = \frac{1 \times 8.314 \times 273}{101325} = 0.02241$ m³ = 22.4 L per mole. This is a key number — 1 mol of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L at STP.

Finding Density and Molar Mass

$PV = nRT$ can be rearranged with $n = m/M$ to find gas density or identify unknown gases:

Gas density from the ideal gas law

$PV = \frac{m}{M}RT$ → $\frac{m}{V} = \frac{PM}{RT}$ → $\rho = \frac{PM}{RT}$

Density depends on P, M, and T. Higher pressure or molar mass → denser. Higher temperature → less dense.

Example: Density of air at sea level

$P = 101325$ Pa, $M_{\text{air}} = 0.029$ kg/mol, $T = 293$ K

$\rho = \frac{101325 \times 0.029}{8.314 \times 293} = \frac{2938}{2436} = \mathbf{1.21 \text{ kg m}^{-3}}$ ✓

Finding molar mass of an unknown gas

$M = \frac{mRT}{PV} = \frac{\rho RT}{P}$

Measure P, V, T, and mass of the gas → calculate M → identify the gas from the periodic table.

Calculate [3]

A gas of mass 1.60 g occupies 1.00 L at 27°C and 101.3 kPa. Find its molar mass and identify it.

$M = \frac{mRT}{PV} = \frac{1.60 \times 10^{-3} \times 8.314 \times 300}{101300 \times 1.00 \times 10^{-3}}$ [1]
$= \frac{3.991}{101.3} = 0.0394$ kg/mol $= 39.4$ g/mol [1]
$M \approx 40$ g/mol → Argon (Ar) [1]

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Calculate [3]

(a) A cylinder contains 0.20 mol of oxygen at 500 kPa and 27°C. Calculate the volume of the gas.

$V = \frac{nRT}{P} = \frac{0.20 \times 8.314 \times 300}{500 \times 10^3}$ [1]
$= \frac{498.8}{500000} = 9.98 \times 10^{-4}$ m³ [1]
$V = \mathbf{0.998 \text{ L} \approx 1.0 \text{ L}}$ [1]
Calculate [3]

(b) A weather balloon is filled with 5.0 mol of helium at ground level (101 kPa, 20°C). At altitude, P = 30 kPa and T = −40°C. Find the new volume.

Using $PV = nRT$ at each state (same n):
$V_2 = \frac{nRT_2}{P_2} = \frac{5.0 \times 8.314 \times 233}{30000}$ [1]
$= \frac{9686}{30000} = 0.323$ m³ = 323 L [1]
Or using combined law: $V_2 = V_1 \times \frac{P_1}{P_2} \times \frac{T_2}{T_1}$ where $V_1 = \frac{5.0 \times 8.314 \times 293}{101000} = 0.120$ m³.
$V_2 = 0.120 \times \frac{101}{30} \times \frac{233}{293} = \mathbf{0.323 \text{ m}^3}$ ✓ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) State the two forms of the ideal gas equation and explain when each is more useful.

$PV = nRT$ uses moles (n) and the gas constant R — useful when you know the mass and molar mass of the gas, or when the problem specifies moles [1].
$PV = Nk_BT$ uses the number of molecules (N) and Boltzmann constant $k_B$ — useful when dealing with individual molecules or connecting to kinetic theory ($\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$) [1].
Calculate [3]

(d) The density of a gas at STP (0°C, 101.3 kPa) is 1.43 kg m⁻³. Calculate its molar mass and identify the gas.

$M = \frac{\rho RT}{P} = \frac{1.43 \times 8.314 \times 273}{101325}$ [1]
$= \frac{3245}{101325} = 0.0320$ kg/mol [1]
$M = 32.0$ g/mol → O₂ (oxygen gas) [1]
B.3 Gas Laws  |  B.3.4 PV = nRTSummary

Key Takeaways

  • $PV = nRT$ (molar) and $PV = Nk_BT$ (molecular) — same equation, different counting units.
  • Units: P in Pa, V in m³, T in K, n in mol. R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹. $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}$ J K⁻¹.
  • $R = N_Ak_B$ — connects molar and molecular forms.
  • Molar volume at STP: 22.4 L per mole (0°C, 1 atm).
  • Gas density: $\rho = PM/(RT)$. Depends on P, M, T.
  • Finding M: $M = mRT/(PV)$ — identify unknown gases from measured P, V, T, m.
  • All gas laws are special cases: hold n and one of P/V/T constant → Boyle, Charles, or Gay-Lussac.
