C.2.4

Intensity & the Inverse Square Law

How wave power spreads out

Move twice as far from a lamp and it looks four times dimmer — not twice. This is the inverse square law, a direct consequence of energy spreading over the surface of an expanding sphere. Here we define intensity, derive the law, and link it to wave amplitude.

Defining Intensity

$$I = \frac{P}{A}$$

Intensity is the power arriving per unit area at right angles to the wave's direction of travel.

  • I — intensity, unit W m⁻² (watts per square metre).
  • P — the power carried by the wave (energy per second), in watts.
  • A — the area the power is spread over, in m².
Intuition: the same power concentrated on a small area is intense; spread over a large area it is weak. A magnifying glass focusing sunlight to a point raises the intensity enough to burn paper — same power, far smaller area.
Perception: for light, intensity relates to brightness; for sound, to loudness. Both fall off with distance in exactly the way the next slide describes.

The Inverse Square Law

A point source radiates power equally in all directions. At distance r, that power P is spread over the surface of a sphere, area $4\pi r^2$. So:

$$I = \frac{P}{4\pi r^2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad I \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$$

Double the distance → 4× the area → ¼ the intensity.

  • Double r → intensity ÷ 4 (not ÷2).
  • Triple r → intensity ÷ 9.
  • Comparing two distances: $\dfrac{I_1}{I_2} = \dfrac{r_2^2}{r_1^2}$ (constant power).

Intensity & Amplitude

A wave's energy depends on the square of its amplitude (recall SHM: $E_T = \tfrac12 m\omega^2 x_0^2$). Since intensity is power per area — energy per time per area — it too scales with the square of the amplitude:

$$I \propto A^2$$
  • Double the amplitude → 4× the intensity.
  • Triple the amplitude → 9× the intensity.
  • Combining with the inverse square law for a point source: since $I \propto 1/r^2$ and $I \propto A^2$, then $A \propto 1/r$ — amplitude falls off as $1/r$, while intensity falls off as $1/r^2$.

Two ways intensity can change

(1) Spreading with distance — $I \propto 1/r^2$. (2) Changing the source amplitude — $I \propto A^2$. Exam questions often combine both, so keep them separate in your working.

Applications & Assumptions

Where it applies

Starlight/sunlight reaching Earth, a bare bulb, a small loudspeaker in the open, radio transmission, radiation from a point source.

When it breaks down

Lasers (a narrow beam, not spreading), sources near reflecting walls, absorbing media, or when you are close to a large (non-point) source.

The law assumes an ideal point source, radiating uniformly in all directions, with no absorption by the medium and no reflection.

Solar constant example

The Sun's power spreads over a huge sphere by the time it reaches Earth's orbit, giving an intensity (the "solar constant") of about 1360 W m⁻² above the atmosphere — a direct application of $I = P/4\pi r^2$ with r = 1 AU.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — intensity from a point source

A 60 W lamp radiates uniformly. Find the intensity 2.0 m away.

$I = P/(4\pi r^2) = 60/(4\pi\times2.0^2) = 60/(50.3)$

$= \mathbf{1.2\ \text{W m}^{-2}}$

Example 2 — comparing two distances

The intensity 3.0 m from a source is 8.0 W m⁻². Find the intensity 6.0 m away.

Distance doubles (3→6), so intensity ÷ 4: $I_2 = I_1(r_1/r_2)^2 = 8.0\times(3/6)^2$

$= 8.0\times0.25 = \mathbf{2.0\ \text{W m}^{-2}}$

Example 3 — amplitude change

The amplitude of a wave is tripled. By what factor does its intensity change?

$I \propto A^2$, so intensity increases by $3^2 = \mathbf{9}$ times.

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

Define [2]

(a) Define intensity and state its SI unit.

Intensity is the power received per unit area (at right angles to the direction of wave travel), $I = P/A$ [1]. Its SI unit is the watt per square metre, W m⁻² [1].
Explain [2]

(b) Explain why the intensity from a point source obeys an inverse square law with distance.

The source radiates power uniformly in all directions, so at distance r that power is spread over the surface of a sphere of area $4\pi r^2$ [1]. Since $I = P/(4\pi r^2)$ and P is constant, intensity is inversely proportional to $r^2$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A point source has an intensity of 12 W m⁻² at 4.0 m. Calculate its power output and the intensity at 8.0 m.

$P = I\times4\pi r^2 = 12\times4\pi\times4.0^2 = 12\times201 = 2.4\times10^3$ W [1]
At 8.0 m the distance doubles, so $I \div 4$: $I = 12/4$ [1] $= \mathbf{3.0\ \text{W m}^{-2}}$ [1]
Apply [2]

(d) The amplitude of a sound wave at a point is halved. State and justify the effect on the intensity there.

Intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude, $I \propto A^2$ [1]. Halving the amplitude gives $(\tfrac12)^2 = \tfrac14$, so the intensity falls to one quarter of its original value [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Intensity: $I = P/A$, power per unit area, unit W m⁻².
  • Point source: $I = P/(4\pi r^2)$, so $I \propto 1/r^2$ (inverse square law).
  • Double r → ¼ I; triple r → ⅑ I; compare with $I_1/I_2 = r_2^2/r_1^2$.
  • Amplitude link: $I \propto A^2$; combined with $I\propto1/r^2$ gives $A \propto 1/r$.
  • Assumes an ideal point source, uniform radiation, no absorption/reflection.
  • Example: the solar constant (~1360 W m⁻²) is $I = P/4\pi r^2$ at Earth's orbit.
