A.1.1

Position, Displacement & Distance

Three quantities that sound similar but mean very different things in physics. Confusing them breaks every calculation that follows.

Atul Soral Physicism

A.1.1 – Position, Displacement & DistanceDefinition

What is Position?

Position tells you where an object is. But "where" only makes sense relative to something else.

To describe position, you need three things:

  • A reference point (origin) — the "zero" mark
  • A direction convention — which way is positive?
  • A unit — metres (SI)

Example: "The car is at position $x = +12$ m" means it's 12 metres to the right of the origin.

Position is a vector. It has both magnitude (how far from origin) and direction (which side of origin).
FYI: "Position = 0" does NOT mean the object doesn't exist — it means it's exactly at the reference point. The choice of origin is arbitrary.
A.1.1 – Position, Displacement & DistanceDefinition

What is Displacement?

Displacement is the change in position. It answers: "How far are you from where you started, and in which direction?"

$$\Delta \vec{x} = x_{final} - x_{initial}$$
  • Vector quantity — has magnitude AND direction
  • SI unit: metre (m) + direction
  • Can be positive, negative, or zero
  • Depends ONLY on start and end positions — not the path taken
Key insight: If you walk 10 km to the shop and 10 km back home, your displacement is zero — you ended where you started.
A.1.1 – Position, Displacement & DistanceDefinition

What is Distance?

Distance is the total length of path travelled. It answers: "How much ground did you actually cover?"

$$d = \text{total path length}$$
  • Scalar quantity — magnitude only, no direction
  • SI unit: metre (m)
  • Always positive or zero — can never be negative
  • Can never decrease during a journey — only accumulates
  • Depends on the actual path taken — every twist and turn counts

In the same example (walk 10 km to shop, 10 km back):

  • Distance = 10 + 10 = 20 km (you walked 20 km total)
  • Displacement = 0 km (back where you started)
The golden rule: Distance $\geq$ |Displacement| — ALWAYS. They are equal only when motion is in a straight line without reversing.
A.1.1 – Position, Displacement & DistanceComparison

Distance vs Displacement — Side by Side

Distance (d)

  • Scalar (magnitude only)
  • Total path length
  • Always $\geq 0$
  • Can never decrease
  • Path-dependent
  • "How much ground covered?"

Displacement ($\Delta x$)

  • Vector (magnitude + direction)
  • Straight line: start → end
  • Can be +, −, or 0
  • Can decrease (if you reverse)
  • Path-independent
  • "How far from start?"
$$|\text{Distance}| \geq |\text{Displacement}| \quad \textbf{always}$$
IB exam tip: When a question says "how far," check if they mean distance or displacement. Context matters. "How far has the ball travelled" = distance. "How far is the ball from its starting point" = displacement.

A.1.1 – Position, Displacement & DistanceSpecial Case

Special Case: Closed Path

When an object returns to its starting point, displacement = 0 regardless of how far it travelled.

1 complete lap:

  • Distance = 400 m
  • Displacement = 0 m

2 complete laps:

  • Distance = 800 m
  • Displacement = still 0 m
This applies to: runners on a track, planets in orbit, pendulums completing full swings, cars doing a round trip.
IB trap: "After 3 complete revolutions, what is the displacement?" Answer: 0. Always.
A.1.1 – Position, Displacement & DistanceWorked Example

Worked Example

Problem: A student walks 3 km East, then 4 km North. Calculate (a) the total distance (b) the magnitude of displacement (c) the direction of displacement.
aDistance = 3 + 4 = 7 km (scalar — just add path lengths)
bDisplacement: use Pythagoras since the path forms a right angle
$$s = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = \mathbf{5 \text{ km}}$$
cDirection: use trigonometry
$$\theta = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) = \mathbf{53.1\degree \text{ North of East}}$$

Check: Distance (7 km) > Displacement (5 km) ✓

Final answer: Distance = 7 km. Displacement = 5 km at 53.1° North of East.

Practice Questions

Calculate

A runner completes exactly 1.5 laps of a 400 m circular track. What is (a) the distance and (b) the displacement?

(a) Distance = 1.5 × 400 = 600 m
(b) Displacement = diameter of track = 2r = 2 × 63.7 = 127.3 m (diametrically opposite start)
Determine

An ant walks 4 m East, 3 m North, then 4 m West. Find the distance and displacement.

Distance = 4+3+4 = 11 m
Displacement: end position is 3 m North of start = 3 m North
Explain

A car drives from A to B along a winding mountain road. Is the distance greater than, equal to, or less than the displacement? Explain.

Distance is greater than displacement. Distance measures the actual winding path (every curve adds length). Displacement is the straight-line A→B. They would only be equal if the road were perfectly straight.
State

Under what condition is distance exactly equal to displacement?

When motion is in a straight line without changing direction. Any curve, detour, or reversal makes distance > displacement.
A.1.1 – Position, Displacement & DistanceSummary

Key Takeaways

  • Position = where you are (relative to origin). Vector.
  • Displacement = change in position = final − initial. Vector. Can be zero.
  • Distance = total path length. Scalar. Always positive. Never decreases.
  • Distance ≥ |Displacement| — always. Equal only for straight-line, one-way motion.
  • Closed path → displacement = 0, distance > 0.
