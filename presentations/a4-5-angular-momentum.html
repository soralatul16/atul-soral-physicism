Angular Momentum

& Its Conservation

A figure skater pulls in her arms and spins faster. A collapsing star shrinks and spins thousands of times per second. No external torque was applied — angular momentum was simply conserved. One of the most powerful principles in physics.

A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.5 Angular MomentumDefinition

Angular Momentum (L)

Angular momentum is the rotational equivalent of linear momentum. It measures how much "rotational motion" an object has.

$$L = I\omega$$
L
Angular momentum
(kg·m² s⁻¹ or N·m·s)
I
Moment of inertia
(kg·m²)
ω
Angular velocity
(rad s⁻¹)

Linear

$p = mv$

momentum = mass × velocity

Rotational

$L = I\omega$

angular momentum = moment of inertia × angular velocity

For a point mass moving in a circle: $L = I\omega = (mr^2)\omega = mr(r\omega) = mvr$. So for a particle, $L = mvr$ where $r$ is the perpendicular distance from the axis.
Direction: L is a vector along the axis of rotation. Direction is given by the right-hand rule: curl fingers in the direction of rotation, thumb points along L.

Angular Impulse — Newton's 2nd Law Revisited

Just as $F = \frac{dp}{dt}$, Newton's rotational law can be written as:

$$\tau = \frac{dL}{dt}$$

Net torque = rate of change of angular momentum

Angular Impulse

$$\tau \cdot \Delta t = \Delta L = I\omega_f - I\omega_i$$

Torque × time = change in angular momentum

Linear Impulse

$F\Delta t = \Delta p$

Angular Impulse

$\tau\Delta t = \Delta L$

Key consequence: If $\sum\tau = 0$ (no external torque), then $\frac{dL}{dt} = 0$, meaning $L$ is constant. This is the conservation of angular momentum.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.5 Angular MomentumConservation

Conservation of Angular Momentum

THE LAW

$$L_{\text{before}} = L_{\text{after}}$$
$$I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$$

when no external torque acts on the system

$I_{\text{large}} \times \omega_{\text{slow}} = I_{\text{small}} \times \omega_{\text{fast}}$  →  Same $L$ throughout
I decreases → ω increases. No torque was applied. The skater does internal work (using muscles) to change shape, but angular momentum is unchanged. KE increases — the energy comes from the skater's muscles.

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Example 1: Figure skater

A skater spins at 2.0 rev s⁻¹ with $I = 4.0$ kg·m². She pulls in her arms, reducing $I$ to 1.6 kg·m². Find her new spin rate.

Convert: $\omega_1 = 2.0 \times 2\pi = 4\pi$ rad s⁻¹

Conserve L: $I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$ → $4.0 \times 4\pi = 1.6 \times \omega_2$

$\omega_2 = \frac{16\pi}{1.6} = 10\pi$ rad s⁻¹ $= \mathbf{5.0 \text{ rev s}^{-1}}$

Example 2: Disc catches mass

A solid disc ($M = 5.0$ kg, $R = 0.30$ m) spins at 10 rad s⁻¹. A 2.0 kg lump of clay is dropped onto the rim. Find the new angular velocity.

$I_{\text{disc}} = \frac{1}{2}MR^2 = \frac{1}{2}(5.0)(0.09) = 0.225$ kg·m²

$I_{\text{after}} = 0.225 + mR^2 = 0.225 + 2.0(0.09) = 0.225 + 0.18 = 0.405$ kg·m²

$L$ conserved: $0.225 \times 10 = 0.405 \times \omega_2$ → $\omega_2 = \frac{2.25}{0.405} = \mathbf{5.56 \text{ rad s}^{-1}}$

KE is NOT conserved in this collision. Some KE is lost to the inelastic collision (heat, deformation). But angular momentum IS conserved because no external torque acts.
A.4 Rigid Body Mechanics  |  A.4.5 Angular MomentumReal-World

Conservation in Nature & Technology

Neutron Stars (Pulsars)

A massive star collapses from ~10⁶ km radius to ~10 km. Radius drops by factor ~10⁵, so $I$ drops by ~10¹⁰. Angular velocity must increase by 10¹⁰. Result: a star spinning hundreds of times per second.

Helicopter Tail Rotor

The main rotor spins one way. By Newton's 3rd law, the body would spin the opposite way. The tail rotor provides an external torque to prevent this. Without it, the helicopter's body would spin uncontrollably.

Gyroscopes & Bicycles

A spinning wheel resists changes to its axis of rotation. This gyroscopic stability keeps bicycles upright, spacecraft oriented, and navigation systems working.

Divers & Gymnasts

A diver tucks to spin fast (small I, large ω), then opens up to slow rotation before entry (large I, small ω). Total L stays constant throughout the dive.

Planetary orbits: Kepler's second law (equal areas in equal times) is actually conservation of angular momentum. A planet moves faster when closer to the Sun (smaller r → smaller I → larger ω).

Angular Momentum in Collisions

When objects collide rotationally (one lands on another, discs couple together), angular momentum is conserved if no external torque acts.

Example: Two discs coupling

Disc A ($I_A = 0.40$ kg·m², $\omega_A = 12$ rad s⁻¹) is dropped onto stationary Disc B ($I_B = 0.60$ kg·m²). They lock together. Find the final angular velocity and the KE lost.

Conserve L: $I_A\omega_A + I_B\omega_B = (I_A + I_B)\omega_f$

$0.40 \times 12 + 0.60 \times 0 = (0.40 + 0.60)\omega_f$ → $4.8 = 1.0\,\omega_f$ → $\omega_f = \mathbf{4.8 \text{ rad s}^{-1}}$

KE before: $\frac{1}{2}(0.40)(12)^2 = 28.8$ J

KE after: $\frac{1}{2}(1.0)(4.8)^2 = 11.52$ J

KE lost: $28.8 - 11.52 = \mathbf{17.3 \text{ J}}$ (60% lost to friction/heat as surfaces slip)

Perfectly inelastic rotational collision: L is conserved, KE is not. Identical to linear inelastic collisions — momentum conserved, energy lost. Always check both in IB problems.

Define [1]

(a) Define angular momentum.

Angular momentum is the product of the moment of inertia and the angular velocity of a rotating body: $L = I\omega$ [1].
State [1]

(b) State the condition for conservation of angular momentum.

Angular momentum is conserved when the net external torque acting on the system is zero [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A turntable ($I = 0.15$ kg·m²) spins at 33 rpm. A 0.20 kg record ($I = \frac{1}{2}mr^2$, radius 0.15 m) is dropped onto it. Calculate the new angular velocity in rpm.

$I_{\text{record}} = \frac{1}{2}(0.20)(0.15)^2 = 0.00225$ kg·m² [1]
$\omega_1 = 33$ rpm. Conserve L: $0.15 \times 33 = (0.15 + 0.00225)\omega_2$ [1]
$\omega_2 = \frac{4.95}{0.15225} = \mathbf{32.5 \text{ rpm}}$ [1]
Calculate & Explain [4]

(d) A merry-go-round ($I = 200$ kg·m²) rotates at 0.50 rad s⁻¹. A 50 kg child walks from the rim ($r = 2.0$ m) to the centre. (i) Calculate the new angular velocity. (ii) Explain where the extra kinetic energy comes from.

(i) $I_{\text{initial}} = 200 + 50(2.0)^2 = 200 + 200 = 400$ kg·m²
$I_{\text{final}} = 200 + 50(0)^2 = 200$ kg·m² [1]
$400 \times 0.50 = 200 \times \omega_2$ → $\omega_2 = \mathbf{1.0 \text{ rad s}^{-1}}$ [1]
(ii) KE increases from $\frac{1}{2}(400)(0.25) = 50$ J to $\frac{1}{2}(200)(1.0) = 100$ J [1]. The extra 50 J comes from the work done by the child's muscles as they walk inward against the centripetal (pseudo) force [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

A solid sphere ($m = 3.0$ kg, $R = 0.10$ m) spins at 20 rad s⁻¹. A torque of 0.060 N·m acts for 4.0 s. Calculate the angular impulse and the new angular velocity.

Angular impulse: $\tau\Delta t = 0.060 \times 4.0 = 0.24$ N·m·s [1]
$I = \frac{2}{5}mR^2 = \frac{2}{5}(3.0)(0.01) = 0.012$ kg·m²
$\Delta L = I\Delta\omega$ → $0.24 = 0.012 \times \Delta\omega$ → $\Delta\omega = 20$ rad s⁻¹ [1]
$\omega_f = 20 + 20 = \mathbf{40 \text{ rad s}^{-1}}$ [1]
Calculate [4]

Two identical discs ($I = 0.30$ kg·m² each) rotate in opposite directions: Disc 1 at +8.0 rad s⁻¹ and Disc 2 at −6.0 rad s⁻¹. They are pressed together and couple. Find the final ω and the fraction of KE lost.

$L_i = 0.30(8.0) + 0.30(-6.0) = 2.4 - 1.8 = 0.6$ kg·m² s⁻¹ [1]
$\omega_f = \frac{0.6}{0.60} = \mathbf{1.0 \text{ rad s}^{-1}}$ [1]
$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}(0.30)(64) + \frac{1}{2}(0.30)(36) = 9.6 + 5.4 = 15.0$ J
$KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(0.60)(1.0) = 0.30$ J [1]
Fraction lost: $\frac{15.0 - 0.30}{15.0} = \mathbf{98\%}$ [1]
Explain [2]

A spinning ice skater has angular momentum L. She stops herself by pressing her skate blade against the ice. Explain how this is consistent with conservation of angular momentum.

The friction between the blade and the ice exerts an external torque on the skater [1]. This external torque changes her angular momentum to zero. Angular momentum is only conserved when there is no net external torque — here there is one, so L is not conserved for the skater alone. The angular momentum is transferred to the Earth [1].

Key Takeaways

  • $L = I\omega$ — angular momentum. For a point mass: $L = mvr$.
  • $\tau = \frac{dL}{dt}$ — torque equals rate of change of angular momentum.
  • Angular impulse: $\tau\Delta t = \Delta L$ — rotational equivalent of $F\Delta t = \Delta p$.
  • Conservation: If $\sum\tau_{\text{ext}} = 0$, then $I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$.
  • I decreases → ω increases (skater, neutron star, diver). I increases → ω decreases (mass added to spinning disc).
  • Rotational collisions: L is conserved, KE generally is not (unless elastic).
  • KE can increase when I decreases — energy comes from internal work (muscles, gravity).
