Heat Engines &
the Carnot Cycle

The Maximum Possible Efficiency

Every power station, car engine, and jet turbine is a heat engine — a device that converts heat into work. The Carnot cycle sets the absolute maximum efficiency any engine can achieve between two temperatures. No real engine can beat Carnot. The second law forbids it.

The Heat Engine — Energy Flow

Energy conservation

$$Q_H = W + Q_C$$

Heat in = work out + heat rejected

Efficiency

$$\eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H}$$

Always < 1 (second law)

$Q_C$ can never be zero. The second law requires some heat to be dumped to the cold reservoir. This is why no engine can be 100% efficient — waste heat is a thermodynamic necessity, not an engineering failure.

The Carnot Efficiency — The Absolute Maximum

$$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}$$

$T_C$ and $T_H$ in kelvin. This is the maximum efficiency for ANY engine operating between $T_H$ and $T_C$.

To increase Carnot efficiency: raise $T_H$ (hotter source) or lower $T_C$ (colder sink). Since $T_C$ is usually ambient (~300 K), the main strategy is raising $T_H$ — which is why gas turbines (1500 K) are more efficient than steam turbines (800 K).
$\eta = 100\%$ only if $T_C = 0$ K — absolute zero, which is unattainable (third law). So 100% efficiency is literally impossible.

The Carnot Cycle — Four Steps

The Carnot cycle consists of four reversible processes — two isothermal and two adiabatic:

1. Isothermal expansion at TH

Gas absorbs $Q_H$ from hot reservoir. Expands slowly. $\Delta U = 0$ (const T). $W = Q_H$.

2. Adiabatic expansion

Gas continues expanding with no heat exchange ($Q = 0$). Temperature drops from $T_H$ to $T_C$. Work comes from internal energy.

3. Isothermal compression at TC

Gas is compressed, rejecting $Q_C$ to cold reservoir. $\Delta U = 0$. Work done ON gas = $Q_C$.

4. Adiabatic compression

Gas compressed with no heat exchange. Temperature rises from $T_C$ back to $T_H$. Returns to initial state.

Why Carnot is the most efficient: All four steps are reversible ($\Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0$). No entropy is created. Any irreversibility (friction, turbulence, finite-rate heat transfer) creates entropy and reduces efficiency below Carnot. Real engines always have irreversibilities → always less efficient than Carnot.

Entropy proof of Carnot efficiency

For a reversible cycle: $\Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0$ → $-Q_H/T_H + Q_C/T_C = 0$ → $Q_C/Q_H = T_C/T_H$

$\eta = 1 - Q_C/Q_H = 1 - T_C/T_H$ ← Carnot efficiency falls out directly from entropy conservation.

Calculate [3]

(a) A heat engine operates between 600 K and 300 K. It absorbs 10,000 J per cycle. Calculate: (i) Carnot efficiency, (ii) maximum work output, (iii) minimum heat rejected.

(i) $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H = 1 - 300/600 = \mathbf{0.50 = 50\%}$ [1]
(ii) $W_{\max} = \eta \times Q_H = 0.50 \times 10000 = \mathbf{5000 \text{ J}}$ [1]
(iii) $Q_C = Q_H - W = 10000 - 5000 = \mathbf{5000 \text{ J}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(b) Explain why real engines always have efficiencies lower than the Carnot efficiency.

The Carnot cycle requires all processes to be reversible — infinitely slow, with no friction, turbulence, or finite temperature differences during heat transfer [1]. Real engines have irreversibilities: friction in moving parts, rapid (non-quasi-static) processes, and heat transfer across finite temperature differences. Each irreversibility creates entropy ($\Delta S > 0$), requiring more waste heat $Q_C$ to be rejected, reducing efficiency below Carnot [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A power station operates at 35% efficiency and produces 500 MW of electrical power. Calculate: (i) the rate of heat input, (ii) the rate of waste heat rejected.

(i) $\eta = W/Q_H$ → $Q_H = W/\eta = 500/0.35 = \mathbf{1429 \text{ MW}}$ [1]
(ii) $Q_C = Q_H - W = 1429 - 500 = \mathbf{929 \text{ MW}}$ [1]
Nearly twice as much energy is wasted as is used! This waste heats rivers/cooling towers [1].
Explain [2]

(d) Describe the four stages of the Carnot cycle and state which are isothermal and which are adiabatic.

1. Isothermal expansion at $T_H$: gas absorbs heat and expands. 2. Adiabatic expansion: gas expands further, cooling from $T_H$ to $T_C$ with no heat exchange [1]. 3. Isothermal compression at $T_C$: gas is compressed, rejecting heat. 4. Adiabatic compression: gas is compressed further, heating from $T_C$ to $T_H$ with no heat exchange, returning to the initial state [1].
B.4 Thermodynamics  |  B.4.3 Heat EnginesSummary

Key Takeaways

  • Heat engine: absorbs $Q_H$, does work $W$, rejects $Q_C$. $Q_H = W + Q_C$.
  • Efficiency: $\eta = W/Q_H = 1 - Q_C/Q_H$. Always < 1.
  • Carnot efficiency: $\eta_C = 1 - T_C/T_H$ — the absolute maximum for any engine between $T_H$ and $T_C$.
  • Carnot cycle: 4 reversible steps — isothermal expansion, adiabatic expansion, isothermal compression, adiabatic compression.
  • Why Carnot is max: all steps reversible → $\Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0$. Any irreversibility → $\eta < \eta_C$.
  • To improve efficiency: raise $T_H$ or lower $T_C$. 100% needs $T_C = 0$ K (impossible).
  • Real engines are 30–60% of Carnot due to friction, turbulence, and finite-rate heat transfer.
