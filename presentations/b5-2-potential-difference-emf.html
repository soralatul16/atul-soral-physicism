The energy of a circuit

Potential Difference (Voltage)

Potential difference across a component is the work done (energy transferred) per unit charge as charge passes through it.

$$V = \frac{W}{Q}$$

voltage = energy transferred ÷ charge

  • $V$ — potential difference, in volts (V). 1 V = 1 joule per coulomb (1 J C⁻¹).
  • $W$ — energy transferred to the component, in joules (J).
  • $Q$ — charge that passes, in coulombs (C).
Physical meaning: a p.d. of 3 V across a bulb means each coulomb of charge delivers 3 J of energy to the bulb as it passes through. Voltage is "energy per charge," current is "charge per time."

Quick example

12 J of energy are transferred when 4.0 C pass through a resistor. Find the p.d.

$V = W/Q = 12/4.0 = \mathbf{3.0\ \text{V}}$

Electromotive Force (EMF)

The EMF of a source is the energy given to each unit of charge by the source (e.g. a battery converting chemical energy to electrical energy).

$$\varepsilon = \frac{W}{Q}$$

EMF = total energy supplied per unit charge (also in volts)

EMF (ε)

Energy the source gives per coulomb. A property of the source itself. Measured across the terminals when no current flows.

Terminal PD (V)

Energy actually delivered to the external circuit per coulomb. What a voltmeter reads across the battery while current flows.

Key distinction: EMF is misnamed — it is not a force. It is an energy-per-charge (volts), the same units as p.d. EMF is the "full" voltage a source could supply; terminal PD is what's left after internal losses.

Internal Resistance: Why Batteries "Sag"

Real sources have internal resistance $r$. Some energy is lost inside the source, so the terminal PD is less than the EMF whenever current flows.

$$\varepsilon = V + Ir = IR + Ir$$
$$\varepsilon = I(R + r)$$
  • $\varepsilon$ = EMF; $V = IR$ = terminal PD across the external load; $Ir$ = "lost volts" inside the source.
  • Terminal PD: $V = \varepsilon - Ir$. As current $I$ rises, the lost volts $Ir$ grow, so $V$ drops.
  • At zero current (open circuit), $V = \varepsilon$ — the voltmeter reads the full EMF.
Everyday example: your car headlights dim briefly when you start the engine. The starter motor draws a huge current, so $Ir$ becomes large and the terminal PD sags — everything else on that battery momentarily gets less voltage.

Worked example

A cell of EMF 6.0 V and internal resistance 0.50 Ω drives a current of 2.0 A. Find the terminal PD.

$V = \varepsilon - Ir = 6.0 - (2.0)(0.50) = 6.0 - 1.0 = \mathbf{5.0\ \text{V}}$

Electrical Energy Transferred

Combining $V = W/Q$ with $Q = It$ gives the energy delivered by a component:

$$W = QV = VIt$$
$$W = VIt$$
  • $W$ = electrical energy transferred (J).
  • Because $Q = It$, substitute into $W = QV$ to get $W = VIt$.
  • Power (energy per second) follows: $P = W/t = VI$ — expanded fully in B.5.6.

Worked example

A 12 V device draws 2.5 A for 3.0 minutes. Find the electrical energy transferred.

$t = 3.0\times60 = 180$ s.

$W = VIt = 12\times2.5\times180 = \mathbf{5400\ \text{J}}$

Energy conservation across the circuit: the total energy per coulomb supplied by the source (ε) equals the energy per coulomb delivered to the load (V) plus that lost internally (Ir). Nothing disappears.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — find the current

A cell of EMF 9.0 V and internal resistance 1.0 Ω is connected to a 5.0 Ω resistor. Find the current.

$I = \dfrac{\varepsilon}{R+r} = \dfrac{9.0}{5.0+1.0} = \mathbf{1.5\ \text{A}}$

Example 2 — lost volts

For the cell in Example 1, find the terminal PD and the energy lost per coulomb inside the cell.

$V = IR = 1.5\times5.0 = 7.5$ V.

Lost volts $= Ir = 1.5\times1.0 = \mathbf{1.5\ \text{V}}$ (check: $7.5 + 1.5 = 9.0 = \varepsilon$ ✓)

Example 3 — finding internal resistance

A battery has EMF 1.5 V. When it delivers 0.30 A, the terminal PD is 1.35 V. Find $r$.

$r = \dfrac{\varepsilon - V}{I} = \dfrac{1.5 - 1.35}{0.30} = \dfrac{0.15}{0.30} = \mathbf{0.50\ \Omega}$

Define & Distinguish [3]

(a) Define potential difference, and explain how EMF differs from the terminal potential difference of a cell.

Potential difference is the energy transferred per unit charge as charge passes through a component, $V = W/Q$ [1]. EMF is the total energy supplied per unit charge by the source (measured across its terminals at zero current) [1]. Terminal PD is the energy delivered per unit charge to the external circuit; it is less than the EMF when current flows because some energy is dissipated in the internal resistance ($V = \varepsilon - Ir$) [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A cell of EMF 12 V and internal resistance 0.80 Ω is connected to an external resistor of 5.2 Ω. Calculate the current and the terminal potential difference.

$I = \varepsilon/(R+r) = 12/(5.2+0.80) = 12/6.0 = 2.0$ A [1]
$V = IR = 2.0\times5.2$ [1]
$= \mathbf{10.4\ \text{V}}$ (equivalently $V = \varepsilon - Ir = 12 - 1.6 = 10.4$ V) [1]
Explain [2]

(c) Explain why the terminal potential difference of a battery decreases as the current drawn from it increases.

The terminal PD is $V = \varepsilon - Ir$ [1]. As the current $I$ increases, the "lost volts" $Ir$ across the internal resistance increase, so a larger fraction of the EMF is dissipated inside the cell, leaving a smaller terminal PD for the external circuit [1].
Calculate [2]

(d) A 6.0 V supply delivers a steady 1.5 A to a heater for 4.0 minutes. Calculate the electrical energy transferred to the heater.

$t = 4.0\times60 = 240$ s [1]
$W = VIt = 6.0\times1.5\times240 = \mathbf{2160\ \text{J}}$ (2.16 kJ) [1]

Key Takeaways

  • Potential difference: $V = W/Q$, in volts (1 V = 1 J C⁻¹) — energy delivered per coulomb.
  • EMF: $\varepsilon = W/Q$ — total energy supplied per coulomb by the source; not actually a force.
  • Internal resistance: $\varepsilon = I(R+r)$, terminal PD $V = \varepsilon - Ir$.
  • At zero current the terminal PD equals the EMF; under load it sags.
  • Electrical energy: $W = QV = VIt$; power $P = VI$.
  • Energy conservation: $\varepsilon = V_{load} + $ (lost volts $Ir$) per coulomb.
