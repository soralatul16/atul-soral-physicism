B.5.1

Electric Current & Charge

The Flow of Charge

Electricity is nothing more than charge in motion. A current is a river of electrons — trillions per second through every wire. Understanding what current is, how charge moves, and how we measure it is the foundation of all circuit physics.

Electric Current — Definition

DEFINITION

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge past a point in a circuit.

$$I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}$$
I
Current
(A = ampere)
ΔQ
Charge flowing
(C = coulomb)

Charge of one electron

$e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C. Charge is quantised — always a multiple of $e$.

$Q = ne$ where $n$ = number of electrons. 1 ampere = $6.25 \times 10^{18}$ electrons per second.

Conventional current

Flows from + to − (positive to negative). This is the historical convention, defined before electrons were discovered.

Electron flow

Actual electrons move from − to +. Opposite to conventional current. IB uses conventional current unless stated otherwise.

The ampere is an SI base unit. Since 2019, it's defined by fixing $e = 1.602176634 \times 10^{-19}$ C exactly. 1 A = 1 C/s.

Example: Drift velocity in copper wire

$I = 3.0$ A, $A = 1.0 \times 10^{-6}$ m², $n = 8.5 \times 10^{28}$ m⁻³, $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C

$v = \frac{I}{nAq} = \frac{3.0}{8.5 \times 10^{28} \times 10^{-6} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = \mathbf{2.2 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m/s} \approx 0.22 \text{ mm/s}}$

Electrons crawl at ~0.2 mm/s! But the electric field travels at near light speed. When you flip a switch, the signal (field) reaches the bulb almost instantly — every electron in the wire starts moving simultaneously. The individual electrons are slow; the collective push is fast.
Thinner wire = faster drift (same I, smaller A → larger v). Semiconductors have much lower $n$ → much higher drift velocity for the same current.

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

Define [1]

(a) Define electric current.

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge: $I = \Delta Q / \Delta t$, measured in amperes (A) where 1 A = 1 C s⁻¹ [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A current of 0.50 A flows through a lamp for 2.0 minutes. Calculate: (i) the total charge, (ii) the number of electrons passing through the lamp.

(i) $Q = It = 0.50 \times 120 = \mathbf{60 \text{ C}}$ [1]
(ii) $n = Q/e = 60 / (1.60 \times 10^{-19}) = \mathbf{3.75 \times 10^{20} \text{ electrons}}$ [1] [1]
Calculate [3]

(c) A copper wire ($n = 8.5 \times 10^{28}$ m⁻³) has diameter 1.0 mm and carries 2.0 A. Calculate the drift velocity.

$A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.5 \times 10^{-3})^2 = 7.85 \times 10^{-7}$ m² [1]
$v = \frac{I}{nAq} = \frac{2.0}{8.5 \times 10^{28} \times 7.85 \times 10^{-7} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}}$ [1]
$v = \mathbf{1.87 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m/s} \approx 0.19 \text{ mm/s}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(d) Explain why a light bulb turns on almost instantly when you flip the switch, even though electrons drift at only ~0.2 mm/s.

The electric field propagates through the wire at close to the speed of light (~$3 \times 10^8$ m/s) [1]. When the switch closes, the field reaches all electrons in the circuit almost simultaneously, causing them all to start drifting at once. The light turns on because all electrons start moving together, not because any single electron travels from switch to bulb [1].

Key Takeaways

  • $I = \Delta Q / \Delta t$ — current = charge per second. 1 A = 1 C/s.
  • $e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C — charge of one electron. $Q = ne$.
  • Conventional current: + to − (historical). Electron flow: − to + (actual).
  • $I = nAvq$ — drift velocity equation. Connects microscopic (n, v, q) to macroscopic (I).
  • Drift velocity is tiny (~0.2 mm/s in copper). Electric field propagates at ~c.
  • Thinner wire → faster drift for same current. Semiconductors: low n → high v.
  • Charge is conserved — current into a junction = current out (Kirchhoff's first law, B.5.5).
