Electric Current & Charge

The flow of charge

Electric current is simply charge in motion. Here we define current precisely as rate of flow of charge, quantise charge in units of the electron, connect the macroscopic current to the microscopic drift of electrons, and sort out the conventions that trip everyone up.

What Is Electric Current?

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-section of a conductor.

$$I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}$$

current = charge passing a point ÷ time taken

  • $I$ — current, in amperes (A). 1 A = 1 coulomb per second (1 C s⁻¹).
  • $\Delta Q$ — charge, in coulombs (C).
  • $\Delta t$ — time, in seconds (s).
  • Rearranged: $\Delta Q = I\,\Delta t$ — the charge delivered in a time is current × time.
The ampere is an SI base unit. Charge is defined from it: 1 coulomb = the charge passing when 1 A flows for 1 second. So current is fundamental and charge is derived, even though it feels the other way round.

Quick example

A charge of 60 C passes a point in 20 s. Find the current.

$I = \Delta Q/\Delta t = 60/20 = \mathbf{3.0\ \text{A}}$

Charge Comes in Packets

Charge is quantised — it exists only in whole-number multiples of the elementary charge $e$, the magnitude of the charge on one electron or proton.

$$Q = Ne$$
$$e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$$
  • $N$ = the number of charge carriers (electrons), a whole number.
  • Electron charge $= -e$; proton charge $= +e$. Same magnitude, opposite sign.
  • You can never have a fraction of $e$ of free charge — charge always comes in these indivisible packets.

Worked example

How many electrons pass a point when a current of 2.0 A flows for 5.0 s?

$Q = It = 2.0\times5.0 = 10$ C.

$N = \dfrac{Q}{e} = \dfrac{10}{1.60\times10^{-19}} = \mathbf{6.25\times10^{19}\ \text{electrons}}$

Scale check: even a modest current moves an astronomical number of electrons each second — which is why current appears perfectly continuous at the everyday scale.

Conventional Current vs Electron Flow

Conventional current

Defined as the direction positive charge would flow: from + terminal to − terminal around the external circuit. All circuit analysis uses this.

Electron flow

In a metal the actual carriers are electrons, which drift the opposite way: from − to +.

Why the mismatch? Convention was fixed by Benjamin Franklin before the electron was discovered — he guessed the moving charges were positive. We keep the convention because all the physics works out consistently; you just remember electrons actually go the other way.

The Microscopic Picture: I = nAvq

Linking the macroscopic current to the actual drifting electrons gives one of the most important microscopic equations in the topic:

$$I = nAvq$$
  • $n$ — number density of charge carriers (carriers per m³).
  • $A$ — cross-sectional area of the conductor (m²).
  • $v$ — drift velocity: the slow average velocity of carriers along the wire (m s⁻¹).
  • $q$ — charge on each carrier (for electrons, $q = e = 1.60\times10^{-19}$ C).
Drift velocity is astonishingly slow — typically less than 1 mm s⁻¹, slower than a snail. Yet a lamp lights instantly because the electric field propagates through the wire near the speed of light, pushing all electrons at once. The signal is fast; the electrons themselves barely move.

Worked example

A copper wire has $n = 8.5\times10^{28}$ m⁻³, area $A = 1.0\times10^{-6}$ m², carrying 2.0 A. Find the drift velocity.

$v = \dfrac{I}{nAq} = \dfrac{2.0}{(8.5\times10^{28})(1.0\times10^{-6})(1.6\times10^{-19})} = \mathbf{1.5\times10^{-4}\ \text{m s}^{-1}}$

Worked Examples

Example 1 — charge from current

A current of 0.50 A flows through a bulb for 2.0 minutes. How much charge passes through it?

$t = 2.0\times60 = 120$ s.

$Q = It = 0.50\times120 = \mathbf{60\ \text{C}}$

Example 2 — electrons per second

How many electrons pass a point per second when the current is 1.0 A?

$N = \dfrac{Q}{e} = \dfrac{1.0}{1.6\times10^{-19}} = \mathbf{6.25\times10^{18}\ \text{per second}}$

Example 3 — area from drift equation

Using $I = nAvq$, if $I = 3.0$ A, $n = 8.5\times10^{28}$ m⁻³, $v = 2.2\times10^{-4}$ m s⁻¹, find $A$.

$A = \dfrac{I}{nvq} = \dfrac{3.0}{(8.5\times10^{28})(2.2\times10^{-4})(1.6\times10^{-19})} = \mathbf{1.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{m}^2}$

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

Define & Calculate [3]

(a) Define electric current. A charge of 45 C flows through a resistor in 90 s; calculate the current.

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-section of a conductor, $I = \Delta Q/\Delta t$ [1]. $I = 45/90$ [1] $= \mathbf{0.50\ \text{A}}$ [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A current of 3.2 A flows for 1.5 minutes. Determine the number of electrons that pass a given point.

$t = 90$ s; $Q = It = 3.2\times90 = 288$ C [1]
$N = Q/e = 288/(1.6\times10^{-19})$ [1]
$= \mathbf{1.8\times10^{21}\ \text{electrons}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) In a metal wire, state the direction of conventional current relative to electron flow, and explain why the two differ.

Conventional current flows from the positive to the negative terminal in the external circuit, whereas electrons (the actual carriers in a metal) flow from negative to positive — the two are in opposite directions [1]. They differ because the convention was chosen (by Franklin) as the direction positive charge would move, before the electron and its negative charge were discovered [1].
Apply [3]

(d) Two wires of the same material carry the same current, but wire X has twice the cross-sectional area of wire Y. Using I = nAvq, compare the drift velocities.

For the same material, $n$ and $q$ are identical; the same current means $nAvq$ is equal for both, so $Av$ is constant [1]. Since $A_X = 2A_Y$, we need $v_X = v_Y/2$ [1]. The drift velocity in the thicker wire X is half that in the thinner wire Y — carriers move more slowly in the wider conductor to deliver the same current [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Current: $I = \Delta Q/\Delta t$, in amperes (1 A = 1 C s⁻¹). The ampere is an SI base unit.
  • Charge is quantised: $Q = Ne$, with $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}$ C.
  • Conventional current flows + → −; electrons flow − → + (opposite).
  • Microscopic current: $I = nAvq$ links current to carrier density, area, drift velocity, and charge.
  • Drift velocity is tiny (< 1 mm s⁻¹), but the field — and hence the effect — travels near light speed.
