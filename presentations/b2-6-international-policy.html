Where Physics Meets Politics

The physics gives us a carbon budget. Policy decides how to spend it. The 1.5°C target, the Paris Agreement, carbon trading, and net zero — all of these are physics-constrained political choices. Understanding the science behind the targets is essential for any informed citizen, and for IB exams.

The Paris Agreement (2015) — The Physics Behind the Targets

THE TARGETS

Hold global average temperature increase to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, and pursue efforts to limit to 1.5°C.

Why 1.5°C?

  • Coral reefs: 70–90% loss at 1.5°C vs >99% at 2°C
  • Arctic ice-free summers: once per century at 1.5°C vs once per decade at 2°C
  • Sea level: 0.26–0.77 m by 2100 at 1.5°C vs 0.30–0.93 m at 2°C
  • Crop yields: significantly less impact at 1.5°C
  • Every fraction of a degree matters

Why 2°C is the "danger line"

  • Multiple tipping points activated near 2°C
  • Greenland ice sheet may become irreversible
  • Permafrost releases become significant
  • Amazon dieback risk increases sharply
  • Non-linear feedbacks may take over
  • Adaptation becomes much harder and more costly
The physics basis for these numbers: Climate sensitivity (~3°C per CO₂ doubling) + carbon budget calculations show that staying below 1.5°C requires net zero by ~2050 and below 2°C by ~2070. These aren't arbitrary political numbers — they emerge directly from the physics of radiative forcing and feedback sensitivity.

NDCs & the Emissions Gap

Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs): Each country sets its own emission reduction targets under Paris. These are voluntary, non-binding, and reviewed every 5 years.

The Emissions Gap

Even if every country meets its current NDC:

  • Warming reaches ~2.5–2.8°C by 2100 — well above both targets
  • Current policies (not pledges) put us on track for ~3.0°C
  • The gap between pledges and the 1.5°C pathway is ~20 Gt CO₂/year by 2030

The world is not on track to meet either target.

Per capita vs total: China emits most total CO₂ but its per capita emissions (~8 t/person) are lower than the US (~15 t/person) or Australia (~16 t/person). Historical cumulative emissions also matter — the US and EU have emitted far more over the past 150 years. This distinction is central to climate justice debates.

Carbon Pricing — Making Pollution Expensive

The physics gives a carbon budget. Economics converts this to a price. If emitting CO₂ costs money, markets shift toward cleaner alternatives.

Carbon Tax

Government sets a price per tonne of CO₂. Emitters pay directly. Simple, predictable, covers all emissions.

Example: Sweden — $130/tonne CO₂. Emissions dropped 27% since introduction while GDP grew 80%.

Advantage: price certainty. Challenge: politically difficult to set high enough.

Emissions Trading (Cap & Trade)

Government sets a cap on total emissions. Companies buy/sell emission permits. Market determines the price.

Example: EU ETS — covers ~40% of EU emissions. Price ~€90/tonne (2024). Cap reduces annually.

Advantage: emission certainty. Challenge: price volatility, free allowances weaken impact.

The social cost of carbon

The estimated economic damage of emitting one additional tonne of CO₂: ~$50–200/tonne (varies by model and discount rate). This includes future crop losses, health impacts, sea level damage, and extreme weather costs. Most current carbon prices are below this — meaning CO₂ is still underpriced relative to the damage it causes.

Physics constrains the price: To stay below 2°C, carbon prices need to reach $100–150/tonne by 2030 (World Bank). Currently, ~85% of global emissions are unpriced or priced below $10/tonne. The gap between the physics-required price and the actual price is the single biggest policy failure.

Net Zero — The Physics of "Stopping" Warming

NET ZERO CO₂ EMISSIONS

The point at which CO₂ added to the atmosphere by human activity equals CO₂ removed from it (by sinks + technology). Net addition = zero.

Why net zero stops warming: Global temperature is approximately proportional to cumulative CO₂ emissions (the TCRE — Transient Climate Response to cumulative Emissions). Every 1000 Gt CO₂ emitted warms the planet by ~0.45°C. When net emissions reach zero, cumulative CO₂ stops rising, and temperature stabilises (approximately).

$$\Delta T \approx \text{TCRE} \times \sum \text{CO}_2 \text{ emitted}$$

TCRE $\approx 0.45°C$ per 1000 Gt CO₂ (likely range: 0.27–0.63)

Net zero ≠ zero emissions

Some emissions (agriculture, cement, aviation) are extremely hard to eliminate. Net zero allows residual emissions if they are balanced by removal (forests, BECCS, DAC). But removal at the required scale (~10 Gt CO₂/year by 2050) doesn't yet exist.

Net zero for CO₂ stabilises temperature. But other GHGs matter too. CH₄ has a short lifetime (~12 years) — reducing CH₄ emissions produces immediate cooling. N₂O and F-gases require their own net zero targets. Full climate stabilisation needs net zero for all GHGs, not just CO₂.

TOK: Science, Uncertainty & Decision-Making

What is certain

  • CO₂ absorbs IR — measured since 1859 (Tyndall)
  • CO₂ is rising due to fossil fuels — isotopes confirm
  • Earth is warming — multiple independent datasets
  • Physics: more GHGs → more trapping → warming
  • IPCC: "unequivocal" that humans cause warming

What has uncertainty

  • Exact climate sensitivity (2.5–4.0°C per doubling)
  • Cloud feedback (positive or negative? how much?)
  • Tipping point thresholds (exact temperatures)
  • Regional impacts (which areas get wetter/drier)
  • Rate of ice sheet collapse

Key TOK questions for IB

  • Does scientific uncertainty justify inaction? Climate models have uncertainty ranges, but the direction of change is certain. Medical decisions are made with far less certainty.
  • Who bears responsibility? Historical emitters (US, EU) vs current emitters (China) vs future emitters (developing nations). Per capita vs total vs cumulative.
  • Can science alone determine policy? Science tells us what will happen at different emission levels. It cannot tell us what we should do — that involves values, economics, equity, and politics.
  • The precautionary principle: When consequences are severe and irreversible, should we act before full certainty? Most risk analysis says yes.
Scientific consensus is not a vote. 97%+ of climate scientists agree on human-caused warming — but consensus in science is based on evidence, not opinion polls. The evidence is overwhelming: 150+ years of physics, chemistry, and observation all converge on the same conclusion.

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

State [2]

(a) State the two temperature targets of the Paris Agreement and explain why the lower target (1.5°C) is considered preferable.

The Paris Agreement aims to limit warming to well below 2°C and pursue efforts to limit it to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels [1]. The 1.5°C target is preferable because the difference between 1.5°C and 2°C is significant: coral reef loss increases from 70–90% to >99%, Arctic ice-free summers change from once per century to once per decade, and several tipping points (Greenland ice sheet, permafrost) are more likely to be triggered at 2°C [1].
Explain [3]

(b) Explain the physics behind the concept of a "carbon budget" and why it imposes a finite limit on total future emissions.

Global warming is approximately proportional to cumulative CO₂ emissions (TCRE ≈ 0.45°C per 1000 Gt CO₂) [1]. This means there is a fixed total amount of CO₂ that can ever be emitted to stay below a given temperature target — the carbon budget [1]. For 1.5°C, the remaining budget is ~200 Gt CO₂ (from 2024). At ~40 Gt/year current emissions, this is exhausted in ~5 years. The budget is finite because CO₂ accumulates in the atmosphere for centuries — each tonne emitted adds to the total, regardless of when it was emitted [1].
Explain [2]

(c) Explain what "net zero emissions" means and why achieving it would stabilise global temperature.

Net zero means the amount of CO₂ added to the atmosphere by human activity equals the amount removed (by natural sinks and technology), so the net addition is zero [1]. Since temperature depends on cumulative emissions ($\Delta T \propto \sum$CO₂), when net emissions reach zero, cumulative CO₂ stops increasing, and temperature approximately stabilises at its current level [1].
Discuss [4]

(d) Discuss the ethical considerations involved in setting global climate targets, considering the different responsibilities of developed and developing nations.

Developed nations (US, EU) have the largest historical cumulative emissions and became wealthy through fossil fuel use, giving them greater historical responsibility [1]. Developing nations (India, sub-Saharan Africa) have low per capita emissions and argue they need fossil fuels for development and poverty reduction [1]. Climate impacts disproportionately affect developing nations (which contributed least to the problem) — raising questions of climate justice [1]. Solutions must balance the physics (global emissions must fall rapidly) with equity (allowing development) — through technology transfer, climate finance, and differentiated responsibilities. The Paris Agreement addresses this through "common but differentiated responsibilities" [1].

More Practice 8/10

Calculate [3]

The TCRE is approximately 0.45°C per 1000 Gt CO₂. Current warming is 1.2°C. Calculate the remaining carbon budget for 1.5°C and 2.0°C targets.

Remaining warming allowed: 1.5 − 1.2 = 0.3°C for 1.5°C target; 2.0 − 1.2 = 0.8°C for 2.0°C target [1].
Budget = remaining warming / TCRE × 1000:
1.5°C: $\frac{0.3}{0.45} \times 1000 = \mathbf{667 \text{ Gt CO}_2}$ (central estimate; actual is lower due to non-CO₂ forcings, giving ~200–400 Gt) [1]
2.0°C: $\frac{0.8}{0.45} \times 1000 = \mathbf{1778 \text{ Gt CO}_2}$ (actual ~900–1200 Gt after non-CO₂ adjustment) [1]
Discuss [3]

Compare a carbon tax and an emissions trading scheme as mechanisms for reducing greenhouse gas emissions. State one advantage and one disadvantage of each.

Carbon tax: Advantage — price certainty allows businesses to plan investments; simple to implement across all sectors [1]. Disadvantage — no guarantee of a specific emission reduction; politically difficult to set high enough [½].
Emissions trading (cap & trade): Advantage — guarantees a specific emission cap; market finds the cheapest reductions [1]. Disadvantage — price volatility creates investment uncertainty; free allowances and offsets can weaken the system [½].
Both aim to internalise the external cost of CO₂ emissions — making polluters pay for the damage they cause [1].
Evaluate [3]

"Scientific uncertainty about exact climate sensitivity means we should wait for more data before acting." Evaluate this statement.

While the exact climate sensitivity has a range (2.5–4.0°C per doubling), the direction of change is certain — more CO₂ means more warming [1]. The uncertainty is in "how much," not "whether." Waiting for more precision means continuing to emit, which uses up the finite carbon budget and makes future action more difficult and expensive [1]. The precautionary principle suggests that when consequences are severe and potentially irreversible (tipping points, sea level rise, ecosystem collapse), the appropriate response is to act on the best available science — not to delay until certainty is complete. Medical decisions, engineering safety margins, and insurance all operate under uncertainty. Climate policy should too [1].

Key Takeaways

  • Paris Agreement (2015): limit to well below 2°C, pursue 1.5°C. 196 nations. NDCs reviewed every 5 years.
  • Emissions gap: current NDCs → ~2.5–2.8°C. Current policies → ~3.0°C. Neither target is being met.
  • Carbon budget: $\Delta T \approx \text{TCRE} \times \sum$CO₂. Finite budget for each target. 1.5°C budget nearly exhausted.
  • Net zero: when emissions = removals. Stabilises temperature. Required by ~2050 for 1.5°C.
  • Carbon pricing: tax (price certainty) or trading (cap certainty). Most emissions still unpriced or underpriced.
  • Climate justice: historical vs current emitters, per capita vs total, developed vs developing responsibility.
  • TOK: Science determines what will happen. Values determine what we should do. Uncertainty ≠ inaction.
  • IPCC: "unequivocal" — the strongest statement in the history of climate science.
