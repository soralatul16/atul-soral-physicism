Renewable vs Non-Renewable

Renewable (will not run out)

  • Solar — photovoltaic cells, thermal panels
  • Wind — turbines convert KE of air
  • Hydroelectric — falling water drives turbines
  • Tidal — Moon's gravity moves water
  • Wave — ocean surface energy
  • Geothermal — heat from Earth's interior
  • Biomass — organic material (wood, crops)

Non-Renewable (will run out)

  • Fossil fuels: coal, oil, natural gas
  • Nuclear fission: uranium-235, plutonium-239

Formed over millions of years. Being consumed far faster than they form.

All fossil fuels are stored solar energy — ancient organisms captured sunlight via photosynthesis, died, and were compressed underground for millions of years.
IB definition: Renewable = replenished on a human timescale. Non-renewable = consumed faster than replenished. Nuclear is non-renewable because uranium ore is finite.

How Each Source Generates Electricity

Fossil Fuels

Chemical → Thermal → KE (steam turbine) → Electrical (generator). Efficiency: ~35%

Nuclear

Nuclear → Thermal → KE (steam turbine) → Electrical. Efficiency: ~33%. No CO₂ but radioactive waste.

Solar PV

Radiant → Electrical directly (photovoltaic effect). No moving parts. Efficiency: ~20%.

Wind

KE of air → KE of blades → Electrical (generator). Efficiency: ~35-45%.

Hydroelectric

GPE of water → KE → Electrical. Efficiency: ~85-90%. Most efficient large-scale source.

Geothermal

Thermal (Earth's core) → KE (steam) → Electrical. Limited to volcanic regions.

Most sources (except solar PV) use the same final step: spin a turbine → drive a generator → produce electricity. The difference is what provides the initial energy to spin the turbine.

Specific Energy & Energy Density

Specific energy = energy per unit mass (J/kg). Energy density = energy per unit volume (J/m³).

FuelSpecific Energy (MJ/kg)
Uranium-23580,000,000
Hydrogen142
Gasoline (petrol)46
Coal24
Wood16
Lithium battery0.5
Nuclear is extraordinary: 1 kg of uranium-235 releases 80 TRILLION joules — equivalent to 2,000 TONNES of coal. This is why nuclear power stations need tiny amounts of fuel compared to coal plants.
Why batteries are limited: A lithium battery stores only 0.5 MJ/kg vs petrol at 46 MJ/kg — nearly 100× less. This is why electric cars need huge, heavy battery packs and still have shorter range than petrol cars.

The Global Energy Challenge

  • ~80% of world energy still comes from fossil fuels (2024)
  • CO₂ emissions from burning fossil fuels → greenhouse effect → climate change
  • Global demand rising — population growth + industrialization
  • Energy poverty: ~750 million people lack electricity access
The greenhouse effect: CO₂ and other gases trap infrared radiation in the atmosphere. More CO₂ → more trapping → higher temperatures → ice melts, sea levels rise, weather extremes. This connects to Topic B.2 (Greenhouse Effect).
TOK connection: Should developing nations be held to the same emission standards as developed nations who industrialized using fossil fuels? Who bears responsibility for climate change — past emitters or current ones?
IB exam context: Energy source questions often include ethical, environmental, and economic dimensions. Be prepared to discuss trade-offs — no energy source is perfect. The "best" choice depends on location, resources, and priorities.

IB-Style Question Set 6/10

State [2]

(a) State one advantage and one disadvantage of using nuclear fission as an energy source.

Advantage: Very high energy density / no greenhouse gas emissions during operation [1].
Disadvantage: Produces radioactive waste that remains hazardous for thousands of years / risk of nuclear accidents [1].
Explain [2]

(b) Explain why solar power is described as "intermittent" and how this affects its use as a primary energy source.

Solar output depends on weather and time of day — zero at night, reduced on cloudy days [1]. This means it cannot provide reliable baseload power without energy storage systems (batteries, pumped hydro) which add cost and complexity [1].
Calculate [3]

(c) A coal power station burns 500 tonnes of coal per day. Specific energy of coal = 24 MJ/kg. Efficiency = 35%. Calculate the electrical power output in MW.

Energy input per day: $500000 \times 24 \times 10^6 = 1.2 \times 10^{13}$ J [1]
Useful output: $0.35 \times 1.2 \times 10^{13} = 4.2 \times 10^{12}$ J/day [1]
Power: $\frac{4.2 \times 10^{12}}{86400} = 4.86 \times 10^7$ W $= \mathbf{48.6 \text{ MW}}$ [1]
Discuss [4]

(d) Discuss the factors a government should consider when choosing between wind power and nuclear power for a new power station.

Wind: renewable, no fuel costs, quick to build, but intermittent, needs backup, visual/noise impact, lower power density [2].
Nuclear: reliable baseload, no CO₂, high power output, but expensive to build, radioactive waste, decommissioning costs, public fear, long construction time [2].
Decision depends on: geography (wind availability), public opinion, existing grid, budget, long-term energy strategy.

Key Takeaways

  • Renewable: solar, wind, hydro, tidal, wave, geothermal, biomass. Won't run out.
  • Non-renewable: fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) + nuclear. Will run out.
  • Most sources spin a turbine → generator → electricity. Solar PV is the exception (direct conversion).
  • Nuclear has extraordinary energy density: 1 kg U-235 = 2000 tonnes coal.
  • Intermittency is the main drawback of solar and wind — need storage or backup.
  • Fossil fuels → CO₂ → greenhouse effect → climate change.
  • No perfect source — every option has trade-offs. IB expects you to discuss these.
