Velocity & Speed

Instantaneous vs Average — two ways to describe "how fast," and why confusing them loses you marks in IB exams.

What is Speed?

Speed tells you how fast an object is moving — but says nothing about direction.

$$\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}} = \frac{d}{t}$$
  • Scalar quantity — magnitude only
  • SI unit: m s$^{-1}$ (metres per second)
  • Always positive — you can't have negative speed
  • Tells you "how much ground per second" — but NOT which direction
FYI: Speed is the scalar partner of velocity — just like distance is the scalar partner of displacement. Same relationship, different context.

What is Velocity?

Velocity tells you how fast an object is moving AND in which direction.

$$\vec{v} = \frac{\Delta \vec{s}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\text{displacement}}{\text{time}}$$
  • Vector quantity — has magnitude AND direction
  • SI unit: m s$^{-1}$ + direction (e.g., "15 m/s East")
  • Can be positive or negative — sign indicates direction
  • Two objects can have the same speed but different velocities (different directions)
Key distinction: A car turning a corner at constant speed has changing velocity because the direction changes. Changing velocity = acceleration, even if the speedometer reads the same number.

Speed vs Velocity — Side by Side

Speed

  • Scalar — magnitude only
  • Rate of change of distance
  • Always $\geq 0$
  • $v = d / t$
  • "How fast?"

Velocity

  • Vector — magnitude + direction
  • Rate of change of displacement
  • Can be +, −, or 0
  • $\vec{v} = \Delta\vec{s} / \Delta t$
  • "How fast AND which way?"
Relationship: Speed is the magnitude of velocity. If velocity = −15 m/s, then speed = 15 m/s. The sign only tells direction.
IB exam tip: "Calculate the speed" → always give a positive number, no direction. "Calculate the velocity" → give magnitude AND direction (or sign convention).

Average Speed

Average speed looks at the entire journey — total distance divided by total time.

$$v_{avg} = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}} = \frac{d_1 + d_2 + d_3 + \ldots}{t_1 + t_2 + t_3 + \ldots}$$
The #1 mistake: Students average the individual speeds — (75 + 90) / 2 = 82.5 km/h. This is WRONG. You must always divide total distance by total time. The stop time is included!
Why it matters: Different legs of the journey take different times. You can't just average the speeds because the car spent MORE time on the slower leg.

Average Velocity

Average velocity uses displacement instead of distance — so direction matters, and it can be zero.

$$\vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\text{total displacement}}{\text{total time}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{s}}{\Delta t}$$

Example: A cyclist rides 5 km North in 10 minutes, then 5 km South in 15 minutes.

1Average speed = $\frac{10 \text{ km}}{25 \text{ min}}$ = $\frac{10}{0.417}$ = 24 km/h
2Average velocity = $\frac{0 \text{ km}}{25 \text{ min}}$ = 0 km/h

Displacement = 0 because the cyclist returned to the starting point!

This is why average velocity and average speed are different quantities. Average speed is always $\geq$ |average velocity|. They're equal only for straight-line, one-direction motion.

Instantaneous Speed & Velocity

Instantaneous means "at this exact moment" — not averaged over a journey.

What measures it?

  • A speedometer shows instantaneous speed
  • A GPS shows instantaneous velocity (speed + bearing)
  • A radar gun measures instantaneous speed of a moving object

On a graph?

Instantaneous velocity = the gradient of the tangent line to a displacement-time graph at that point.

HL Enrichment: Mathematically, instantaneous velocity is the derivative: $v = \frac{ds}{dt}$. This is the limit of average velocity as the time interval approaches zero. SL students don't need calculus — just know it's "the gradient of the tangent."

Reading Velocity from a Graph

On a displacement-time graph, velocity is the gradient.

Straight line on s-t graph

  • Gradient = constant velocity
  • Average velocity = instantaneous velocity (they're the same!)

Curved line on s-t graph

  • Gradient changes → velocity is changing
  • Average velocity = chord gradient (connect two points)
  • Instantaneous velocity = tangent gradient (at one point)
FYI: The tangent approximation gets better as the two points move closer together — this is the fundamental idea behind calculus.

Worked Example

Problem: A car travels 120 km East in 1.5 hours, then 80 km West in 1 hour. Calculate: (a) average speed (b) average velocity.
aTotal distance = 120 + 80 = 200 km
Total time = 1.5 + 1 = 2.5 hours
$$v_{avg\text{ speed}} = \frac{200}{2.5} = \mathbf{80 \text{ km h}^{-1}}$$
bDisplacement = 120 East − 80 West = 40 km East
$$\vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{40 \text{ km East}}{2.5 \text{ h}} = \mathbf{16 \text{ km h}^{-1} \text{ East}}$$

Average speed (80) is much larger than average velocity (16) because the car reversed direction!

Practice Questions

Click to reveal answers.

Calculate

A car's speedometer reads 90 km/h. Is this average or instantaneous? Speed or velocity?

Instantaneous speed. It shows the speed at that exact moment (instantaneous), and it shows magnitude only without direction (speed, not velocity).
Calculate

A student walks 400 m North in 5 minutes, then 300 m East in 4 minutes. Calculate the average speed and average velocity.

Avg speed = 700/540 = 1.30 m/s
Displacement = $\sqrt{400^2+300^2}$ = 500 m at 36.9° E of N
Avg velocity = 500/540 = 0.93 m/s at 36.9° E of N
Explain

Can an object have zero average velocity but non-zero average speed? Give an example.

Yes. Any closed path: run one lap of a track. Distance > 0 so speed > 0, but displacement = 0 so velocity = 0.
Distinguish

A car moves at constant speed around a circular track. Is its velocity constant? Explain.

No. Speed is constant but direction changes continuously. Since velocity = speed + direction, and direction is changing, velocity is NOT constant. The car is accelerating (centripetal acceleration).

Key Takeaways

  • Speed = scalar (how fast). Velocity = vector (how fast + direction).
  • Average = total distance or displacement / total time. Include rest periods!
  • Instantaneous = at one moment. Speedometer reading. Tangent gradient on s-t graph.
  • Average speed $\geq$ |average velocity| — always.
  • Constant speed around a curve = changing velocity (direction changes).
  • Never average individual speeds — always use $\frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}$.
