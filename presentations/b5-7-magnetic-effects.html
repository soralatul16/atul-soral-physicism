B.5.7

Magnetic Effects of Electric Current

Electricity makes magnetism

A current doesn't just heat wires — it creates a magnetic field around them. And a current in an external field feels a force. These two facts, discovered by Ørsted and formalised by the motor effect, connect electricity to magnetism and power every electric motor on Earth.

Magnetic Field Around a Straight Wire

Ørsted (1820) discovered that a current-carrying wire deflects a nearby compass — a current produces a circular magnetic field around it.

Right-hand grip rule

Point your right thumb along the (conventional) current; your curled fingers show the direction of the circular field lines. Reverse the current → reverse the field direction.

Field strength increases with larger current and decreases with distance from the wire. The field lines are closer together near the wire (stronger field) and spread out farther away (weaker field).

The Solenoid — An Electromagnet

Wind the wire into a coil (a solenoid) and the fields of all the loops add together, giving a strong, uniform field inside — just like a bar magnet.

Making it stronger

  • Increase the current.
  • Increase the number of turns per unit length.
  • Insert a soft-iron core — it becomes magnetised and greatly amplifies the field.
Why electromagnets are useful: unlike a permanent magnet, a solenoid's field can be switched on/off and reversed just by controlling the current — used in scrapyard cranes, relays, doorbells, and MRI machines.

The Motor Effect: Force on a Current

A current-carrying wire placed in an external magnetic field experiences a force — the interaction of the wire's own field with the external field.

$$F = BIL\sin\theta$$

maximum force $F = BIL$ when the wire is perpendicular to the field ($\theta = 90°$)

  • $B$ — magnetic flux density (tesla, T).
  • $I$ — current in the wire (A).
  • $L$ — length of wire in the field (m).
  • $\theta$ — angle between the current and the field. Force is zero when the wire is parallel to the field ($\theta = 0$).
This defines the tesla: 1 T is the flux density that produces a force of 1 N on 1 m of wire carrying 1 A perpendicular to the field. Magnetic flux density is also called the "B-field."

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule

Hold the thumb and first two fingers of the left hand at right angles:

  • Thumb → Thrust (force / motion)
  • First finger → Field (B, N to S)
  • SeCond finger → Current (conventional)
Left hand for the motor effect (force on a current). Don't confuse it with the right-hand rules used for field direction and for induction (B.5.8). Left = motor/force; right = generator/field.

Force on a Moving Charge & Worked Examples

$$F = qvB\sin\theta$$

force on a single charge $q$ moving at speed $v$ through field $B$

A current is just moving charge, so $F = BIL$ and $F = qvB$ are the same physics at different scales. A charge moving perpendicular to B follows a circular path (the force is always perpendicular to velocity).

Example 1 — force on a wire

A 0.25 m wire carries 3.0 A perpendicular to a 0.40 T field. Find the force.

$F = BIL = 0.40\times3.0\times0.25 = \mathbf{0.30\ \text{N}}$

Example 2 — force on a charge

An electron ($q = 1.6\times10^{-19}$ C) moves at $2.0\times10^{6}$ m s⁻¹ perpendicular to a 0.50 T field. Find the force.

$F = qvB = (1.6\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^{6})(0.50) = \mathbf{1.6\times10^{-13}\ \text{N}}$

IB-Style Question Set 7/10

Describe [2]

(a) Describe the magnetic field produced by a current in a long straight wire and state how its direction can be found.

The field consists of concentric circles centred on the wire, in planes perpendicular to it, becoming weaker with distance [1]. Its direction is found by the right-hand grip rule: point the right thumb in the direction of conventional current, and the curled fingers give the direction of the circular field lines [1].
Calculate [3]

(b) A straight wire of length 0.15 m carries a current of 4.0 A at 90° to a uniform magnetic field of 0.60 T. Calculate the force on the wire.

$F = BIL\sin\theta$ with $\theta = 90°$ so $\sin\theta = 1$ [1]
$F = 0.60\times4.0\times0.15$ [1]
$= \mathbf{0.36\ \text{N}}$ [1]
Explain [2]

(c) State the effect on the force if the wire in (b) is rotated so that it lies parallel to the magnetic field, and explain why.

The force becomes zero [1]. Since $F = BIL\sin\theta$ and the angle between current and field is now 0°, $\sin 0° = 0$, so no force acts — a wire parallel to the field experiences no motor-effect force [1].
Apply [3]

(d) Describe two ways to increase the strength of an electromagnet made from a solenoid, and name a practical application.

Increase the current through the coil, and increase the number of turns per unit length [1]; also inserting a soft-iron core greatly strengthens the field [1]. A practical application is a scrapyard crane (or relay/electric bell), where the electromagnet can be switched on to lift steel and off to release it [1].

Key Takeaways

  • A current makes a magnetic field: concentric circles around a straight wire (right-hand grip rule).
  • Solenoid = electromagnet with a uniform internal field; strengthen with more current, more turns, or an iron core.
  • Motor effect: $F = BIL\sin\theta$; maximum when perpendicular, zero when parallel.
  • Fleming's left-hand rule gives the force direction (thumb = force, first = field, second = current).
  • Moving charge: $F = qvB\sin\theta$ — same physics; perpendicular motion gives circular paths.
  • The tesla (T) is defined via $F = BIL$: 1 N on 1 m of wire carrying 1 A at 90°.
