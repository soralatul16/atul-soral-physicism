Module 1 — What IB Expects From a Physics Teacher
Start Here

Atul, you have 7 years of IB experience and deep physics knowledge. This course isn't about content — it's about the HABIT shift from "I explain, they copy" to "they investigate, I guide." We start from zero, no assumptions. Every idea explained fully.

Concept The One Sentence That Changes Everything

Here is the entire difference between CBSE and IB teaching, in one sentence:

In CBSE, the teacher does the thinking and students record it. In IB, students do the thinking and the teacher guides it.

That's it. Everything else — inquiry, ATL, the 5E model, notebooks, TOK — flows from this one shift. When you lecture and students copy, YOUR brain is active and theirs is passive. When students investigate and you facilitate, THEIR brains are active. IB believes learning happens in the active brain, not the passive one.

Why this matters for you specifically: Your HOS said you have capability but aren't using it. What they mean is: you're doing the thinking that your students should be doing. You're SO good at physics that you explain everything beautifully — and rob students of the struggle that creates real understanding.

Concept The 8 Things IB Expects From You

  • 1. Facilitator, not lecturer. Your job is to design experiences where students discover, not to deliver information. You talk less; they think more.
  • 2. Student agency. Students own their learning. They ask questions, design investigations, and reach conclusions. You don't hand them conclusions.
  • 3. Concept-driven teaching. Teach through big ideas (energy, systems, interactions, fields) not through chapters. "Chapter 4: Work and Energy" is CBSE. "How is energy conserved across systems?" is IB.
  • 4. The inquiry cycle. Every unit follows: question → predict → investigate → analyse → conclude → reflect. Not: explain → practise → test.
  • 5. The 3-minute rule. Never talk continuously for more than 3 minutes. After 3 minutes of teacher talk, students disengage. Break it with a question, a prediction, an activity.
  • 6. Formative assessment in real time. Check understanding DURING the lesson (exit tickets, thumbs up/down, quick questions), not just in the exam AFTER.
  • 7. ATL skills planned, not accidental. Decide before the lesson: which thinking/research/communication skill will this develop? Then design for it.
  • 8. The IB mindset. CBSE: "I taught it, so they should know it." IB: "They investigated it, so they understand it."

Example Facilitator vs Lecturer (Momentum)

Lecturer (CBSE)
"Today we'll learn momentum. Momentum p = mv. It's the quantity of motion. Let me derive the relation between KE and momentum. Copy this. Now solve these 5 problems."
Facilitator (IB)
"Here are two trolleys of different mass and a barrier. Predict: which will be harder to stop, and why? Now test it. What did you find? What pattern connects mass, speed, and 'hardness to stop'? Let's name what you discovered."

Same physics. Same 40 minutes. But in the second version, students DISCOVER momentum. You didn't tell them p=mv — they found that mass and velocity both matter, and THEN you gave the discovery its name.

Your Turn Rewrite a Lecturer Moment

Think of a topic you teach. Write how you currently OPEN that lesson (the lecturer way). Then rewrite it as a facilitator — turning your opening into a question or investigation instead of an explanation.

Topic: Refraction.
CURRENT (lecturer): "Today we'll study refraction. Light bends when it moves between media because speed changes. Snell's law is n1 sinθ1 = n2 sinθ2. Copy this."

REWRITTEN (facilitator): "Look at this pencil in a glass of water — why does it look broken? Predict what happens if I use oil instead of water. Let's investigate how much light bends in different liquids and see if YOU can find the pattern that connects them."

Self-Check Before Your Next Lesson

Carry this question into your next class: "In this lesson, whose brain is doing the thinking — mine or theirs?" If the answer is "mine," you're lecturing. If it's "theirs," you're facilitating. That single question, asked honestly, will transform your teaching faster than any workshop.

Module 2

You told me you create ManageBac plans with AI but don't understand the fields. Let's fix that. By the end of this module, you'll understand every field so deeply that when AI generates a plan, you can read it and say "this is weak because..." You become the editor, not the accepter.

Concept What a Unit Planner Actually Is

A ManageBac unit planner is NOT a lesson plan. It's the conceptual blueprint for an entire unit (usually 4-6 weeks). Think of it like the design document for a whole experiment series, not a single experiment. Let's go through every field, one by one, explaining exactly what each word means and what goes in it.

Concept Field 1: Key Concept

What the word means: A key concept is a BIG, transferable idea that applies across many subjects — not just physics. MYP has 16 of them (Change, Systems, Relationships, Interactions, Models, etc.).

What goes in this field: ONE key concept that your unit is fundamentally about. For a unit on forces and motion, you might choose "Relationships" (how force, mass, and acceleration relate) or "Systems" (how objects interact within a system).

How to choose: Ask "What is the deepest, most transferable idea my unit is really about?" Not the topic (forces) — the CONCEPT (relationships, systems, change).
Weak choice
Key Concept: "Forces" (this is a TOPIC, not a concept — it doesn't transfer to history or art)
Strong choice
Key Concept: "Relationships" (transfers everywhere — the relationship between variables in physics, characters in literature, events in history)

Concept Field 2: Related Concepts

What the word means: Related concepts are subject-specific ideas that add depth. Unlike key concepts (shared across subjects), these belong to physics. MYP Sciences related concepts include: Energy, Models, Movement, Patterns, Transformation, Environment, Form, Function, Interaction, Balance, Consequences, Evidence, Development.

What goes in this field: 2-3 related concepts that deepen your key concept. For a forces unit under "Relationships," you might add Movement and Interaction.

The formula: Key concept (broad) + Related concepts (physics-specific) = the conceptual spine of your unit.

Concept Field 3: Global Context

What the word means: A global context makes learning MEANINGFUL by connecting it to the real world. MYP has 6: (1) Identities and relationships, (2) Orientation in space and time, (3) Personal and cultural expression, (4) Scientific and technical innovation, (5) Globalization and sustainability, (6) Fairness and development.

What goes in this field: ONE global context that frames your unit's real-world relevance. A forces/motion unit fits naturally under "Scientific and technical innovation" (how understanding forces enables engineering) or "Orientation in space and time" (motion, navigation, GPS).

Concept Field 4: Statement of Inquiry (SOI) — the hardest one

What the word means: The SOI is a single sentence that combines your key concept + related concepts + global context into a meaningful, DEBATABLE statement. It's the thesis of your whole unit. Students investigate whether it's true.

What goes in this field: A statement that is conceptual (not a topic) and contestable (not an obvious fact).

Weak SOI
"Students will learn about forces and motion." (This is a topic description, not a statement of inquiry. It's not debatable.)
Strong SOI
"The relationships between interacting forces determine how objects move, enabling the technical innovations that shape modern life." (Conceptual + contestable + connects to global context)
The recipe: [Key concept] + [related concepts] + [global context] = SOI. Example: Relationships + Movement/Interaction + Scientific innovation = the strong SOI above.

Concept Fields 5-6: Inquiry Questions & ATL Skills

Inquiry Questions (3 types):

  • Factual (recall): "What is Newton's second law?"
  • Conceptual (relationships): "How do force and mass together determine acceleration?"
  • Debatable (multiple views): "Should self-driving cars prioritise passenger or pedestrian safety when braking forces are limited?"

Every unit needs all three levels. This scaffolds students from knowing → understanding → evaluating.

ATL Skills: Choose which specific skill your unit develops. For a forces unit with lab work, you might choose "Thinking: transfer" (applying force concepts to new situations) and "Research: information literacy" (evaluating data reliability). Then you must actually PLAN activities that develop these — not just write them in the box.

Your Turn Build a Statement of Inquiry

Pick a physics unit you teach. Write its Key Concept, Related Concepts, Global Context, then combine them into a Statement of Inquiry. Use the recipe.

Unit: Energy
Key Concept: Systems
Related Concepts: Energy, Transformation
Global Context: Globalization and sustainability
Statement of Inquiry: "Energy transformation within systems determines how societies innovate to address sustainability challenges."

Notice: this SOI is conceptual (systems, transformation), debatable (do all societies innovate the same way?), and connected to a real-world global context (sustainability).

Self-Check The AI Test

You'll know you've mastered ManageBac when you can look at any AI-generated plan and immediately spot the weak field. When you can say "this SOI is just a topic, not a statement of inquiry" — you've become the editor, not the accepter. That's the goal.

Module 3

You said you've used the 5E model but aren't sure you're really doing it. Let's learn it properly — as if for the first time. By the end, you'll be able to plan any 40-minute physics lesson using 5E, and know exactly when to talk and when to shut up.

Concept The 5E Model, Explained Simply

5E is a lesson structure with five phases. The magic is the ORDER — especially that EXPLAIN comes AFTER students explore, not before.

PhaseWhat it meansWho's active
EngageHook curiosity with a phenomenon, question, or demo. NOT "today we'll learn X."Students (curious)
ExploreStudents investigate — predict, experiment, observe. Teacher watches, doesn't explain.Students (investigating)
ExplainNOW the teacher connects student discoveries to formal physics. Formulas go here, not earlier.Teacher (guiding)
ElaborateApply to new contexts. Harder problems, "what if" scenarios, real-world connections.Students (applying)
EvaluateCheck understanding. Exit ticket, reflection, quick question.Both
The #1 mistake: Explaining BEFORE students explore. If you explain first, there's nothing left to discover — you've turned Explore into "verify what teacher said." Explain must come AFTER Explore.

Example A Complete 5E Lesson: Momentum (40 min)

TimePhaseWhat happens
0-5EngageShow Newton's cradle. "What do you notice? Write 2 observations and 1 question."
5-12Explore"Predict: heavy trolley vs light trolley hitting a barrier — which is harder to stop? Now test it with the trolleys. Record what you find."
12-25Explore + ExplainStudents collect collision data on PASCO. "Calculate mv before and after. What do you notice?" THEN you name it: "You discovered momentum, p=mv, and its conservation."
25-35Elaborate"A 2000kg truck at 2m/s vs a 70kg cyclist at 10m/s — which has more momentum? What about a bullet?"
35-40EvaluateExit ticket: "Explain in your own words why a small bullet can be so dangerous, using today's concept."

Notice: you don't say "p=mv" until minute 20. Students DISCOVER that mass and velocity both matter, THEN you give it a name and a formula. That's the 5E order working.

Reality Check Your Improvisation

You told me things "pop into your head" during class and you add them. That's a GIFT — the best IB teachers improvise based on student responses. The 5E structure doesn't kill improvisation; it gives it a home. You improvise WITHIN Explore (following student curiosity) and WITHIN Elaborate (adding examples). The structure keeps your improvisation purposeful instead of random.

Your Turn Plan a 5E Lesson

Pick a physics topic. Plan a 40-minute lesson using all five phases. Write what happens in each phase and, crucially, mark WHO is active (you or students).

Topic: Refraction
ENGAGE (0-5): Pencil-in-water illusion. "Why does it look broken? Write a prediction." [Students]
EXPLORE (5-15): Students shine a laser into water/oil/glass blocks, measure the bending angle. [Students]
EXPLAIN (15-25): "You found denser media bend light more. This is refraction — here's Snell's law connecting your data." [Teacher]
ELABORATE (25-35): "Why do stars twinkle? Why do mirages form? Apply refraction." [Students]
EVALUATE (35-40): Exit ticket: "Explain why a straw looks bent, using refraction." [Both]

Self-Check The Order Test

After planning any lesson, ask: "Does EXPLAIN come after EXPLORE?" If you're explaining the concept before students investigate it, flip the order. Let them discover first. That single fix converts most CBSE lessons to IB lessons.
