MYP Physics eAssessment

This is not a content review. This is a mark-saving strategy guide. The exam has 3 tasks, 100 marks, and a fixed structure. If you know the structure and follow these rules, you protect every mark you deserve. This year's Global Context: Identities & Relationships.

Task 1: Knowing & Understanding (Criterion A)

1. Read the command term

Critical

The command term tells you exactly how much to write. Students lose marks by writing too much for "State" or too little for "Explain."

Command termWhat it meansHow much to write
State / IdentifyGive the answer, no explanation1 sentence or a word
DescribeSay what happens, step by step2-3 sentences
ExplainSay what happens AND why3-4 sentences with "because"
CalculateShow formula, substitution, answer3 lines minimum
AnalyseBreak it down, find patterns4-5 sentences with data references
EvaluateWeigh up both sides, then judgeA short paragraph with a conclusion

2. Calculations: the 3-line rule

Easy marks

Every calculation must have 3 lines. Each line can earn a mark independently.

  • Line 1: Write the formula — e.g. F = ma
  • Line 2: Substitute the numbers — e.g. F = 5 × 3
  • Line 3: Answer with correct units and sig figs — e.g. F = 15 N

DO: Round to 2 or more significant figures. Include units on the final answer. Write "seen or implied" working even if you can do it in your head.

DON'T: Write just the answer. Skip units. Round to 1 sig fig. Use calculator notation like 1.5E4 instead of 1.5 × 10&sup4;.

3. Multi-step calculations

If your first step is wrong, you can still get marks for correct method in the next step. This is called ECF (Error Carried Forward). But only if you show your working clearly.

Example: Calculate power if work = 500 J and time = 8 s, then find current if voltage = 230 V.

  • Step 1: P = W/t = 500/8 = 62.5 W ← even if this is wrong...
  • Step 2: I = P/V = 62.5/230 = 0.27 A ← ...you get the method mark here

4. "Scientifically supported judgment" questions

High marks

These appear at the end of Task 1. You'll be given data, a claim, or experimental results and asked to judge them.

DO: Reference specific data from the question. Use phrases like "The data shows that..." or "This value of 4.5 suggests..." State whether the claim is valid or not, with evidence.

DON'T: Give a vague opinion. Ignore the data. Just say "I agree" without reasoning.

Task 2: Investigation Skills (Criteria B + C)

5. Research question

1 mark

Use this exact format: "What is the relationship between [IV] and [DV]?" or "How does [IV] affect [DV]?"

DO: Name both variables clearly. Make it testable.

DON'T: Write "How does changing things affect the experiment?" or use vague words.

Example: "What is the relationship between the length of a wire and its resistance?"

6. Hypothesis

2-4 marks

Three parts, three marks:

  • Mark 1: "If [IV] increases, then [DV] will increase/decrease"
  • Mark 2: "because [scientific reason]"
  • Mark 3: Scientific reasoning is correct and specific (name the law, principle, or mechanism)

Example: "If the length of the wire increases, then the resistance will increase, because electrons have to travel a further distance and collide with more ions, so there is more opposition to the flow of current."

DON'T: Write just a prediction without "because." Your hypothesis does not need to be correct to earn marks — it needs to be testable and scientifically reasoned.

7. Variables

3 marks

Use these exact words:

  • Independent variable (IV): the one YOU change — name it specifically
  • Dependent variable (DV): the one YOU measure — name it specifically
  • Controlled variables (CV): everything you keep the same — list at least two specific ones

DON'T: Say "keep everything else the same" — name the specific things you're controlling. Don't accept "temperature" as a CV unless it's actually relevant. Don't list calculated values as DV (e.g., don't say "resistance" if you're measuring voltage and current).

8. Method design (THE BIG ONE)

14-16 marks

This single question is worth more than any other. The examiner checks 6 things. Hit all 6:

What they checkWhat to writeCommon mistake
Variables (V)State IV, DV, and justify one CVNot justifying why a CV matters
Hypothesis (H)If-then + scientific reasoningNo "because" or wrong science
Equipment (E)Name tools to measure IV AND DVForgetting the measuring tool for one variable
Method (M)Step-by-step, another student could follow itToo vague — "do the experiment and record results"
Data (D)At least 5 different values of IV, repeat 3 times each, plan to averageOnly 3 values, no repeats, no average
Safety (S)One specific hazard + one specific precaution"Be careful" or "wear goggles" without linking to a hazard

The golden rule: Could a stranger read your method and do the experiment exactly? If not, add more detail.

9. Data processing (Criterion C)

20-25 marks

Tables:

  • Headers MUST include units — "Mass / kg" not just "Mass"
  • Data in ascending or descending order
  • Consistent decimal places in each column
  • Units in the header ONLY, never repeated in cells

Graphs:

  • Label both axes with quantity AND unit — "Force / N"
  • Use even scale increments (0, 10, 20, 30... not 0, 7, 15, 28)
  • Plot points accurately (±1 grid line)
  • Line of best fit: roughly equal points above and below the line. Don't connect dot-to-dot.

10. Interpreting results

Use this 3-sentence pattern:

  • Sentence 1: "As [IV] increases, [DV] increases/decreases."
  • Sentence 2: "The relationship is proportional / inversely proportional / non-linear because [evidence from data]."
  • Sentence 3: "This is because [scientific reasoning]."

DON'T: Just say "they are related." Always specify the direction (increase/decrease) and the type of relationship.

11. Evaluating the hypothesis

3 marks

Three marks, three steps:

  • Mark 1: Reference specific data — "At 20 cm, the bounce height was 15 cm, and at 40 cm it was 25 cm"
  • Mark 2: State whether the data supports the hypothesis with reasoning — "The data shows that doubling the drop height does not double the bounce height"
  • Mark 3: Conclusion — "Therefore the hypothesis is not fully supported" (only awarded if marks 1 and 2 are earned)

DON'T: Just say "my hypothesis was correct" without data evidence.

12. Errors and improvements

For errors: Name a specific error + explain how it affects results.

  • Good: "The wire may have heated up, which would increase resistance, making the calculated values unreliable."
  • Bad: "There were some errors in the experiment."

For improvements: Don't just say "repeat more times." Instead suggest something specific:

  • "Use a data logger instead of a stopwatch to reduce reaction time errors."
  • "Allow the wire to cool between readings to prevent resistance changing due to temperature."

Task 3: Applying Science (Criterion D)

13. The reflection question structure

9-15 marks

This is worth up to 15 marks and most students throw them away. The examiner uses a grid with rows. You must hit every row.

This year's GC is Identities & Relationships. Expect questions about how physics affects individuals, their health, identity, lifestyle choices, or relationships with technology and the environment.

The grid always looks like this:

RowFor 1 markFor 2 marksFor 3 marksFor 4 marks
Theme A (e.g., benefits)A statement2 statements OR 1 with support2 statements, 1 supported2 statements, both supported
Theme B (e.g., limitations)A statement2 statements OR 1 with support2 statements, 1 supported2 statements, both supported
ConclusionA simple opinionAn appraisal linking to your arguments

14. How to score maximum on Criterion D

Game changer

Follow this recipe for each theme row:

  • Statement 1: "[Technology/application] provides [benefit/limitation]."
  • Support for Statement 1: "This is because..." or "For example..." with a specific detail.
  • Statement 2: "Additionally, [second benefit/limitation]."
  • Support for Statement 2: Another specific reason or example.

For the conclusion:

  • DON'T just write "I think it is good." That's 1 mark.
  • DO write: "Overall, while [technology] offers [benefit A and B], the [limitations] suggest that [balanced judgment]. Therefore, [opinion with justification]." That's 2 marks.

The conclusion MUST reference arguments you already made. An opinion disconnected from your earlier points earns only 1 mark.

15. Identities & Relationships examples

The factor you're asked about will be one of these. Have a sentence ready for each:

FactorExample sentence you could adapt
Social"This technology changes how individuals interact with their community by..."
Ethical"It raises ethical questions about whether it is right to... because..."
Economic"The cost of implementing this means that... which affects individuals who..."
Environmental"The environmental impact on communities includes... leading to..."
Health"This directly affects individuals' health because... resulting in..."
Cultural"Different cultures may respond differently to this because..."

Every mark you lose on structure is a mark you knew the physics for.

Universal Rules: Apply to Every Question

16. The 5 things that silently lose marks

Memorize
  • Missing units. Every final numerical answer needs a unit. Every single one. N, m, s, kg, J, W, Pa, V, A, Ω, Hz.
  • Wrong sig figs. Round to 2 or more significant figures unless told otherwise. Never write 16.6666666.
  • Contradictions. If you write two things that contradict each other, the examiner awards 0 for that point — even if one of them was correct. Cross out mistakes clearly.
  • Vague language. "It increases" — what increases? "They are different" — how? Always name the specific quantity.
  • Blank responses. Write something. An attempt at a method, even incomplete, can earn partial marks. A blank earns nothing.

17. Time management

  • Task 1 (25 marks): ~30 minutes
  • Task 2 (50 marks): ~60 minutes — spend the most time here, especially on the method design
  • Task 3 (25 marks): ~25 minutes
  • Review: ~5 minutes — check units, sig figs, blank answers

If you're stuck: Write the formula. Even if you can't solve it, the formula alone can earn 1 mark. Then move on.

18. The formula sheet is your friend

You get a formula sheet on screen. Don't waste time memorizing formulas. Instead, practice:

  • Rearranging formulas — if V = IR, then I = V/R and R = V/I
  • Choosing the right formula for the situation
  • Substituting correctly (watch your units — convert km to m, g to kg, mA to A, kW to W)

Quick Reference Card

Before you submit

Run through this 30-second checklist on every answer:

  • □ Did I answer what the command term asked? (State ≠ Explain)
  • □ Do my calculations show formula → substitution → answer?
  • □ Does every number have a unit?
  • □ Is my final answer rounded to 2+ sig figs?
  • □ For "Evaluate" / "Discuss": did I give BOTH sides?
  • □ For the method: did I mention at least 5 values, 3 repeats, and averaging?
  • □ For the conclusion: did I reference my earlier arguments?
  • □ Did I leave any response box completely blank? (Write something.)
