Do's & Don'tsCriterion-by-Criterion Mark Protection Guide

Four criteria, four sets of rules. Each criterion is graded differently. The mistakes that lose you marks in Criterion A are completely different from Criterion D. Learn the rules for each, and you protect marks you already know the physics for. GC this year: Identities & Relationships.
Criterion A: Knowing & Understanding
✓ DO
25 marks
  • Match answer length to the command term. "State" = one line. "Explain" = 3-4 lines with "because."
  • Show ALL working: formula → substitution → answer with units. Each step earns a mark independently.
  • Round to 2 or more significant figures. Write 62.5, not 62.5000000.
  • Always include units: N, J, W, m/s, kg, Pa, V, A, Ω, Hz.
  • For multi-step calculations, show every intermediate step. Wrong first answer + correct method on step 2 = ECF marks.
  • When asked to "Analyse" or "Evaluate," reference specific numbers: "The value of 4.5 N shows that..."
  • Convert units before substituting: km → m, g → kg, mA → A, kW → W.
  • Scientific notation: 1.5 × 10&sup4; not 1.5E4.
✗ DON'T
  • Don't write just the final answer. A wrong answer with clear working earns more than a right answer without.
  • Don't write a paragraph for "State." You waste time and risk contradicting yourself.
  • Don't leave out units. "15" ≠ "15 N." Unit mark is independent.
  • Don't round to 1 sig fig. "60" when the answer is 62.5 loses the mark.
  • Don't use calculator notation: 1.5E4, 6.67e-11.
  • Don't contradict yourself. Two conflicting statements = 0, even if one was correct.
  • Don't skip questions. Formula alone = 1 mark. Blank = 0.
  • Don't confuse mass (kg) and weight (N). Weight = mass × g.
Example

Q: Calculate the kinetic energy of a 1200 kg car at 25 m/s.

WRONG (1 mark): 375000

RIGHT (3 marks):
Eₖ = ½mv²
Eₖ = ½ × 1200 × 25²
Eₖ = 375,000 J = 375 kJ

Criterion B: Inquiring & Designing
✓ DO
~25 marks
  • RQ format: "What is the relationship between [IV] and [DV]?" or "How does [IV] affect [DV]?"
  • Hypothesis in three parts: "If [IV] increases, then [DV] will increase/decrease, because [scientific reason]."
  • Use exact terms: "Independent variable," "Dependent variable," "Controlled variable."
  • List at least TWO specific controlled variables and explain WHY each matters.
  • Method: at least 5 different values of the IV.
  • State: repeat each measurement 3 times and calculate an average.
  • Name actual equipment: "metre ruler," "digital ammeter," "stopwatch."
  • Method must be followable by a stranger step-by-step.
  • ONE specific safety precaution linked to a specific hazard.
✗ DON'T
  • Don't write a hypothesis without "because." Without reasoning = 1 mark instead of 3-4.
  • Don't say "keep everything else the same." Name specific controlled variables.
  • Don't list calculated values as DV. If you measure V and I, DV is current, not resistance.
  • Don't write "do the experiment and record results." This earns almost nothing.
  • Don't test only 3 values. The examiner expects at least 5.
  • Don't forget repeats. "3 measurements, calculate the mean" earns marks.
  • Don't write "wear goggles" as safety unless linked to a specific hazard in YOUR experiment.
  • Don't suggest "increase the number of trials" as an improvement. Not accepted.
Example

WRONG: "The longer the wire, the more resistance."

RIGHT: "If the length of the wire increases, then the resistance will increase, because electrons have to travel a greater distance through the metal lattice and collide with more ions, increasing opposition to current flow."

Criterion C: Processing & Evaluating
✓ DO
~25 marks

Tables:

  • Units in header only: "Mass / kg" — never in cells.
  • Data in ascending or descending order.
  • Consistent decimal places within each column.

Graphs:

  • Label both axes: quantity AND unit. "Force / N."
  • Even scale: 0, 10, 20, 30... not 0, 7, 15, 28.
  • Line of best fit: equal points above and below. Not dot-to-dot.

Interpreting:

  • "As [IV] increases, [DV] increases/decreases."
  • Name the relationship: proportional, inversely proportional, or non-linear.
  • Quote specific data: "At 20 cm it was 15 N, at 40 cm it was 30 N."

Evaluating:

  • Hypothesis: quote data → match check → conclusion. Three steps.
  • Method: name a specific error + explain its effect.
  • Improvements: specific suggestion + why it helps.
✗ DON'T
  • Don't put units in every cell. Header once only.
  • Don't scramble data in random order.
  • Don't connect dot-to-dot. Draw a smooth best-fit line.
  • Don't leave axes without labels or units.
  • Don't say "they are related." Specify HOW.
  • Don't evaluate with "my hypothesis was correct" alone. Quote data.
  • Don't say "there were some errors." Name one + its effect.
  • Don't suggest "do more repeats." Not accepted.
  • Don't confuse accuracy (close to true value) with reliability (consistent).
Example

WRONG: "The hypothesis was correct because the results showed it."

RIGHT: "At 20 cm drop height, bounce was 12 cm; at 40 cm, bounce was 19 cm. Doubling drop height did not double bounce height, so the relationship is not proportional. The hypothesis is not supported."

Criterion D: Reflecting on the Impacts of Science
✓ DO
25 marks
  • Structure in clear paragraphs. Examiner grades each theme row separately.
  • Per theme: TWO points, each SUPPORTED. Two supported = max marks.
  • Use the given factor. "Ethical" = connect to ethics. "Economic" = costs, jobs.
  • Always give BOTH positive AND negative. One-sided = half marks max.
  • Conclusion must reference your earlier arguments.
  • Use scientific language. "Electromagnetic radiation" not "rays."
  • Link to GC: Identities & Relationships — individuals, identity, health, lifestyle.
✗ DON'T
  • Don't write only benefits OR only limitations. Must have both.
  • Don't make unsupported statements. "Bad for environment" = 1 mark. With reasoning = 2-3.
  • Don't write "I think it is good" as conclusion. Weigh both sides.
  • Don't ignore the factor. Social question answered with economics doesn't count.
  • Don't repeat the same point differently. "Costs a lot" and "is expensive" = one point.
  • Don't write generically. Name the specific technology from the question.
  • Don't disconnect conclusion from arguments.
✍ The Winning Template

Paragraph 1 (Benefits):
"One benefit of [technology] is [Point 1]. This is significant because [support]."
"Additionally, [Point 2]. For instance, [example]."

Paragraph 2 (Limitations):
"However, a limitation is [Point 1]. This is because [support]."
"Furthermore, [Point 2], which means [consequence]."

Paragraph 3 (Conclusion):
"Overall, while [technology] offers [benefits], the [limitations] suggest [judgment]. I believe [opinion] because [reference to arguments]."

This template = 8-10 marks on any D question.

Example

WRONG: "Location tracking is good because it helps people but bad because of privacy. I think it is good."

RIGHT: "One social benefit of location-tracking technology is that it allows parents to monitor their children's safety. Additionally, emergency services can locate individuals more quickly during accidents, saving lives.

However, continuous tracking may violate privacy rights, as movements are recorded without awareness. Furthermore, collected data could be sold to third parties for targeted advertising.

Overall, while location tracking offers safety benefits, the ethical risks to privacy are significant. I believe it should be opt-in only, as benefits are most meaningful when individuals retain control over their data."

The exam doesn't test what you know. It tests whether you can show what you know in the format the examiner is looking for.
30 Second Pre-Submit Checklist

Criterion A Knowing

  • Every calculation: formula → substitution → answer
  • Every answer has a unit
  • Rounded to 2+ sig figs
  • "Evaluate" answers reference specific data

Criterion B Designing

  • RQ names both IV and DV
  • Hypothesis: "If... then... because [science]"
  • Method: 5 values, 3 repeats, average, named equipment, 1 safety

Criterion C Processing

  • Table: units in headers, data ordered, consistent decimals
  • Graph: labeled axes, even scale, line of best fit
  • Hypothesis evaluation cites specific data
  • Error named specifically + its effect

Criterion D Reflecting

  • Both positives AND negatives
  • Each point supported with evidence
  • Correct factor addressed
  • Conclusion references earlier arguments

Final sweep All

  • No blank response boxes (something always beats nothing)
  • No contradictions (cross out mistakes)
  • Command term matched: State = 1 line, Explain = 3-4 lines
