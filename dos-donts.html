Do's & Don'ts
by Criterion

Four criteria, four sets of rules. Each criterion is graded differently. The mistakes that lose you marks in Criterion A are completely different from the ones in Criterion D. Learn the rules for each, and you protect marks you already know the physics for. GC this year: Identities & Relationships.

Criterion A: Knowing & Understanding

DO

25 marks
  • Match your answer length to the command term. "State" = one line. "Explain" = 3-4 lines with "because."
  • Show ALL working in calculations: formula → substitution → answer with units. Each step can earn a mark independently.
  • Round final answers to 2 or more significant figures. Write 62.5, not 62.5000000.
  • Always include units on your final answer: N, J, W, m/s, kg, Pa, V, A, Ω, Hz.
  • For multi-step calculations, show every intermediate step clearly. If your first answer is wrong, you still earn marks on later steps through ECF (Error Carried Forward).
  • When asked to "Analyse" or "Evaluate" information, reference specific numbers from the data given. "The value of 4.5 N shows that..." not "The data shows that..."
  • Convert units before substituting: km → m, g → kg, mA → A, kW → W, minutes → seconds.
  • Use scientific notation correctly: 1.5 × 10&sup4; not 1.5E4.

DON'T

  • Don't write just the final answer without working. A correct number with no formula shown often earns fewer marks than a wrong answer with clear working.
  • Don't write a paragraph when the question says "State." You waste time and you can accidentally contradict yourself.
  • Don't leave out units. "15" is not the same as "15 N." The unit mark is awarded independently.
  • Don't round to 1 significant figure. "60" when the answer is 62.5 loses the mark.
  • Don't use calculator display notation: 1.5E4, 6.67e-11. Write 1.5 × 10&sup4; properly.
  • Don't contradict yourself. If you write two statements that conflict, the examiner awards 0 for that point, even if one was correct. Cross out mistakes clearly instead.
  • Don't skip a question entirely. Write the formula even if you can't solve it. The formula alone earns 1 mark.
  • Don't confuse mass and weight. Mass is in kg. Weight is in N. Weight = mass × g.

Example

Question: Calculate the kinetic energy of a 1200 kg car travelling at 25 m/s.

WRONG (1 mark max): 375000

RIGHT (3 marks):
Eₖ = ½mv²
Eₖ = ½ × 1200 × 25²
Eₖ = 375000 J = 375 kJ

Criterion B: Inquiring & Designing

DO

~25 marks
  • Write the research question in this format: "What is the relationship between [IV] and [DV]?" or "How does [IV] affect [DV]?"
  • Write the hypothesis in three parts: "If [IV] increases, then [DV] will increase/decrease, because [scientific reason]." Each part earns a separate mark.
  • Name variables using the exact words: "Independent variable," "Dependent variable," "Controlled variable." The examiner looks for these terms.
  • List at least TWO specific controlled variables and explain WHY each one matters.
  • In the method: describe at least 5 different values of the IV you will test.
  • State that you will repeat each measurement at least 3 times and calculate an average.
  • Name the actual equipment: "metre ruler," "digital ammeter," "stopwatch," "electronic balance." Not just "measuring tool."
  • Write the method so a stranger could follow it step-by-step without asking you questions.
  • Include ONE specific safety precaution linked to a specific hazard. "Use tongs when handling hot beakers to prevent burns" — not just "be careful."

DON'T

  • Don't write a hypothesis without "because." A prediction without reasoning earns only 1 mark instead of 3-4.
  • Don't say "keep everything else the same" for controlled variables. Name the specific things you control.
  • Don't accept calculated values as your DV. If you measure voltage and current, your DV is current, not resistance (which you calculate).
  • Don't write "do the experiment and record results" as your method. This earns almost nothing.
  • Don't test only 3 values of the IV. The examiner expects at least 5.
  • Don't forget repeats. "Take 3 measurements at each value and calculate the mean" earns marks. Doing it once doesn't.
  • Don't write "wear goggles" or "wear a lab coat" as safety unless you link it to a specific hazard in YOUR experiment.
  • Don't say "increase the number of trials" as an improvement. This is not accepted.
  • Don't confuse your RQ with your hypothesis. The RQ is a question. The hypothesis is a testable prediction with reasoning.

Example

WRONG hypothesis: "The longer the wire, the more resistance."

RIGHT hypothesis: "If the length of the wire increases, then the resistance will increase, because the electrons have to travel a greater distance through the metal lattice and collide with more ions, which increases opposition to the flow of current."

Criterion C: Processing & Evaluating

DO

~25 marks

Tables:

  • Put units in the column header only: "Mass / kg" — never repeat units in each cell.
  • Arrange data in ascending or descending order of the IV.
  • Use consistent decimal places within each column.
  • If you convert units, do it for the entire column, not just one value.

Graphs:

  • Label both axes: quantity AND unit. "Force / N" not just "Force."
  • Use even scale increments: 0, 10, 20, 30... not 0, 7, 15, 28.
  • Plot every point accurately. Examiners allow ±1 grid square.
  • Draw a line of best fit with roughly equal numbers of points above and below the line. Don't connect dot-to-dot.

Interpreting:

  • Always state the direction: "As [IV] increases, [DV] increases/decreases."
  • State the type of relationship: proportional, inversely proportional, or non-linear.
  • Back it up with specific data points: "At 20 cm the value was 15 N, at 40 cm it was 30 N, showing a proportional relationship."

Evaluating:

  • When evaluating the hypothesis: quote data, state whether it matches, give a conclusion. Three steps, three marks.
  • When evaluating the method: name a specific source of error AND explain how it affected the results.
  • When suggesting improvements: describe something specific and explain why it would help.

DON'T

  • Don't put units in every cell of a table. They go in the header once.
  • Don't scramble your data in random order. Ascending or descending only.
  • Don't connect points dot-to-dot on a graph. Draw a smooth line or curve of best fit.
  • Don't leave axes without labels or units. Both are required for the mark.
  • Don't say "they are related" without specifying HOW: proportional? Inversely proportional? Non-linear?
  • Don't evaluate the hypothesis with just "my hypothesis was correct." You must reference specific data.
  • Don't say "there were some errors" without naming a specific one and its effect.
  • Don't suggest "do more repeats" as an improvement. The examiner does not accept this.
  • Don't confuse accuracy with reliability. Accuracy = close to the true value. Reliability = results are consistent when repeated.
  • Don't round values in your data table inconsistently: if one cell says 3.45, don't put 3.5 in the next.

Example

WRONG evaluation: "The hypothesis was correct because the results showed it."

RIGHT evaluation: "At a drop height of 20 cm, the bounce height was 12 cm, and at 40 cm it was 19 cm. Doubling the drop height did not double the bounce height, so the relationship is not proportional. Therefore, the hypothesis that bounce height is directly proportional to drop height is not supported by the data."

Criterion D: Reflecting on the Impacts of Science

DO

25 marks
  • Structure your long answer in clear paragraphs. The examiner grades each theme (benefits, limitations, conclusion) as a separate row. Make each paragraph match a row.
  • For each theme: write TWO separate points, and SUPPORT each with a reason or example. Two supported points = maximum marks for that row.
  • Use the factor they give you. If they ask about "ethical implications," every point must connect to ethics. If they ask about "economic," talk about costs, jobs, investment.
  • Always give BOTH positive AND negative perspectives. One-sided answers cap your marks.
  • End with a concluding appraisal that references your earlier arguments. "Considering both the health benefits outlined above and the economic limitations discussed, I believe that... because..."
  • Use scientific language throughout. Use correct physics terms, not everyday language. "Electromagnetic radiation" not "rays."
  • Link your points back to the GC: Identities & Relationships. How does this technology/application affect individuals, their identity, health, lifestyle, or relationships?

DON'T

  • Don't write only benefits OR only limitations. You MUST have both. One-sided = half marks maximum.
  • Don't make unsupported statements. "It is bad for the environment" earns 1 mark. "It releases CO&sub2; during combustion, which contributes to the greenhouse effect and increases global temperatures" earns 2-3.
  • Don't write a one-sentence conclusion. "I think it is good" = 1 mark. A conclusion that weighs up both sides and justifies your position = 2 marks.
  • Don't ignore the factor. If the question asks about "social implications" and you write about economics, those points don't count for that row.
  • Don't use MCQ-style thinking for these questions. They require extended writing. Plan your structure before you start typing.
  • Don't repeat the same point in different words. The examiner counts distinct points. "It costs a lot" and "It is expensive" count as one point, not two.
  • Don't forget to link back to the specific application in the stimulus. Don't write generically about "technology" — name the specific technology from the question.
  • Don't disconnect your conclusion from your arguments. If your conclusion says something you didn't discuss in the body, it earns only 1 mark.

The winning template

Paragraph 1 (Theme A — e.g., benefits):

  • "One benefit of [technology] is [Point 1]. This is significant because [support/example]."
  • "Additionally, [Point 2]. For instance, [specific example or data]."

Paragraph 2 (Theme B — e.g., limitations or a second factor):

  • "However, a limitation is [Point 1]. This is because [support]."
  • "Furthermore, [Point 2], which means that [consequence]."

Paragraph 3 (Conclusion):

  • "Overall, while [technology] offers [summary of benefits], the [summary of limitations] suggest that [balanced judgment]. I believe [opinion] because [reference to strongest argument from above]."

This template alone, applied to any D question, earns 8-10 marks out of the row totals.

Example

WRONG: "Location tracking is good because it helps people but it is also bad because of privacy. I think it is good."

RIGHT: "One social benefit of location-tracking technology is that it allows parents to monitor the safety of their children, providing reassurance about their whereabouts. Additionally, emergency services can locate individuals more quickly during accidents, potentially saving lives.

However, an ethical concern is that continuous tracking may violate an individual's right to privacy, as their movements are constantly recorded without their explicit awareness. Furthermore, data collected by companies could be sold to third parties, leading to targeted advertising that manipulates consumer behaviour.

Overall, while location tracking offers clear safety benefits for individuals and families, the ethical risks to personal privacy and data security are significant. I believe that location tracking should be opt-in only, as the benefits are most meaningful when individuals retain control over their own data."

The exam doesn't test what you know. It tests whether you can show what you know in the format the examiner is looking for.

30 Second Pre-Submit Checklist

Criterion A

Knowing
  • □ Every calculation shows: formula → substitution → answer
  • □ Every final answer has a unit (N, J, W, m/s, V, A, Ω)
  • □ All values rounded to 2+ significant figures
  • □ Every "Evaluate" answer references specific data from the question

Criterion B

Designing
  • □ Research question names BOTH the IV and the DV
  • □ Hypothesis has "If... then... because [science]"
  • □ Method lists at least 5 values of the IV
  • □ Stated: 3 repeats + calculate average
  • □ Named specific equipment for measuring IV and DV
  • □ One safety precaution linked to a specific hazard

Criterion C

Processing
  • □ Table: units in headers only, data in order, consistent decimal places
  • □ Graph: both axes labeled with units, even scale, line of best fit
  • □ Interpretation states the direction AND type of relationship
  • □ Hypothesis evaluation cites specific data points, not vague statements
  • □ Error identification names a specific error + its effect on results

Criterion D

Reflecting
  • □ Both positives AND negatives discussed (not one-sided)
  • □ Each point has supporting evidence or reasoning
  • □ The correct factor is addressed (ethical / social / economic / etc.)
  • □ Conclusion references arguments from earlier paragraphs
  • □ Scientific language used precisely throughout

Final sweep

All criteria
  • □ No blank response boxes anywhere (something always beats nothing)
  • □ No contradictions (cross out mistakes, don't leave both answers)
  • □ Command term matched: State = 1 line, Explain = 3-4 lines with reasons
